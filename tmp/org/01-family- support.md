# **Section 1: Executive Summary**

## **Purpose**

Lost Limb Riders, Inc. ("Lost Limb Riders" or "the Organization") is a nonprofit organization dedicated to supporting amputees, limb‑different individuals, and their families through community, mentorship, public awareness, adaptive opportunities, and practical resources—including housing and independence advocacy. Founded by John Thompson — an amputee, cancer survivor, heart attack survivor, recovering addict, motorcycle rider, and motivational speaker — Lost Limb Riders was created from lived experience and a conviction that no one should face limb loss alone.

This Program Operations Manual ("Manual") establishes the complete operational framework for the **Family Support Initiative**, the Organization's signature program designed to provide comprehensive, wraparound support to families navigating the challenges of limb loss. The Manual defines governance structures, administrative processes, program delivery standards, risk controls, performance metrics, and quality improvement mechanisms required to operate the Family Support Initiative at a level consistent with the expectations of board oversight, grant review, corporate sponsorship, healthcare partnerships, and government accountability.

## **Need**

Each year, more than 185,000 amputations occur in the United States. Beyond the surgical event, amputees and their families face cascading challenges: physical rehabilitation, prosthetic fitting and adjustment, mental health impacts, financial strain, employment disruption, transportation barriers, housing accessibility, caregiver fatigue, social isolation, and the long-term work of rebuilding identity and independence.

The healthcare system treats the wound. It does not systematically address what happens after discharge — the loneliness, the financial crisis, the family strain, the confidence collapse, the gap between clinical care and real life. Lost Limb Riders exists to fill that gap. The Family Support Initiative is the Organization's most comprehensive response to this need, providing structured, sustained, family-centered support from the moment of referral through long-term community integration.

## **Program Philosophy**

The Family Support Initiative is built on five foundational principles:

**1. Whole-Person Support.** Limb loss is never only physical. It reaches into mental health, family dynamics, finances, employment, housing, faith, identity, and social connection. The Initiative addresses the full scope of need, not just the medical event.

**2. Family-Centered Design.** Limb loss affects entire households. Spouses become caregivers. Parents grieve for their children. Children struggle to understand. Siblings feel helpless. The Initiative treats families as co-participants in recovery, not as bystanders.

**3. Peer-Driven Connection.** The most powerful intervention after limb loss is meeting someone who has walked the same road and is living a full life. Peer mentors, trained amputees with lived experience, are the backbone of the Initiative.

**4. Motorcycle Culture as Catalyst.** Motorcycles are central to Lost Limb Riders' identity. They represent freedom, independence, and the refusal to be defined by limitation. Group rides, community events, and the riding lifestyle break down barriers and reframe the conversation from loss to capability.

**5. Dignity Above All.** Every interaction, every form, every policy, and every procedure in this Manual is designed to preserve and restore the dignity of the people we serve. We do not pity. We do not perform. We walk alongside.

## **Strategic Vision**

Lost Limb Riders envisions a future in which every amputee and every family affected by limb loss has immediate access to peer support, practical resources, and a community that refuses to let them disappear. The Family Support Initiative is the primary vehicle for delivering that vision. Over time, the Initiative will grow from a single-community program into a replicable model that other organizations can adopt, adapt, and implement in their own regions.

## **Program Goals**

1. **Reduce isolation** among amputees and their families within 30 days of referral or hospital contact.
2. **Provide structured peer mentorship** to every participating family, with matched mentors available within 14 days of intake.
3. **Deliver family education and support services** that address communication, caregiver wellness, children's needs, financial navigation, and community integration.
4. **Establish and maintain community partnerships** with hospitals, rehabilitation centers, prosthetic providers, mental health professionals, schools, businesses, and government agencies.
5. **Create safe, accessible community events** that build connection, visibility, and public awareness.
6. **Track measurable outcomes** that demonstrate program effectiveness to funders, partners, and the communities served.
7. **Build organizational capacity** through trained volunteers, documented procedures, financial accountability, and governance structures that ensure long-term sustainability.

## **Expected Community Impact**

When fully operational, the Family Support Initiative is expected to serve an estimated 100–300 families annually in the initial service area, with capacity to scale as partnerships, funding, and volunteer infrastructure grow. The Initiative will:

- Reduce emergency department visits and hospital readmissions related to post-discharge complications, mental health crises, and preventable injuries.
- Improve family stability by addressing caregiver burnout, financial stress, and communication breakdowns.
- Increase community awareness of limb loss, disability inclusion, and adaptive capability.
- Strengthen referral networks between clinical providers and community-based support organizations.
- Create a replicable program model for adoption by peer organizations nationally.

# **Section 2: Governance Structure**

## **Board of Directors**

The Board of Directors ("Board") is the governing body of Lost Limb Riders. The Board provides strategic oversight, fiduciary responsibility, policy approval, executive leadership supervision, and long-term organizational sustainability. The Board does not manage day-to-day operations; it sets direction, monitors performance, and ensures the Organization fulfills its mission with integrity.

### **Board Composition**

The Board shall consist of no fewer than five (5) and no more than eleven (11) voting directors, as established in the Corporate Bylaws, Article IV, Section 4.02. The recommended launch size is seven (7) voting directors. Board composition should reflect diversity of perspective, professional expertise, and community representation. At minimum, the Board should include:

- A representative with nonprofit governance or management experience
- A representative with financial, accounting, or audit expertise
- A representative with healthcare, rehabilitation, or disability services expertise
- An amputee or limb-different individual with lived experience relevant to the mission
- A family member or caregiver of an amputee
- A representative with fundraising, development, or grant management experience
- A representative with safety, risk management, or motorcycle safety experience

**Note:** Board composition, size, and term requirements are governed by the Corporate Bylaws. In the event of any discrepancy between this Manual and the Bylaws, the Bylaws control. See `DOCUMENT-CONTROLLED-MISSION-STATEMENT.md` and the Governance Framework for authoritative governance language.

### **Board Officers**

The Board shall elect from among its members the following officers:

**Chairperson**
Presides over Board meetings, sets meeting agendas in collaboration with the Executive Director, serves as the primary liaison between the Board and executive leadership, and provides strategic leadership on behalf of the Board. The Chairperson does not have unilateral authority; all governance decisions require Board action.

**Vice Chairperson**
Serves in the absence of the Chairperson, supports the Chairperson in governance duties, and chairs any Board committee as assigned.

**Secretary**
Maintains Board meeting minutes, ensures proper notice of meetings, manages organizational records subject to Board direction, and coordinates Board communications.

**Treasurer**
Oversees financial reporting to the Board, reviews annual budgets and financial statements, ensures compliance with financial policies, and chairs the Finance Committee or designates a committee chair.

### **Board Meeting Standards**

- The Board shall meet at least quarterly, with additional meetings as needed.
- Minutes shall be recorded for every meeting and maintained in organizational records.
- A quorum, defined as a simple majority of voting members, is required for any Board action.
- Board decisions shall be documented in written minutes signed by the Secretary.
- Board members shall receive financial reports, program updates, and performance metrics at every regular meeting.
- Executive sessions may be held as needed for personnel, legal, or sensitive matters.

### **Board Committees**

Standing committees are established by the Corporate Bylaws, Article VI, Section 6.01. The eight (8) standing committees are:

**Executive Committee**
Handles urgent governance matters between Board meetings. Acts only within authority delegated by the Board. All actions reported to the full Board at the next meeting.

**Finance Committee**
Responsible for budget oversight, financial policy review, audit coordination, investment oversight (if applicable), and financial risk assessment. The Treasurer chairs this committee.

**Governance Committee**
Responsible for Board recruitment, nominations, orientation, self-evaluation, succession planning, policy review, bylaw maintenance, and governance culture.

**Audit Committee**
Provides independent oversight of audits, reviews, financial integrity, fraud risk, and whistleblower matters involving finance. Applicable when audit or financial review is required.

**Fundraising Committee**
Responsible for ethical fundraising, donor stewardship, sponsorships, grants, campaigns, and community partnerships.

**Programs Committee**
Responsible for reviewing program performance, participant outcomes, partnership health, participant benefit, program evaluation, family support, education, advocacy, and community engagement.

**Safety and Risk Committee**
Responsible for participant safety, motorcycle event risk, volunteer risk, insurance coordination, incident review, emergency planning, and risk culture.

**Membership Committee**
Responsible for building an inclusive community of participants, riders, families, volunteers, donors, chapters, and supporters.

The Board may also create temporary committees or task forces for defined projects per Bylaws Article VI, Section 6.02. Committee charters are maintained in the Governance Framework (`GOVERNANCE-FRAMEWORK/06-Committee-Charters.md`).

**Note:** Committee structure is governed by the Corporate Bylaws. In the event of any discrepancy between this Manual and the Bylaws, the Bylaws control.

### **Board Member Expectations**

All Board members shall:

- Attend a minimum of 75% of regular Board meetings annually
- Participate actively in at least one committee
- Contribute to annual fundraising efforts through personal giving and cultivation
- Complete a conflict of interest disclosure annually
- Complete Board orientation within 90 days of appointment
- Comply with all organizational policies, including confidentiality, conflict of interest, and code of ethics
- Serve staggered terms of two (2) years, with a maximum of three (3) consecutive terms, as established in the Corporate Bylaws, Article IV, Section 4.05

### **Succession Planning**

The Board shall maintain a written succession plan for Board leadership positions. The plan shall address:

- Identification and cultivation of future Board leaders
- Transition procedures for officer positions
- Emergency succession protocols for unexpected vacancies
- Onboarding process for new Board members
- Annual review of succession readiness

- Manage all staff, contractors, and operational personnel
- Oversee program delivery, partnership development, and community engagement

# **Section 4: Mission Alignment**

## **How the Family Support Initiative Fulfills the Lost Limb Riders Mission**

Lost Limb Riders exists to support amputees, limb‑different individuals, and their families through community, mentorship, public awareness, adaptive opportunities, and practical resources—including housing and independence advocacy—that restore hope, rebuild confidence, and remind every person that nobody is left behind and nobody stands alone. The Family Support Initiative is the Organization's most comprehensive expression of this mission, translating aspirational values into structured, measurable, sustained program delivery.

### **Community**

The Initiative creates community through peer mentorship, family support groups, community events, group rides, and social gatherings. Every element of the program is designed to reduce isolation and build belonging. When a new amputee meets an experienced amputee who is living a full, active life, the message is immediate and powerful: *You are not alone. This is not the end. There is a road forward, and we will walk it with you.*

### **Mentorship**

The Initiative's peer mentor program matches new amputees and their families with trained mentors who have lived experience with limb loss. Mentors provide emotional support, practical guidance, resource navigation, and the irreplaceable gift of shared understanding. This is not clinical therapy. It is human connection at its most direct and effective.

### **Public Awareness**

Through community events, speaking engagements, hospital partnerships, and media outreach, the Initiative raises public awareness of limb loss, disability inclusion, and the capabilities of amputees. Every ride, every event, every speech, and every partnership expands the public's understanding of what is possible after limb loss.

### **Adaptive Opportunities**

The Initiative connects participants with adaptive recreation, physical activity, riding opportunities, and community experiences that demonstrate capability and build confidence. These opportunities are not peripheral; they are core to the recovery process.

### **Practical Resources**

The Initiative provides direct assistance with prosthetic costs, adaptive equipment, transportation, emergency needs, housing advocacy, and resource navigation. These practical services address the real-world barriers that prevent amputees and families from rebuilding stability.

## **Long-Term Strategic Impact**

The Family Support Initiative is designed not only to serve individual families but to build the organizational infrastructure required for sustainable, scalable impact. As the Initiative matures, it will:

- Establish a replicable program model that other organizations can adopt
- Generate outcome data that strengthens grant applications and partnership proposals
- Build a volunteer corps trained to deliver peer support, family education, and community outreach at scale
- Create a network of hospital, rehabilitation, prosthetic, mental health, and community partners that amplifies the Organization's reach
- Position Lost Limb Riders as a national leader in community-based limb-loss support
- Contribute to the broader evidence base for peer-driven, family-centered disability support

The Initiative is not an isolated program. It is the operational foundation upon which Lost Limb Riders builds its long-term capacity to fulfill its mission. Every intake form, every mentor match, every event, every partnership, and every family supported brings the Organization closer to its vision: a trusted national model for post‑limb‑loss community support—connecting amputees and families with peer encouragement, accessible programs, public education, and partnerships that help people live boldly, independently, and with renewed purpose.

# **Section 5: Program Administration**

## **Administrative Workflow**

All Family Support Initiative activities follow a structured administrative workflow to ensure consistency, accountability, and quality. The workflow encompasses intake, assessment, service planning, delivery, documentation, follow-up, and reporting.

### **Workflow Stages**

**Stage 1: Inquiry and Screening**
A family contacts the Organization or is referred by a hospital, rehabilitation center, prosthetic provider, or community partner. The Family Support Coordinator conducts an initial screening to determine eligibility, urgency, and appropriate entry point.

**Stage 2: Intake and Orientation**
Eligible families complete the intake process, including application, consent forms, and initial orientation. The Family Support Coordinator explains available services, program expectations, confidentiality protections, and the participant's rights and responsibilities.

**Stage 3: Assessment**
The Family Support Coordinator conducts or coordinates a comprehensive family assessment covering medical, emotional, financial, housing, transportation, employment, caregiving, and community integration needs. The assessment informs the individualized service plan.

**Stage 4: Service Planning**
Based on the assessment, the Family Support Coordinator develops an individualized service plan in collaboration with the family. The plan identifies priority needs, recommended services, assigned peer mentor, referral resources, and measurable goals.

**Stage 5: Service Delivery**
Services are delivered according to the individualized service plan. This may include peer mentor matching, family support group enrollment, educational workshop participation, resource navigation, hospital visit coordination, event attendance, and emergency assistance.

**Stage 6: Documentation**
All interactions, services, referrals, and outcomes are documented in the family's case record. Documentation follows the Organization's record-keeping standards and confidentiality requirements.

**Stage 7: Follow-Up and Evaluation**
The Family Support Coordinator conducts regular follow-up contacts to monitor progress, address emerging needs, adjust the service plan, and gather participant feedback. Follow-up frequency is determined by the family's needs and stage of engagement.

**Stage 8: Transition and Closure**
When the family's immediate needs have been met and they have established sustainable support networks, the Family Support Coordinator facilitates a transition plan. This may include continued connection to community resources, invitation to ongoing events, enrollment in alumni activities, or formal case closure with a summary of services provided and outcomes achieved.

## **Office Procedures**

### **Operating Hours**

Lost Limb Riders shall maintain clearly communicated operating hours for administrative staff, program activities, and participant access. At minimum:

- Administrative office: Monday through Friday, 8:00 AM to 5:00 PM (or as determined by the Board)
- Program activities: Evenings and weekends as scheduled for events, support groups, and rides
- Crisis contact: A designated emergency contact line or referral protocol shall be available outside operating hours

### **Mail and Correspondence**

All incoming mail shall be opened, dated, logged, and routed to the appropriate staff member within one business day. Outgoing correspondence shall be reviewed for accuracy, authorized by the appropriate staff member, and retained in organizational records as required.

### **Telephone and Electronic Communication**

Staff shall respond to participant inquiries within one (1) business day of receipt. Emergency inquiries shall receive immediate attention according to the Organization's crisis response protocol. All electronic communication containing participant information shall comply with confidentiality and data security policies.

## **Record Management**

### **Record Types**

Lost Limb Riders maintains the following categories of records:

**Participant Records**
Intake forms, consent documents, assessments, service plans, case notes, referral records, follow-up documentation, and closure summaries.

**Volunteer Records**
Applications, screening results, background checks, training certifications, assignment records, performance evaluations, and separation documentation.

**Financial Records**
Budgets, receipts, invoices, bank statements, audit reports, grant files, donation records, and tax documents.

**Governance Records**
Board minutes, committee reports, bylaws, policies, conflict of interest disclosures, and organizational filings.

**Partnership Records**
Memoranda of understanding, referral agreements, vendor contracts, sponsorship agreements, and correspondence.

**Event Records**
Event plans, budgets, registration lists, attendance records, evaluation reports, and post-event summaries.

### **Record Retention**

- Participant records: Retained for seven (7) years from date of case closure or as required by applicable law
- Financial records: Retained for seven (7) years from date of transaction
- Governance records: Retained permanently
- Volunteer records: Retained for five (5) years from date of separation
- Partnership records: Retained for the duration of the agreement plus five (5) years
- Event records: Retained for five (5) years from event date

### **Record Security**

All records containing personally identifiable information shall be stored in secure locations, whether physical or digital. Access shall be limited to authorized staff with a legitimate need. Electronic records shall be stored on encrypted systems with regular backup procedures. Physical records shall be stored in locked cabinets with access logs.

## **Scheduling**

### **Program Scheduling**

The Program Director shall maintain a master program calendar that includes all scheduled activities, events, support groups, workshops, ride dates, and partnership engagements. The calendar shall be updated at least monthly and shared with all program staff and volunteers.

### **Meeting Standards**

**Staff Meetings:** The Program Director shall hold regular staff meetings (at minimum bi-weekly) to review program activity, participant updates, upcoming events, staffing needs, and administrative matters. Minutes or notes shall be recorded.

**Board Meetings:** Conducted according to the governance standards established in Section 2.

**Partner Meetings:** The Events & Partnerships Coordinator shall maintain regular contact with community partners, with at minimum quarterly check-in meetings for active partnerships.

- Participant information shall not be transmitted through unsecured channels
- Staff shall respond to internal communications within one (1) business day

# **Section 7: Family Assessment Framework**

## **Purpose**

The Family Assessment Framework establishes standardized procedures for evaluating the needs, strengths, barriers, and goals of families participating in the Family Support Initiative. The assessment informs individualized service planning, resource allocation, peer mentor matching, referral decisions, and outcome measurement.

## **Assessment Principles**

- Assessments are collaborative, not interrogative. The family is a partner in the process.
- Assessments are strengths-based. Every family has assets, skills, and resources that can be built upon.
- Assessments are trauma-informed. The process respects the family's experience and avoids retraumatization.
- Assessments are comprehensive but not burdensome. The scope is determined by the family's needs and capacity.
- Assessments are confidential. Information is shared only with the family's written consent or as required by law.

## **Assessment Areas**

The comprehensive family assessment covers the following domains:

### **1. Medical Needs**

- Type, date, and cause of limb loss (or anticipated limb loss)
- Current medical status and ongoing treatment
- Prosthetic status: fitted, pending, not applicable
- Rehabilitation services: physical therapy, occupational therapy, other
- Pain management: phantom pain, residual limb pain, other
- Additional medical conditions or complications
- Insurance status and coverage
- Outstanding medical bills or financial obligations related to care
- Transportation to medical appointments
- Accessibility of current medical providers and facilities

### **2. Mental Health and Emotional Well-Being**

- Self-reported emotional status (anxiety, depression, grief, anger, fear, isolation)
- History of mental health conditions or treatment
- Current mental health support (counselor, therapist, support group, medication)
- Suicidal ideation or self-harm concerns (assessed with validated screening tools)
- Substance use history or current concerns
- Coping strategies and resilience factors
- Interest in peer support, counseling referrals, or support groups
- Family members' emotional well-being

### **3. Housing and Accessibility**

- Current housing type (rental, owned, temporary, shelter, other)
- Accessibility of current housing (stairs, bathroom access, kitchen access, bedroom access)
- Hot water availability, functional plumbing, working appliances
- Safety hazards in the home
- Proximity to medical facilities and services
- Landlord responsiveness (if renting)
- Interest in housing advocacy, home modification, or relocation assistance
- Veteran housing resources (if applicable)

### **4. Transportation**

- Access to reliable transportation
- Vehicle type and adaptability
- Distance to medical appointments, pharmacy, grocery, workplace
- Public transportation availability
- Interest in transportation assistance, ride coordination, or adaptive driving referrals
- Impact of transportation barriers on recovery and daily life

### **5. Financial Stability**

- Current employment status (employed, unemployed, disabled, retired)
- Income sources and adequacy
- Insurance coverage (health, auto, disability, other)
- Outstanding debts, bills, or financial obligations
- Awareness of disability benefits (SSI, SSDI, VA, state programs)
- Interest in financial counseling, benefits navigation, or emergency assistance
- Prosthetic or adaptive equipment costs not covered by insurance
- Impact of financial stress on family stability

### **6. Employment and Education**

- Current employment status and workplace accessibility
- Need for workplace accommodations or modifications
- Interest in vocational rehabilitation, job retraining, or career counseling
- Educational status (self or family members)
- Impact of limb loss on employment or educational goals
- Awareness of workplace rights under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

### **7. Children and Family Dynamics**

- Number and ages of children in the household
- Children's emotional and behavioral response to the family member's limb loss
- Need for age-appropriate education or counseling for children
- School-related concerns (attendance, performance, bullying, accommodations)
- Co-parenting or custody considerations
- Impact on family routines, roles, and responsibilities
- Need for family counseling or communication support

### **8. Caregiver Needs**

- Primary caregiver identification and relationship to the amputee
- Caregiver physical and emotional well-being
- Caregiver burden and stress level
- Caregiver access to respite, support groups, or counseling
- Impact of caregiving on the caregiver's employment, health, and relationships
- Interest in caregiver support programs, education, or peer connection

### **9. Support Systems**

- Family support network (extended family, friends, community)
- Faith or spiritual community involvement
- Existing peer support or amputee community connections
- Social isolation level
- Interest in building new support connections
- Awareness of available community resources

### **10. Community Integration**

- Participation in community activities, groups, or organizations
- Interest in adaptive recreation, sports, or physical activity
- Interest in motorcycle riding or riding community events
- Barriers to community participation (transportation, accessibility, confidence, social anxiety)
- Goals for community engagement
- Interest in volunteerism or giving back

### **11. Accessibility Needs**

- Mobility aids in use (prosthetic, wheelchair, crutches, walker, other)
- Home accessibility requirements
- Vehicle accessibility needs
- Workplace accessibility needs
- Community accessibility barriers
- Interest in adaptive equipment, home modification, or accessibility advocacy

### **12. Long-Term Goals**

- Participant's stated goals for recovery, independence, and quality of life
- Family goals for stability and well-being
- Timeline and priorities
- Barriers to goal achievement
- Resources needed to achieve goals
- Interest in mentorship, advocacy, or leadership opportunities within the organization

## **Assessment Procedures**

### **Administration**

- The Family Support Coordinator administers the assessment, either in person, by phone, or by secure video conference, depending on the participant's preference and accessibility needs.
- The assessment may be conducted in a single session or across multiple sessions, depending on the complexity of the family's needs and the participant's capacity.
- A family member or support person may participate in the assessment with the participant's consent.
- For youth participants, age-appropriate assessment tools may be used.

### **Documentation**

Assessment results are documented using the standardized Family Assessment Form (see Section 17: Forms). The completed form is filed in the participant's case record and used to develop the individualized service plan.

### **Reassessment**

Assessments are reassessed at the following intervals:

- At intake (initial assessment)
- At 90 days from intake
- At 6 months from intake
- At 12 months from intake
- Whenever there is a significant change in the family's circumstances (new medical event, housing change, financial crisis, family emergency, or other material change)

### **Confidentiality**

Assessment information is confidential and may not be shared with external parties without the participant's written consent, except as required by law (mandatory reporting, court order, or imminent safety risk). Internal sharing of assessment information is limited to staff and volunteers with a legitimate need to know for service delivery purposes.

# **Section 8: Peer Mentor Operations**

## **Purpose**

The Peer Mentor Program connects amputees and limb-different individuals with trained mentors who have lived experience with limb loss. Peer mentors provide emotional support, practical guidance, resource navigation, and the irreplaceable power of shared understanding. The program is the cornerstone of the Family Support Initiative's approach to reducing isolation and building confidence.

## **Recruitment**

### **Mentor Eligibility Criteria**

Peer mentors must meet the following requirements:

- Be at least 18 years of age
- Have personal experience with limb loss or limb difference (minimum 12 months since amputation or diagnosis)
- Be at least 12 months removed from any active substance abuse treatment
- Be in stable mental health (not currently in crisis)
- Be willing to commit to a minimum 12-month mentoring commitment
- Complete the full peer mentor training program
- Pass a background check (criminal history and sex offender registry)
- Provide two personal references
- Provide one reference from a healthcare professional, counselor, or community leader
- Demonstrate emotional maturity, empathy, reliability, and good communication skills
- Be willing to follow all organizational policies, including confidentiality, boundaries, and mandatory reporting

### **Recruitment Channels**

- Lost Limb Riders events and rides
- Hospital and rehabilitation center referrals
- Prosthetic provider referrals
- Existing participant alumni
- Community outreach and awareness campaigns
- Social media and website
- Partner organizations and support groups
- Motorcycle organizations and clubs
- Veterans organizations

### **Recruitment Materials**

The Organization shall maintain current recruitment materials, including:

- Peer Mentor Recruitment Flyer
- Peer Mentor Role Description
- Application form
- FAQ sheet addressing common questions about the mentoring commitment

## **Qualifications**

In addition to meeting the eligibility criteria above, effective peer mentors possess:

- The ability to listen without judgment
- The ability to share personal experience without centering themselves
- The ability to maintain appropriate boundaries
- The ability to recognize when a situation requires professional referral
- The ability to follow through on commitments
- The ability to communicate respectfully with people of diverse backgrounds, experiences, and perspectives
- The ability to maintain confidentiality
- The ability to manage their own emotional well-being while supporting others

## **Training**

### **Training Program Structure**

The Peer Mentor Training Program consists of the following components:

**Module 1: Orientation and Organizational Overview (2 hours)**
- Lost Limb Riders mission, values, and history
- The Family Support Initiative: purpose, structure, and programs
- The peer mentor role: scope, boundaries, expectations
- Organizational policies: confidentiality, mandatory reporting, code of conduct
- Documentation requirements

**Module 2: Peer Support Fundamentals (4 hours)**
- Active listening skills
- Empathy and validation
- Sharing personal experience effectively
- Avoiding advice-giving and fixing
- Recognizing and responding to emotional distress
- Setting and maintaining boundaries
- Self-care for mentors

**Module 3: Limb Loss and Recovery (4 hours)**
- Types of limb loss: traumatic, medical, congenital
- The recovery journey: medical, physical, emotional, social, financial
- Prosthetic fitting, adjustment, and daily management
- Phantom pain and residual limb pain
- Common psychological responses to limb loss
- The role of peer support in recovery
- When to refer to professional resources

**Module 4: Family Dynamics and Caregiver Support (3 hours)**
- How limb loss affects families
- Supporting the family system, not just the individual
- Communicating with spouses, parents, children, and caregivers
- Recognizing caregiver burnout
- Family support resources and referrals

**Module 5: Crisis Recognition and Referral (3 hours)**
- Recognizing signs of mental health crisis
- Suicide risk awareness and response protocol
- Substance use concerns
- Domestic violence awareness
- Mandatory reporting requirements
- Emergency referral procedures
- When and how to involve staff

**Module 6: Documentation, Communication, and Professionalism (2 hours)**
- Case documentation requirements
- Communication with the Peer Mentor Coordinator
- Reporting concerns and incidents
- Maintaining professionalism in all interactions
- Social media and public representation guidelines

**Module 7: Practical Skills Workshop (2 hours)**
- Role-playing common mentoring scenarios
- Hospital visit protocols
- Support group facilitation basics
- Navigating community resources
- Technology tools for mentor communication

**Total Training Hours: 20 hours**

### **Training Delivery**

Training may be delivered in a combination of in-person sessions, online modules, and supervised practice activities. All mentors must complete the full training program before being assigned to a mentee. Training is provided at no cost to the mentor.

### **Certification**

Upon successful completion of training, mentors receive a Peer Mentor Certification, which is valid for two (2) years. Recertification requires completion of a refresher training (minimum 8 hours) and a performance review by the Peer Mentor Coordinator.

## **Assignment Process**

### **Matching Criteria**

The Peer Mentor Coordinator matches mentors with mentees based on:

- Type of limb loss (amputee-to-amputee preferred, but not required)
- Age range (youth with youth, adults with adults)
- Gender preference of the mentee
- Geographic proximity (for in-person mentoring)
- Lifestyle interests and compatibility (motorcycle riding, sports, parenting, etc.)
- Language and cultural considerations
- Specific needs identified in the assessment (caregiver support, youth support, veteran support)
- Mentor availability and capacity

### **Assignment Procedure**

1. The Peer Mentor Coordinator reviews the mentee's assessment and service plan.
2. The Coordinator identifies potential mentor matches from the active mentor pool.
3. The Coordinator contacts the potential mentor to confirm availability and interest.
4. The Coordinator introduces the mentor and mentee (in person, by phone, or by video call).
5. The Coordinator facilitates an initial meeting to establish rapport, discuss expectations, and set the mentoring plan.
6. The Coordinator documents the match in the mentor and mentee files.

### **Mentoring Agreement**

At the initial meeting, the mentor and mentee complete a Mentoring Agreement that specifies:

- Frequency and method of contact (in-person, phone, text, video)
- Goals and expectations for the mentoring relationship
- Boundaries and confidentiality commitments
- Duration of the initial match (minimum 6 months, renewable)
- Process for raising concerns or requesting a reassignment

## **Mentoring Standards**

### **Contact Frequency**

Mentors shall maintain contact with their mentees at a minimum of:

- Weekly contact for the first 3 months
- Bi-weekly contact for months 4–6
- Monthly contact for months 7–12
- As agreed upon for ongoing relationships

Contact may be by phone, text, video call, or in-person meeting. All contacts shall be documented using the Mentor Contact Log.

### **Scope of Mentoring**

Peer mentors provide:

- Emotional support and encouragement
- Shared personal experience with limb loss
- Practical guidance on daily living, prosthetic adjustment, and recovery milestones
- Resource navigation and referral assistance
- Accompaniment to events, appointments, or activities (when feasible and appropriate)
- Connection to the broader Lost Limb Riders community

Peer mentors do **not** provide:

- Clinical therapy, counseling, or medical advice
- Financial advice or direct financial assistance
- Transportation (unless specifically authorized and insured)
- Services outside the scope of the mentoring agreement
- Any service that creates a dual relationship or conflict of interest

### **Boundaries**

Mentors shall maintain professional and personal boundaries at all times. Specifically:

- No romantic or sexual relationships with mentees
- No financial transactions with mentees (lending money, co-signing, etc.)
- No unauthorized sharing of mentee information
- No provision of clinical services
- No gifts or favors that create dependency or imbalance
- No social media connections with mentees except through Organization-approved channels

## **Documentation**

Mentors shall document each mentoring contact using the Mentor Contact Log, including:

- Date and method of contact
- Topics discussed
- Action items or referrals made
- Participant's self-reported status
- Any concerns or issues identified

Contact logs are submitted to the Peer Mentor Coordinator monthly or upon request.

## **Performance Evaluations**

### **Mentor Evaluation Schedule**

- 30-day check-in (informal, with the Peer Mentor Coordinator)
- 90-day formal evaluation
- 6-month formal evaluation
- Annual formal evaluation
- As-needed evaluation in response to concerns or incidents

### **Evaluation Criteria**

Evaluations assess:

- Adherence to contact frequency standards
- Quality of the mentoring relationship (mentee feedback)
- Documentation completeness and timeliness
- Compliance with organizational policies
- Professionalism and boundary maintenance
- Self-care and burnout indicators
- Progress toward mentoring goals
- Responsiveness to Coordinator communication

### **Evaluation Process**

1. The Peer Mentor Coordinator schedules the evaluation meeting.
2. The Coordinator reviews documentation, contact logs, and mentee feedback.
3. The Coordinator and mentor discuss strengths, challenges, and goals.
4. The Coordinator documents the evaluation results and any action items.
5. A copy of the evaluation is placed in the mentor's file.

## **Removal Procedures**

A peer mentor may be removed from the program under the following circumstances:

- Violation of organizational policies (confidentiality, boundaries, code of conduct)
- Failure to maintain required contact frequency
- Failure to complete documentation requirements
- Conduct that is harmful, inappropriate, or inconsistent with the mentoring role
- Request by the mentee
- Mentor's inability or unwillingness to continue
- Failure to complete recertification requirements
- Criminal conduct or substance abuse that compromises safety

### **Removal Process**

1. The Peer Mentor Coordinator documents the concern.
2. The Coordinator meets with the mentor to discuss the issue.
3. If appropriate, a corrective action plan is developed with a defined timeline.
4. If the issue is not resolved, or if immediate removal is warranted, the Coordinator recommends removal to the Program Director.
5. The Program Director reviews the recommendation and makes the final determination.
6. The removed mentor is notified in writing.
7. The mentee is reassigned to a new mentor with minimal disruption.
8. The removal is documented in the mentor's file.

## **Recognition**

Lost Limb Riders shall recognize peer mentors through:

- Annual Peer Mentor Appreciation Event
- Letters of recognition in the mentor's personnel file
- Public acknowledgment at Board meetings and organizational events
- Milestone recognition (1 year, 2 years, 5 years of service)
- Feature stories in organizational communications (with mentor's consent)
- Certificates of service and letters of reference
- Gifts or tokens of appreciation within Board-approved budget limits

## **Discipline**

Volunteer discipline follows a progressive approach:

**Step 1: Verbal Counseling**
For minor issues (lateness, documentation gaps, minor policy deviations), the Volunteer Coordinator provides verbal counseling and documents the conversation.

**Step 2: Written Warning**
For repeated minor issues or moderate concerns, the Coordinator issues a written warning specifying the issue, expected correction, and timeline.

**Step 3: Suspension**
For serious issues or failure to correct after written warning, the volunteer may be suspended from volunteer activities for a defined period. Suspension is documented.

**Step 4: Termination**
For gross misconduct, repeated serious violations, or failure to improve after suspension, the volunteer's service is terminated. Termination is documented and communicated in writing.

### **Gross Misconduct (Immediate Termination)**

The following behaviors result in immediate termination:

- Physical or verbal abuse of a participant, staff member, or volunteer
- Sexual misconduct or harassment
- Theft or misappropriation of organizational resources
- Falsification of records or documents
- Violation of confidentiality that causes harm
- Reporting to volunteer activities under the influence of alcohol or controlled substances
- Conduct that endangers the safety of participants or others
- Criminal conduct during volunteer activities

## **Termination**

Volunteer termination may be initiated by:

- The volunteer (voluntary resignation)
- The Volunteer Coordinator (performance or conduct-based)
- The Program Director (organizational need or safety concern)

Upon termination:

1. The volunteer is notified in writing of the reason for termination (unless voluntary).
2. Organizational property (ID badges, keys, materials) is returned.
3. Access to organizational systems and records is revoked.
4. The volunteer's file is updated with termination documentation.
5. If the termination involves a participant impact, the Program Director is notified and appropriate participant follow-up is conducted.

# **Section 10: Family Support Services**

## **Purpose**

The Family Support Services section describes the direct services, activities, and resources available to families participating in the Family Support Initiative. These services are designed to address the full scope of needs identified in the family assessment and to support families through every stage of recovery and community integration.

## **Support Groups**

### **Description**

Support groups provide structured, facilitated opportunities for amputees and family members to share experiences, process emotions, build connections, and learn from one another. Groups are led by trained facilitators and follow an established agenda and set of group guidelines.

### **Group Types**

**Amputee Support Group**
For individuals who have experienced limb loss. Topics may include adjustment challenges, prosthetic experiences, pain management, emotional health, return to work, adaptive recreation, and peer support.

**Family and Caregiver Support Group**
For spouses, partners, parents, children, and caregivers of amputees. Topics may include communication, caregiver wellness, grief, role changes, and navigating support systems.

**Youth Support Group**
For children and adolescents with limb differences or who have a family member with limb loss. Age-appropriate activities, discussion, and peer connection.

**Virtual Support Group**
For participants who cannot attend in-person groups due to distance, mobility, or scheduling. Conducted via secure video conferencing.

### **Group Standards**

- Groups meet at least monthly, with frequency increased based on demand
- Groups are facilitated by a trained staff member or volunteer facilitator
- Groups have a maximum capacity determined by the facilitator and room size
- Groups follow written guidelines for confidentiality, respect, and participation
- Attendance is recorded using the Group Attendance Log
- Facilitators complete the group facilitation training module
- Groups are evaluated quarterly using participant satisfaction surveys

## **Educational Workshops**

### **Description**

Educational workshops provide practical information, skill-building, and resource awareness on topics relevant to amputees and their families. Workshops are led by staff, volunteers, or invited subject-matter experts.

### **Workshop Topics**

The following topics are recommended for the annual workshop calendar:

- Understanding your prosthetic: care, maintenance, and troubleshooting
- Navigating insurance and disability benefits
- Financial planning after limb loss
- Communication skills for couples and families
- Caregiver wellness and self-care
- Youth and limb difference: supporting your child
- Return to work: rights, accommodations, and advocacy
- Mental health resources and when to seek help
- Adaptive recreation and physical activity
- Community resources and how to access them
- Legal rights under the ADA
- Housing accessibility and home modification
- Motorcycle safety and adaptive riding

### **Workshop Standards**

- Workshops are offered at least quarterly
- Workshops are free of charge to participants
- Workshops are accessible (physical accessibility, language accessibility as needed)
- Workshops are evaluated using participant feedback forms
- Workshop content is reviewed annually for accuracy and relevance
- Workshop presenters are vetted for expertise and alignment with organizational values

## **Family Events**

### **Description**

Family events provide informal, low-pressure opportunities for families to connect, build community, and experience joy. Events are family-friendly, accessible, and designed to serve the whole family system.

### **Event Types**

- Family picnics and cookouts
- Holiday celebrations (Thanksgiving, Christmas, Fourth of July)
- Movie nights and game nights
- Outdoor activities (fishing days, park outings, nature walks)
- Adaptive recreation events
- Welcome events for newly enrolled families
- Milestone celebrations (recovery anniversaries, prosthetic milestones)
- Community service projects (families serving together)

### **Event Standards**

- Events are held at least quarterly
- Events are accessible and family-friendly
- Events include activities for children and youth
- Events follow the Event Operations standards described in Section 12
- Events are evaluated using brief participant feedback forms
- Events are documented with attendance records and photographs (with consent)

## **Children's Programming**

### **Description**

Children's programming addresses the specific needs of children and youth who are amputees, have limb differences, or have a family member with limb loss. Programming is age-appropriate, engaging, and designed to build confidence, normalize differences, and provide peer connection.

### **Programming Types**

- Youth meetups and social events
- School-age education sessions on limb difference
- Camp and outdoor adventure opportunities (through partnerships)
- Art, music, and creative expression activities
- Mentoring matches between youth and young adult amputees
- Family activities designed for all ages
- Bullying prevention and disability awareness school programs

### **Programming Standards**

- Youth programming is supervised by trained adults at all times
- Youth programming follows all child safety and mandatory reporting policies
- Parental/guardian consent is obtained for all youth participation
- Youth programming is evaluated annually for safety, quality, and impact

## **Caregiver Support**

### **Description**

Caregiver support services address the needs of spouses, partners, parents, and other individuals who provide care to amputees. Caregivers often experience their own physical, emotional, and social challenges that are secondary to but no less real than the amputee's experience.

### **Services**

- Caregiver support group (see Support Groups above)
- Caregiver wellness workshops
- Respite resource referrals
- One-on-one support consultations with the Family Support Coordinator
- Caregiver appreciation events
- Educational materials on caregiver self-care, communication, and boundary-setting
- Referrals to counseling and mental health services

## **Community Outings**

### **Description**

Community outings provide structured opportunities for participants to engage with their communities, practice adaptive skills, build confidence, and experience recreation and social connection outside of clinical or program settings.

### **Outing Types**

- Group motorcycle rides (Ride Forward events)
- Adaptive sports and recreation outings
- Fishing, camping, hiking, or outdoor adventure days
- Restaurant and social gatherings
- Cultural events (concerts, festivals, sporting events)
- Shopping and errand outings for newly recovering participants
- Community service outings (participants giving back together)

### **Outing Standards**

- Outings are planned with accessibility, safety, and participant input
- Outings include clear itineraries, emergency contacts, and safety plans
- Outings are documented with attendance and evaluation records
- Transportation assistance is coordinated for participants who need it

## **Hospital Visits**

### **Description**

Hospital visits are a core Lost Limb Riders activity. Trained amputee volunteers visit new amputees in hospitals and rehabilitation centers to offer support, share experience, and demonstrate that life continues after limb loss.

### **Visit Standards**

- Visits are conducted only through approved hospital or rehabilitation center partnerships
- Visits are scheduled through the hospital's social work, case management, or patient relations department
- Visitor consent is obtained from the patient (or guardian) before the visit
- Visitors follow the hospital's infection control, identification, and conduct policies
- Visitors carry official Lost Limb Riders identification and visitation materials
- Visitors complete a Hospital Visit Report after each visit
- Visitors do not provide medical advice, clinical opinions, or services outside the scope of peer support
- Visitors do not solicit personal information, financial information, or donations during visits
- Visitors represent the Organization with professionalism, compassion, and respect

## **Holiday Events**

### **Description**

Holiday events provide emotional support, community connection, and celebration during times of year that can be particularly difficult for individuals and families coping with limb loss.

### **Event Types**

- Thanksgiving community meal
- Holiday gift or care package distribution for families in need
- Holiday party or gathering
- New Year's goal-setting and vision workshop
- Valentine's Day caregiver appreciation event
- Spring renewal and hope gathering

### **Event Standards**

- Holiday events follow the general event standards described in Section 12
- Events are inclusive of diverse traditions and beliefs
- Events address the emotional dimensions of holidays (grief, loneliness, family stress)
- Events are evaluated and documented

## **Crisis Support Referrals**

### **Description**

When participants experience mental health crises, suicidal ideation, domestic violence, substance abuse, or other emergencies, the Organization provides immediate support and referral to appropriate professional services.

### **Crisis Referral Protocol**

1. The staff member or volunteer who becomes aware of the crisis takes immediate action to ensure the participant's safety.
2. If the participant is in immediate danger, call 911 or the local emergency number.
3. If the participant is experiencing suicidal ideation, contact the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988) or the local crisis hotline.
4. Notify the Program Director and the Executive Director as soon as possible.
5. Document the incident using the Incident Report form.
6. Follow up with the participant within 24 hours to confirm connection with services.
7. Provide ongoing support as appropriate, in coordination with professional providers.

### **Referral Resources**

The Organization shall maintain a current referral directory that includes:

- Mental health crisis hotlines (988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, Crisis Text Line)
- Local mental health providers accepting new patients
- Domestic violence hotlines and shelters
- Substance abuse treatment facilities
- Emergency financial assistance programs
- Housing emergency resources
- Legal aid organizations
- Veterans crisis resources (Veterans Crisis Line: 988, press 1)
- Child protective services (for mandatory reporting situations)

The referral directory is reviewed and updated quarterly by the Family Support Coordinator.

## **Resource Navigation**

### **Description**

Resource navigation helps participants identify, access, and utilize available community resources, government programs, and organizational services. Many amputees and families are unaware of the resources available to them or face barriers in accessing those resources.

### **Resource Categories**

- Disability benefits (SSI, SSDI, VA benefits)
- Prosthetic assistance programs and foundations
- Adaptive equipment providers
- Transportation assistance programs
- Housing assistance and accessibility resources
- Employment and vocational rehabilitation services
- Legal aid and disability rights organizations
- Mental health and counseling services
- Financial assistance and emergency funds
- Educational resources and scholarship programs
- Adaptive recreation and sports programs
- Faith-based support services
- Veterans services and organizations
- State and local disability services

### **Navigation Process**

1. The Family Support Coordinator reviews the participant's assessment to identify resource needs.
2. The Coordinator identifies relevant resources from the referral directory and other sources.
3. The Coordinator provides the participant with resource information, including contact details, eligibility requirements, and application processes.
4. The Coordinator offers to assist with applications, referrals, phone calls, or appointments as needed.
5. The Coordinator follows up to confirm the participant accessed the resource and to assess its effectiveness.
6. The Coordinator updates the participant's case record with referral information and outcomes.

# **Section 11: Community Partnerships**

## **Purpose**

Community partnerships are essential to the Family Support Initiative's ability to serve participants comprehensively. No single organization can address every need that arises after limb loss. Strategic partnerships with hospitals, rehabilitation centers, prosthetic providers, mental health professionals, schools, businesses, faith organizations, government agencies, and motorcycle organizations extend the Initiative's reach, credibility, and resource base.

## **Partnership Categories**

### **Hospitals and Health Systems**

**Purpose:** To establish Lost Limb Riders as a trusted community resource for patients and families during and after medical care for limb loss.

**Partnership Activities:**
- Referral pathway for new amputees to the Family Support Initiative
- Distribution of Lost Limb Riders informational materials in orthopedic, surgical, rehabilitation, and social work departments
- Hospital visit program coordination with social work and case management staff
- Presentations to hospital staff on peer support and community resources
- Participation in discharge planning processes
- Co-hosted educational events for patients and families
- Inclusion in hospital community resource directories

**Partnership Standards:**
- Lost Limb Riders materials are current, professional, and approved by hospital administration
- Hospital visit protocols comply with facility infection control, identification, and conduct policies
- Patient referrals require patient consent
- Lost Limb Riders representatives identify themselves and carry official identification during all hospital activities
- Hospital partnership activities are documented and reviewed quarterly

### **Rehabilitation Centers**

**Purpose:** To connect with patients during active rehabilitation and establish referral pathways for post-discharge community support.

**Partnership Activities:**
- Patient referral for peer mentor matching and family support services
- Presentation to rehabilitation staff on peer support models
- Co-hosted patient and family education sessions
- Inclusion in rehabilitation center resource directories
- Participation in rehabilitation center community events

### **Prosthetic Providers**

**Purpose:** To build relationships with the professionals who provide prosthetic limbs and components, creating a natural referral pathway and partnership for patient support.

**Partnership Activities:**
- Patient referral for peer support and family services
- Distribution of Lost Limb Riders materials in prosthetic clinics
- Co-hosted educational events on prosthetic care, adjustment, and daily management
- Sponsorship and partnership opportunities for prosthetic companies
- Feedback loop on patient needs and service gaps

### **Mental Health Professionals**

**Purpose:** To ensure that participants experiencing mental health challenges can access appropriate clinical support.

**Partnership Activities:**
- Referral relationship with licensed counselors, psychologists, and psychiatrists
- Co-facilitation of support groups (when clinically appropriate)
- Training for Lost Limb Riders staff and mentors on mental health awareness
- Consultation on crisis response protocols
- Referral pathway for participants requiring clinical intervention

### **Schools and Youth Organizations**

**Purpose:** To support children and youth with limb differences and to educate school communities about disability awareness and inclusion.

**Partnership Activities:**
- School presentations on limb difference, disability awareness, and bullying prevention
- Student referral for peer mentor matching and youth programming
- Partnership with school counselors and special education staff
- Inclusion in school resource directories
- Co-hosted awareness events (Limb Loss Awareness Month activities)

### **Businesses and Corporate Partners**

**Purpose:** To build financial support, sponsorship, employee engagement, and community goodwill through mission-aligned corporate partnerships.

**Partnership Activities:**
- Corporate sponsorship of events, programs, and rides
- Employee volunteer programs
- Workplace disability awareness presentations
- In-kind donations of goods, services, or meeting space
- Co-branded awareness campaigns
- Employer education on supporting employees returning after limb loss

### **Faith Organizations**

**Purpose:** To engage faith communities in supporting amputees and families through volunteerism, awareness, and spiritual support.

**Partnership Activities:**
- Community presentations and testimony-style events
- Volunteer recruitment from congregations
- Donation drives and fundraising support
- Facility use for events and support groups
- Spiritual care referrals for participants requesting faith-based support

### **Government Agencies**

**Purpose:** To coordinate with government programs and agencies that serve people with disabilities, veterans, and families in need.

**Partnership Activities:**
- Referral coordination with state vocational rehabilitation agencies
- Collaboration with VA medical centers and veterans service organizations
- Coordination with county social services for emergency assistance referrals
- Compliance with government grant requirements and reporting
- Participation in government-sponsored disability awareness and inclusion initiatives

### **Motorcycle Organizations**

**Purpose:** To engage the motorcycle community as supporters, volunteers, sponsors, and advocates for the mission.

**Partnership Activities:**
- Co-hosted rides and awareness events
- Volunteer recruitment from motorcycle clubs and organizations
- Sponsorship from motorcycle dealerships, gear companies, and manufacturers
- Joint awareness campaigns
- Inclusion in motorcycle community calendars and directories

## **Partner Onboarding**

New partners are onboarded through the following process:

1. **Initial Contact:** The Events & Partnerships Coordinator or Executive Director makes initial contact with the prospective partner.
2. **Discovery Meeting:** A meeting is held to discuss mutual interests, potential partnership activities, and expectations.
3. **Partnership Proposal:** The Coordinator prepares a written partnership proposal outlining the scope, activities, expectations, and benefits of the partnership.
4. **Review and Approval:** The proposal is reviewed by the Program Director and, for significant partnerships, the Executive Director and Board.
5. **Memorandum of Understanding (MOU):** For formal partnerships, an MOU is drafted, reviewed by legal counsel if appropriate, and executed by both parties.
6. **Orientation:** The partner organization receives Lost Limb Riders materials, contact information, and procedural guidelines relevant to the partnership.
7. **Activation:** The partnership is activated, and regular communication and activity begin.
8. **Documentation:** The partnership is documented in the Organization's Partnership Records.

## **Memoranda of Understanding (MOUs)**

### **MOU Requirements**

All formal partnerships involving patient referrals, co-hosted events, shared resources, or ongoing collaborative activities shall be documented through a written MOU. MOUs shall include:

- Names and contact information of both organizations
- Purpose and scope of the partnership
- Specific activities and responsibilities of each party
- Duration and renewal terms
- Confidentiality and data-sharing provisions
- Insurance and liability provisions
- Termination procedures
- Signatures of authorized representatives from both organizations

### **MOU Review**

MOUs are reviewed:

- Annually for active partnerships
- Upon any significant change in organizational structure, leadership, or program delivery
- At the request of either party
- Upon expiration

## **Purpose**

Events are a core mechanism for building community, raising awareness, generating revenue, engaging volunteers, and delivering the mission in a visible, tangible way. This section establishes the standards for planning, executing, evaluating, and documenting all events conducted under the Lost Limb Riders banner.

## **Planning Timelines**

### **Major Events (Ride Forward, Annual Fundraiser)**

| Timeframe | Activity |
|---|---|
| 12 months before | Concept approval, initial budget, date selection, venue scouting |
| 9 months before | Sponsorship outreach, partnership coordination, committee formation |

| 2 months before | Final logistics, program development, media outreach |

| 1 week before | Final communications, supply preparation, emergency kit assembly |
| Event day | Execution according to event plan |
| 1 week after | Debrief meeting, thank-you communications, financial reconciliation |
| 1 month after | Post-event report, evaluation summary, lessons learned documentation |

### **Minor Events (Support Groups, Family Events, Community Outings)**

| Timeframe | Activity |
|---|---|
| 3 months before | Concept approval, date selection, venue confirmation |
| 2 months before | Promotion, registration (if applicable), supply planning |
| 1 month before | Volunteer assignments, logistics confirmation |
| 1 week before | Final communications, supply preparation |
| Event day | Execution |
| 1 week after | Attendance documentation, brief evaluation, thank-you notes |

## **Volunteer Assignments**

Every event shall have a designated Volunteer Coordinator (or Event Lead) responsible for:

- Determining the number of volunteers required for each role
- Recruiting and confirming volunteers for each assignment
- Creating a volunteer assignment schedule
- Providing role-specific training or briefing before the event
- Ensuring all volunteers have necessary supplies, identification, and contact information
- Managing volunteer check-in and check-out on event day
- Addressing volunteer questions, concerns, or issues during the event
- Debriefing volunteers after the event and collecting feedback

## **Safety**

### **General Safety Standards**

- A written safety plan shall be developed for every event
- First aid supplies shall be available at every event
- At least one person with current first aid/CPR certification shall be present at every event
- Emergency contact information (911, local hospital, event coordinator phone) shall be posted and distributed
- All volunteers and staff shall be briefed on emergency procedures before the event
- Incident reports shall be completed for any injury, illness, or safety concern that occurs during an event

### **Motorcycle Event Safety**

- All riders shall wear helmets and appropriate safety gear
- Ride routes shall be pre-planned, inspected, and communicated to all participants
- Ride leaders and sweep riders shall be designated for every ride
- Safety briefing shall be conducted before every ride departure
- Emergency contact information shall be carried by all ride participants
- Weather conditions shall be assessed before and during rides; rides may be postponed or cancelled for safety
- Written route maps and turn-by-turn directions shall be provided to all riders
- Rest stops shall be planned at regular intervals

## **Insurance**

Lost Limb Riders shall maintain appropriate insurance coverage for all events, including:

- General liability insurance
- Participant accident insurance (if applicable)
- Vehicle liability insurance for organizational vehicles (if applicable)
- Event-specific insurance riders for major events

Insurance coverage shall be reviewed annually and updated as needed. Event planners shall confirm insurance coverage before finalizing event plans.

## **Accessibility**

All events shall be planned with accessibility as a core consideration:

- Venue accessibility (parking, entrances, restrooms, seating)
- Communication accessibility (written materials in accessible formats, sign language interpreters as needed)
- Activity accessibility (adapted activities for varying mobility levels)
- Dietary accessibility (food options accommodating allergies and dietary restrictions)
- Sensory accessibility (appropriate lighting, sound levels, and environmental considerations)
- Transportation accessibility (ride coordination for participants who need it)

## **Emergency Procedures**

### **Medical Emergency**

1. Call 911 immediately for life-threatening situations.
2. Provide first aid as trained and appropriate.
3. Notify the Event Coordinator and Program Director.
4. Do not transport a participant in a personal vehicle unless emergency personnel advise it.
5. Stay with the participant until emergency services arrive.
6. Document the incident using the Incident Report form.
7. Follow up with the participant within 24 hours.

### **Severe Weather**

1. Monitor weather conditions before and during outdoor events.
2. Identify shelter locations and communicate them to all event personnel.
3. If severe weather threatens, postpone or cancel the event.
4. If severe weather occurs during an event, move participants to designated shelter immediately.
5. Do not resume outdoor activities until conditions are confirmed safe.

### **Security Threat**

1. If a security threat is identified, notify law enforcement immediately.
2. Follow law enforcement instructions.
3. Evacuate the venue if directed.
4. Account for all participants, staff, and volunteers.
5. Notify the Executive Director and Board Chairperson.
6. Document the incident.

## **Permits**

Event planners shall identify and obtain all required permits, including:

- Special event permits (city/county)
- Road use permits for motorcycle rides
- Food service permits (if serving food)
- Alcohol permits (if serving alcohol)
- Sound permits (if using amplified sound)
- Parking permits (if using public parking areas)
- Insurance certificates as required by permit authorities

Permit requirements shall be identified during the planning phase and obtained at least 30 days before the event.

## **Registration**

Events requiring registration shall use a standardized registration process that captures:

- Participant name and contact information
- Emergency contact information
- Dietary restrictions or accessibility needs
- Photography/video consent
- Participant Release of Liability, Assumption of Risk, Medical Authorization, and Photo Release (for rides and physical activities; see `Doc/Lost_Limb_Riders_Packet/Participant_Release_of_Liability.md`)
- Payment (if applicable)
- T-shirt size or other merchandise selections (if applicable)

Registration data shall be stored securely and used only for event management purposes.

## **Post-Event Evaluation**

Every event shall be evaluated using the following process:

1. **Debrief Meeting:** Held within one week of the event. Attended by Event Coordinator, staff leads, and key volunteers. Covers what worked, what didn't, and what to improve.
2. **Participant Feedback:** Collected via brief survey (in-person, email, or online) within one week of the event.
3. **Volunteer Feedback:** Collected via brief survey within one week of the event.
4. **Financial Reconciliation:** Completed within two weeks of the event. All income and expenses documented and reconciled against the event budget.
5. **Post-Event Report:** Compiled within one month of the event. Includes attendance data, financial summary, evaluation findings, photographs (with consent), and recommendations for future events.
6. **Documentation:** Post-event report filed in Event Records.

# **Section 13: Financial Administration**

## **Budgeting**

The Executive Director shall prepare an annual operating budget for Board approval. The budget shall include:

- Revenue projections (donations, grants, sponsorships, events, merchandise, in-kind)

# **Section 14: Risk Management**

## **Mental Health Crises**

### **Recognition**

Staff and volunteers shall be trained to recognize signs of mental health crisis, including:

- Expressions of suicidal ideation (direct or indirect)
- Self-harm behaviors
- Severe depression or withdrawal
- Agitation, aggression, or threatening behavior
- Substance use or abuse
- Psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thinking)
- Extreme anxiety or panic

### **Response Protocol**

1. **Assess immediate safety.** If the person is in immediate danger, call 911.
2. **Listen and remain calm.** Do not leave the person alone unless doing so would put you in danger.
3. **Do not judge or argue.** Validate the person's feelings without reinforcing hopelessness.
4. **Encourage professional help.** Offer to connect the person with crisis resources.
5. **Contact crisis resources:**
   - 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988)
   - Crisis Text Line (text HOME to 741741)
   - Local crisis hotline
   - Veterans Crisis Line (988, press 1)
6. **Notify the Program Director** and the Executive Director.
7. **Document the incident** using the Incident Report form.
8. **Follow up** with the person within 24 hours.

## **Suicide Prevention Referral Protocol**

Lost Limb Riders takes suicide prevention seriously. The following protocol applies whenever a participant, volunteer, or staff member expresses suicidal ideation or when there is reason to believe a person is at risk:

1. **Do not leave the person alone.** Stay with the person or ensure someone trustworthy is with them.
2. **Call 988** (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) for immediate crisis support.
3. **Call 911** if the person has a plan, means, or imminent intent to harm themselves.
4. **Remove access to lethal means** if safely possible.
5. **Listen with compassion.** Do not dismiss or minimize the person's pain.
6. **Encourage the person to seek professional help.** Offer to make the call with them.
7. **Notify the Program Director immediately.**
8. **Document the incident** in detail.
9. **Follow up within 24 hours** to confirm the person is connected with professional support.
10. **Debrief with staff** to process the event and identify any organizational response needs.

## **Weather Emergencies**

Outdoor events and rides shall have weather monitoring procedures in place:

- Event coordinators check weather forecasts 72 hours, 24 hours, and 2 hours before outdoor events.
- If severe weather is forecast, the event coordinator consults with the Program Director about postponement or cancellation.
- If severe weather occurs during an event, all participants are moved to designated shelter immediately.
- Outdoor activities do not resume until conditions are confirmed safe.
- Lightning protocol: All outdoor activities cease when lightning is observed or thunder is heard. Activities resume 30 minutes after the last observed lightning or thunder.

## **Motorcycle Event Safety**

### **Pre-Ride Safety**

- Ride route is pre-planned, inspected, and communicated to all riders.
- Ride briefing covers route, pace, hand signals, rest stops, emergency procedures, and weather contingency.
- All riders are confirmed to have valid motorcycle endorsement and insurance.
- All riders are required to wear helmets and appropriate safety gear.
- Emergency contact information is distributed to all ride participants.
- Lead rider and sweep rider are designated.

### **During Ride**

- Riders maintain safe following distances and formation.
- Lead rider sets a pace appropriate for the least experienced rider.
- Sweep rider ensures no rider is left behind.
- Rest stops are planned at regular intervals.
- If a rider has mechanical difficulty or medical issue, the sweep rider stops with them while the ride continues.

### **Post-Ride**

- All riders are accounted for at the ride endpoint.
- Any incidents or near-misses are documented.
- Post-ride debrief includes safety review.

## **Business Continuity**

Lost Limb Riders shall maintain a basic business continuity plan that addresses:

- Continuation of essential services in the event of Executive Director absence
- Data backup and recovery procedures
- Communication protocols during emergencies
- Alternative meeting and activity locations
- Critical contact information for staff, Board, partners, and emergency services
- Financial reserve targets to sustain operations during funding disruptions

The business continuity plan is reviewed and updated annually by the Executive Director and Board.

5. Listen with compassion; do not judge or dismiss.
6. Notify Program Director immediately.
7. Notify Executive Director for serious incidents.
8. Document the incident using Incident Report within 24 hours.
9. Follow up with the person within 24 hours to confirm connection with services.
10. Debrief with staff.
11. Review in quarterly quality improvement review.

**Documentation:** Incident Report and Crisis Response Log.

**Quality Assurance:** Executive Director reviews all crisis incidents. Board Chairperson notified of serious incidents. Annual review of crisis protocols.

### **SOP-010: Financial Transaction Processing**

**Purpose:** To ensure all financial transactions are documented, authorized, and recorded accurately.

**Scope:** All organizational income and expenses.

**Authority:** Executive Director

**Responsibilities:** Finance Staff (processing), Executive Director (authorization), Treasurer (oversight)

**Required Forms:** Deposit Log, Expense Request Form, Check Request Form, Purchase Order (for large purchases)

**Procedure:**

**Income:**
1. Receive and record all income (cash, check, online, in-kind).
2. Issue donor acknowledgment within 5 business days.
3. Deposit funds within 2 business days.
4. Record in financial system with appropriate coding.

**Expenses:**
1. Receive completed Expense Request Form or Check Request with documentation.
2. Verify budget availability and policy compliance.
3. Obtain appropriate approval signature.
4. Process payment (check, electronic transfer, or petty cash).
5. Record in financial system with appropriate coding.
6. File documentation.

**Documentation:** Financial records maintained in accounting system. Supporting documents filed by transaction.

**Quality Assurance:** Monthly bank reconciliation. Monthly financial report. Quarterly Treasurer review. Annual audit or review.

# **Section 19: Quality Improvement Program**

## **Purpose**

The Quality Improvement (QI) Program ensures that the Family Support Initiative continuously learns from its experience, addresses deficiencies, and strengthens program quality over time. Quality improvement is not an occasional activity; it is embedded in the daily operations and governance of the Organization.

## **Continuous Improvement Process (CIP)**

Lost Limb Riders adopts a Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) cycle for quality improvement:

**Plan:** Identify an area for improvement, define the problem, analyze root causes, and develop an improvement plan.

**Do:** Implement the improvement plan on a small scale (pilot) when possible.

**Study:** Evaluate the results of the pilot. Did the change produce the desired outcome?

**Act:** If successful, scale the change. If not, revise the plan and repeat the cycle.

## **Quarterly Reviews**

The Program Director conducts quarterly reviews of all program areas. Each quarterly review covers:

- Program performance against KPIs
- Participant feedback and outcome data
- Volunteer and mentor performance
- Partnership activity and health
- Financial performance against budget
- Incident and safety review
- Staff capacity and training needs
- Quality improvement items identified and addressed

Quarterly reviews are documented using the Quarterly Review Form (Section 17.20) and presented to the Executive Director.

## **Annual Program Evaluation**

The Executive Director, in coordination with the Program Director, conducts a comprehensive annual program evaluation. The annual evaluation covers:

- Full-year performance against all KPIs
- Participant outcome analysis
- Financial performance and sustainability assessment
- Partnership portfolio review
- Volunteer corps health and capacity
- Organizational capacity and growth assessment
- Comparison to previous year performance
- Identification of strategic priorities for the coming year
- Board reporting and strategic planning input

The annual evaluation is documented using the Annual Review Form (Section 17.21) and presented to the Board of Directors.

## **Corrective Action Plans**

When performance falls below targets, a corrective action plan is developed. A corrective action plan includes:

- Specific area of underperformance
- Root cause analysis
- Corrective actions to be taken
- Responsible party
- Timeline for implementation
- Measurable success criteria
- Follow-up review date

Corrective action plans are reviewed monthly until the issue is resolved.

## **Board Reporting**

The Board receives quality improvement information through:

- Quarterly Performance Reports (aggregate KPI data, trends, and issues)
- Annual Performance Report (comprehensive evaluation and strategic recommendations)
- Incident and safety reports (as needed)
- Corrective action plan updates (as needed)

The Board uses this information to fulfill its governance oversight responsibility and to inform strategic decision-making.

## **Member Surveys**

Participant satisfaction and experience are measured through:

- Intake satisfaction survey (administered at intake)
- 90-day satisfaction survey
- 6-month satisfaction survey
- Event satisfaction surveys (after each event)
- Annual participant satisfaction survey
- Exit survey (upon case closure or departure)

Survey results are analyzed quarterly and inform program improvement.

## **Volunteer Surveys**

Volunteer satisfaction and experience are measured through:

- Post-training satisfaction survey
- Semi-annual volunteer satisfaction survey
- Annual volunteer satisfaction and engagement survey
- Exit survey (upon volunteer separation)

Survey results are analyzed semi-annually and inform volunteer program improvement.

## **Strategic Planning**

The Executive Director and Board conduct strategic planning on a regular cycle (recommended every 3–5 years, with annual updates). Strategic planning considers:

- Organizational mission and vision alignment
- Community need and demographic trends
- Program performance and impact data
- Financial sustainability and growth capacity
- Partnership ecosystem and expansion opportunities
- Organizational capacity and workforce needs
- Competitive landscape and peer organization benchmarks
- Regulatory and compliance environment

## **Lessons Learned**

Lost Limb Riders maintains a Lessons Learned Log that captures insights from:

- Program successes and failures
- Event experiences
- Partnership outcomes
- Financial performance
- Volunteer experiences
- Participant feedback
- Incident reviews
- Staff and Board observations

The Lessons Learned Log is reviewed during quarterly and annual evaluations and informs continuous improvement.

## **Program Revisions**

Program modifications may be initiated by:

- Staff recommendations based on performance data and participant feedback
- Board directives based on strategic planning
- Funder requirements or conditions
- Community need assessments
- Regulatory or compliance changes
- Lessons learned from program experience

All program revisions are documented, communicated to affected staff and volunteers, and tracked through implementation.
