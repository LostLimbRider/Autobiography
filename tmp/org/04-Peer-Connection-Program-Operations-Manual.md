# **Section 1: Program Overview**

## 1.1 Introduction

The Peer Connection Program is the flagship support program of Lost Limb Riders. It is a structured, scalable peer mentorship initiative that connects amputees, limb‑different individuals, and their families with trained peer mentors who have lived experience with limb loss and recovery.

The program exists because recovery does not end at hospital discharge. It continues in living rooms, doctor's offices, workplaces, schools, churches, garages, and on the open road. Medical treatment saves lives. Peer support gives people a reason to keep living them fully.

This manual provides the complete operational framework for launching, operating, auditing, expanding, and replicating the Peer Connection Program across any Lost Limb Riders chapter — locally, statewide, or nationally. Every chapter that operates the Peer Connection Program must follow this manual without deviation unless a variation is approved in writing by the Program Director and ratified by the Board of Directors.

## 1.2 Program Description

The Peer Connection Program pairs participants with trained peer mentors through a structured matching process. Mentors provide emotional support, practical guidance, resource navigation, and lived‑experience perspective. The program operates through multiple contact modalities including phone calls, video meetings, hospital visits, home visits, public meetings, ride events, and group gatherings.

The program is not clinical treatment. It is peer support — powerful, structured, boundaried, and life‑changing. Participants are not patients. Mentors are not therapists. The relationship is built on shared experience, mutual respect, and the understanding that nobody should navigate limb loss alone.

# **Section 2: Mission**

The mission of the Peer Connection Program is to reduce isolation, rebuild confidence, and restore purpose for amputees, limb‑different individuals, and their families by connecting them with trained peer mentors who understand the emotional, practical, and social realities of life after limb loss.

This mission directly supports the organizational mission of Lost Limb Riders: to support amputees, limb‑different individuals, and their families through community, mentorship, public awareness, adaptive opportunities, and practical resources — including housing and independence advocacy — that restore hope, rebuild confidence, and remind every person that nobody is left behind and nobody stands alone.

Every decision made within the Peer Connection Program must advance this mission. If an action, policy, partnership, or practice does not serve participants, it does not belong in the program.

---

# **Section 3: Purpose**

The Peer Connection Program exists to:

1. **Reduce isolation** by creating immediate, meaningful connection between participants and mentors who have walked a similar path
2. **Accelerate emotional recovery** by providing consistent, boundaried peer support during the most vulnerable stages of adjustment
3. **Improve resource access** by connecting participants with vetted medical, rehabilitative, financial, employment, housing, and mental health resources
4. **Strengthen family systems** by supporting caregivers, spouses, parents, and children alongside the primary participant
5. **Build long‑term community** by creating relationships that outlast formal program enrollment
6. **Reduce hospital readmission and crisis events** through early intervention, consistent follow‑up, and proactive risk identification
7. **Develop peer mentors** by training individuals with lived experience to serve others with skill, compassion, and professionalism
8. **Create a nationally replicable model** that any chapter can implement with fidelity, consistency, and measurable outcomes

# **Section 4: Philosophy**

The Peer Connection Program is built on the following philosophical foundations:

## 4.1 Lived Experience Matters

People who have survived limb loss carry knowledge that cannot be taught in a classroom. That knowledge — of pain, adaptation, grief, fear, humor, resilience, and renewal — is the program's most valuable asset. When shared responsibly, it becomes medicine.

## 4.2 Isolation Is the Enemy

The most dangerous moment in recovery is not the surgery. It is the silence that follows. When a person believes nobody understands, they stop reaching out. When they stop reaching out, they become invisible. The Peer Connection Program exists to break that silence before it becomes a wall.

## 4.3 Recovery Is Not Linear

Progress does not move in a straight line. A participant who seems fine may be drowning. A participant who seems stuck may be processing. Mentors must hold space for the full range of human experience without rushing the timeline, imposing expectations, or measuring success by external standards alone.

## 4.4 People Are Not Projects

Participants are whole human beings. They are not diagnoses, case numbers, or charity cases. They have names, families, humor, talent, fear, ambition, and dignity. The program treats every participant as a complete person — not a project to be fixed.

## 4.5 Boundaries Protect Everyone

Compassion without boundaries becomes chaos. The program operates within clear ethical, professional, and safety boundaries. These boundaries are not obstacles to connection. They are the structure that makes genuine, sustainable connection possible.

## 4.6 Consistency Builds Trust

One‑time contact changes nothing. Trust is built through repeated, reliable presence. The program demands consistency from its mentors because participants deserve more than a single conversation. They deserve a relationship.

## 4.7 Growth Requires Accountability

Good intentions are not enough. The program measures outcomes, tracks performance, collects data, and holds itself to measurable standards. Accountability is not a burden — it is proof that the program respects its participants enough to demand excellence.

# **Section 5: Goals**

## 5.1 Program Goals

1. **Reduce self‑reported isolation** among participants by at least 40% within six months of program enrollment, as measured by standardized intake and follow‑up assessments
2. **Achieve first contact** with every referred participant within 48 hours of intake completion
3. **Maintain minimum monthly contact** with every active participant for the duration of their enrollment
4. **Connect 90% of participants** with at least one community resource within 30 days of intake
5. **Achieve participant satisfaction ratings** of 4.0 or higher on a 5.0 scale across all program surveys
6. **Maintain mentor retention rates** of 75% or higher annually
7. **Complete all required documentation** within 72 hours of every participant contact
8. **Zero tolerance for confidentiality breaches** — maintain 100% compliance with privacy policies
9. **Train 100% of mentors** before any direct participant contact
10. **Establish the program** in a minimum of three new chapters within five years

## 5.2 Participant Goals

1. Every participant will have a named peer mentor within two weeks of intake
2. Every participant will receive a personalized resource referral packet within 30 days
3. Every participant will be offered a family support connection if applicable
4. Every participant will be screened for crisis risk at intake and at 90‑day intervals
5. Every participant will have an updated contact log reflecting all program interactions

# **Section 7: Scope**

## 7.1 Geographic Scope

The Peer Connection Program operates at three levels:

1. **Local:** Within a single chapter's designated territory
2. **Statewide:** Across all chapters within a single state
3. **National:** Across all Lost Limb Riders chapters in the United States

This manual applies equally at all levels. A mentor in Iowa follows the same policies, uses the same forms, and meets the same standards as a mentor in Texas, California, or any future chapter state.

## 7.2 Population Scope

The program serves:

- Adults (18 and older) with acquired or congenital limb loss or limb difference
- Adults preparing for scheduled amputation
- Family members, caregivers, spouses, partners, parents, and adult children of amputees
- Minor participants (under 18) with parental or guardian consent, served through age‑appropriate protocols
- Veterans and active‑duty service members with limb loss
- First responders with line‑of‑injury limb loss

## 7.3 Service Scope

The program provides:

- One‑on‑one peer mentorship
- Group peer support meetings
- Hospital and rehabilitation facility visitation (where permitted)
- Resource navigation and referral
- Family support connections
- Crisis identification and escalation
- Community event participation support
- Follow‑up and case management documentation

The program does not provide:

- Clinical mental health treatment
- Medical advice or diagnosis
- Legal advice
- Financial planning or management
- Emergency medical services
- Transportation (unless separately funded and approved)
- Housing placement
- Employment placement
- Prosthetic equipment or funding

# **Section 8: Organizational Authority**

## 8.1 Authority Chain

The Peer Connection Program operates under the following authority structure:

1. **Board of Directors** — Ultimate governance authority. Approves program policy, major budget allocations, insurance requirements, and legal compliance standards.
2. **Founder / CEO** — Mission authority. Protects founding vision, approves public messaging, and represents the organization when authorized.
3. **Executive Director** — Operational authority. Translates board strategy into action, supervises department heads, and manages program budgets.
4. **Program Director, Peer Connection Program** — Program authority. Directly responsible for all program operations, training, quality assurance, volunteer supervision, and chapter support.
5. **Coordinator** — Operational management. Handles day‑to‑day intake, matching, documentation, volunteer coordination, and participant tracking.
6. **Lead Mentors** — Supervisory authority. Oversee peer mentors, review documentation, provide mentoring support, and escalate concerns.
7. **Peer Mentors** — Direct service delivery. Provide peer support to participants within the scope of their training and authorization.

## 8.2 Decision Authority

| Decision Level | Examples | Authority |
|----------------|----------|-----------|
| **Strategic** | Program expansion, new chapter launch, budget over $5,000, policy changes, legal compliance | Board of Directors |
| **Operational** | Hiring recommendations, partnership agreements, training curriculum changes, annual program plan | Executive Director / Program Director |
| **Tactical** | Mentor assignments, intake approvals, scheduling, resource referrals, documentation standards | Coordinator |
| **Clinical Escalation** | Crisis response, safety concerns, participant removal, mandatory reports | Program Director with immediate board notification |

## 8.3 Reporting Chain

- **Peer Mentors** report to **Lead Mentors**
- **Lead Mentors** report to the **Coordinator**
- **Coordinator** reports to the **Program Director**
- **Program Director** reports to the **Executive Director**
- **Executive Director** reports to the **Board of Directors**

Any mentor, volunteer, or staff member may bypass any level of the reporting chain to report a safety concern, confidentiality breach, or ethics violation directly to the Program Director, Executive Director, or Board Chairperson. Retaliation for such reporting is prohibited and grounds for immediate removal.

# **Section 10: Eligibility**

## 10.1 Participant Eligibility

Individuals are eligible for the Peer Connection Program if they meet all of the following criteria:

1. They are an amputee, limb‑different individual, or family member/caregiver of an amputee or limb‑different individual
2. They have voluntarily consented to participate
3. They have completed the intake process, including consent forms
4. They are not currently receiving services that would create a conflict of interest with a Lost Limb Riders mentor

## 10.2 Priority Enrollment

Priority enrollment may be given to:

- Individuals within the first 12 months following amputation
- Individuals preparing for scheduled amputation
- Individuals experiencing acute isolation, grief, or adjustment difficulty
- Individuals referred by hospital or rehabilitation partners
- Veterans transitioning from military medical care to civilian life
- Caregivers experiencing burnout or crisis

## 10.3 Exclusion Criteria

Individuals may be excluded from the program if:

1. They present a clear and immediate safety threat to themselves, mentors, or other participants
2. They have been removed from the program for cause within the previous 12 months
3. They are seeking services outside the program's scope (clinical treatment, legal representation, financial management)
4. They refuse to complete required intake documentation or consent forms
5. They are currently engaged in active stalking, harassment, or threatening behavior toward any Lost Limb Riders member

Exclusion decisions must be made by the Program Director in consultation with the Coordinator and documented in writing. Excluded individuals may appeal in writing to the Executive Director within 30 days.

# **Section 13: Confidentiality**

## 13.1 Confidentiality Policy

All participant information is confidential. This includes but is not limited to: names, contact information, medical history, family details, personal stories, photographs, intake assessments, contact logs, progress notes, incident reports, and any other information collected during program participation.

## 13.2 Permitted Disclosures

Confidential information may be shared only with:

1. Authorized program personnel who need the information to perform their duties
2. Emergency services when a participant's life or safety is at immediate risk
3. Legal authorities when required by court order, subpoena, or mandatory reporting law
4. The participant themselves, upon verified request

## 13.3 Prohibited Disclosures

Volunteers and staff may not:

1. Share participant information with anyone outside the program without written participant consent
2. Discuss participant cases in public settings, on social media, or with unauthorized individuals
3. Identify participants by name in any external communication without explicit written permission
4. Photograph or record participants without prior written consent
5. Use participant information for personal purposes, fundraising appeals, or media stories without consent

## 13.4 Confidentiality Breach Response

Any suspected or confirmed confidentiality breach must be reported immediately to the Coordinator or Program Director. The Program Director will initiate an investigation within 24 hours. Breaches may result in immediate removal from the program, mandatory retraining, and reporting to appropriate authorities if legally required.

## 13.5 Duration of Confidentiality

Confidentiality obligations survive the termination of a volunteer's service or a participant's enrollment. Former volunteers and staff remain bound by these obligations indefinitely.

# **Section 14: HIPAA Awareness**

## 14.1 Overview

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) establishes national standards for the protection of individually identifiable health information. While Lost Limb Riders is not a covered entity under HIPAA in all circumstances, the program adopts HIPAA‑aligned privacy practices as an organizational standard.

## 14.2 Application

HIPAA‑aligned practices apply to:

1. Any health information shared by participants during program interactions
2. Medical records, diagnoses, treatment histories, or prognosis information disclosed to mentors
3. Referral communications with hospitals, clinics, prosthetists, or other healthcare providers
4. Information exchanged through hospital visitation protocols

## 14.3 Minimum Necessary Standard

Mentors and staff must limit access to health information to the minimum necessary to accomplish the intended purpose. Mentors do not need a participant's complete medical history to provide peer support. They need enough context to be effective and safe.

## 14.4 Training Requirement

All mentors must complete HIPAA awareness training before any direct participant contact. This training covers: what constitutes protected health information, who may access it, how it must be stored and transmitted, what constitutes a breach, and how to report suspected violations.

## 14.5 Business Associate Agreements

When Lost Limb Riders shares participant health information with external entities (hospitals, clinics, referral partners), a Business Associate Agreement must be executed unless an exception applies. The Program Director and Legal Advisor will determine when BAAs are required.

# **Section 15: Mandatory Reporting**

## 15.1 Mandatory Reporting Obligation

Certain program personnel are mandatory reporters under applicable state law. Mandatory reporters include any volunteer or staff member who has reasonable cause to suspect that a vulnerable person — including a minor, an adult with a disability, or an elderly individual — has been abused, neglected, or exploited.

## 15.2 What Must Be Reported

Mandatory reports include:

1. Suspected child abuse or neglect
2. Suspected abuse or neglect of a vulnerable adult
3. Suspected sexual abuse or assault
4. Suspected financial exploitation of a vulnerable person
5. Any situation where a participant describes or implies imminent danger to themselves or others

## 15.3 Reporting Procedure

1. The volunteer or staff member must report immediately to the Coordinator or Program Director
2. The Program Director will contact the appropriate state reporting agency (e.g., Iowa Department of Human Services, Adult Protective Services, or local law enforcement) within the timeframe required by law
3. The volunteer or staff member must not investigate the allegation independently
4. The volunteer or staff member must not promise confidentiality to the reporting party
5. A written report must be completed within 24 hours using the Mandatory Report Form
6. The Program Director will notify the Executive Director and Board Chairperson within 48 hours

## 15.4 Protection from Retaliation

Lost Limb Riders prohibits retaliation against any person who makes a good‑faith mandatory report. Retaliation is grounds for immediate removal from the program.

# **Section 16: Boundaries**

## 16.1 Professional Boundaries

Peer mentors are not therapists, counselors, doctors, nurses, social workers, psychologists, psychiatrists, lawyers, financial advisors, or emergency responders — unless separately licensed and serving in that capacity outside their mentor role. Mentors must never represent themselves as professionals they are not.

## 16.2 Scope of Mentor Role

Mentors may:

1. Share their personal lived experience with limb loss and recovery
2. Listen actively and compassionately
3. Offer encouragement and emotional support
4. Help participants identify goals and obstacles
5. Connect participants with vetted community resources
6. Accompany participants to appointments or events when approved and appropriate
7. Participate in group support meetings

Mentors may not:

1. Diagnose medical, psychological, or psychiatric conditions
2. Prescribe, recommend, or discourage medications or treatments
3. Provide therapy, counseling, or psychiatric support
4. Make guarantees about outcomes, recovery, prosthetic performance, or emotional progress
5. Manage a participant's finances, legal affairs, or housing
6. Provide transportation without prior Coordinator approval and documented authorization
7. Enter a participant's home without a second person present or without explicit Coordinator approval
8. Engage in any romantic, sexual, or financial relationship with a participant
9. Accept gifts from participants valued over $25 without Coordinator approval
10. Provide any service outside the scope defined in this manual

## 16.3 Dual Relationship Policy

Mentors who have a pre‑existing personal relationship with a potential participant must disclose that relationship to the Coordinator before any program interaction. The Coordinator will determine whether the match is appropriate or whether an alternative mentor should be assigned.

## 16.4 Physical Contact

Appropriate physical contact (handshake, brief hug initiated by the participant) is permitted. Mentors must not initiate prolonged physical contact, embrace, or any touch that could be perceived as intimate. When in doubt, err on the side of verbal support.

## 16.5 Gift Policy

Mentors may not give participants gifts valued over $25. Mentors may not accept gifts from participants valued over $25 without Coordinator approval. Holiday cards, birthday cards, and small tokens of encouragement are acceptable. Cash, gift cards, loans, and financial transactions between mentors and participants are prohibited.

# **Section 17: Ethics**

## 17.1 Ethical Standards

All program personnel must conduct themselves with honesty, integrity, humility, and respect. Ethical conduct includes:

1. Telling the truth about the program's capabilities and limitations
2. Representing personal experience honestly without exaggeration or fabrication
3. Maintaining confidentiality except where disclosure is legally required or safety demands
4. Respecting participant autonomy and self‑determination
5. Avoiding exploitation of participant vulnerability for personal gain, emotional gratification, or public recognition
6. Reporting concerns about participant safety, volunteer conduct, or program integrity without delay
7. Accepting supervision and accountability as a condition of service

## 17.2 Ethical Violations

Ethical violations include but are not limited to: dishonesty, manipulation, boundary violations, confidentiality breaches, exploitation of participants, falsification of records, failure to report safety concerns, and any conduct that places participants at risk.

## 17.3 Ethical Review

Reported ethical violations will be investigated by the Program Director in consultation with the Executive Director. Findings and actions will be documented. Repeated or serious violations will result in permanent removal from the program and potential referral to legal authorities.

# **Section 18: Conflict of Interest**

## 18.1 Policy

No program volunteer, mentor, coordinator, or director may use their position to benefit themselves, their family members, their business interests, or any entity in which they have a personal financial interest — except as disclosed and approved.

## 18.2 Disclosure Requirement

All program personnel must disclose any potential conflict of interest in writing to the Program Director before assuming their role. Conflicts must be re‑disclosed annually or when circumstances change.

## 18.3 Examples of Conflicts

1. A mentor who is employed by a prosthetic company and is matched with a participant seeking prosthetic resources
2. A coordinator who refers participants to a family member's private practice
3. A volunteer who uses the program to recruit participants for a separate business venture
4. Any situation where a mentor stands to benefit financially, professionally, or personally from a participant relationship

## 18.4 Management

Confirmed conflicts will be managed through reassignment, recusal, additional oversight, or removal from the conflicting role, as determined by the Program Director and Executive Director.

# **Section 20: Harassment Policy**

## 20.1 Policy

Lost Limb Riders prohibits harassment of any kind within the Peer Connection Program. Harassment includes but is not limited to: verbal abuse, threats, intimidation, unwelcome physical contact, sexual harassment, racial harassment, religious harassment, disability‑based harassment, bullying, hazing, and any conduct that creates a hostile, intimidating, or offensive environment.

## 20.2 Application

This policy applies to all interactions between participants, volunteers, mentors, coordinators, directors, and any other person involved in program activities — including hospital visits, ride events, group meetings, phone calls, video calls, text messages, email, and social media.

## 20.3 Reporting

Any person who experiences or witnesses harassment must report it immediately to the Coordinator or Program Director. Reports may also be made directly to the Executive Director or Board Chairperson.

## 20.4 Investigation

All harassment reports will be investigated promptly. Interim measures may be taken to protect the reporting party during the investigation, including temporary reassignment or suspension of the accused.

## 20.5 Consequences

Confirmed harassment will result in corrective action up to and including immediate permanent removal from the program. Patterns of behavior, threats, physical violence, or sexual misconduct will result in immediate removal and potential referral to law enforcement.

# **Section 22: Privacy Policy**

## 22.1 Information Collection

The Peer Connection Program collects the minimum information necessary to operate effectively and serve participants. Collected information includes: name, contact information, emergency contact, limb loss details, recovery status, communication preferences, referral source, consent records, contact logs, progress notes, and incident reports.

## 22.2 Information Storage

All participant records must be stored in a secure digital repository with access controls. Paper records, if used, must be stored in a locked file cabinet accessible only to authorized personnel. Participant records may not be stored on personal devices, personal email accounts, or unsecured cloud services.

## 22.3 Information Retention

Participant records are retained for a minimum of seven years after case closure, or longer if required by law, grant terms, or organizational policy. Records related to minors are retained until the participant reaches age 25 or seven years after case closure, whichever is longer.

## 22.4 Information Disposal

Records scheduled for disposal must be permanently destroyed using secure methods — digital deletion with no recovery, or physical shredding. Disposal must be documented.

## 22.5 Participant Access

Participants may request access to their own program records by submitting a written request to the Coordinator. Access will be provided within 30 days. Participants may also request correction of inaccurate information.

All Peer Connection Program volunteers must:

5. Complete Volunteer Orientation before beginning any program duties
6. Sign the Volunteer Agreement, Code of Conduct, and Confidentiality Agreement
7. Have no disqualifying criminal history (see Section 24.4)

## 24.3 Experience Requirements

**Peer Mentors** must have:

1. Lived experience with limb loss or limb difference
2. Minimum 12 months post‑amputation or post‑diagnosis stability
3. Personal stability sufficient to support others without becoming overwhelmed
4. Willingness to complete full mentor training and certification

**General Volunteers** do not need personal experience with limb loss. Compassion, reliability, and willingness to learn are the primary requirements.

## 24.4 Interview Process

1. Application review by Coordinator
2. Phone screening (15–30 minutes) to assess motivation, availability, and basic fit
3. In‑person or video interview (45–60 minutes) with Coordinator or designated interviewer
4. Reference checks completed within 10 business days
5. Background check submitted within 5 business days of interview
6. Decision communicated to applicant within 15 business days of completed interview

## 24.5 Reference Checks

The Coordinator will contact each reference by phone or email and ask:

1. How long have you known the applicant, and in what capacity?
2. Would you trust this person to support someone in emotional distress?
3. Do you have any concerns about this person's reliability, boundaries, or judgment?
4. Is there anything else we should know?

Reference responses must be documented and retained in the volunteer file.

## 24.6 Background Checks

Background checks must be conducted through an approved vendor. Checks include criminal history, sex offender registry, and any additional screening required by state law or organizational policy.

**Disqualification Standards:**

1. Any felony conviction for violence, sexual offense, fraud, or crimes against vulnerable persons
2. Any pending felony charges for violent or sexual offenses
3. Listing on any sex offender registry
4. History of documented abuse, neglect, or exploitation of a vulnerable person
5. Falsification of application materials

**Non‑Automatic Disqualifiers:**

A criminal record does not automatically disqualify an applicant. The Coordinator and Program Director will consider: the nature and severity of the offense, time elapsed since the offense, evidence of rehabilitation, relevance to the volunteer role, and the applicant's honesty in disclosure.

## 24.7 Acceptance Standards

Applicants who complete all steps satisfactorily will receive a written offer of volunteer service, contingent on:

1. Completion of required training
2. Signed agreements
3. Final background check clearance (if applicable)
4. Confirmation of availability

## 24.8 Disqualification Standards

Applicants may be declined if:

1. Background check reveals disqualifying offenses
2. References raise serious concerns about safety, boundaries, or reliability
3. The applicant demonstrates inability or unwillingness to maintain required boundaries
4. The applicant refuses to complete required training or sign required agreements
5. The applicant misrepresents information on the application or during the interview process

# **Section 28: Matching Process**

## 28.1 Matching Philosophy

A good match between participant and mentor is the single most important factor in program success. A poor match can do more harm than no match at all. The matching process must be thoughtful, deliberate, and grounded in available information — not random, not rushed, and not based on convenience.

## 28.2 Matching Criteria

The Coordinator considers the following factors when making a match. No single factor is disqualifying. The goal is the best available match, not a perfect match.

**Age:**
When possible, match participants with mentors within a similar age range or life stage. A 22‑year‑old traumatic amputee may relate more effectively with a younger mentor. A 60‑year‑old medical amputee may prefer someone with more life experience. Age is a preference, not a requirement.

**Gender Preference:**
Some participants express a preference for a mentor of a specific gender, particularly for discussions involving body image, intimacy, or gender‑specific experiences. Honor expressed preferences when possible. If a same‑gender mentor is not available, discuss the alternative with the participant before proceeding.

**Type of Amputation:**
Match participants with mentors who share a similar amputation level when possible. Upper‑limb amputees face different daily challenges than lower‑limb amputees. Bilateral amputees may benefit from a mentor with bilateral experience. Congenital limb‑difference individuals may prefer a mentor with congenital experience.

**Cause of Limb Loss:**
Consider the cause of limb loss. Traumatic amputees may benefit from a mentor who also experienced trauma. Medical amputees (diabetes, cancer, vascular disease) may prefer someone with a similar medical journey. Veterans may benefit from veteran mentors. Match with sensitivity to the emotional weight of cause.

**Veteran Status:**
Veteran participants should be offered veteran mentors when available. Military culture, identity, and the transition from military medical care create unique needs that veteran mentors are best positioned to address.

**Geographic Location:**
When possible, match participants with mentors in the same geographic area to facilitate in‑person meetings, hospital visits, and ride event participation. Virtual and phone mentorship is equally valid when geographic distance prevents in‑person contact.

**Personality Considerations:**
Consider the communication style and personality of both the participant and the mentor. A highly energetic mentor may not be the best match for a participant who needs calm, quiet support. A reserved mentor may not be ideal for a participant who needs high‑energy encouragement. The Coordinator should use intake notes and personal knowledge to make this assessment.

**Communication Preferences:**
Match based on preferred communication modality. If a participant prefers phone calls and the proposed mentor prefers video calls, discuss compatibility before finalizing the match.

**Special Interests:**
Shared interests (motorcycles, sports, hunting, fishing, music, faith, parenting, employment field) can strengthen the mentor‑participant relationship by providing common ground beyond limb loss.

**Language Needs:**
If a participant requires a mentor who speaks a specific language, that requirement takes priority. If no same‑language mentor is available, arrange interpreter support or identify an alternative resource.

## 28.3 Matching Approval

1. The Coordinator prepares a proposed match with written rationale
2. The proposed match is reviewed by the Lead Mentor or Program Director
3. For standard matches, the Coordinator has authority to approve
4. For complex matches (crisis participants, minors, high‑risk individuals, dual‑diagnosis), Program Director approval is required
5. The match is documented in both the participant and mentor files

## 28.4 Match Reassignment

A participant may request a mentor change at any time, for any reason, without penalty. A mentor may request reassignment through the Lead Mentor if the match is not working. Reassignment decisions are made by the Coordinator. Reasons for reassignment may include: personality mismatch, communication style conflict, geographic inconvenience, boundary concerns, or participant preference.

Reassignment must be handled with care. The departing mentor should be informed respectfully. The new mentor should receive a proper handoff briefing. The participant should be informed of the change and reassured that their needs remain the program's priority.

The matched peer mentor must initiate first contact with the newly assigned participant within **5 business days** of receiving the assignment. If first contact is not made within 5 business days, the Lead Mentor must be notified. If first contact is not made within 10 business days, the Coordinator must be notified and the match must be reassessed.

## 29.2 Introduction Script

The following script provides a recommended framework for first contact. Mentors should adapt it to their personal style while covering all required elements.

**Phone Call Script:**

> "Hi, is this [Participant Name]? This is [Mentor Name] calling from Lost Limb Riders. I've been matched with you through our Peer Connection Program, and I wanted to introduce myself and let you know I'm here for you.
>
> I'm an amputee too — [brief, relevant personal detail, e.g., "I lost my right leg below the knee about three years ago"]. I know the Peer Connection Program is here to support you, and I'd like to start by just getting to know you a little, if you're comfortable with that.
>
> First, I want you to know that everything you share with me is confidential. The only time I would need to involve someone else is if I believed you were in immediate danger. Other than that, this is a safe space.
>
> I'd like to set up a regular time for us to talk — whatever works best for your schedule. There's no pressure. I'm not here to fix anything. I'm just here to listen, share what I've been through, and help connect you with any resources that might be useful.
>
> Do you have any questions for me right now?"

## 29.3 Required First Contact Elements

During first contact, the mentor must:

1. Identify themselves and their role clearly
2. Explain the confidential nature of the relationship
3. Describe the program's purpose and boundaries
4. Ask the participant about their current needs and concerns
5. Begin building rapport through shared experience (if appropriate)
6. Discuss communication preferences: frequency, modality, best times
7. Establish a tentative schedule for ongoing contact
8. Provide their contact information (program‑issued phone number or email only — not personal contact information unless approved by Coordinator)
9. Answer initial questions within scope
10. Document the contact using the First Contact Log form within 72 hours

## 29.4 Safety Procedures

During first contact, the mentor must assess for:

1. Immediate safety concerns (suicidal ideation, domestic violence, medical emergency)
2. Signs of crisis or severe emotional distress
3. Unmet urgent needs (housing, food, medical care, medication)
4. Any indication of abuse, neglect, or exploitation

If any safety concern is identified, the mentor must follow the Emergency Procedures outlined in Section 37.

## 29.5 Documentation Requirements

First contact documentation must include:

1. Date, time, and duration of contact
2. Method of contact (phone, video, in person)
3. Summary of conversation
4. Participant's stated current needs and concerns
5. Communication preferences agreed upon
6. Follow‑up plan and next scheduled contact
7. Any safety concerns identified and actions taken
8. Mentor's assessment of rapport and match quality

# **Section 30: Meeting Standards**

## 30.1 Contact Modalities

The Peer Connection Program supports the following contact modalities:

**Phone Calls:**
Standard contact method. Available to all participants and mentors. Phone calls allow for personal connection without requiring technology access or visual appearance concerns. Recommended minimum duration: 20 minutes.

**Video Meetings:**
Useful when in‑person contact is not possible. Must use a secure, approved platform. Both parties must consent. Video allows visual connection and can be helpful for participants who benefit from seeing a mentor's prosthetic, mobility, or demeanor.

**Hospital Visits:**
Require facility approval, participant consent, infection control compliance, and adherence to all facility rules. Hospital visits must be pre‑approved by the Coordinator. A second Lost Limb Riders representative must be present or available. Mentors must not interfere with medical care, discuss medical treatment, or provide information beyond their training scope.

**Home Visits:**
Require Coordinator pre‑approval. A second person must be present or immediately available. The mentor must inform the Lead Mentor of the date, time, location, and expected duration before the visit. Home visits must occur during daylight hours. The mentor must not enter a home if they feel unsafe. Home visits are limited to 60 minutes unless extended by Coordinator approval.

**Public Meetings:**
Meetings in public spaces (coffee shops, restaurants, parks, community centers) are encouraged when they meet the participant's comfort and accessibility needs. Public meetings must be in accessible locations. The mentor should arrive first to secure appropriate seating.

**Ride Events:**
Participation in Lost Limb Riders ride events provides community connection and shared experience. Mentors may invite participants to ride events with Coordinator approval. Participant safety, consent, and comfort must be confirmed in advance. Ride event participation is governed by the organization's ride safety policies.

**Group Meetings:**
Group support meetings may be organized by the Coordinator or Lead Mentor. Group meetings follow specific guidelines: trained facilitator present, ground rules established, confidentiality expectations set, no recording without consent, and documentation of attendance.

# **Section 31: Documentation**

## 31.1 Documentation Standards

All program documentation must be:

1. **Factual** — record what was observed, stated, and done; avoid opinions, assumptions, or diagnoses
2. **Objective** — describe events without editorializing or using emotionally charged language
3. **Timely** — completed within the required timeframes
4. **Confidential** — stored securely and accessed only by authorized personnel
5. **Complete** — all required fields populated; no blank required fields
6. **Legible** — written clearly enough to be understood by any authorized reader

## 31.2 Required Documentation

**Contact Logs:**

Every participant interaction — phone call, video meeting, text exchange, email, in‑person meeting, hospital visit, home visit, ride event, or group meeting — must be recorded on a Contact Log. Contact Logs must include:

1. Participant name and ID number
2. Date and time of contact
3. Method of contact
4. Duration
5. Summary of conversation (2–5 sentences)
6. Participant's apparent emotional state
7. Any referrals made
8. Follow‑up actions or next scheduled contact
9. Mentor name and signature
10. Date documented

**Deadline:** Within 72 hours of contact.

**Progress Notes:**

Progress notes provide a narrative summary of a participant's trajectory. They are completed at minimum every 30 days. Progress notes must include:

1. Participant name and ID number
2. Date of note
3. Period covered
4. Summary of participant's stated progress, challenges, and goals
5. Referrals made or resources connected
6. Risk status update
7. Mentor assessment of match quality and participant engagement
8. Recommended next steps
9. Mentor name and signature

**Referral Records:**

When a participant is connected with an external resource, the mentor must complete a Referral Record including:

1. Participant name and ID number
2. Date of referral
3. Resource name and contact information
4. Reason for referral
5. Whether the referral was initiated by the participant, mentor, or Coordinator
6. Follow‑up status: did the participant connect with the resource?
7. Outcome (if known)
8. Mentor name and signature

**Incident Reports:**

Any incident involving participant safety, volunteer misconduct, confidentiality breach, medical emergency, mental health crisis, or policy violation must be documented on an Incident Report within 24 hours. Incident Reports must include:

1. Date, time, and location of incident
2. People involved (participants, mentors, witnesses)
3. Description of incident (factual, detailed, chronological)
4. Actions taken during and after the incident
5. Emergency services contacted (if applicable)
6. Notifications made (Coordinator, Program Director, Executive Director)
7. Follow‑up actions required
8. Reporter name, title, and signature

**Follow‑Up Forms:**

After any crisis, incident, hospital visit, or significant event, a Follow‑Up Form must be completed within 72 hours documenting: the event, the participant's current status, actions taken, referrals made, and next steps.

**Case Closure Forms:**

When a participant's enrollment ends, a Case Closure Form must be completed documenting:

1. Participant name and ID number
2. Date of enrollment and date of closure
3. Reason for closure (participant request, goal completion, inactivity, transfer, safety removal, other)
4. Summary of services provided
5. Final risk status
6. Referrals made at closure
7. Participant satisfaction (if obtained)

## 31.3 Documentation Review

The Coordinator reviews all contact logs and progress notes for completeness and compliance within 7 business days of submission. Deficient documentation must be corrected within 5 business days of notification.

## 31.4 Record Retention

All participant records are retained for a minimum of seven years after case closure, or longer as required by law, grant terms, or organizational policy. Records related to minors are retained until the participant reaches age 25 or seven years after case closure, whichever is longer.

# **Section 32: Risk Management**

## 32.1 Risk Management Policy

Every participant interaction carries inherent risk. The Peer Connection Program manages risk through training, supervision, documentation, escalation protocols, and continuous quality improvement.

## 32.2 Medical Emergencies

If a participant experiences a medical emergency during a program interaction:

1. Call 911 immediately
2. Provide first aid only within the scope of your training
3. Stay with the participant if safe to do so
4. Notify the Lead Mentor and Coordinator immediately
5. Contact the participant's emergency contact
6. Do not transport the participant unless no other option exists and you are licensed to do so
7. Complete an Incident Report within 24 hours
8. Coordinator notifies Program Director within 24 hours
9. Program Director notifies Executive Director within 48 hours for serious medical emergencies

## 32.3 Mental Health Crises

If a participant exhibits signs of a mental health crisis (severe dissociation, psychotic episode, extreme agitation, inability to function):

1. Stay calm and maintain a safe distance
2. Speak in a calm, clear, reassuring voice
3. Do not touch the participant without consent
4. Do not argue, confront, or attempt to reason with psychotic thinking
5. If the participant is a danger to themselves or others, call 911
6. Notify the Lead Mentor and Coordinator immediately
7. Do not leave the participant alone until professional help arrives or you have transferred care to a responsible party
8. Complete an Incident Report within 24 hours

## 32.4 Suicide Protocol

If a participant expresses suicidal ideation:

**Imminent Risk (participant has a plan, means, and intent):**

1. Do not leave the participant alone
2. Call 911
3. Stay on the line with emergency services
4. Keep the participant engaged if safe to do so
5. Notify the Lead Mentor and Coordinator immediately
6. Contact the participant's emergency contact
7. Do not promise confidentiality
8. Complete an Incident Report within 24 hours

**Non‑Imminent Risk (participant expresses thoughts of death or wishing they were dead, but no plan or intent):**

1. Ask directly: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" — this question does not plant the idea; it opens the door
2. Listen without judgment
3. Do not minimize, argue, or promise secrecy
4. Tell the participant you are concerned and need to involve someone who can help
5. Contact the Coordinator immediately
6. Coordinate a professional mental health referral
7. Follow up with the participant within 24 hours
8. Complete an Incident Report within 24 hours

**Post‑Incident:**

After any suicide‑related event, the mentor receives:

1. Immediate debriefing with Lead Mentor or Coordinator
2. Offer of professional support or counseling
3. Clear guidance on continued involvement
4. Written documentation of the event and response

## 32.5 Domestic Violence Concerns

If a participant discloses domestic violence:

1. Take the disclosure seriously
2. Do not promise confidentiality — explain the limits
3. Assess for immediate safety
4. Provide the National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1‑800‑799‑7233
5. Connect with local domestic violence resources through the referral network
6. Notify the Coordinator immediately
7. Do not attempt to intervene in the domestic situation directly
8. If a child is involved, consider mandatory reporting obligations
9. Complete documentation within 24 hours

## 32.6 Abuse Reporting

See Section 15: Mandatory Reporting for complete procedures.

## 32.7 Missing Participant Procedures

If a participant becomes unreachable and there is concern for their safety:

1. Attempt contact through all known methods over a 72‑hour period
2. Contact the participant's emergency contact
3. If the participant is a veteran, contact the VA and Veterans Crisis Line
4. Notify the Coordinator
5. If welfare concern is serious, request a welfare check from local law enforcement
6. Document all contact attempts and actions taken
7. Maintain the participant's enrollment status until confirmed safe or case closure is authorized

## 32.8 Volunteer Misconduct

If a volunteer is suspected of misconduct:

1. The supervisor (Lead Mentor or Coordinator) immediately suspends the volunteer from direct participant contact pending investigation
2. The Coordinator notifies the Program Director within 24 hours
3. The Program Director initiates an investigation
4. The volunteer is interviewed and given an opportunity to respond
5. Findings and recommendations are documented
6. Corrective action is taken based on severity: coaching, retraining, suspension, permanent removal, or referral to authorities
7. All documentation is retained in the volunteer file

## 32.9 Media Incidents

If a participant, mentor, or volunteer contacts or is contacted by media about the program:

1. Refer all media inquiries to the designated Communications Director or Media Coordinator
2. Do not provide statements, interviews, or participant information to media without explicit authorization
3. If a participant's story appears in media without consent, the Program Director will address it immediately
4. Mentors may share their own personal story with media only with Coordinator approval and after reviewing boundaries and consent requirements

# **Section 33: Referral Network**

## 33.1 Referral Network Purpose

The Peer Connection Program maintains a vetted referral network to connect participants with services beyond the program's scope. The network ensures that no participant leaves the program without a clear path to additional support.

## 33.2 Referral Categories

**Hospitals and Medical Centers:**
Acute care hospitals, trauma centers, and specialty clinics in the chapter's service area. Contact: social work department, discharge planning, or patient navigation.

**Prosthetists:**
Licensed prosthetic providers with experience in the chapter's geographic area. Contact: clinic manager or lead prosthetist.

**Physical Therapy:**
Physical therapy practices experienced in amputee rehabilitation. Contact: clinic director or referring physician.

**Occupational Therapy:**
Occupational therapy practices focused on adaptive daily living skills, upper‑extremity function, and return‑to‑work preparation. Contact: clinic director.

**Mental Health Providers:**
Licensed counselors, psychologists, psychiatrists, and trauma specialists experienced with limb loss, PTSD, chronic pain, and adjustment disorders. Contact: intake coordinator or clinical director.

**VA Resources:**
VA medical centers, VA prosthetic clinics, VA mental health services, Veterans Service Organizations, and VA community‑based outreach centers. Contact: veteran patient advocate or coordinator.

**Housing Resources:**
HUD‑approved housing counseling agencies, accessible housing providers, emergency shelter programs, and housing assistance programs. Contact: intake coordinator.

**Employment Resources:**
Vocational rehabilitation services, disability employment programs, job training programs, and employer engagement services. Contact: counselor or program manager.

**Financial Assistance:**
Emergency assistance programs, utility assistance, disability‑related benefit counseling, and charitable funds. Contact: intake coordinator or case manager.

**Transportation:**
Paratransit services, medical transportation programs, volunteer driver programs, and ride‑share accessibility programs. Contact: dispatcher or program coordinator.

**Family Support:**
Family counseling services, caregiver support groups, respite care programs, and family education resources. Contact: program director or intake coordinator.

## 33.3 Referral Procedures

1. Identify the participant's specific need through intake assessment or ongoing mentor conversation
2. Search the referral network for the most appropriate resource
3. Discuss the referral with the participant, explaining what the resource offers and how to access it
4. With participant consent, initiate the referral (warm referral preferred over cold referral)
5. Document the referral in the Contact Log and Referral Record
6. Follow up within 14 days to determine whether the participant connected with the resource
7. If the participant did not connect, explore barriers and identify alternative resources
8. Update the referral record with outcome information

## 33.4 Referral Network Maintenance

The Coordinator is responsible for maintaining the referral network:

1. Update contact information quarterly
2. Verify provider credentials and licensure annually
3. Solicit participant feedback on referral experiences
4. Remove providers that receive consistently negative feedback or lose licensure
5. Add new providers identified through community outreach, hospital partnerships, or participant recommendations
6. Maintain the network in a secure, accessible digital format available to all authorized program personnel

# **Section 34: Family Integration**

## 34.1 Family Support Philosophy

Limb loss affects the entire family system. Spouses become caregivers. Parents worry about their children. Children struggle to understand. Siblings feel helpless. The Peer Connection Program recognizes that supporting the family is not an add‑on — it is a core program function.

## 34.2 Family Meetings

When appropriate and with participant consent, the Coordinator or designated Family Advocate may organize family meetings to:

1. Educate family members about the limb‑loss experience
2. Facilitate communication between the participant and family
3. Address caregiver concerns and emotional fatigue
4. Connect families with appropriate resources
5. Set realistic expectations for recovery and adjustment
6. Provide space for family members to ask questions and express their own feelings

Family meetings are not therapy sessions. They are educational and supportive. If clinical intervention is needed, the family should be referred to a licensed therapist.

## 34.3 Caregiver Education

The program provides caregiver education on topics including:

1. Understanding the physical and emotional stages of recovery
2. Communication strategies for supporting an amputee without enabling dependency
3. Recognizing signs of caregiver burnout and compassion fatigue
4. Self‑care practices for caregivers
5. Navigating insurance, medical appointments, and advocacy
6. Supporting children through a parent's limb loss
7. Managing household adjustments and accessibility needs
8. When to seek professional help

## 34.4 Resource Referrals

Families should be connected with:

1. Caregiver support groups (in‑person and virtual)
2. Family counseling services
3. Respite care programs
4. Parenting resources for families affected by disability
5. Financial counseling for medical expenses and benefit navigation
6. Legal resources for disability rights, guardianship, or advance directives
7. Educational resources for schools and childcare providers

## 34.5 Family Support Groups

Where feasible, the Coordinator may organize family support groups — separate from or combined with participant groups — to provide:

1. Peer connection among caregivers and family members
2. Shared coping strategies and practical advice
3. A safe space to discuss the unique challenges of supporting an amputee
4. Access to program resources and referral information

Family support groups must be facilitated by a trained facilitator (Coordinator, Lead Mentor, or approved volunteer) and must follow the same documentation, confidentiality, and safety standards as participant support groups.

# **Section 35: Quality Assurance**

## 35.1 Quality Assurance Philosophy

Quality assurance is not an administrative burden. It is how the program proves — to participants, donors, partners, and the community — that it works. Measurement is accountability. Accountability is respect.

## 35.2 Participant Satisfaction Surveys

The Coordinator administers participant satisfaction surveys:

1. At 90 days after enrollment
2. At 6 months after enrollment
3. At case closure (exit survey)
4. Annually for participants enrolled longer than 12 months

Surveys measure:

1. Overall satisfaction with the program
2. Mentor quality (listening, respect, reliability, helpfulness)
3. Ease of access to program services
4. Quality of resource referrals
5. Feeling of safety and confidentiality
6. Likelihood of recommending the program to others
7. Open‑ended feedback on what worked and what could improve

Survey results are compiled quarterly and reviewed by the Coordinator and Program Director.

## 35.3 Mentor Evaluations

Lead Mentors conduct formal evaluations of each assigned peer mentor:

1. At 90 days after certification
2. At 6 months
3. Annually thereafter

Evaluations assess:

1. Documentation compliance (completeness, timeliness, accuracy)
2. Contact frequency compliance
3. Participant feedback (aggregated, de‑identified)
4. Boundary adherence
5. Escalation appropriateness
6. Continuing education completion
7. Overall contribution to the program

Evaluations result in one of four ratings: Exceeds Expectations, Meets Expectations, Needs Improvement, or Unsatisfactory. Mentors rated "Needs Improvement" receive a written improvement plan with 60‑day follow‑up. Mentors rated "Unsatisfactory" are removed from active caseload pending review.

## 35.4 Program Audits

The Program Director conducts or oversees an annual program audit reviewing:

1. Documentation compliance across all chapters
2. Training completion and certification records
3. Participant demographics and enrollment data
4. Contact frequency and retention rates
5. Incident reports and resolution outcomes
6. Referral network utilization and outcomes
7. Participant satisfaction survey results
8. Mentor evaluation outcomes
9. Budget utilization and cost‑per‑participant analysis
10. Compliance with this operations manual

Audit findings are documented in the Annual Program Audit Report and submitted to the Executive Director and Board of Directors.

## 35.5 Annual Reviews

The annual review process includes:

1. Program Director's Annual Report to the Board
2. Chapter‑level performance reviews
3. Individual mentor performance summaries
4. Participant outcome analysis
5. Policy review and update recommendations
6. Strategic planning for the following year

## 35.6 Outcome Measurements

The program tracks the following outcome metrics:

1. **Isolation reduction:** Pre‑ and post‑enrollment self‑reported isolation scores
2. **Confidence improvement:** Self‑reported confidence ratings at intake and follow‑up
3. **Resource connection rate:** Percentage of participants connected with at least one community resource
4. **Retention rate:** Percentage of participants remaining enrolled for 6+ months
5. **Contact compliance:** Percentage of participants receiving minimum monthly contact
6. **Documentation compliance:** Percentage of contacts documented within the 72‑hour deadline
7. **Mentor retention rate:** Percentage of certified mentors active after 12 months
8. **Crisis response rate:** Percentage of crises appropriately identified, escalated, and resolved

1. Identified deficiencies and root causes

7. Metrics for measuring improvement

# **Section 37: Emergency Procedures**

## 37.1 Immediate Response Checklist

When any emergency occurs during a program activity:

1. **Assess** the situation for immediate danger to self and others
2. **Call 911** if there is a life‑threatening medical emergency, imminent safety threat, or criminal activity
3. **Protect** the scene if safe to do so
4. **Provide** first aid only within the scope of your training
5. **Notify** Lead Mentor and Coordinator immediately
6. **Contact** participant's emergency contact
7. **Stay** with the participant until professional help arrives or care is transferred
8. **Preserve** facts: note times, actions, statements, and observations
9. **Do not** leave the participant alone if there is any safety concern
10. **Document** the event using the Incident Report form within 24 hours

## 37.2 Emergency Contacts

Every program volunteer must have access to the following emergency contacts at all times:

## 37.3 Escalation Process

| Severity Level | Description | Action | Notification |
|---------------|-------------|--------|--------------|

# **Section 39: Performance Standards**

## 39.1 Response Time Standards

| Action | Required Timeline |
|--------|------------------|
| Referral logged | Within 24 hours of receipt |
| Initial screening completed | Within 48 hours of receipt |
| First contact with participant | Within 5 business days of match assignment |
| Participant inquiry response | Within 48 hours |
| Coordinator response to mentor question | Within 24 hours |
| Lead Mentor response to mentor escalation | Within 4 hours (Level 3–4: immediately) |
| Program Director response to critical incident | Immediately |
| Contact log completion | Within 72 hours of contact |
| Incident report completion | Within 24 hours |
| Documentation deficiency correction | Within 5 business days of notification |

## 39.2 Minimum Contact Standards

| Standard | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| Active participant contact | Minimum monthly |
| Progress note | Minimum every 30 days |
| Risk screening | At intake and every 90 days |
| In‑person contact (when feasible) | Quarterly minimum |
| Group meeting attendance | Monthly when available |

## 39.3 Documentation Standards

| Standard | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| Contact log completion | Within 72 hours |
| Progress note completion | Within 7 days of the 30‑day interval |
| Incident report completion | Within 24 hours |
| Follow‑up form completion | Within 72 hours |
| Case closure form completion | Within 7 days of closure |
| Documentation accuracy rate | 95% or higher |
| Documentation timeliness rate | 90% or higher |

## 39.4 Mentor Performance Expectations

Every certified peer mentor must:

1. Maintain certification through annual recertification
2. Complete all assigned continuing education
3. Maintain minimum monthly contact with each active participant
4. Complete all documentation within required timelines
5. Adhere to all boundary, confidentiality, and ethics policies
6. Attend monthly mentor meetings
7. Respond to Lead Mentor and Coordinator communications within 24 hours
8. Escalate safety concerns immediately
9. Participate in supervision and evaluation
10. Represent Lost Limb Riders with professionalism and compassion

Mentors who fail to meet these standards will receive:

1. First deficiency: Verbal coaching from Lead Mentor
2. Second deficiency: Written notice from Coordinator with improvement plan
3. Third deficiency: Suspension from active caseload pending Program Director review
4. Continued deficiency or serious violation: Permanent removal from the program

## 39.5 Chapter Performance Expectations

Each chapter operating the Peer Connection Program must:

1. Maintain a minimum of two certified peer mentors at all times
2. Achieve first contact within 5 business days for 90% or more of referrals
3. Maintain documentation compliance rate of 90% or higher
4. Submit monthly reports to Program Director by the 10th of the following month
5. Participate in annual program audit
6. Achieve participant satisfaction rating of 4.0 or higher (on 5.0 scale)
7. Conduct at least one group support meeting per month
8. Maintain active referral network with quarterly updates
9. Complete all required training and certification for volunteers

# **Section 40: Five-Year Growth Plan**

## 40.1 Growth Philosophy

Growth must be intentional, sustainable, and mission‑driven. The Peer Connection Program will not expand faster than it can maintain quality. Every new chapter must replicate the program with fidelity — identical policies, identical training, identical forms, identical standards. Consistency is what makes the program credible, fundable, auditable, and effective.

## 40.2 Year One: Foundation

**Objective:** Establish the Peer Connection Program as a fully operational, documented, and auditable program within the founding chapter.

**Milestones:**

1. Complete all program forms, policies, and training curriculum
2. Train and certify a minimum of four peer mentors
3. Establish hospital and rehabilitation center referral partnerships (minimum three)
4. Achieve 50 active participant enrollments
5. Conduct first annual program audit
6. Establish participant satisfaction baseline
7. Build referral network with minimum 15 vetted community resources
8. Launch group support meetings (monthly)
9. Document all standard operating procedures for chapter replication
10. Submit first grant application referencing the Peer Connection Program

## 40.3 Year Two: Strengthening and First Expansion

**Objective:** Strengthen program quality, deepen community partnerships, and launch the program in a second chapter.

**Milestones:**

1. Achieve participant satisfaction rating of 4.0 or higher
2. Achieve mentor retention rate of 75% or higher
3. Certify four additional peer mentors (minimum eight total across all chapters)
4. Launch Peer Connection Program in one additional Iowa chapter
5. Establish minimum five hospital partnerships
6. Conduct 100 cumulative participant enrollments
7. Publish first annual outcomes report
8. Develop virtual mentoring capability for rural and underserved areas
9. Implement participant database for centralized tracking
10. Establish program budget sustainability plan

## 40.4 Year Three: Statewide Presence

**Objective:** Establish Peer Connection Program presence in a minimum of three Iowa chapters and develop infrastructure for out‑of‑state expansion.

**Milestones:**

1. Launch program in third Iowa chapter
2. Certify 12 total peer mentors across all chapters
3. Achieve 200 cumulative participant enrollments
4. Establish minimum 10 hospital partnerships statewide
5. Launch veteran‑specific peer mentoring track
6. Launch family support integration within the Peer Connection Program
7. Develop national training curriculum for out‑of‑state replication
8. Present program model at a national limb‑loss or disability conference
9. Apply for program accreditation through a recognized peer support certifying body
10. Establish state coordinator position for Iowa operations

## 40.5 Year Four: Multi‑State Launch

**Objective:** Launch the Peer Connection Program in at least one additional state and develop the infrastructure for national scalability.

**Milestones:**

1. Launch program in one additional state (minimum)
2. Total chapters operating Peer Connection Program: minimum four
3. Certify 20 total peer mentors across all chapters
4. Achieve 400 cumulative participant enrollments
5. Establish minimum 15 hospital partnerships across all chapters
6. Launch virtual group support meetings for participants without local chapter access
7. Develop chapter launch toolkit for turnkey replication
8. Establish national training schedule (in‑person and virtual cohorts)
9. Publish national outcomes report
10. Establish program accreditation standards and seek initial accreditation

## 40.6 Year Five: National Model

**Objective:** Position the Peer Connection Program as a nationally recognized, accredited, replicable peer support model for the limb‑loss community.

**Milestones:**

1. Program operating in minimum six chapters across minimum three states
2. Certify 40 total peer mentors across all chapters
3. Achieve 750 cumulative participant enrollments
4. Establish minimum 25 hospital partnerships nationwide
5. Launch state coordinator positions in each operating state
6. Achieve program accreditation from a recognized certifying body
7. Develop and publish the Peer Connection Program replication guide
8. Establish formal training partnership with a national limb‑loss organization
9. Present program outcomes at minimum two national conferences
10. Explore strategic partnerships for international expansion
11. Establish endowment or sustained funding mechanism for long‑term program viability
12. Achieve measurable, documented impact on participant isolation, confidence, and resource access

## 40.7 Scalability Standards

To ensure consistency across all chapters, the following standards must be maintained at every stage of growth:

1. Every chapter must use this operations manual without modification unless approved in writing by the Program Director
2. Every chapter must use the identical training curriculum, forms, and documentation standards
3. Every chapter must achieve the same performance standards outlined in Section 39
4. Every chapter must participate in the annual program audit
5. Every chapter must submit monthly reports to the Program Director
6. Every chapter must maintain a minimum of two certified peer mentors at all times
7. No chapter may launch the Peer Connection Program without Program Director approval and a completed chapter readiness assessment
8. Virtual and hybrid mentoring models must meet the same standards as in‑person delivery

## 40.8 Hospital Partnership Strategy

Hospital partnerships are the lifeblood of participant recruitment and program credibility. The partnership strategy includes:

1. **Initial outreach:** Program Director and Coordinator present the program to hospital social work departments, rehabilitation directors, and prosthetic clinic managers
2. **Partnership package:** Provide the hospital with program overview materials, referral forms, contact information, and a point of contact
3. **MOU execution:** Where appropriate, execute a Memorandum of Understanding defining the referral process, consent procedures, and mutual expectations
4. **Ongoing relationship:** Quarterly check‑ins with hospital partners, annual program updates, and invitation to program events
5. **Feedback loop:** Solicit hospital partner feedback on referral experience, participant outcomes, and program value
6. **Expansion:** Replicate the hospital partnership model in every new chapter territory

## 40.9 Virtual Mentoring Strategy

Virtual mentoring extends the program's reach to rural areas, underserved communities, and participants with transportation or mobility barriers. Virtual mentoring standards:

1. Virtual mentors complete the same training and certification as in‑person mentors
2. Virtual contacts are documented identically to in‑person contacts
3. Virtual meetings must use secure, approved platforms
4. Crisis protocols apply equally to virtual interactions
5. Virtual mentors participate in monthly meetings and supervision through video conference
6. Virtual group meetings are facilitated with the same standards as in‑person groups
7. Virtual mentoring is not a lesser service — it is a different modality with equal dignity and accountability

## 40.10 State Coordinator Role

As the program expands to multiple chapters within a state, a State Coordinator position is established to:

1. Oversee program operations across all chapters within the state
2. Ensure consistency and fidelity to the operations manual
3. Coordinate state‑level hospital partnerships and referral networks
4. Facilitate statewide mentor training cohorts
5. Compile state‑level data and reporting
6. Represent the program to state agencies, healthcare systems, and media
7. Support chapter leaders with training, troubleshooting, and resource coordination
8. Report to the Program Director

## 40.11 National Expansion Criteria

Before launching the Peer Connection Program in a new state, the following criteria must be met:

1. A chapter leader or state champion has been identified and approved
2. The chapter leader has completed the Peer Connection Program Coordinator Training
3. A minimum of two prospective peer mentors have been identified and are enrolled in training
4. At least one hospital or rehabilitation center partnership has been initiated
5. The chapter has completed the Chapter Readiness Assessment
6. The Program Director has approved the launch plan
7. The chapter has committed to all standards in this operations manual
8. Insurance and legal compliance have been confirmed for the new state
