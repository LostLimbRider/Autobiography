# **Chapter 5: Losing My Way**

After the illness, I thought I was invincible again.

I wasn’t.

The doctors told me I was in remission. The guys at work threw me a party. Genie cried when she heard the news. The girls made me a card with crayon drawings and crooked letters that spelled out *We love you Daddy*. Everything looked like it was supposed to be fine.

But I didn’t feel fine.

The thing nobody tells you about surviving something terrible is that the scars don’t all show. They cut the tumor out of my body, but they couldn’t cut the fear out of my head. It stayed there, curled up like a snake, waiting. It talked to me in the quiet moments. It told me I was weak. That it could come back at any time. That I was living on borrowed time and the bank was going to call in the debt.

I didn’t know how to handle those voices. So I drowned them out.

It started small. A beer after work. Then two. Then a six‑pack. I told myself I deserved it. I’d been through hell. I’d earned the right to let loose a little. And for a while, it worked. The beer quieted the voices. It let me sleep without staring at the ceiling. It let me laugh without the fear creeping in.

But it didn’t stop at beer.

I don’t even remember who gave me the first pill. Some guy at the plant, probably. A little white tablet he said would take the edge off. I said okay and swallowed it and waited. When it hit, I understood. The noise in my head didn’t just quiet down — it disappeared. For a few hours, I felt like the old John. The John before the diagnosis. The John who didn’t lie awake wondering if every ache and pain was the cancer coming back.

I chased that feeling.

It didn’t take long for the habit to dig its claws in. I started keeping pills in my truck, in my toolbox, in the back of my sock drawer. I’d pop one before work, during lunch, after dinner. I told myself I had it under control. I told myself I could stop anytime. I told everyone who asked that I was fine.

I wasn’t fine.

Genie noticed first. She always noticed. She’d look at me across the dinner table and I’d see her studying me, trying to figure out what was different. I’d make sure to meet her eyes and smile, but she wasn’t buying it. One night she said:

> “John, you seem different lately. Distant. Are you okay?”

I said:

> “I’m fine. Just tired.”

She said:

> “You’re always tired lately.”

I said:

> “I work twelve hours a day. I’m supposed to be tired.”

She let it drop. But I could see she wasn’t convinced.

The thing about addiction is that it makes you an expert liar. You learn how to hide things. You learn how to read people’s faces and tell them exactly what they want to hear. You learn how to keep the people who love you at arm’s length, because if they get too close, they’ll see what you don’t want them to see.

I’d come home and kiss Genie on the cheek and go straight to the bathroom and lock the door. I’d take a pill, wash it down, look at myself in the mirror, and tell myself I deserved this. I’d been through so much. I just needed something to take the edge off. There was nothing wrong with that.

But there was something wrong. I knew it. And every night I’d lie in bed and promise myself I’d stop tomorrow. And every morning I’d wake up and reach for the bottle beside my bed.

One night, Genie found the pills.

I had left my jacket draped over a chair in the kitchen. She went to hang it up and something fell out of the pocket. A little orange bottle with no label. She opened it and found the pills and when I walked in, she was standing there holding them.

She said:

> “What are these?”

I said:

> “Nothing. Just something for my back.”

She said:

> “Your back doesn’t hurt, John. I’ve never heard you complain about your back.”

I said:

> “Well it does. Okay?”

I could hear the lie in my own voice. It was thin and obvious and it embarrassed me. But I couldn’t stop. I couldn’t tell her the truth. I couldn’t admit what I’d become.

She said:

> “John, I’m not stupid. I know something’s wrong. Please talk to me.”

I didn’t talk to her. I got angry. I said things I shouldn’t have said. I told her to mind her own business. I told her she didn’t understand. I grabbed the bottle out of her hand and walked out of the house.

I drove around for hours that night. I ended up at a bar I’d never been to before, a place on the edge of town where nobody knew my name. I sat at the counter and drank whiskey until my thoughts went blurry. I thought about Genie’s face when she’d found those pills. I thought about what I’d said to her. I thought about what a worthless piece of garbage I was.

But I didn’t go home. I stayed until the bartender told me I’d had enough. I stumbled out to my truck and slept in the front seat. When I woke up, the sun was coming up and I was shivering and I felt like I’d been hit by a train.

I went home. Genie was sitting at the kitchen table, still in her robe, a cup of coffee in front of her. The coffee was cold. She’d been sitting there all night. She looked at me and didn’t say anything. That was worse than if she’d screamed.

I said:

> “I’m sorry.”

She looked at me for a long time. Then she said:

> “I know you are. But sorry isn’t enough this time.”

I hit bottom a few weeks later. I don’t mean that as a metaphor. I mean there was a moment where I looked at myself and didn’t know who I was anymore. I was standing in the bathroom, shaking, sweating, needing a fix. I looked in the mirror and saw a stranger. Hollow eyes. Gray skin. A man I didn’t recognize.

I thought about my daughters. I thought about them growing up and remembering me like this. I thought about them telling their own children about their father — the man who couldn’t stop. The man who chose pills over his family.

I sat down on the bathroom floor and I cried. I hadn’t cried since I was a kid. I sat there and sobbed like a child, and I prayed. I hadn’t prayed in years. I said:

> “God, if you’re real, help me. I can’t do this alone.”

Nothing happened. No voice from heaven. No sudden clarity. But something shifted. Some small part of me decided that I was done. Not because I wanted to be. Because I had run out of other options.

I walked out of the bathroom and found Genie. I told her everything. The pills. The lies. The mornings I couldn’t get out of bed without something in my system. The nights I prayed I wouldn’t wake up. I told her the truth, every ugly piece of it.

She didn’t say much. She listened. When I was done, she said:

> “I’ll call a program in the morning.”

I said:

> “I don’t know if I can do it.”

She said:

> “That’s fine. You don’t have to know. You just have to try.”

Getting clean was harder than the cancer treatments. At least with the cancer, there was a plan. A schedule. A team of doctors telling me what to do and when to do it. With addiction, there was just me and the empty space where the drugs used to be. The cravings hit like waves. I’d be fine for an hour, then I’d be drowning. Genie would hold me and tell me to breathe.

I went to meetings. I sat in folding chairs and listened to other people talk about their own battles. I heard stories that sounded like mine. I felt less alone. I started to believe that maybe I could make it through.

But I also learned something important about myself. I learned that I had been running from the fear instead of facing it. The cancer had scared me in a way I didn’t want to admit. I thought I could drink and drug my way past it. But you can’t outrun fear. It will always catch up.

The only way out was through.

I’ve been clean for years now. But I still think about those days sometimes. I think about the man I used to be — the one who lied and stole and hurt the people he loved. I don’t pretend that man doesn’t exist. He’s part of me. But he’s not who I am anymore.

I got clean because I had to. Not because I wanted to. Not because I was ready. Because I had run out of other options. And sometimes that’s what it takes. Sometimes you have to lose everything before you can start to find yourself again.

That was the hardest thing I ever did.

Until the next thing.
