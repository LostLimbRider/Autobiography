# **Chapter 15: The Lost Limb Riders**

The first time I heard about them, I almost didn't go.

It was a Tuesday afternoon, and I was sitting in a support group at the rehab center. One of those mandatory meetings they make you attend after the amputation, where everyone sits in a circle and talks about their feelings. I hated those meetings. I'm not a feelings guy. Not in a group setting, anyway. I'd rather sit in a garage with a buddy and drink a beer and not talk about anything important than sit in a circle and talk about my feelings with strangers.

But I went. Because they told me it would help. And I was desperate enough to try anything.

There was a guy in that group named Mike. Mike was maybe fifty years old, built like a refrigerator, with a gravelly voice and a laugh that sounded like a cement mixer. He'd lost his left arm in a construction accident about ten years before I met him. He was sitting across from me in the circle, wearing a T‑shirt that said *Lost Limb Riders* across the front, with a picture of a motorcycle and a skeleton riding it, missing one leg.

I stared at the shirt for half the meeting. Finally, after everyone was done sharing, I pointed at him and said:

> What's that?

He looked down at his shirt like he'd forgotten he was wearing it.

> Lost Limb Riders, he said. Group I'm in. Amputees who ride motorcycles.

I snorted. I couldn't help it.

> You ride? I asked. With one arm?

He grinned. Held up his prosthetic arm — a hook attachment, old‑school, the kind you see in pirate movies.

> Damn right I ride, he said. You don't need two arms to work a throttle. You need balance, a good bike, and a stubborn streak. You got any of that?

I told him I had a stubborn streak the size of Texas. He laughed that cement‑mixer laugh and said:

> Then you should come to a meeting. See what you think.

I took his number. Stuck it in my pocket. Told myself I'd call. I didn't call. Not that week, not the next. Not because I didn't want to. Because I didn't know how to show up to something like that. I wasn't a joiner. I'd never been part of a club, a team, a fraternity. I was a solo operator. Always had been. The idea of walking into a room full of strangers — amputees, no less — and trying to fit in felt harder than the physical therapy.

But Mike kept showing up at the support group. Every Tuesday. And every Tuesday, he'd look at me and say:

> You call that number yet?

And every Tuesday, I'd say:

> Not yet.

And he'd say:

> You will.

He was right. After about a month, I finally called. I don't know what changed. Maybe I was lonely. Maybe I was curious. Maybe I was tired of sitting in my house feeling sorry for myself. But I called the number, and a woman named Diane answered. She told me they were meeting that Saturday at a diner on the south side of town. Ten o'clock. Breakfast first, then a ride.

I showed up at nine forty-five. Sat in my truck in the parking lot for twenty minutes, trying to talk myself into walking through the door. I almost left three times. I had my hand on the ignition key. I was ready to go home, climb back into bed, and pretend I'd never made the call.

But I thought about Mike. His laugh. His hook hand. The way he said *damn right I ride* like it was the most obvious thing in the world. If he could do it — one arm, ten years after his accident — I could at least walk through a diner door.

So I did.

The first thing I noticed was the laughter. I heard it before I saw anything. I walked in and there was a group of maybe fifteen people in the back corner, pushed two tables together, and they were laughing. Not polite laughter. Not the kind of laughter you do at a work function to be nice. Real laughter. The kind that comes from the gut. The kind that makes you lean back and slap the table. A woman at the end of the table was telling a story about trying to put on her prosthetic leg in a moving car, and by the time she got to the punchline — something about her leg falling out the window at a toll booth — half the table was crying.

I stood there for a second, just watching. They were missing every combination of limbs you could imagine. Arms. Legs. Hands. Feet. Some were in wheelchairs. Some had canes. Some walked fine with prosthetics you'd barely notice if you weren't looking. One guy had a blade runner foot, carbon fiber, the kind you see in the Paralympics. Another guy had a prosthetic arm with a custom‑painted skull on the bicep.

And they were laughing. Having breakfast. Living their lives.

Mike saw me standing there and waved me over with his hook.

> There he is, he said. The stubborn son of a bitch I told you about.

He pulled out a chair next to him and I sat down. Diane handed me a menu. Someone pushed a pot of coffee toward me. Nobody stared at my leg. Nobody asked awkward questions like *what happened to you?* or *does it hurt?* Nobody treated me like I was broken. They treated me like I was one of them. Because I was. I just didn't know it yet.

I learned a lot that morning. I learned that the Lost Limb Riders started as a small group of amputee veterans who wanted to keep riding after their injuries. It grew from there. Now it's a national organization, with chapters all over the country. They ride together, raise money for amputee programs, visit people in the hospital who've just lost limbs, and remind each other that life isn't over. It's different. But it's not over.

I learned that Diane lost her leg to cancer when she was twenty‑two. She was a college athlete, track and field. She thought her life was over. Now she rides a Harley and runs a nonprofit. The guy with the skull bicep, Jerry, lost his arm in a factory accident. He was a mechanic before. Now he builds custom bikes with modified controls for amputees. The woman who told the story about her leg falling out the window, Sharon, lost both legs below the knee to a blood infection. She rides a trike now, and she's faster than half the people on two wheels.

These were my people. I felt it in my bones.

After breakfast, they asked if I wanted to ride. I told them I didn't have a bike. Mike looked at me like I'd said something ridiculous.

> That's what the garage is for, he said. Jerry's got a loaner. Old Honda. Needs some work, but it runs. You ride it today, you figure out if you like it. If you do, we help you find your own.

So I rode. A beat-up Honda with a slightly crooked handlebar and a seat that had seen better days. I followed the group out of the diner parking lot, onto the highway, through the back roads outside town. Fifteen amputees on motorcycles, riding in formation. Some with prosthetics. Some with adaptive controls. All of them moving forward, together.

I won't lie to you. It wasn't easy. My balance was off. My prosthetic leg didn't bend the way a real leg bends, so shifting gears took concentration. I had to think about every movement, every turn, every stop. But somewhere around the third mile, something clicked. The sun was warm. The wind was loud. The road was open. And for the first time in months — maybe years — I felt free.

I felt whole.

Not in spite of what I'd lost. But because of where I was and who I was with. These people understood. They didn't need me to explain. They looked at my prosthetic and they didn't stare. They didn't ask if I was okay. They knew I'd been through hell, because they'd been through their own versions of it. And they were still here. Still riding. Still laughing.

That first ride changed something in me. It gave me a picture of what my future could look like. Not a future of limitation and loss. A future of movement and community and purpose. The Lost Limb Riders became my tribe. We ride together. We support each other. We show up for each other when things get hard. And we visit other amputees — people who are just starting their journey, still raw, still scared, still wondering if they'll ever feel normal again — and we show them what's possible.

That's the thing I didn't know when I lost my leg. There's a whole community of people out there who have been through it. And they're not sitting around feeling sorry for themselves. They're living. They're thriving. They're riding.

Finding the Lost Limb Riders saved my life in a different way than the surgeries did. The surgeries kept me alive. The Lost Limb Riders gave me a reason to stay that way. They gave me a mission. If I could help one person — just one — who just lost their leg see that they can still ride, still laugh, still live, still love their life — then all of this was worth it.

That's what I tell the new guys now. The ones I visit in the hospital. The ones who look at me the way I looked at Mike that first day in the support group. I tell them:

> It's going to get better. Not right away. Not all at once. But it's going to get better. You're going to find your people. You're going to find your ride. And one day, you're going to look back at this moment and realize it was the beginning of something, not the end.

I never thought I'd be the guy saying that. But here I am. A Lost Limb Rider. Riding with my tribe. Moving forward. Not in spite of what I lost, but because of it.
