# **Chapter 1: Growing Up**

My father woke up at 4:30 every morning.

I’d hear his boots hit the floor before the sun even thought about coming up. The floorboards in the hallway creaked in the same spot every time — third board from the bedroom door. Then the coffee pot would start perking, and the smell would drift back to my room. That’s how I knew it was time to get up, even on Saturdays.

He was a maintenance worker for the city of Grinnell. Mowers, tools, grease, grass clippings that stuck to his forearms like glitter you couldn’t wash off. He came home with dirt under his fingernails and grease embedded in his knuckles. I watched him leave for work in the dark and come home in the dark, day after day, year after year. He never complained about it. He never asked if it was fair. He just did what needed to be done.

Once, he took me along on a Saturday. The place was the city shop — not loud like a machine shop, but full of the hum of equipment waiting to be fixed. The air smelled like gasoline and cut grass. I stood in the doorway and watched my father work on a mower. He didn’t know I was watching. He was in his element, hands moving with a kind of economy I didn’t recognize at the time. No wasted motion. Just work.

I never told him this, but standing in that doorway watching him was the first time I was proud of him in a way I could name. Not because of what he did, but because of how he did it. Like it mattered to him. Like he cared about getting it right even when nobody was watching.

One morning, I must have been about eight, I asked him:

> “Dad, don’t you ever get tired?”

He looked at me and said:

> “Every day. But being tired doesn’t mean you stop.”

That was the closest thing to a philosophy lesson I ever got from him. And I didn’t fully understand it until I was a grown man, flat on my back in a hospital bed, trying to find a reason to keep fighting.

I remember one Saturday he was fixing the sink in our bathroom. The pipe under the cabinet had been dripping for weeks. He was on his back, half inside the cabinet, wrench in hand, muttering. I sat in the doorway watching. After a while, he handed me a flashlight and told me to hold it steady.

I held it for what felt like an hour. My arm ached. I wanted to quit. But he kept working, so I kept holding.

When he finished, he stood up, wiped his hands on a rag, and said:

> “Next time, you’re handing me the tools.”

He didn’t say “good job.” He didn’t have to. The fact that he expected me to do it again next time meant I’d done it right.

My mother was the quiet force that held everything together. She kept the house running on a budget that shouldn’t have worked. She made sure there was dinner on the table every night — not fancy, but hot. She stretched leftovers into another meal. She patched clothes instead of buying new ones. She made sure we felt loved even when the cupboards were getting thin.

I remember coming home from school one day — must have been sixth grade. I was upset about something, some kid said something, the kind of thing you think matters at that age. I came storming through the back door, ready to unload on whoever would listen.

She was at the kitchen sink, peeling potatoes. She didn’t turn around.

She said:

> “John, you can’t control what other people say. You can only control what you do next.”

That was it. No lecture. No long talk. Just a sentence delivered to the window over the sink while potato skins curled into the basin. I stood there for a second, then went to my room. The thing that had been burning in my chest didn’t seem so big anymore.

Another lesson I didn’t fully appreciate until life beat it into me years later.

My parents weren’t wealthy. We weren’t poor either, not really. But there wasn’t much extra. If I wanted something beyond what they could provide, I worked for it. I mowed lawns up and down the street. I shoveled driveways in the winter. I collected bottles and turned them in for change — five cents a piece, and I’d walk a mile carrying a bag full of them just to have a dollar in my pocket.

I remember one old guy a few blocks over, Mr. Fletcher, who paid me to rake his leaves every fall. He’d sit on his porch in a lawn chair and watch me work. He didn’t say much, but at the end he’d hand me a five‑dollar bill folded in half and say:

> “You did a good job.”

That was the first time I remember feeling proud of work I’d done for someone else. It wasn’t the money. It was the fact that he noticed.

Nobody handed me anything, and I wasn’t raised to expect it.

If I broke something, I fixed it.  
If I made a mistake, I owned it.  
If I started something, I finished it.

Simple rules. The kind that sound obvious when you say them out loud. Not exactly a secret formula for success. But when life starts throwing punches — and it will — those simple rules are the ones that keep you standing.

They weren’t perfect parents. Nobody is. There were raised voices sometimes. There were nights when money was tight and you could feel the tension in the air like humidity before a storm — that heaviness that sits in the house and makes everyone speak softer, move quieter. But they showed up. Every day. They did what needed to be done, even when they were tired, even when they didn’t want to, even when they were probably scared themselves.

That image — both of them, just showing up — stayed with me longer than any lecture ever could.

I carried it into school, into my first jobs, into every relationship that mattered. I didn’t always get it right. I made plenty of mistakes. I was stubborn when I should have listened, proud when I should have apologized, and hard‑headed in ways that cost me time I can’t get back.

But I never forgot where I came from.

And when the hard times came — and they came hard, one after another, like life was testing whether I’d learned anything at all — that foundation is what held.

I couldn’t see it back then. I was just a kid living his life. I didn’t know that every morning watching my father lace up his boots, every evening watching my mother peel potatoes at the sink, every Saturday holding a flashlight until my arm burned, was building something in me.

Something I’d need.

Something that would have to hold against things a kid could never imagine.
