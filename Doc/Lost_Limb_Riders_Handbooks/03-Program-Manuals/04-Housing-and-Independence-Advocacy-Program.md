# Section 1: Executive Overview

## 1.1 Purpose of the Housing & Independence Advocacy Division

The Housing & Independence Advocacy Division of Lost Limb Riders exists to address a critical gap in the limb loss and disability community: the gap between medical discharge and independent, dignified community living. Clinical care addresses the physical recovery from amputation. Prosthetic rehabilitation addresses mobility. But neither addresses where someone will live, whether their home is accessible, or how they navigate a world not built for them.

This Division closes that gap. It provides direct advocacy, resource navigation, and peer-supported guidance to help amputees and individuals with disabilities secure safe, accessible housing and achieve genuine independence.

## 1.2 Program Mission

To ensure that every amputee and individual with a disability has access to safe, accessible housing and the advocacy support needed to live independently, with dignity, and without barriers.

## 1.3 Program Goals

1. **Housing Security:** Assist at least fifty individuals per year in securing safe, accessible housing or maintaining their current housing through advocacy and resource connection.

2. **Accessibility Advocacy:** Resolve at least forty accessibility-related housing barriers per year through accommodation requests, landlord negotiations, and resource referrals.

3. **Independent Living Outcomes:** Support at least sixty individuals per year in achieving measurable independent living goals, including self-advocacy, mobility confidence, and community engagement.

4. **Community Integration:** Connect every program participant with at least one community resource, peer support network, or recreational opportunity within thirty days of intake.

5. **Program Sustainability:** Maintain documentation standards that support grant funding, donor reporting, and measurable outcome demonstration.

## 1.4 Community Need

Housing is the foundation of stability. Without safe, accessible housing, every other outcome—health, employment, mental wellness, community connection—is compromised.

Amputees and individuals with disabilities face unique housing challenges:

- Fewer than five percent of housing units in the United States are considered moderately accessible for individuals with mobility disabilities.
- Landlords and property managers frequently deny reasonable accommodation requests due to lack of understanding or unwillingness to modify policies.
- Individuals transitioning from rehabilitation facilities or hospitals often face homelessness or unsafe housing situations because discharge planning does not include adequate housing coordination.
- Veterans with limb loss face additional barriers navigating VA housing benefits and accessible housing resources.
- Low-income amputees often lack the advocacy support needed to navigate public housing, Section 8, and disability-specific housing programs.

Lost Limb Riders is uniquely positioned to address this need because our advocates and peer mentors have lived experience. We do not just understand the policies. We understand what it means to navigate the world after limb loss.

## 1.5 Who Is Served

The Housing & Independence Advocacy Division serves:

- Individuals with limb loss or limb difference
- Individuals who use prosthetic devices
- Individuals with mobility disabilities
- Family members and caregivers of the above
- Veterans with limb loss or mobility disabilities
- Individuals transitioning from hospital or rehabilitation settings to community living
- Individuals at risk of homelessness due to disability-related housing barriers

## 1.6 Support of Lost Limb Riders’ Overall Mission

This Division directly advances every pillar of the Lost Limb Riders mission:

- **Independence:** Accessible housing is the prerequisite for independent living.
- **Freedom:** Freedom is meaningless without a safe place to live.
- **Confidence:** Advocacy support builds the confidence to navigate systems independently.
- **Brotherhood and Sisterhood:** Peer-supported advocacy creates connection and mutual support.
- **Peer Support:** Advocates with lived experience provide credibility and empathy that professional caseworkers cannot replicate.
- **Disability Advocacy:** Housing advocacy is one of the most impactful forms of disability advocacy.
- **Community Reintegration:** Accessible housing makes community reintegration possible.
- **Suicide Prevention Awareness:** Housing insecurity is a known risk factor for suicide. Addressing housing need is suicide prevention.
- **Quality of Life:** All of the above contributes to improved quality of life after disability.

# Section 2: Program Philosophy

## 2.1 Disability Empowerment

The Housing & Independence Advocacy Division operates from a disability empowerment model. This model holds that the primary barriers faced by individuals with disabilities are not their medical conditions but external factors: inaccessible environments, discriminatory policies, lack of resources, and social stigma.

Our role is not to fix people. Our role is to remove barriers. When barriers are removed, individuals exercise their own agency, make their own choices, and live their own lives. Empowerment means the individual drives their own outcomes. We provide the tools, information, and advocacy. They provide the direction.

## 2.2 Independent Living Philosophy

The independent living philosophy, developed by the disability rights movement, holds that individuals with disabilities have the right to:

- Live in the community, not in institutions
- Make choices about their own lives, including where and with whom they live
- Take risks and make mistakes, like anyone else
- Control the services and supports they receive

This Division applies the independent living philosophy to every interaction. We do not make decisions for participants. We provide information, identify options, and support the participant in making their own informed choice. Even when a participant’s choice differs from what an advocate would choose, we respect and support it.

## 2.3 Person-Centered Advocacy

Person-centered advocacy places the individual at the center of every decision. The advocate’s role is to:

- Listen without assuming
- Ask what the participant wants before offering solutions
- Tailor advocacy strategies to the participant’s specific circumstances, preferences, and goals
- Adjust approaches as the participant’s needs and priorities evolve

No two cases are identical. A person-centered approach ensures that each participant receives advocacy that fits their unique situation rather than being forced into a standardized program model.

## 2.4 Dignity and Respect

Every interaction conducted under this Division treats participants with unconditional dignity and respect. This means:

- Addressing participants by their preferred name and pronouns
- Speaking to participants as capable adults, not as patients or dependents
- Explaining options clearly without jargon
- Never speaking about a participant in their presence as if they are not there
- Respecting a participant’s right to decline services at any time
- Acknowledging that the participant is the expert on their own life

## 2.5 Peer-to-Peer Support

Peer-to-peer support is the foundation of Lost Limb Riders’ effectiveness. Advocates in this Division have lived experience with limb loss, disability, or both. This lived experience provides:

- Credibility with participants who may distrust professional systems
- Empathy that professional caseworkers may not be able to offer
- Practical knowledge of navigating housing, benefits, and accessibility systems
- Hope: participants see someone who has faced similar challenges and succeeded

Peer support does not replace professional services. It complements them. The Housing & Independence Advocacy Division coordinates with professional caseworkers, social workers, and service providers, not in competition with them.

## 2.6 Removing Barriers Instead of Creating Dependency

The goal of every advocacy interaction is to remove barriers so that the participant can act independently in the future. The Division does not create dependency by doing things for participants that they could learn to do themselves.

This distinction guides every decision:

- An advocate may accompany a participant to a housing authority appointment. The participant speaks for themselves. The advocate is there for support and to ensure the participant’s rights are respected.
- An advocate may help a participant complete a reasonable accommodation request form. The participant signs the form and submits it. The advocate does not impersonate the participant or sign on their behalf.
- An advocate may provide information about fair housing rights. The participant decides whether and how to exercise those rights.

Empowerment means the participant gains skills, confidence, and knowledge from every interaction. Dependency means the participant must return for the same assistance repeatedly. This Division is designed for the former.

# Section 3: Program Scope of Services

## 3.1 Housing Advocacy

Housing advocacy includes:

- Assisting participants in identifying accessible housing options that meet their mobility and functional needs
- Navigating rental applications, public housing waitlists, and Section 8 voucher processes
- Communicating with landlords and property managers on behalf of participants (with participant consent)
- Advocating for reasonable accommodations in existing or prospective housing
- Addressing housing discrimination complaints and connecting participants with legal resources when appropriate
- Providing information about tenant rights and fair housing laws

Housing advocacy does not include:

- Providing legal representation
- Guaranteeing housing placement
- Paying rent, deposits, or housing costs
- Serving as a co-signer or guarantor on any lease

## 3.2 Accessibility Advocacy

Accessibility advocacy includes:

- Assessing housing units for accessibility barriers (stairs, narrow doorways, inaccessible bathrooms, lack of grab bars, inadequate maneuvering space)
- Advocating for physical modifications to existing housing, including ramps, grab bars, widened doorways, roll-in showers, and accessible kitchen modifications
- Assisting with reasonable accommodation requests to landlords and property managers
- Connecting participants with funding sources for home modifications, including VA grants, state assistive technology programs, and nonprofit home modification programs
- Advocating for service animal accommodations where applicable
- Providing information about accessibility standards (ADA, Fair Housing Act, state and local building codes)

Accessibility advocacy does not include:

- Performing physical modifications or construction
- Certifying a housing unit as ADA-compliant
- Providing legal representation in accommodation disputes

## 3.3 Independent Living Support

Independent living support includes:

- Assessing a participant’s independent living goals and current barriers
- Developing an independent living plan with participant-driven goals and action steps
- Connecting participants with independent living skills training through community partners
- Providing information about personal care assistance, home health services, and attendant care
- Supporting participants in building daily living skills after amputation or disability onset
- Coordinating with Centers for Independent Living (CILs) and state independent living programs

Independent living support does not include:

- Providing personal care, home health, or attendant services directly
- Performing activities of daily living for participants
- Serving as a legal guardian or representative payee

## 3.4 Resource Navigation

Resource navigation includes:

- Identifying and connecting participants with community resources relevant to their housing and independence needs
- Providing information about federal, state, and local benefit programs, including SSI, SSDI, Medicaid, Medicare, SNAP, and VA benefits
- Assisting with benefit applications and appeals (procedural support only, not legal representation)
- Connecting participants with financial assistance programs for housing, utilities, and medical expenses
- Providing referrals to legal aid, mental health services, transportation services, and employment programs

Resource navigation does not include:

- Guaranteeing benefit approval
- Providing legal representation in benefit appeals
- Managing participants’ finances or benefits

## 3.5 Disability Rights Education

Disability rights education includes:

- Providing participants with information about their rights under the Fair Housing Act, Americans with Disabilities Act, Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, and applicable state laws
- Educating participants about reasonable accommodation processes and their rights in landlord-tenant relationships
- Providing information about disability rights in employment, public accommodations, and government services
- Offering group educational sessions on disability rights topics

## 3.6 Community Reintegration

Community reintegration support includes:

- Connecting participants with Lost Limb Riders chapter activities, rides, and events
- Identifying adaptive recreation opportunities in the participant’s community
- Providing peer mentor matching for community engagement goals
- Supporting participants in building social connections after limb loss or disability onset

## 3.7 Peer Mentoring

Peer mentoring includes:

- One-on-one mentoring relationships between program participants and trained peer mentors with lived experience
- Goal-oriented mentoring focused on housing stability, independent living, and community reintegration
- Regular check-ins, typically by phone, video call, or in-person meeting (in public or approved settings)
- Mentor support and supervision through the Division’s volunteer advocate program

## 3.8 Emergency Resource Coordination

Emergency resource coordination includes:

- Responding to participants facing housing crisis, eviction risk, or unsafe living conditions
- Connecting participants with emergency housing, shelter, and crisis intervention resources
- Coordinating with partner organizations for emergency assistance (food, clothing, transportation, medical needs)
- Documenting emergency situations and providing follow-up advocacy to prevent recurrence

# Section 4: Eligibility and Participation Standards

## 4.1 Who Qualifies

An individual is eligible for services through the Housing & Independence Advocacy Division if they meet any of the following criteria:

- Has undergone amputation or was born with limb difference
- Uses a prosthetic device or mobility aid
- Has a documented mobility disability that affects housing access or independent living
- Is a family member or primary caregiver of an individual who meets any of the above criteria
- Is a veteran with limb loss, mobility disability, or housing need related to service-connected disability

No income threshold, geographic limitation, or membership requirement applies to initial services. Resource navigation and advocacy are provided regardless of the participant’s location within the United States, though direct advocacy services (in-person visits, landlord negotiation) may be limited by the Division’s geographic capacity.

## 4.2 Referral Sources

Participants may be referred from any of the following sources:

- Self-referral (individual contacts Lost Limb Riders directly)
- Hospital or rehabilitation facility discharge planners
- Prosthetic and orthotic clinics
- Physical or occupational therapy providers
- VA medical centers and veteran service organizations
- Centers for Independent Living (CILs)
- State and local housing authorities
- Homeless service providers and shelters
- Other nonprofit organizations serving individuals with disabilities
- Lost Limb Riders chapters and peer support networks
- Family members or caregivers

Referrals are accepted verbally, by email, through the Lost Limb Riders website, or by referral form from partner organizations.

## 4.3 Service Limitations

The Housing & Independence Advocacy Division cannot:

- Guarantee housing placement, benefit approval, or accommodation approval
- Provide legal representation
- Pay rent, deposits, utility bills, or housing costs
- Perform physical home modifications or construction
- Provide personal care, home health, or attendant services
- Serve as a co-signer, guarantor, or representative payee
- Provide emergency shelter or operate a housing facility
- Provide mental health counseling or medical treatment
- Transport participants or operate a transportation service
- Replace services provided by government agencies, legal aid organizations, or licensed professionals

## 4.4 Participant Expectations

Program participants are expected to:

- Provide accurate and complete information during intake and throughout the advocacy relationship
- Communicate honestly about their needs, goals, and circumstances
- Participate actively in their own advocacy, including completing tasks agreed upon in their independent living plan
- Treat advocates, mentors, and staff with respect
- Notify the Division if their circumstances change or if they no longer need services
- Sign required consent and release forms before services begin
- Acknowledge that the Division provides advocacy support, not guarantees of outcomes

Failure to meet these expectations may result in modification or discontinuation of services at the Program Director’s discretion.

## 4.5 Non-Discrimination Policy

The Housing & Independence Advocacy Division serves all eligible individuals without regard to:

- Race, color, or national origin
- Religion or creed
- Sex, gender identity, or sexual orientation
- Age
- Disability type or cause of disability
- Source of income
- Political affiliation
- Membership status in Lost Limb Riders or any other organization

This Division is committed to serving individuals from all communities and backgrounds. Discrimination of any kind by staff, volunteers, or advocates is prohibited and will result in immediate removal from the program.

## 4.6 Accessibility Commitments

Lost Limb Riders is committed to making its services accessible to all individuals with disabilities. This includes:

- Providing program information in accessible formats upon request (large print, electronic text, audio)
- Conducting intakes and meetings in accessible locations
- Offering virtual meetings by phone or video call for individuals who cannot travel
- Accommodating service animals in all program settings
- Providing reasonable accommodations for individuals with communication, cognitive, or sensory disabilities

# Section 6: Client Intake Procedures

## 6.1 Initial Contact Procedure

When a potential participant contacts the Housing & Independence Advocacy Division, the following procedure is followed:

1. **Response Timeline:** All initial contacts receive a response within two business days. Emergency contacts (individuals reporting homelessness, eviction risk, or unsafe living conditions) receive a response within four business hours.

2. **Initial Information Gathering:** The intake worker collects the following information:
   - Name and preferred contact method
   - Brief description of the need or request
   - Urgency of the situation
   - How the individual learned about Lost Limb Riders

3. **Program Explanation:** The intake worker explains:
   - What the Division can and cannot do
   - The advocacy model (participant-driven, not case management)
   - Confidentiality and consent requirements
   - That services are free and voluntary

4. **Next Steps Communication:** The individual is informed of the next step (screening and intake interview) and given an expected timeline.

## 6.2 Screening Process

Before a full intake is scheduled, the intake worker conducts a brief screening to determine:

- Whether the individual meets eligibility criteria as defined in Section 4
- Whether the request falls within the Division’s scope of services
- Whether the situation requires emergency response (Section 11) or can proceed through standard intake
- Whether the individual has previously received services from the Division

If the individual is ineligible or the request is outside the Division’s scope, the intake worker provides appropriate referrals and closes the contact.

If eligible and within scope, the intake is scheduled.

## 6.3 Intake Interview

The intake interview is conducted by a Case Advocate or Housing Advocacy Coordinator. It may be conducted in person, by phone, or by video call, based on the participant’s preference and accessibility needs.

The intake interview covers:

- Full participant information (name, contact details, date of birth, emergency contact)
- Disability and amputation information relevant to housing and independence needs
- Current housing situation and stability
- Housing barriers identified by the participant
- Accessibility barriers in current or desired housing
- Independent living goals
- Benefits and resources currently accessed
- Other resources or supports involved
- Privacy and consent discussion
- Participant’s priorities and desired outcomes

The intake interview typically takes forty-five to ninety minutes.

## 6.4 Needs Assessment

Following the intake interview, the Case Advocate completes a Housing Needs Assessment. The assessment evaluates:

- Current housing status (stable, at-risk, unstable, homeless)
- Accessibility of current housing (barriers identified)
- Urgency of housing need
- Independent living skills and support needs
- Benefits and income status
- Support network
- Barriers to independence
- Participant-defined goals and priorities

The Housing Needs Assessment is documented using the form in Appendix A. The assessment is reviewed with the participant, and the participant is given the opportunity to correct or add information.

## 6.5 Consent Process

Before any advocacy services begin, the participant must sign:

- **Consent for Services Form:** Acknowledges the scope and limitations of services, the advocacy model, and the participant’s rights and responsibilities.
- **Release of Information Form:** Authorizes the Division to communicate with specified third parties (landlords, housing authorities, benefit agencies, medical providers) for advocacy purposes. The participant specifies which entities may be contacted and what information may be shared.
- **Participant Acknowledgment Form:** Confirms the participant understands that Lost Limb Riders provides advocacy support, not legal representation, and cannot guarantee outcomes.

All consent forms are explained verbally before the participant signs. The participant receives copies of all signed forms.

## 6.6 Documentation Requirements

Every new participant has a case file created in the Division’s secure system. The case file includes:

- Participant Intake Form (Appendix A)
- Housing Needs Assessment (Appendix B)
- Signed Consent for Services
- Signed Release of Information
- Signed Participant Acknowledgment
- Initial case notes documenting the intake interview and needs assessment

Documentation is completed within five business days of the intake interview.

## 6.7 Case Assignment

After intake and needs assessment are complete, the Housing Advocacy Coordinator assigns the case to a Case Advocate.

Assignment considers:

- Advocate caseload and capacity
- Geographic location (for in-person advocacy needs)
- Advocate expertise (housing law knowledge, VA benefits knowledge, accessibility experience)
- Participant preference (if a participant requests a specific advocate or type of advocate, every reasonable effort is made to accommodate)

The participant is notified of their assigned advocate and given the advocate’s contact information within two business days of assignment.

# Section 7: Housing Advocacy Operations

## 7.1 Housing Search Assistance

### 7.1.1 Accessible Housing Identification

The Case Advocate assists the participant in identifying housing options that meet their accessibility and functional needs. This includes:

- Searching accessible housing databases, including the ADA National Network housing locator, HUD’s accessible housing resources, and state and local accessible housing registries
- Identifying apartment complexes and rental properties with known accessibility features
- Coordinating with housing authorities to identify accessible public housing units and Section 8 voucher opportunities
- Researching disability-friendly housing resources, including supportive housing programs and independent living facilities

The advocate provides the participant with a list of potential housing options, including:

- Property name and address
- Contact information for leasing office or property manager
- Known accessibility features (or known barriers)
- Rental price and utility information
- Application requirements and process
- Waitlist status (if applicable)

The participant selects which properties to pursue. The advocate does not pressure the participant toward any particular option.

### 7.1.2 Rental Assistance Navigation

The Case Advocate assists the participant in identifying and applying for rental assistance programs, including:

- Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher program
- Public housing programs
- State and local rental assistance programs
- Veterans Affairs Supportive Housing (VASH) vouchers for veterans
- Project-Based Rental Assistance programs
- Nonprofit rental assistance programs

Advocacy includes:

- Explaining eligibility requirements and application processes
- Assisting with application completion (participant signs and submits)
- Gathering required documentation
- Coordinating with housing authority caseworkers
- Following up on application status
- Assisting with appeals and waitlist management

### 7.1.3 Public Housing Resources

For participants pursuing public housing, the advocate assists with:

- Identifying applicable public housing agencies
- Completing and submitting applications
- Understanding waitlist procedures and estimated wait times
- Requesting reasonable accommodations in the application and selection process
- Navigating transfer and portability procedures (for Section 8 vouchers)

### 7.1.4 Disability-Friendly Housing Resources

The advocate maintains current knowledge of disability-friendly housing resources, including:

- HUD Section 811 Supportive Housing for Persons with Disabilities
- State-funded supportive housing programs
- Accessible housing trust fund programs
- Local affordable housing developers with accessibility commitments
- Community land trusts and shared equity housing models

## 7.2 Housing Barriers Assessment

### 7.2.1 Accessibility Barrier Identification

For participants in existing housing, the Case Advocate conducts a Housing Barriers Assessment to identify accessibility challenges. The assessment covers:

- **Stairs:** Number of steps at entry, internal stairs between floors, steps to essential areas (bedroom, bathroom, kitchen)
- **Bathroom Access:** Door width, shower entry type (step-over, curb, roll-in), toilet height and grab bar availability, sink accessibility
- **Doorways:** Width of all doorways the participant must navigate, doorway thresholds, door handle type
- **Mobility Limitations:** Space for mobility aid maneuvering (wheelchair, walker, crutches), turning radius in bathrooms, kitchens, and hallways
- **Safety Hazards:** Loose rugs, inadequate lighting, lack of handrails, uneven flooring, electrical cord hazards
- **Transportation Barriers:** Proximity to public transit, availability of accessible parking, condition of sidewalks and curb cuts
- **Kitchen Access:** Counter height, sink accessibility, appliance reachability, storage accessibility

### 7.2.2 Assessment Documentation

The Housing Barriers Assessment is documented using the Accessibility Assessment Checklist (Appendix C). Photographs may be taken with the participant’s and property owner’s permission. The assessment is shared with the participant, and a copy is maintained in the case file.

## 7.3 Advocacy Strategies

Based on the Housing Barriers Assessment and the participant’s goals, the Case Advocate develops an advocacy strategy. Strategies may include:

- **Reasonable Accommodation Requests:** Formal written requests to the landlord or property manager for modifications or policy changes
- **Home Modification Funding Applications:** Applications to VA Home Modification grants, state assistive technology programs, nonprofit home modification programs, and Medicaid waiver programs
- **Relocation Planning:** Assistance in identifying accessible housing when current housing cannot be made accessible
- **Fair Housing Complaints:** Connection to legal resources when discrimination is suspected

# Section 8: Disability Accommodation Advocacy

## 8.1 Reasonable Accommodation Assistance

The Division assists participants in requesting reasonable accommodations under the Fair Housing Act and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. Reasonable accommodations are changes to rules, policies, practices, or services that are necessary to give a person with a disability an equal opportunity to use and enjoy a dwelling.

The Case Advocate assists with:

- Identifying the accommodation needed based on the participant’s disability-related limitations
- Drafting the formal accommodation request letter, including:
  - Statement that the participant has a disability (definition under fair housing law)
  - Description of the functional limitation caused by the disability
  - Description of the specific accommodation requested
  - Connection between the accommodation and the functional limitation
  - Medical documentation if required (advocate helps participant obtain from their provider)
- Submitting the request to the landlord or property manager
- Following up on the request within the legally required response timeframe
- Negotiating with the landlord if the initial request is denied or met with resistance
- Escalating to legal resources if the landlord unlawfully denies the accommodation

## 8.2 Service Animal Advocacy

The Division advocates for participants who use service animals or emotional support animals and face barriers from landlords, property managers, or homeowners associations.

Advocacy includes:

- Educating housing providers about the legal distinction between service animals, emotional support animals, and pets
- Providing information about Fair Housing Act protections for assistance animals
- Assisting participants in obtaining necessary documentation from their healthcare provider
- Drafting accommodation request letters for assistance animals
- Responding to denial letters with factual and legal counter-arguments
- Connecting participants with legal resources for unresolved disputes

The Division does not certify, register, or train service animals and does not provide documentation purporting to certify an animal as a service animal.

## 8.3 Accessibility Requests

For physical accessibility modifications, the Case Advocate assists participants in:

- Identifying specific modifications needed (ramps, grab bars, widened doorways, roll-in showers, accessible kitchen modifications)
- Obtaining cost estimates from contractors or home modification programs
- Requesting landlord permission for modifications (participant bears cost unless other funding is secured)
- Applying for funding through VA Home Modification grants, state assistive technology programs, nonprofit programs, and Medicaid waivers
- Coordinating with contractors and modification program administrators

## 8.4 Communication with Landlords and Property Managers

All communications with landlords, property managers, and housing providers are conducted with professionalism and respect, even in adversarial situations.

Standards for communication:

- All written communication is copied to the participant
- The advocate identifies themselves as a representative of Lost Limb Riders, Housing & Independence Advocacy Division
- The advocate states the participant’s name and the purpose of the communication
- The advocate does not make demands but makes requests supported by law and policy
- The advocate does not threaten legal action but may inform the housing provider of the participant’s legal rights
- All communication is documented in the case file

## 8.5 Documentation Support

The Case Advocate assists participants in obtaining and organizing medical documentation needed for accommodation requests, benefit applications, and housing applications. This includes:

- Requesting medical records from healthcare providers
- Obtaining letters of medical necessity
- Organizing documentation for submission
- Ensuring documentation is complete before submission

## Important Disclaimer

Lost Limb Riders provides advocacy support and information about legal rights. Lost Limb Riders does not provide legal representation. No advocate, staff member, or volunteer of Lost Limb Riders is authorized to:

- Represent a participant in legal proceedings
- Provide legal advice or legal opinions
- Interpret laws or regulations as a legal professional
- File legal complaints on behalf of a participant
- Negotiate legal settlements

Participants who need legal representation are referred to legal aid organizations, private attorneys, or state bar association referral services.

# Section 9: Independent Living Program

## 9.1 Returning to Independence After Amputation

The independent living journey after amputation is different for every individual. Some return to independent living quickly with strong support systems and resources. Others face significant barriers—physical, financial, emotional—that make independence feel distant or impossible.

The Independent Living Program meets participants where they are. There is no standard timeline and no single definition of success. The participant defines what independence means for them.

## 9.2 Adaptive Living Resources

The Case Advocate connects participants with adaptive living resources relevant to their needs:

- Adaptive equipment for daily living (dressing aids, bathing aids, kitchen tools)
- Home modification resources (ramps, grab bars, accessible fixtures)
- Assistive technology (environmental controls, voice-activated systems, smart home devices)
- Mobility aids and equipment (wheelchairs, walkers, prosthetics, adaptive driving equipment)
- Personal care and attendant care resources

Adaptive living resources are identified through the Community Resource Network and provided as referrals. The Division does not supply adaptive equipment directly.

## 9.3 Mobility Support

Mobility is central to independent living. The Case Advocate supports participants in:

- Identifying and accessing mobility aid resources
- Navigating prosthetic and orthotic care
- Coordinating with physical and occupational therapy providers
- Connecting with adaptive recreation and sports programs
- Addressing transportation barriers that limit mobility

## 9.4 Self-Advocacy Training

A core outcome of the Independent Living Program is that participants develop the skills and confidence to advocate for themselves. Self-advocacy training includes:

- Understanding disability rights under federal and state law
- Practicing communication skills for housing, benefits, and medical settings
- Learning how to document and request reasonable accommodations
- Building confidence in interactions with authority figures and service providers
- Identifying personal advocacy goals and developing strategies to achieve them

Self-advocacy training is provided one-on-one by the Case Advocate or Peer Mentor, and through group workshops when available.

## 9.5 Confidence Rebuilding

Amputation and disability can shake an individual’s confidence in their ability to live independently. The Division addresses this through:

- Peer mentor relationships with individuals who have navigated similar challenges
- Goal setting with achievable short-term milestones
- Positive reinforcement and acknowledgment of progress
- Connection with community activities and social opportunities
- Referrals to mental health support when appropriate

## 9.6 Community Involvement

Community involvement is a key outcome of independent living. The Case Advocate supports participants in:

- Identifying community activities that match the participant’s interests and abilities
- Connecting with Lost Limb Riders chapter activities and events
- Finding adaptive recreation and sports programs
- Accessing community centers, senior centers, and disability-focused organizations
- Building social connections outside of formal support systems

# Section 10: Case Management System

## 10.1 Case Creation

A case is created in the Division’s secure system when:

- A participant completes the intake process (Sections 6.1 through 6.5)
- Consent forms are signed
- A Housing Advocacy Coordinator assigns the case to a Case Advocate

Each case is assigned a unique case number using the format: HIA-YYYY-XXXX (Housing & Independence Advocacy, year, sequential number).

## 10.2 Case Notes

Case notes are entered for every substantive interaction with or about a participant. Each case note includes:

- Date and time of interaction
- Type of interaction (phone call, email, in-person meeting, correspondence with third party)
- Individuals involved
- Summary of discussion
- Actions taken or decisions made
- Action items and responsible party
- Follow-up date

Case notes are factual, objective, and professional. They do not include personal opinions, judgments, or speculation.

## 10.3 Progress Tracking

Progress toward the participant’s goals is tracked in the case file. The Case Advocate documents:

- Goal status (not started, in progress, completed, modified, discontinued)
- Progress notes for each goal
- Barriers encountered and adjustments made
- Resources connected or referrals provided
- Participant’s own assessment of progress

## 10.4 Follow-Up Schedule

Follow-up is conducted on the following schedule:

- **Active Advocacy:** The Case Advocate communicates with the participant at least once every fourteen days during active advocacy.
- **Post-Placement Follow-Up:** After the participant secures housing or achieves a major goal, follow-up occurs at thirty, sixty, and ninety days.
- **Maintenance Check-Ins:** After ninety days, the participant is transitioned to maintenance status with optional quarterly check-ins.

The participant may request more or less frequent contact at any time.

## 10.5 Goal Setting

Goals are participant-driven and documented in the Independent Living Plan. Each goal includes:

- Description of the goal in the participant’s own words
- Action steps to achieve the goal
- Resources or support needed
- Target completion date
- Status updates

Goals are reviewed and updated at least every sixty days.

## 10.6 Case Closure Procedures

A case is closed when:

- The participant has achieved their stated goals and no longer needs advocacy support
- The participant requests closure
- The participant cannot be reached after three documented attempts over thirty days
- The participant is found to be ineligible after services have begun
- The participant has been referred to a more appropriate service provider and transition is complete
- The Division determines, after consultation with the participant, that further advocacy is unlikely to be productive

Case closure documentation includes:

- Closure reason
- Summary of outcomes achieved
- Recommendations for future support (if applicable)
- Participant satisfaction survey administered or attempted
- Final case note

## 10.7 Outcome Measurements

For each closed case, the Case Advocate documents:

- Primary outcome (housing secured, accommodation approved, goal achieved, referral completed, other)
- Number of advocacy contacts
- Number of referrals provided
- Resources connected
- Participant satisfaction rating
- Duration of advocacy relationship

These outcomes are aggregated for program metrics reporting (Section 17).
# Section 11: Emergency Assistance Procedures

## 11.1 Housing Crisis Response

A housing crisis exists when a participant faces:

- Imminent homelessness (eviction notice, lease termination, condemnation of unit)
- Current homelessness (sleeping in shelter, vehicle, or unsheltered)
- Unsafe living conditions (no heat, no running water, structural danger, active mold, vermin infestation)
- Domestic violence or safety threat in current housing

When a participant reports a housing crisis:

1. The intake worker or Case Advocate assesses the immediacy of the danger.
2. If the participant is in immediate danger, the advocate instructs the participant to call 911 if appropriate and connects them with emergency shelter resources.
3. If the situation is urgent but not immediately life-threatening, the advocate provides emergency housing resources (shelters, rapid re-housing programs, hotel voucher programs if available through community partners).
4. The advocate documents the crisis and all actions taken.
5. The Housing Advocacy Coordinator is notified within one business day.
6. The advocate follows up within twenty-four hours to confirm the participant’s safety.

## 11.2 Eviction Risk Response

When a participant reports eviction risk:

1. The advocate assesses the status of the eviction process (notice received, court date scheduled, judgment entered).
2. The advocate provides information about tenant rights and eviction defenses.
3. The advocate connects the participant with legal aid or tenant advocacy organizations immediately.
4. The advocate assists the participant in identifying rental assistance resources that may prevent eviction.
5. If the participant has a pending court date, the advocate ensures they are connected with legal representation before the court date if possible.
6. The advocate documents all actions and referrals.

## 11.3 Unsafe Living Conditions Response

When a participant reports unsafe living conditions:

1. The advocate documents the specific conditions reported.
2. The advocate assesses whether the conditions pose an immediate health or safety risk.
3. If immediate risk exists, the advocate guides the participant to emergency resources (emergency shelter, hotel, friend or family).
4. The advocate provides information about tenant rights to habitable housing and the process for reporting code violations.
5. The advocate connects the participant with legal aid or tenant advocacy organizations.
6. If the participant is a veteran, the advocate coordinates with VA social work services.
7. The advocate documents all actions and referrals.

## 11.4 Medical Transition Situations

When a participant is transitioning from a hospital, rehabilitation facility, or skilled nursing facility to the community and has urgent housing needs:

1. The advocate coordinates with the facility’s discharge planner or social worker.
2. The advocate assesses the participant’s housing situation and identifies barriers to safe discharge.
3. If the participant does not have safe housing to return to, the advocate works with the discharge planner to identify options, including short-term rehabilitation placement, bridge housing, family placement, or emergency shelter.
4. The advocate coordinates with the Community Resource Network to identify rapid re-housing resources.
5. The advocate continues to work with the participant after discharge to secure stable housing.

## 11.5 Emergency Referrals

In any emergency situation where the Division cannot directly meet the need, the advocate provides referrals to:

- Emergency shelters and housing crisis hotlines
- Crisis intervention and suicide prevention hotlines (988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
- Domestic violence hotlines and shelters
- Food banks and emergency meal programs
- Emergency financial assistance programs
- Crisis medical and mental health services
- Veterans Crisis Line (dial 988, press 1)

The advocate does not leave a participant in crisis without at least one concrete referral or resource.

## 12.1 Partnership Development

The Housing & Independence Advocacy Division maintains an active Community Resource Network of organizations that serve amputees, individuals with disabilities, and individuals facing housing insecurity. The Housing Advocacy Coordinator is responsible for developing and maintaining these partnerships.

- **Rehabilitation Services:** Inpatient and outpatient rehabilitation facilities, vocational rehabilitation programs, substance use disorder treatment providers accepting individuals with disabilities
- **Transportation Providers:** Public transit authorities with ADA paratransit services, nonprofit medical transportation programs, volunteer driver programs
- **Legal Aid Organizations:** Fair housing organizations, tenant rights organizations, disability rights legal advocacy organizations, state bar association referral services

- **Mental Health Providers:** Community mental health centers, sliding-scale therapy providers, amputee-specific support groups, crisis services

- Confirming contact information is current

# Section 13: Volunteer Advocate Program

## 13.1 Volunteer Requirements

Volunteer Advocates in the Housing & Independence Advocacy Division must:

- Be at least eighteen years of age
- Complete a volunteer application and screening process
- Pass a background check (criminal history check)
- Complete required training before beginning advocacy activities
- Sign a Volunteer Confidentiality Agreement
- Sign a Code of Conduct acknowledgment
- Agree to supervision and reporting requirements

Lived experience with limb loss, disability, or housing advocacy is strongly preferred but not required.

## 13.2 Training Requirements

All Volunteer Advocates complete the following training modules before beginning advocacy activities:

1. **Program Overview:** Mission, scope, limitations, and organizational structure of the Division
2. **Advocacy Skills:** Communication, boundary setting, person-centered advocacy, self-advocacy support
3. **Housing Rights:** Fair Housing Act, reasonable accommodations, landlord-tenant law basics
4. **Disability Rights:** ADA, Section 504, disability rights philosophy, independent living philosophy
5. **Confidentiality and Privacy:** HIPAA awareness, confidentiality requirements, record security
6. **Safety:** Home visit safety, participant safety, incident reporting
7. **Documentation:** Case notes, forms, reporting requirements
8. **Cultural Competency:** Serving diverse populations, disability culture, accessibility awareness

Annual refresher training is required to maintain active volunteer status.

## 13.3 Code of Conduct

Volunteer Advocates agree to:

- Treat all participants with dignity, respect, and professionalism
- Maintain strict confidentiality of all participant information
- Respect participant autonomy and choices
- Stay within the scope of their role and authority
- Report any conflicts of interest to the Housing Advocacy Coordinator
- Refrain from any romantic, sexual, or financial relationships with participants
- Refrain from proselytizing, political advocacy, or solicitation during advocacy activities
- Follow all safety policies and reporting procedures
- Represent Lost Limb Riders professionally in all communications

## 13.4 Confidentiality Requirements

Volunteer Advocates sign a Confidentiality Agreement that covers:

- All participant information, including name, contact information, disability status, housing status, and personal circumstances
- All case documentation, including notes, forms, and correspondence
- All internal program information, including policies, procedures, and operational details
- Prohibition on discussing participant information with anyone outside the program without participant consent or legal requirement

Violation of the Confidentiality Agreement results in immediate removal from the program.

## 13.5 Boundaries

Volunteer Advocates maintain clear professional boundaries:

- Do not provide personal contact information to participants (program-provided communication methods only)
- Do not socialize with participants outside of program activities
- Do not accept gifts or money from participants
- Do not provide transportation to participants in personal vehicles
- Do not enter participants’ homes alone (home visits are conducted in pairs or with advance safety protocols per Section 14)
- Do not make promises or guarantees about outcomes
- Do not provide legal advice, medical advice, or financial advice

## 13.6 Reporting Procedures

Volunteer Advocates report to the Housing Advocacy Coordinator. Reporting requirements include:

- Case activity report at least monthly
- Incident report within twenty-four hours of any incident as defined in Section 14
- Supervision meeting at least monthly
- Annual competency review

# Section 14: Safety Policies

## 14.1 Home Visit Safety

When home visits are conducted:

- Visits are scheduled in advance with participant consent
- The advocate notifies the Housing Advocacy Coordinator of the visit location, time, and expected duration before departure
- The advocate checks in upon arrival and checks out upon departure
- Visits are conducted during daylight hours whenever possible
- The advocate carries a fully charged mobile phone
- If the advocate feels unsafe at any time, they may end the visit immediately and depart
- Home visits are conducted in pairs when the participant has a history of volatile behavior, when the neighborhood is known to have safety concerns, or when the advocate requests a companion

## 14.2 Transportation Safety

Lost Limb Riders does not operate a transportation service. Volunteer Advocates do not transport participants in personal vehicles under any circumstances.

When transportation is needed for program purposes (e.g., accompanying a participant to a housing authority appointment):

- The participant arranges their own transportation
- If the participant cannot arrange transportation, the advocate provides referrals to transportation resources
- If the advocate and participant travel to the same location independently, they may meet there but the advocate does not provide transportation

## 14.3 Participant Safety

If a participant reports a safety concern (domestic violence, unsafe housing, threats, stalking):

- The advocate takes the report seriously and responds without judgment
- If the participant is in immediate danger, the advocate instructs them to call 911
- The advocate provides safety planning resources and referrals to domestic violence services
- The advocate documents the report and all actions taken
- The Housing Advocacy Coordinator is notified within one business day

## 14.4 Volunteer Safety

If a Volunteer Advocate experiences:

- Threats, harassment, or intimidation from a participant or third party
- Unsafe conditions during a home visit or program activity
- Injury during program activities
- Any incident that causes concern for personal safety

The volunteer immediately removes themselves from the unsafe situation, notifies the Housing Advocacy Coordinator, and completes an Incident Report within twenty-four hours.

## 14.5 Incident Reporting

Any of the following constitutes a reportable incident:

- Injury to a participant, volunteer, or staff member during program activities
- Threat or act of violence
- Theft or property damage
- Breach of confidentiality
- Participant complaint about a volunteer or staff member
- Law enforcement involvement
- Any situation that could result in liability to Lost Limb Riders

Incident Reports are submitted to the Housing Advocacy Coordinator within twenty-four hours using the Incident Report Form (Appendix H). The Housing Advocacy Coordinator reviews the incident and determines whether escalation to the Program Director or CEO is needed.

The Program Director reviews all incident reports and determines whether corrective action, policy changes, or additional training are needed.

# Section 15: Confidentiality and Privacy

## 15.1 Personal Information Protection

All personal information collected by the Housing & Independence Advocacy Division is protected under this confidentiality policy. Personal information includes:

- Name, address, telephone number, email address
- Date of birth, Social Security number (when provided for benefit applications)
- Disability and medical information
- Housing status and history
- Income and benefit information
- Family and household information
- Any other information that could identify the participant

Personal information is collected only when necessary for program services. It is used only for program purposes and is not shared outside Lost Limb Riders without the participant’s written consent, except as required by law.

## 15.2 Medical Information Confidentiality

Medical information shared by participants or obtained through release of information is afforded the highest level of protection. Medical information is:

- Stored separately from general case notes where possible
- Accessible only to staff and volunteers with a direct need to know
- Never shared with third parties without specific written authorization from the participant
- Retained only as long as necessary for program purposes

## 15.3 Consent Requirements

No participant information is shared with any third party without the participant’s written consent, except:

- As required by mandatory reporting laws (child abuse, elder abuse, dependent adult abuse)
- As required by valid subpoena or court order
- To emergency services when the participant is in immediate danger and unable to consent

Consent forms specify:

- What information may be shared
- With whom it may be shared
- The purpose of sharing
- Expiration date or condition triggering expiration

## 15.4 Record Security

All case records are stored in a secure electronic system with:

- Password protection and multi-factor authentication
- Role-based access controls (staff access only to records they need for their work)
- Encryption for data at rest and in transit
- Automatic backup
- Audit logging of access and changes

Paper records are stored in locked filing cabinets in a locked office. Paper records are scanned and shredded within thirty days of scanning.

## 15.5 Breach Notification

If a breach of confidentiality occurs:

1. The staff member or volunteer who discovers the breach immediately notifies the Housing Advocacy Coordinator and Program Director.
2. The Program Director assesses the scope of the breach and determines whether participants need to be notified.
3. If participant notification is required, the Program Director notifies affected participants within seven days of discovery, describing what information was involved and what steps are being taken to prevent recurrence.
4. The Program Director documents the breach and corrective actions.
5. If the breach involves protected health information, the Program Director consults legal counsel regarding HIPAA notification requirements.

# Section 19: Policies and Procedures Appendix

## 19.1 Records Retention Policy

Case records are retained for three years from the date of case closure. After three years, records are securely destroyed by electronic deletion or shredding of paper documents.

Records related to financial transactions are retained for seven years per IRS requirements.

Records related to any pending legal matter, audit, or investigation are retained until the matter is resolved.

## 19.2 Conflict of Interest Policy

No staff member, volunteer, or Board member of the Housing & Independence Advocacy Division may:

- Approve services or assistance for themselves or a family member
- Refer a participant to a business or organization in which they have a financial interest
- Accept gifts, payments, or favors from participants or their family members
- Use program resources for personal benefit
- Engage in any activity that creates an actual or apparent conflict of interest

All actual or potential conflicts of interest must be disclosed to the Program Director immediately.

## 19.3 Non-Solicitation Policy

Staff and volunteers of the Housing & Independence Advocacy Division may not use their position to:

- Solicit participants for religious, political, or personal purposes
- Sell products or services to participants
- Recruit participants for outside organizations or activities without Program Director approval
- Request personal favors or financial contributions from participants

## 19.4 Grievance Procedure

Participants who wish to file a grievance about any aspect of their services may:

1. Discuss the concern with their Case Advocate
2. If unresolved, contact the Housing Advocacy Coordinator
3. If still unresolved, contact the Program Director
4. If still unresolved, contact the CEO / Founder

Grievances are documented and responded to in writing within ten business days. No participant is penalized for filing a grievance, and services continue during the grievance process.

## 19.5 Media and Public Relations Policy

No staff member, volunteer, or participant may speak to the media on behalf of Lost Limb Riders Housing & Independence Advocacy Division without prior authorization from the CEO / Founder or Program Director.

Requests for participant stories or testimonials are handled through the Program Director, and participant consent is obtained before any information is shared publicly.
