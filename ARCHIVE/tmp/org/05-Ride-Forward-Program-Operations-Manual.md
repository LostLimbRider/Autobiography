# **Section 1: Program Overview**

## 1.1 Introduction

The Ride Forward Program is the signature event program of Lost Limb Riders. It creates visible, empowering, community‑driven motorcycle events that reconnect amputees, limb‑different individuals, and their families with freedom, movement, brotherhood, and public support. Ride Forward events are where the mission becomes unmistakable — riders on the road, families gathered, sponsors engaged, and the public reminded that limb loss does not end the ride.

This manual provides the complete operational framework for planning, executing, evaluating, and replicating Ride Forward events across any Lost Limb Riders chapter — locally, statewide, or nationally. Every chapter that conducts a Ride Forward event must follow this manual without deviation unless a variation is approved in writing by the Director of Riding Operations and ratified by the Board of Directors.

Consistency is the standard. A Ride Forward event in Iowa must look, feel, operate, and protect participants at the same level as a Ride Forward event in Texas, California, or any future chapter state. This consistency builds trust with participants, sponsors, hospitals, grant funders, insurers, and law enforcement.

## 1.2 Program Description

The Ride Forward Program encompasses all motorcycle‑related events operated under the Lost Limb Riders banner, including signature annual rides, awareness rides, charity rides, memorial rides, scenic rides, family rides, adaptive rides, veteran rides, community gatherings, and fundraising events. Each event type serves a distinct purpose but operates within the same safety, quality, and organizational standards.

The program is built on motorcycle culture because motorcycles represent freedom, independence, courage, and the refusal to be parked by hardship. But the program is intentionally inclusive — a person does not need to ride a motorcycle to participate. Passengers, families, volunteers, sponsors, hospital partners, and community supporters are all part of every Ride Forward event.

## 1.3 Program Identity

- **Program Name:** Ride Forward Program
- **Parent Organization:** Lost Limb Riders, Inc.
- **Motto:** "I Can. I Will."
- **Tagline:** "Nobody Is Left Behind. Nobody Stands Alone."
- **Document Type:** Administrative Operations Manual — Program‑Specific
- **Applicability:** All Lost Limb Riders chapters, current and future

## 1.4 Who This Manual Serves

This manual is designed for:

- Director of Riding Operations and Ride Captains
- Event Coordinators and Volunteer Coordinators
- Safety Officers and Road Guard Teams
- Chapter Leaders responsible for local event implementation

# **Section 2: Mission**

The mission of the Ride Forward Program is to create visible, accessible, and empowering motorcycle events that demonstrate the strength, freedom, and community of life after limb loss — while raising funds for programs, recruiting volunteers, building partnerships, and connecting new participants with the support they need.

This mission directly supports the organizational mission of Lost Limb Riders: to support amputees, limb‑different individuals, and their families through community, mentorship, public awareness, adaptive opportunities, and practical resources — including housing and independence advocacy — that restore hope, rebuild confidence, and remind every person that nobody is left behind and nobody stands alone.

Every Ride Forward event must advance this mission. If an event element does not serve participants, raise awareness, build community, or support the organization's sustainability, it does not belong in the event.

# **Section 3: Purpose**

The Ride Forward Program exists to:

1. **Create visible demonstrations of resilience** — riders on the road prove that limb loss does not end the journey
2. **Build community** by bringing together amputees, families, riders, sponsors, hospital partners, and supporters in a shared experience
3. **Raise funds** for Lost Limb Riders programs, including the Peer Connection Program, family support, and emergency assistance
4. **Recruit volunteers** by giving the public a tangible way to experience the mission
5. **Strengthen partnerships** with hospitals, prosthetic providers, veterans organizations, motorcycle dealerships, businesses, and civic groups
6. **Generate media attention** that educates the public about limb loss, disability inclusion, and community resilience
7. **Connect new participants** with the organization through event outreach, leading to Peer Connection Program referrals
8. **Honor the journey** of every amputee, veteran, survivor, and family member who has rebuilt after loss

# **Section 4: Philosophy**

## 4.1 The Motorcycle Is the Symbol, the People Are the Mission

Motorcycles give Ride Forward events energy, visibility, and cultural identity. But the events exist to serve people. Every logistical decision — from route design to rest stop placement to stage programming — must be evaluated through the lens of participant experience, safety, and mission impact.

## 4.2 Safety Is Non‑Negotiable

No event, no route, no schedule, and no fundraising goal justifies cutting corners on safety. Every Ride Forward event must meet the same safety standards regardless of size, location, weather pressure, or budget constraints. Safety is not a line item. It is the foundation.

## 4.3 Inclusion Is Intentional

Ride Forward events must be accessible to amputees, limb‑different individuals, wheelchair users, adaptive riders, families with children, elderly participants, non‑riders, and people with varying physical abilities. Inclusion does not happen by accident. It is designed.

## 4.4 Consistency Builds Trust

Participants, sponsors, law enforcement, and hospital partners must know what to expect from a Ride Forward event. Consistent execution — same safety standards, same volunteer training, same registration process, same quality — is what separates a professional organization from a casual gathering.

## 4.5 Every Event Is a Recruitment Opportunity

Every Ride Forward event is an opportunity to identify new participants, new volunteers, new mentors, new sponsors, and new hospital partners. Events must be designed with intentional outreach, follow‑up, and connection pathways.

# **Section 9: Eligibility Requirements**

## 9.1 Participant Eligibility

Ride Forward events are open to amputees and limb‑different individuals (riders and non‑riders), family members and caregivers, licensed motorcycle riders, approved adaptive riders, passengers meeting requirements, volunteers meeting requirements, and sponsors, hospital partners, community supporters, and guests.

## 9.2 Rider Requirements

All motorcycle riders must hold a valid motorcycle license or endorsement, carry current liability insurance, operate a roadworthy motorcycle, wear a DOT‑approved helmet (organizational policy requires helmets regardless of state law), wear eye protection, wear appropriate footwear (over‑the‑ankle boots recommended; open‑toe shoes prohibited), complete the Rider Briefing, sign the Ride Waiver, and not be impaired.

## 9.3 Passenger Requirements

All passengers must be at least 12 years of age or as required by state law, be seated on a motorcycle equipped for passengers, wear a DOT‑approved helmet, wear eye protection, wear appropriate footwear, sign the Ride Waiver, not be impaired, and receive a passenger safety briefing.

## 9.4 Minor Participants

Participants under 18 must have signed parental consent, be accompanied by a parent or guardian, may not operate a motorcycle, may ride as passengers only if meeting all requirements, and the parent or guardian must sign all applicable waivers.

## 9.5 Volunteer Eligibility

Volunteers must be at least 16 years of age, complete Volunteer Orientation, sign the Volunteer Agreement and Waiver, be assigned to a specific role, and not be impaired during service.

# **Section 10: Ride Classifications**

## 10.1 Signature Ride Forward

The flagship annual event combining a community motorcycle ride with a festival‑style gathering. Typical scale: 100–500+ riders, 300–1,000+ total attendees. Includes a scenic ride route (30–100 miles), staging area, law enforcement escort, rest stops, endpoint gathering with stage, food, music, activities, sponsor activation area, family zone, adaptive rider showcase, participant story stage, media coordination, and post‑ride celebration.

## 10.2 Awareness Ride

A ride designed to raise public awareness about limb loss and the organization. Typical scale: 25–150 riders. Includes ride route through populated areas, organized formation with branding, pre‑ride media outreach, community greeting, participant story sharing, and social media coverage.

## 10.3 Charity Ride

A registration‑based ride to raise funds for a specific program or cause. Typical scale: 50–300 riders. Includes fundraising registration, raffle or auction, sponsor‑backed rest stops, fundraising thermometer, top fundraiser recognition, and post‑event fund reporting.

## 10.4 Memorial Ride

A ride honoring the memory of a specific individual or group. Typical scale: 25–200 riders. Includes personalized materials, moment of silence, route significance, memory board, family participation, and celebratory tone.

## 10.5 Scenic Ride

A recreational ride for community enjoyment and connection. Typical scale: 10–75 riders. Includes scenic route, relaxed pace, group photo opportunities, destination meal, and low or no fundraising requirement.

## 10.6 Family Ride

A family‑inclusive ride experience. Typical scale: 20–100 attendees. Includes short accessible route, passenger opportunities, non‑riding activities, kid‑friendly programming, family photo station, and family resource table.

## 10.7 Adaptive Ride

A ride specifically designed for adaptive riders and participants with mobility challenges. Typical scale: 10–50 riders. Includes accessible route design, pre‑screening, trained road guards, extended briefing, backup transportation plan, and enhanced first aid.

## 10.8 Veteran Ride

A ride honoring and serving veterans with limb loss. Typical scale: 25–200 riders. Includes veterans organization partnerships, color guard ceremony, veteran recognition, VA resource table, and VA medical center collaboration.

# **Section 11: Event Planning Procedures**

## 11.1 Planning Timeline

**12 Weeks Before:** Event concept approved, Commander and Captain assigned, budget drafted, date confirmed, venue reserved, sponsor outreach initiated, law enforcement notified, insurance carrier notified.

**8 Weeks Before:** Route scouted and approved, volunteer recruitment launched, registration system live, sponsor packages sent, EMS standby confirmed, media outreach initiated, merchandise ordered, communication plan finalized.

**4 Weeks Before:** All permits obtained, volunteer roster finalized, sponsor commitments confirmed, briefing materials prepared, waivers printed, emergency response plan finalized, route markers prepared, hydration supplies ordered, AV confirmed, weather monitoring confirmed.

**1 Week Before:** Final volunteer briefing scheduled, supplies inventoried, final route check completed, communication equipment tested, registration database printed as backup, weather forecast reviewed, emergency contact list distributed, day‑of timeline finalized.

**Day Before:** Staging area set up, signage placed, final weather assessment, command staff confirmation call, rest stops prepared.

**Day of Event:** Command staff arrives minimum 90 minutes before registration opens, safety walkthrough, volunteer check‑in, registration set up and tested, sound system tested, final weather check, registration opens, rider briefing conducted, ride departs on schedule, post‑ride activities commence, teardown and cleanup, final debrief.

**Post‑Event (Within 7 Days):** After‑Action Report completed, financial reconciliation, satisfaction survey distributed, volunteer thank‑you sent, sponsor thank‑you and impact report sent, media follow‑up, incident reports filed, equipment inventoried and stored, lessons learned documented.

# **Section 12: Safety Policies and Rider/Passenger Requirements**

## 12.1 Safety Philosophy

Safety is not a policy to be followed when convenient. Safety is the organizational commitment that makes every other activity possible. No fundraising goal, sponsor expectation, schedule pressure, or participant request justifies compromising safety. When in doubt, the answer is no.

## 12.2 Mandatory Safety Equipment

**Riders:** DOT‑approved helmet (required by organizational policy regardless of state law), eye protection, over‑the‑ankle footwear, long pants, long sleeves or riding jacket (strongly recommended), gloves (recommended), reflective vest or sash (required for sweep rider and road guards).

**Passengers:** DOT‑approved helmet, eye protection, closed‑toe footwear, long pants.

## 12.3 Prohibited Conduct

1. Riding while impaired by alcohol, drugs, medication, or fatigue
2. Speeding, reckless riding, stunt riding, wheelies, stoppies, or formation disruption
3. Riding without a valid license or insurance
4. Carrying a passenger on a motorcycle not equipped for passengers
5. Consuming alcohol before or during the ride
6. Carrying firearms or weapons without pre‑approval by Event Commander
7. Removing or altering safety equipment during the ride
8. Departing from the planned route without Ride Captain authorization
9. Passing the Ride Captain without authorization
10. Leaving a rider behind without notifying the Sweep Rider

## 12.4 Riding Formation Standards

1. Staggered formation unless Ride Captain designates single file
2. Minimum 1‑second following distance in staggered formation (2‑second minimum in single file)
3. Lateral separation of approximately one lane width in staggered formation
4. No riding alongside another rider in the same lane
5. Signal all lane changes and turns
6. Headlights on at all times
7. Ride within personal skill limits

## 12.5 Speed Standards

1. Obey all posted speed limits
2. Ride Captain sets pace for the least experienced rider
3. Aggressive acceleration, braking, or weaving is prohibited
4. Extra caution in residential areas, school zones, and construction zones
5. Ride Captain may designate reduced speed zones at any time

# **Section 13: Motorcycle Inspection Standards**

## 13.1 Pre‑Event Inspection

Every motorcycle participating in a Ride Forward event must pass a safety inspection. The inspection may be conducted by the rider (self‑inspection) with verification at registration, or by a designated inspector at the staging area.

## 13.2 Inspection Checklist

| Item | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| Tires | Proper inflation, adequate tread depth, no visible damage or bulges |
| Brakes | Front and rear brakes functional, no excessive pad wear, brake light operational |
| Lights | Headlight (high and low beam), tail light, brake light, turn signals all functional |
| Horn | Operational |
| mirrors | Both mirrors present and properly adjusted |
| Controls | Throttle, clutch (if applicable), and brakes operate smoothly without sticking |
| Fluids | No visible leaks of oil, coolant, or brake fluid |
| Chain/Belt/Shaft | Proper tension and lubrication (chain/belt); no visible damage |
| Exhaust | Secure, no excessive noise beyond manufacturer specification |
| Seat | Secure, passenger seat and foot pegs present if carrying passengers |
| Frame | No visible cracks, bends, or damage |
| Helmet | DOT‑approved, undamaged, proper fit |
| Insurance | Current liability insurance card accessible |
| License | Valid motorcycle license or endorsement |

## 13.3 Inspection Process

1. Rider completes self‑inspection form before arriving at staging area
2. At registration, a designated inspector verifies critical safety items (tires, lights, brakes, helmet)
3. Motorcycles that fail inspection are not permitted to participate until deficiencies are corrected
4. Inspection results are documented on the Motorcycle Inspection Form
5. Motorcycles with safety deficiencies are offered basic repair assistance when available (air compressor, basic tools)

## 13.4 Adaptive Motorcycle Inspection

Adaptive motorcycles and modified equipment require additional inspection by a qualified inspector or the adaptive rider's certifying professional. Modifications to controls, frame, or balance systems must be verified as safe before event participation. The Safety Officer has final authority on adaptive motorcycle approval.

# **Section 14: Rider Briefing Procedures**

## 14.1 Mandatory Attendance

Every rider and passenger must attend the Rider Briefing before departing. No exceptions. Riders who do not attend the briefing may not participate in the ride.

## 14.2 Briefing Content

The Rider Briefing covers:

1. Welcome and event overview
2. Route description, distance, estimated duration, and key turns
3. Rest stop locations and timing
4. Riding formation instructions (staggered vs. single file)
5. Speed expectations and pace vehicle information
6. Hand signals and communication procedures
7. Road guard positions and what to do if you see a road guard signaling
8. Sweep rider introduction — what to do if you have a problem
9. What to do if you are separated from the group
10. Emergency procedures: motorcycle breakdown, accident, medical emergency, weather
11. Emergency contact numbers
12. Prohibited conduct review
13. Alcohol policy (no alcohol before or during the ride)
14. Passenger-specific safety reminders
15. Adaptive rider considerations (if applicable)
16. Photography and social media guidelines
17. Post‑ride activities overview
18. Questions and answers

## 14.3 Briefing Delivery

The Rider Briefing is delivered by the Ride Captain or Safety Officer. The briefing must be delivered in a location where all riders can see and hear the speaker. For large events (100+ riders), use a PA system. Briefing materials may include printed route maps, hand signal cards, and emergency contact cards.

## 14.4 Briefing Documentation

The Ride Captain confirms that the Rider Briefing has been completed. The fact that a briefing was conducted is documented in the event file. Attendance is confirmed through the registration check‑in process.

# **Section 15: Road Captain and Sweep Rider Responsibilities**

## 15.1 Road Captain

**Role:** The Road Captain leads the ride formation and is responsible for on‑road rider safety, route navigation, pace control, and communication with road guards.

**Responsibilities:**

1. Ride at the front of the formation in the designated lead position
2. Set a pace appropriate for the least experienced rider
3. Signal all turns, lane changes, stops, and hazards using standard hand signals
4. Maintain communication with the Safety Officer, sweep rider, and road guards via radio or phone
5. Make real‑time decisions about formation changes, speed adjustments, and route modifications based on road conditions
6. Coordinate with law enforcement escorts
7. Pull over safely if a mechanical issue, medical concern, or safety hazard is identified
8. Do not leave any rider behind — if a rider drops back, the Road Captain must be notified

**Qualifications:**

1. Minimum 3 years of riding experience
2. Completion of Road Captain Training Module
3. Familiarity with the planned route
4. Current motorcycle license and insurance
5. Functional communication equipment (radio or phone)
6. Designated by the Event Commander and approved by the Director of Riding Operations

## 15.2 Sweep Rider

**Role:** The Sweep Rider rides at the rear of the formation and is the safety net for the entire ride. No rider is left behind.

**Responsibilities:**

1. Ride at the rear of the formation — last motorcycle on the road
2. Carry a visible Lost Limb Riders flag or marker identifying the sweep position
3. Monitor for riders falling behind, experiencing mechanical issues, or needing assistance
4. Carry a basic tool kit, tire repair kit, and first aid kit
5. Carry a fully charged phone and communication equipment
6. If a rider stops, the Sweep Rider stops with them, assesses the situation, and communicates with the Road Captain
7. If a rider cannot continue, arrange safe motorcycle storage and participant transportation
8. Ensure the formation is complete at every rest stop and at the endpoint
9. Report the total rider count to the Road Captain at each stop

**Qualifications:**

1. Minimum 2 years of riding experience
2. Completion of Sweep Rider Training Module
3. Mechanical competency (basic roadside repair)
4. First aid training (recommended)
5. Functional communication equipment
6. Designated by the Event Commander

# **Section 16: Event Command Structure and Chain of Command**

## 16.1 Command Structure

Every Ride Forward event operates under a clear command structure. The chain of command ensures that decisions are made by the right person, communication flows efficiently, and every role is filled with a trained, accountable individual.

| Position | Authority | Reports To |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| Event Commander | Overall event authority (non‑riding and riding) | Director of Riding Operations |
| Ride Captain | Ride route, rider conduct, on‑road safety | Event Commander |
| Safety Officer | Event safety, risk assessment, emergency response | Event Commander (with independent safety override) |
| Coordinator | Logistics, registration, volunteer management | Event Commander |
| Road Captain | Lead rider, formation, pace, navigation | Ride Captain |
| Sweep Rider | Rear safety, rider accounting, mechanical backup | Ride Captain |
| Road Guards | Route monitoring, traffic management, hazard communication | Ride Captain |
| Volunteer Coordinator | Volunteer scheduling, assignment, check‑in | Coordinator |
| Registration Lead | Check‑in, waivers, forms, rider packets | Coordinator |
| Media Coordinator | Photography, social media, press relations | Event Commander |
| Family Zone Lead | Children's activities, family resource area | Coordinator |

## 16.2 Communication Chain

During the event, communication follows this chain:

1. Road guards communicate with the Road Captain via radio or phone
2. Road Captain communicates with the Ride Captain and Sweep Rider
3. Ride Captain communicates with the Safety Officer and Event Commander
4. Safety Officer communicates directly with 911 and emergency services when required
5. Event Commander communicates with the Coordinator, Media Coordinator, and external partners

## 16.3 Emergency Communication

In an emergency, any participant or volunteer may call 911 directly. The chain of command is a communication structure, not a barrier to emergency action. Safety takes precedence over protocol.

# **Section 17: Volunteer Positions and Duties**

## 17.1 Position Descriptions

**Registration Desk (2–4 volunteers):**

1. Greet riders and participants at check‑in
2. Verify registration and collect signed waivers
3. Distribute rider packets (route map, event schedule, emergency contact card, sponsor materials)
4. Verify motorcycle inspection completion
5. Direct riders to the staging area
6. Maintain a clean, organized, and welcoming registration area

**Staging Area Managers (2–3 volunteers):**

1. Organize the staging area for orderly rider departure
2. Direct riders to appropriate formation positions
3. Ensure the Rider Briefing area is set up and accessible
4. Coordinate with the Road Captain on departure timing
5. Manage the passenger staging area

**Rest Stop Volunteers (2–4 per rest stop):**

1. Set up and staff the rest stop before the ride arrives
2. Provide water, hydration supplies, and basic snacks
3. Maintain accessible restroom facilities
4. Assist participants with mobility needs
5. Monitor rest stop timing to keep the ride on schedule
6. Communicate with the Ride Captain on group departure readiness

**Road Guards (4–12 depending on event size):**

1. Position at designated intersections and hazard points along the route
2. Direct traffic and manage intersections during ride passage
3. Communicate with the Road Captain via radio or phone
4. Signal hazards, road conditions, and route changes to riders
5. Carry basic first aid and a charged phone
6. Remain at post until the Sweep Rider confirms the formation has passed

**Family Zone Volunteers (2–4 volunteers):**

1. Set up and supervise children's activities at the endpoint
2. Ensure all activities are safe, accessible, and age‑appropriate
3. Maintain a parent‑supervised environment at all times
4. Coordinate with the Family Zone Lead on programming and schedule
5. Distribute family resource materials

**Merchandise and Fundraising Volunteers (2–3 volunteers):**

1. Set up and staff the merchandise table
2. Handle cash and card transactions accurately
3. Manage raffle ticket sales, silent auction bids, and donation collection
4. Maintain accurate financial records for all transactions
5. Secure cash and merchandise at event close

**Cleanup Crew (4–8 volunteers):**

1. Assist with staging area teardown
2. Collect and dispose of trash and recycling
3. Return rented equipment
4. Restore the venue to its pre‑event condition
5. Inventory and secure organizational equipment

## 17.2 Volunteer Assignment

All volunteer assignments are made by the Volunteer Coordinator in consultation with the Event Commander. Volunteers receive their assignment in writing before the event and may not change assignments without Coordinator approval.

# **Section 18: Registration Procedures and Participant Check‑In**

## 18.1 Pre‑Event Registration

Online registration opens a minimum of 8 weeks before the event. Pre‑registration includes:

1. Participant information (name, contact, emergency contact)
2. Rider or non‑rider designation
3. Motorcycle information (if rider): make, model, year, license plate
4. Insurance information (if rider)
5. Passenger information (if applicable)
6. Waiver acknowledgment (digital signature accepted for pre‑registration; original signature required at check‑in)
7. Registration fee or donation (if applicable)
8. Dietary restrictions or accessibility needs
9. T‑shirt size (if applicable)
10. Photo/media release preference

## 18.2 Day‑Of Registration

Walk‑up registration is available at the staging area. Day‑of registration follows the same information requirements as pre‑registration. Day‑of participants must complete all forms on‑site before receiving their rider packet.

## 18.3 Check‑In Process

1. Participant approaches the registration desk
2. Registration volunteer verifies identity and registration status

# **Section 21: Weather Monitoring and Cancellation Procedures**

## 21.1 Weather Monitoring

The Safety Officer monitors weather conditions beginning 72 hours before the event and continuously on the day of the event. Weather monitoring sources include:

1. National Weather Service forecasts and alerts
2. Local weather radar and satellite imagery
3. Road condition reports from the state department of transportation
4. Law enforcement road condition notifications

## 21.2 Go/No‑Go Decision Framework

| Condition | Decision |
|-----------|----------|
| Clear or partly cloudy, temperatures 40°F–95°F, dry roads | GO |
| Light rain forecast with clearing expected, temperatures above 40°F, roads damp but passable | GO with enhanced safety briefing and reduced pace |
| Heavy rain forecast, lightning within 10 miles, high winds (gusts over 40 mph), temperatures below 32°F or above 100°F | DELAY — reassess at 30‑minute intervals |
| Active lightning, severe thunderstorm warning, tornado warning, ice on roads, flooding, visibility below 1/4 mile | NO GO — cancel or postpone |
| Fog, extreme heat, or other unusual conditions | SAFETY OFFICER DISCRETION |

## 21.3 Cancellation Authority

The Safety Officer has sole authority to cancel or delay an event due to weather. This decision may be made at any time — before the event, during registration, during the ride, or at any rest stop.

## 21.4 Cancellation Communication

If the event is cancelled or delayed:

1. The Safety Officer notifies the Event Commander immediately
2. The Event Commander notifies the Ride Captain, Coordinator, and all command staff
3. Volunteers at the registration desk announce the decision to participants
4. If the ride has already departed, the Ride Captain and Road Captain implement the emergency weather protocol: seek immediate shelter, do not continue the route, await further instructions
5. Social media and website are updated
6. Pre‑registered participants are notified by phone or email if the event is cancelled before the start time
7. A make‑up date or refund procedure is communicated within 48 hours

## 21.5 Ride‑Day Weather Hold

If conditions deteriorate during the ride:

1. The Safety Officer directs the Ride Captain to hold at the nearest safe location
2. Riders are directed to seek shelter at the nearest substantial structure (not under trees or metal structures)
3. The Road Captain and sweep rider ensure all riders are accounted for and sheltered
4. The Safety Officer monitors conditions and communicates go/resume/cancel decisions
5. If conditions do not improve within 30 minutes, the ride is cancelled for the day
6. Transportation home is arranged for participants who cannot safely continue

# **Section 22: Emergency Response and Accident Protocols**

## 22.1 Immediate Response — Motorcycle Accident

If a motorcycle accident occurs during the ride:

1. The Road Captain brings the formation to a safe stop ahead of the accident if possible
2. The Sweep Rider stops at or near the accident scene
3. Any participant may call 911 immediately — do not wait for authorization
4. The Sweep Rider assesses the scene for safety (fuel spill, fire risk, traffic hazard)
5. Do not move the injured rider unless there is an immediate life threat (fire, traffic)
6. Provide first aid only within your training
7. The Sweep Rider communicates with the Road Captain and Safety Officer
8. The Safety Officer responds to the scene or coordinates response remotely
9. The Event Commander is notified immediately
10. Road guards at the nearest positions assist with traffic control
11. The ride is held until the Safety Officer determines it is safe to continue
12. Documentation begins immediately: who, what, when, where, witnesses, actions taken

## 22.2 Medical Emergency (Non‑Accident)

If a participant experiences a medical emergency (heart attack, stroke, seizure, diabetic emergency, allergic reaction):

1. Call 911 immediately
2. Stay with the person and provide first aid within your training
3. Do not give food, water, or medication to an unconscious or semi‑conscious person
4. Notify the Safety Officer and Event Commander
5. Clear space around the person for emergency responders
6. If the event has an EMS standby unit, activate them immediately
7. Direct emergency responders to the location
8. Notify the participant's emergency contact
9. Complete an Incident Report within 24 hours

## 22.3 Mechanical Breakdown

If a motorcycle experiences a mechanical breakdown during the ride:

1. The rider safely pulls off the road to a safe location
2. The Sweep Rider stops with the rider
3. Assess whether the issue can be resolved with roadside tools
4. If repairable, the rider rejoins the group when safe
5. If not repairable, the Sweep Rider arranges motorcycle storage (tow service, local shop, or event support vehicle) and participant transportation to the endpoint
6. The Road Captain is informed of the rider's status
7. No rider is left stranded — the Sweep Rider stays with the rider until transportation is arranged

# **Section 27: Route Planning Standards and Scouting Procedures**

## 27.1 Route Planning Principles

1. Routes should be scenic, engaging, and appropriate for the event type and participant skill level
2. Routes must avoid high‑risk roads when safer alternatives exist (narrow bridges, blind curves on high‑speed roads, poorly maintained surfaces)
3. Routes should include a mix of road types to maintain rider interest while prioritizing safety
4. Routes must account for the least experienced rider in the group
5. Routes must include planned rest stops at appropriate intervals (maximum 60–80 miles between stops, or more frequently for hot weather or events with novice riders)
6. Routes must have accessible rest stops with restroom facilities
7. Routes must be scouted before the event by the Ride Captain and Safety Officer

## 27.2 Route Scouting

Route scouting must occur within 30 days of the event. The scout team includes:

1. Ride Captain (mandatory)
2. Safety Officer (mandatory)
3. At least one road guard team lead (recommended)

**Scouting Checklist:**

1. Drive the complete route at the planned riding speed
2. Identify hazards: construction zones, railroad crossings, sharp curves, steep grades, narrow lanes, poor pavement, gravel shoulders, limited visibility areas
3. Identify rest stop locations and confirm accessibility, parking, restroom availability, and permission to use
4. Confirm fuel availability at rest stops
5. Verify road conditions match the route map
6. Photograph key turns, hazards, and rest stops for the rider briefing
7. Note mileages and estimated travel times between points
8. Identify potential emergency shelter locations in case of severe weather
9. Identify the nearest hospitals and urgent care facilities along the route
10. Confirm cell phone coverage along the route (identify dead zones)

## 27.3 Route Documentation

The Ride Captain produces a Route Plan document including:

1. Turn‑by‑turn directions with mileages
2. Route map (printed and digital)
3. Rest stop locations with addresses and amenities
4. Hazard locations and mitigation notes
5. Emergency services locations along the route
6. Estimated time for the complete ride
7. Road guard positioning map (for medium and large events)
8. Communication dead zone notes

The Route Plan is distributed to all command staff minimum 1 week before the event.

# **Section 28: Fuel, Rest Stop, and Hydration Planning**

## 28.1 Fuel Planning

1. Identify fuel availability at the start, at rest stops, and at the endpoint
2. Riders are responsible for arriving at the staging area with a full tank of fuel
3. The Ride Captain announces fuel availability at the Rider Briefing
4. For routes exceeding 100 miles, a fuel stop is mandatory
5. Rest stop fuel availability is included in the route plan

## 28.2 Rest Stop Planning

Rest stops are planned at maximum 60–80 mile intervals, or more frequently based on:

1. Weather conditions (heat increases hydration needs)
2. Event type (adaptive rides may require more frequent stops)
3. Rider experience level (novice groups may need more stops)
4. Route terrain (mountainous or winding routes cause more fatigue)

**Rest Stop Requirements:**

1. Parking sufficient for the full ride formation
2. Accessible restroom facilities (portable restrooms if permanent facilities are unavailable)
3. Hydration supplies: water minimum; electrolyte drinks recommended in hot weather
4. Basic snacks (granola bars, fruit, crackers)
5. Shade or shelter (if available)
6. First aid kit
7. Accessible pathway for participants with mobility needs
8. Lost Limb Riders signage identifying the rest stop

## 28.3 Hydration Standards

1. Water must be available at every rest stop and at the staging area
2. In temperatures above 80°F, electrolyte drinks must also be available
3. Riders are encouraged to carry a hydration system or water bottle on the motorcycle
4. The Safety Officer may mandate additional hydration stops based on heat index
5. Heat illness awareness is included in the Rider Briefing when temperatures exceed 80°F

# **Chapter A: Motorcycle Safety Standards**

## A.1 Organizational Helmet Policy

Lost Limb Riders requires DOT‑approved helmets for all riders and passengers at every Ride Forward event, regardless of state law. This is non‑negotiable. A helmet does not guarantee safety, but riding without one guarantees increased risk.

## A.2 Rider Protective Gear Standards

| Gear | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| Helmet | DOT‑approved, undamaged, proper fit — required for all riders and passengers |
| Eye protection | Required — shield, glasses, or goggles |
| Footwear | Over‑the‑ankle boots recommended; open‑toe shoes, sandals, flip‑flops prohibited |
| Pants | Long pants required — jeans minimum; riding pants recommended |
| Jacket | Long sleeves or riding jacket strongly recommended |
| Gloves | Recommended — riding gloves with knuckle protection preferred |
| Reflective vest | Required for sweep rider and road guards; recommended for all riders |

## A.3 Mechanical Standards

Every motorcycle must pass the inspection checklist in Section 13. Motorcycles that do not meet minimum safety standards are excluded from the ride.

## A.4 Impairment Policy

No rider or volunteer may participate while impaired by alcohol, drugs, prescription medication that affects alertness, or any medical condition that compromises safe operation. The Safety Officer may administer a field sobriety assessment if impairment is suspected. Confirmed impairment results in immediate removal.

## A.5 Fatigue Management

Riders are encouraged to rest before the event. The Ride Captain announces rest stop locations at the briefing. If a rider appears fatigued, the Sweep Rider encourages rest at the next stop. Fatigued riders are not pressured to continue.

---

# **Chapter B: Adaptive Rider Inclusion**

## B.1 Philosophy

Adaptive riders are not exceptions to the ride — they are riders. Lost Limb Riders creates ride experiences where adaptive riders participate fully, safely, and with dignity.

## B.2 Adaptive Rider Registration

Adaptive riders must pre‑register minimum 4 weeks before the event for equipment assessment, route evaluation, road guard training, backup transportation planning, and first aid preparation.

## B.3 Equipment Considerations

| Modification | Assessment |
|-------------|------------|
| Hand controls (brake/clutch) | Functional test at staging area |
| Prosthetic‑compatible mounts | Visual inspection and secure attachment verification |
| Modified seat or balance system | Functional assessment by inspector or certifying professional |
| Sidecar or trike conversion | Full mechanical inspection per Section 13 |
| Custom handlebars or grips | Secure attachment and control function verification |

## B.4 Route Considerations

Routes for adaptive rides prioritize smooth pavement, wide lanes, closer rest stop intervals (maximum 40 miles), accessible restroom facilities, and avoidance of steep grades, tight switchbacks, and gravel sections.

## B.5 Backup Transportation

Every adaptive ride has a backup transportation plan with a support vehicle capable of carrying the motorcycle and the rider. The rider's dignity is prioritized — transportation is framed as support, not rescue.

# **Chapter C: Family‑Friendly Event Standards**

## C.1 Philosophy

Families are the backbone of the limb‑loss community. Family‑friendly events are the foundation of community, not lesser rides.

## C.2 Family Ride Requirements

1. Ride route under 30 miles with minimal highway riding
2. Relaxed pace suitable for less experienced riders and passengers
3. Passenger opportunities for family members meeting requirements
4. Endpoint activities: games, food, face painting, adaptive demonstrations, photo stations
5. Kid‑friendly programming staffed by trained volunteers
6. Family resource table with program information and referrals

## C.3 Minor Safety Standards

1. All minors accompanied by parent or guardian at all times
2. Minors may not operate a motorcycle during the event
3. Minors as passengers must meet all requirements and state law
4. Family zone supervised by trained volunteers at all times
5. Photography consent obtained for all minor participants
6. Lost child procedures: immediate lockdown, PA announcement, search team, law enforcement if not found within 15 minutes

# **Chapter D: Public Relations and Media Management**

## D.1 Media Philosophy

Media coverage extends the mission. Every interaction is an opportunity to educate, recruit, attract sponsors, and build credibility — never at the expense of participant privacy, dignity, or safety.

## D.2 Media Coordinator Responsibilities

Participants who agree to media interviews are briefed on key messages, reminded not to disclose confidential information about others, and told they may decline any question or end the interview at any time.

## D.4 Photography Standards

3. Sensitive moments photographed only with explicit consent
4. Photos used only for authorized organizational purposes

# **Chapter E: Crisis Communications**

## E.1 Activation Triggers

Crisis communications activates when: a serious accident occurs, a fatality occurs, a participant requires hospitalization, an event is cancelled for safety reasons, a media story threatens reputation, or any event generates significant public concern.

## E.2 Crisis Communication Team

1. **Lead Spokesperson:** Founder/CEO or Executive Director
2. **Media Coordinator:** Manages media logistics and message distribution
3. **Legal Advisor:** Reviews all public statements before release
4. **Program Director:** Provides accurate program information (de‑identified)

## E.3 Communication Principles

1. Respond quickly — silence creates speculation
2. Be factual — do not speculate
3. Be compassionate — express concern
4. Be brief — deliver key facts
5. Be consistent — all spokespersons deliver the same message
6. Protect privacy — do not identify participants without family authorization
7. Designate a single point of contact for media

## E.4 Holding Statement

> "Lost Limb Riders is aware of an incident involving participants at [Event Name] on [Date]. We are cooperating with authorities and our immediate priority is the safety and well‑being of everyone involved. We will provide additional information as it becomes available."

## E.5 Social Media During Crisis

1. Pause scheduled posts immediately
2. Post the holding statement if the incident is public
3. Do not post photos or videos from the incident
4. Monitor comments and respond only with the approved statement
5. Resume normal posting only after the crisis lead authorizes it

# **Chapter G: Accessibility Standards for Every Ride**

## G.1 Commitment

Every Ride Forward event meets minimum accessibility standards. Accessibility is not reserved for adaptive rides — it is a baseline for all events.

## G.2 Physical Accessibility

1. Wheelchair‑accessible registration area with smooth, firm pathway
2. Accessible parking close to registration and activity areas
3. At least one accessible restroom unit per rest stop and at the endpoint
4. Firm, stable, wide pathways between event areas
5. Seating at all activity areas
6. Accessible viewing locations at stage and presentation areas

## G.3 Communication Accessibility

1. Event announcements made audibly and displayed in writing when feasible
2. Large print materials available upon request
3. Assistive listening devices available at events with amplified sound
4. Staff trained to communicate clearly and patiently

## G.4 Participation Accessibility

1. Non‑riding options clearly communicated in all event materials
2. Passenger opportunities available
3. Endpoint activities designed for all abilities
4. Family zone activities accessible to all
5. Dietary accommodations available

## G.5 Accessibility Audit

Before each event, the Coordinator audits the venue and route for accessible parking, restroom accessibility, pathway surfaces and widths, seating availability, signage visibility, and barriers to participation with mitigation plans.

# **Chapter H: Volunteer Emergency Response Training**

## H.1 All Volunteers — Minimum Training

All volunteers must be able to: recognize a medical emergency, call 911 with accurate location, direct emergency responders, protect the scene, notify command staff, and perform basic first aid (wound pressure, recovery position, choking response).

## H.2 Road Guard — Enhanced Training

Road guards must additionally: manage traffic at intersections, recognize motorcycle accidents from a distance, provide traffic control at accident scenes, use radio communication for hazard/emergency reporting, and administer basic first aid including tourniquet application.

## H.3 Sweep Rider — Advanced Training

The sweep rider must additionally: assess an accident scene for safety, provide first aid including spinal injury awareness, use a fire extinguisher, perform basic motorcycle troubleshooting, and manage a stranded rider situation.

## H.4 First Aid Certification

At least two volunteers per event must hold current first aid/CPR certification from an accredited provider.

## H.5 Training Documentation

All emergency response training is documented with volunteer name, role, training completed, date, certifying provider, and next recertification date. Records are maintained in the volunteer file and verified before each event.
