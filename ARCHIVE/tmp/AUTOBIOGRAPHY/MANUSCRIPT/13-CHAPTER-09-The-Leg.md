# **Chapter 9: The Leg**

I don’t remember the exact moment they told me they were going to take it.

Maybe I blocked it out. Maybe my brain decided that particular memory wasn’t worth keeping, like deleting an old file to free up space. Or maybe nobody had to tell me because I already knew. The leg had been giving me trouble for a while. Pain that started as a dull ache and grew into something sharper. Circulation problems from years of everything I’d put my body through — the drinking, the smoking, the poor diet, the heart attack, the cancer. Your body keeps score. Every bad decision, every night you stayed out too late, every cigarette you swore was the last one — it all goes into the ledger. And eventually, the bill comes due.

My right leg was the bill.

The vascular specialist was a younger guy. Early forties, maybe. Sharp. He didn’t waste time with small talk. He sat me down in his office, pulled up the images on his monitor, and pointed at the screen. I could see the problem before he even explained it. The blood flow was almost nonexistent. The tissue was dying. He said:

> “John, we’ve tried everything we can to save the leg. The stents aren’t holding. The bypass didn’t take. If we don’t amputate, the tissue damage will spread, and you’ll be looking at a much bigger problem. Sepsis. Organ failure. We need to take it below the knee.”

I sat there for a minute. The words sat in the air between us like smoke. Below the knee. Below the knee. Below the knee. I kept repeating it in my head, trying to make it feel real. It didn’t feel real. It felt like he was talking about somebody else’s leg. Somebody else’s life.

I asked:

> “What are my options?”

He shook his head.

> “There aren’t any.”

I appreciated that. No sugarcoating. No false hope. Just the truth, delivered clean and straight. That’s what you want from a surgeon. You want someone who isn’t afraid to tell you the hard thing, because the hard thing is reality, and the sooner you accept reality, the sooner you can start dealing with it.

I drove home that afternoon in a fog. I don’t remember the drive. I don’t remember stopping at any lights or turning any corners. I just remember pulling into the driveway and sitting in the car with the engine running, staring at the garage door, trying to figure out how I was going to tell Genie.

She already knew something was wrong the second I walked in. You can’t hide much from a woman you’ve been married to for decades. She can read your face like a billboard. I sat down at the kitchen table. She sat across from me. I told her:

> “They have to take the leg.”

She didn’t cry. She didn’t panic. She just reached across the table, took my hand, and said:

> “Then we’ll figure it out. Together.”

That word again. *We.* I was starting to realize that “we” was the most important word in any marriage.

The surgery was scheduled for a Thursday morning. I spent the night before lying awake, staring at the ceiling, listening to Genie breathe beside me. I thought about all the places that leg had taken me. All the miles I’d walked on it. All the times I’d stood on it when I needed to be steady. It had carried me through a lot, that leg. And I was about to say goodbye to it.

They came to get me at six in the morning. A nurse with a cheerful voice and a wheelchair. I remember thinking how strange it was that they made you sit in a wheelchair to go to the operating room when you were about to lose the ability to walk anyway. Some kind of hospital irony. I kissed Genie goodbye. She said:

> “I’ll be right here when you wake up.”

I believed her.

The surgery took about four hours. I woke up in the recovery room with a fog in my head and a strange sensation in my right leg that I couldn’t quite place. It took me a minute to remember. Then I looked down. The blanket was flat where my leg should have been. There was a bandage. There was pain. There was a strange emptiness that my brain couldn’t quite process. It wasn’t like looking at something missing. It was like looking at something that was there and not there at the same time. My brain was still sending signals to a leg that didn’t exist anymore. Phantom messages to an address that was no longer occupied.

The first time I tried to stand, I fell.

It was two days after the surgery. A physical therapist named Diane came in to help me try to get on my feet. She was a small woman with a big voice and an attitude that said *I’m not going to let you feel sorry for yourself.* I liked her immediately. She helped me swing my legs — leg, I mean — over the side of the bed. She positioned a walker in front of me. She said:

> “Alright, John. Let’s see what you’ve got.”

I pushed myself up. The world tilted. My balance was gone. I didn’t know where my body ended and the space around me began. I took one unsteady hop and felt my hip twist. I went down hard. Diane caught me before I hit the floor, but not before I felt the full weight of my new reality pressing down on me. I sat on the edge of the bed, breathing hard, and said:

> “That was pathetic.”

Diane said:

> “That was the first time. The first time is always pathetic. You’ll get better.”

She was right. But “better” took a long time.

The first fitting for the prosthetic was a whole different kind of humbling. The prosthetist was a man named Carlos, a veteran of the business who’d fitted hundreds of amputees. He measured my residual limb, made a plaster cast, and told me to come back in two weeks. When I came back, he handed me a socket made of carbon fiber and a metal pylon with a prosthetic foot attached to the end. It looked like something from a science fiction movie. Lightweight. Modern. Nothing like the wooden peg legs you see in pirate movies. I was grateful for that.

Putting it on for the first time was a nightmare. The socket had to fit tight — tight enough to create a seal, tight enough to hold suction. I pushed my stump into it and felt the pressure immediately. It was like putting your foot into a shoe that was three sizes too small. Carlos adjusted it. Adjusted it again. Handed me a pair of crutches and said:

> “Alright. Let’s take a walk.”

I stood up. The prosthetic clicked against the floor. I took a step. The socket shifted. I took another step. Pain shot up my residual limb. I stopped, closed my eyes, and breathed through it. Carlos said:

> “It’s going to hurt for a while. That’s normal. Your body has to get used to bearing weight in a way it’s never done before. But it will get easier.”

He was right about that too. But “easier” didn’t mean “easy.”

The first time I looked in the mirror and saw myself without the leg, I wanted to look away. It was about a week after the surgery. I was home, standing in the bathroom, supporting myself on the sink. I’d taken off the bandage to let the air get to the incision. I looked down. I saw the stump. I saw the stitches. I saw the swelling and the bruising and the strange shape of what was left. I saw a body that looked like it had been through a war. And I guess it had.

I didn’t look away.

I forced myself to look. I stared at it for a long time. I told myself: *This is you now. This is your body. This is your life. You don’t have to like it, but you have to accept it. Because acceptance is the first step toward moving forward.*

That phrase — “get back up” — it stopped being a motto and started being a job. Every single day, I had to decide that I was going to try again. Every single day, I had to choose between staying on the ground and getting back on my feet. Some days I chose right. Some days I chose wrong. But I never stopped choosing.

I remember the first time I walked from the living room to the kitchen without holding onto anything. It was maybe twenty feet. It took me thirty seconds and I was sweating by the end of it. But I did it. I stood in the kitchen, breathing hard, and I laughed. Genie came in and asked what was funny. I said:

> “I just walked across the room. Twenty feet. And it feels like I climbed a mountain.”

She smiled and said:

> “Then you climbed a mountain.”

I chose to get back up. Not because I was brave. Not because I was strong. Because the alternative was unacceptable. I had people counting on me. I had a life that wasn’t finished. I had a story that wasn’t done being written. And I had a wife who used words like “we” when she talked about hard things.

So I got back up. One step at a time. One day at a time. One fall and one recovery at a time.
