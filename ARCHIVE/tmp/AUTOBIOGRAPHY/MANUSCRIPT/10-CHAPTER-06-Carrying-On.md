# **Chapter 6: Carrying On**

I’ve lost people.

That’s a sentence that sounds simple but isn’t. When you lose someone you love, it’s not like losing your keys. You don’t find them later under the couch. They’re just gone. And the world keeps spinning like nothing happened.

The first loss that really broke me open was my father.

He’d been sick for a while. Not the dramatic kind of sick that lands you in the hospital with tubes and machines. The slow kind. The kind that eats away at a man piece by piece until there’s nothing left. He’d worked his whole life — railroad man, hands calloused and back bent — and his body had finally had enough.

I got the call at work. My mother’s voice on the other end, shaky but controlled. She said:

> “John, you need to come home. Your father’s taken a turn.”

I dropped everything. I drove two hours to the old house where I grew up, speeding the whole way, praying under my breath. When I got there, my father was in the bed he’d shared with my mother for forty‑three years. He was thin. So thin. The cancer had eaten him from the inside out, and what was left was a skeleton wrapped in papery skin.

I sat beside him and held his hand. He was awake, barely. His eyes found mine and he tried to smile. He said:

> “You came.”

I said:

> “Of course I came, Dad.”

He squeezed my hand. His grip was weak, nothing like the hands that had taught me how to throw a baseball, how to change a tire, how to be a man. He said:

> “Take care of your mother. She’s going to need you.”

I said:

> “I will.”

He closed his eyes and I thought he was gone. But he opened them again and looked at me and said:

> “You did good, son. You did real good.”

Those were the last words he ever said to me. He died three hours later, with my mother on one side and me on the other. The room went quiet. The machine that had been beeping stopped beeping. And my mother let out a sound I’d never heard before — a sound that came from somewhere deeper than crying.

I didn’t cry at first. I couldn’t. I sat in that room and stared at my father’s face and waited for something to hit me. But nothing came. I was hollow.

The funeral was a blur. I shook hands with people I hadn’t seen in years. I accepted hugs from cousins I barely recognized. I said the right things — *thank you for coming*, *he was a good man*, *he’s in a better place* — but I didn’t feel any of them. I was going through the motions. My body was there, but the rest of me was somewhere else, floating above the crowd, watching the scene play out like it was a movie about someone else.

The grief didn’t hit me until three weeks later.

I was at the grocery store. Standing in the cereal aisle, trying to decide between two brands. And suddenly I couldn’t breathe. The fluorescent lights were too bright. The colors on the boxes were too loud. I dropped my basket and walked out of the store. I stood in the parking lot and I wept. Right there, in the middle of the afternoon, with strangers walking past me pushing their carts.

That’s the thing about grief. It doesn’t follow a schedule. It doesn’t care if you have things to do. It hits you in the middle of the grocery store, or while you’re driving, or when you see something that reminds you of them and suddenly you can’t breathe.

I learned that you don’t get over it. You get through it. There’s a difference. Getting over it means it stops hurting. Getting through it means you learn to carry the hurt with you without letting it stop you.

I carried a lot after my father died. I carried the memory of his hand in mine, papery and weak. I carried the sound of my mother’s grief. I carried the words he’d said to me — *you did good* — and I held them close, because I knew they were the last gift he’d ever give me.

A few years later, I lost my mother. That was different. My mother had been the constant in my life, the one who wrote letters and remembered birthdays and kept the family together. When my father died, she’d been strong. But after he was gone, something in her started to fade. She didn’t get sick — not with anything you could name. She just got tired. She’d lived fifty years with a man she loved, and without him, the color drained out of the world for her.

I visited her every weekend. We’d sit in her living room and drink coffee and talk about the old days. She’d tell me stories about my father that I’d never heard before — how he’d proposed to her on a park bench, how he’d held her hand through three labors, how he still called her beautiful even after forty years.

One weekend she looked at me and said:

> “I miss him, John. Every day.”

I said:

> “I know, Mom.”

She said:

> “It gets easier, doesn’t it?”

I didn’t know how to answer that. The truth was that it got different. Not easier. But I didn’t say that. I just nodded and held her hand.

She died in her sleep six months later. Peaceful. Quiet. The way she’d wanted to go. When I got the call, I didn’t cry. I sat in my truck for a long time, staring at nothing. Then I drove home and told Genie and let her hold me.

Losing my parents taught me something I couldn’t have learned any other way. It taught me that time is short. It taught me that the people you love won’t be here forever. It taught me to say the things that matter while you still can.

I started telling Genie I loved her more often. Not just at night before bed. In the middle of the day, for no reason. I’d call her from work and say:

> “Just calling to say I love you.”

She’d laugh and say:

> “Is everything okay?”

I’d say:

> “Everything’s fine. I just wanted to say it.”

I started calling my daughters just to hear their voices. I’d ask them about their day, about their friends, about the small things that filled their lives. I listened more than I talked. I paid attention to the moments that most people rush through.

I remember one afternoon with my oldest daughter. She was sixteen. We were sitting on the porch, watching the rain. Neither of us was talking. Just sitting. After a while, she said:

> “Dad, are you okay?”

I said:

> “Yeah, honey. I’m okay.”

She said:

> “You seem different lately.”

I thought about how to answer. Then I said:

> “I’ve been thinking about Grandpa a lot.”

She nodded. She knew what I meant. She’d seen what losing him did to me. She said:

> “I miss him too.”

We sat there a little longer. Then she leaned her head against my shoulder. It was a small thing. But it meant everything.

Loss took people from me. It took my father and my mother and friends I’d known my whole life. But it also gave me something I didn’t expect: the determination to not waste the time I have left.

I think about my father’s last words to me. *You did good.* I want to earn those words. I want to be able to look back at the end of my life and know that I did good. That I loved the people who mattered. That I showed up when it counted. That I didn’t let the fear of losing people stop me from loving them with everything I had.

Grief doesn’t ever really leave. It sits in a corner of your heart and waits. But over time, you learn to make room for it. You learn to carry it the way you’d carry a wounded child — carefully, gently, without letting it weigh you down so much that you can’t move forward.

I’m still carrying it. I’ll probably carry it for the rest of my life. But I’ve learned something important: the carrying doesn’t stop you from living. It just reminds you why living matters. And as long as I have people to love, I’ll keep showing up. I’ll keep calling just to hear their voices. I’ll keep saying the things that matter while I still can.

Because that’s what my father would have wanted. And that’s what my mother would have wanted. And that’s what I want my daughters to remember about me when I’m gone.
