# **Chapter 11: Faith**

I don’t talk about my faith to convince anyone of anything.

I talk about it because it’s part of my story, and I can’t tell my story honestly without it. I’m not a preacher. I’m not a theologian. I’m just a guy who’s been through a lot and came out the other side, and I know — I know — that I didn’t do it alone.

There was one night in the hospital that I think about more than any other. It was during the cancer treatments, before the amputation. I was in a room on the third floor, alone, around two in the morning. The nurses had done their rounds. The monitors had been checked. The IV was dripping at its usual pace. Everything was fine, medically speaking. But I couldn’t sleep. I was lying there, staring at the ceiling tiles, counting the little holes in them the way you do when you can’t turn your brain off.

I was scared.

Not the kind of scared that comes from something specific. The kind of scared that comes from nowhere and everywhere at once. The kind that sits on your chest and makes it hard to breathe. I thought about my daughters. I thought about Genie. I thought about all the things I hadn’t done yet, all the things I might never get to do. I thought about dying. Not in a dramatic, movie-of-the-week way. Just in a quiet, matter-of-fact way. *This could be it,* I thought. *This could be how it ends.*

And in that moment, I prayed.

I didn’t fold my hands. I didn’t close my eyes. I didn’t kneel beside the bed. I just lay there, looking at the ceiling, and I talked to God the way you’d talk to a friend who was sitting in the room with you. I said:

> “I can’t do this alone. I need help.”

And I felt something.

I can’t explain it better than that. I’m not going to tell you the room filled with light or that I heard a voice. I didn’t. What happened was quieter than that. It was like a weight lifting off my chest. Like someone had been sitting on me and decided to stand up. I took a deep breath — the first deep breath I’d taken in hours — and the tightness in my throat loosened. The fear didn’t disappear, but it moved over. It made room for something else. Something that felt like peace.

I didn’t fall asleep right away. But I stopped counting the ceiling tiles. And I stopped being afraid of the dark.

I wasn’t raised in a religious household. My family believed in God the way many families do — casually, quietly, without making a big production out of it. We went to church on Easter and Christmas. We said grace before Thanksgiving dinner. We knew the Lord’s Prayer well enough to mumble along when the occasion called for it. But faith wasn’t something we talked about much. It was just there, in the background, like a radio playing low in another room.

That changed when my life started falling apart.

It wasn’t a dramatic conversion. It wasn’t a lightning bolt from the sky. It was slow. Gradual. Each time something went wrong, I found myself reaching for something to hold onto. And the only thing that was always there, the only thing that never ran out or let me down, was the sense that I wasn’t alone. I started praying more. Not fancy prayers. Not church prayers. Just honest conversations with God.

Sometimes they sounded like: *What the hell is going on?*

Sometimes they sounded like: *Please let the scan be clear.*

Sometimes they didn’t have words at all. Just silence and the hope that Someone was listening.

I believe Someone was.

There were moments during recovery when I was in so much pain I couldn’t think straight. After the amputation, the phantom pain would hit at night, and I’d lie in bed with my teeth clenched, gripping the sheets, trying not to wake Genie. In those moments, I couldn’t form a coherent thought. I couldn’t string together a sentence. But I could still pray. I could still say a single word, over and over, like a mantra: *Help. Help. Help.*

And help came.

Not always the way I wanted. Not always when I thought I needed it. But it came. A nurse would walk in at just the right moment. The pain would subside. Genie would wake up on her own and put her hand on my arm. Nothing miraculous. Just small graces. Tiny reminders that I was being carried.

I had a conversation with a hospital chaplain once. His name was Father Mike. He was an older guy, maybe seventy, with a face that looked like it had seen a lot of suffering and a lot of joy and couldn’t quite tell the difference anymore. He came into my room one afternoon, introduced himself, and asked if I wanted to talk. I said:

> “I don’t know what to say. I’m not even sure what I believe.”

He nodded and said:

> “That’s a good place to start.”

We talked for about an hour. I told him about my life. The drinking. The addiction. The heart attack. The cancer. The leg. He listened without interrupting. When I was done, he said:

> “You’ve been through a lot, John. And you’re still here. That’s not luck. That’s grace.”

I didn’t know what to say to that. So I just nodded. But I thought about it for a long time after he left. *Grace.* It’s a word I’d heard before, but I’d never really understood it. I always thought it meant something you didn’t deserve. And maybe that’s exactly what it is. I didn’t deserve to survive the things I’d been through. I didn’t deserve a wife like Genie, daughters who loved me, friends who showed up. But I got it anyway. That’s grace.

I remember one specific prayer that I thought was answered in the worst possible way, only to realize later it was answered in the best possible way. When the doctors told me they had to take my leg, I prayed for a miracle. I asked God to save my leg. I begged. I bargained. *If you let me keep my leg, I’ll be a better man. I’ll go to church. I’ll help people. Just let me keep it.* I said that prayer a hundred times. And every time, the answer was the same. No.

I was angry about that for a long time. I thought God had ignored me. I thought my faith had failed me. But now, looking back, I see it differently. If I’d kept that leg, I would have stayed in constant pain. I would have lost my mobility anyway, just slower and more painfully. The infection could have spread. I could have died. The amputation wasn’t a punishment. It was a solution. The prayer was answered — just not the way I wanted it to be answered. I wanted a miracle on my terms. God gave me a miracle on His terms: a chance to keep living.

That’s the thing about faith. It doesn’t give you what you want. It gives you what you need. And sometimes you don’t recognize the difference until years later.

I’m not here to preach. I’m not here to tell you what to believe. But I know this: I should have died a dozen times. I should have given up a hundred times. I should have stayed down after the first blow, let alone the tenth. Something kept me going. Something that wasn’t just me.

Call it God. Call it the universe. Call it the human spirit. I don’t care what you call it. But I know it’s real, because I felt it carry me when I couldn’t walk on my own. I felt it in the middle of the night when I was alone and afraid. I felt it in the voice of a nurse, the hand of my wife, the silence of a hospital room at two in the morning.

I don’t have all the answers. I don’t know why some people get healed and some don’t. I don’t know why some prayers are answered and others seem to disappear into the void. I don’t know why I’m still here when so many good people have gone too soon. I don’t know. And I’ve stopped pretending I do.

But I know this: Faith didn’t make the hard things easy. It made them bearable.

And sometimes, bearable is enough.
