# **Chapter 16: Speaking to Inspire**

I never planned to be a speaker. I never wanted to be a speaker. If you'd told me ten years ago that I'd be standing in front of rooms full of people, telling my story for a living, I would have laughed in your face. I'm not a performer. I'm not a natural storyteller. I'm just a guy who lived through some things and didn't quit. That's not a career path. That's just survival.

But people started asking.

It happened slowly at first. A friend from church asked if I'd come talk to his small group. Just my story, he said. Twenty minutes. Nothing formal. I said yes because I didn't know how to say no. I showed up, stood in front of about twelve people in a church basement, and told them what had happened. The addiction. The heart attack. The cancer. The amputation. The recovery. I talked for maybe fifteen minutes, my voice shaking the whole time, my hands clammy. When I finished, there was a long silence. I thought I'd done something wrong. Then a woman in the front row started crying.

Not sad crying. Not pity crying. The kind of crying that comes from recognition. From seeing yourself in someone else's story. She came up to me afterward and grabbed my hand and said:

> I've been sober for three years, and I've never told anyone how hard it still is. Hearing you talk about wanting to give up — that's what I needed to hear today.

I didn't know what to say. I mumbled something about being glad it helped and walked out to my truck feeling like I'd been hit by a truck. Something had shifted. I didn't understand it yet, but I felt it.

The next request came from a recovery center. A guy I'd met in treatment years before was working there now. He called me up and said:

> John, I've got a room full of guys who are right where you were. They're fresh out of detox, they're shaky, they're scared, they don't think they can make it. I need someone to come in and tell them it's possible.

I said yes. I drove to the center, walked through a metal detector, sat in a room with about thirty men in various stages of early sobriety. Some of them looked like they hadn't slept in weeks. Some of them looked like they'd given up before I even opened my mouth. I told them my story — the drinking, the rock bottom, the hospital bed, the long climb back. I kept it simple. No fancy language. No inspirational quotes. Just the truth.

Afterward, a young guy came up to me. He couldn't have been more than twenty-five. He had track marks on his arms and tears in his eyes. He said:

> I thought I was too far gone. I thought I'd done too much damage. But you went through all that and you're still standing. That means I can do it too.

That's when I understood. My story isn't mine. It belongs to everyone who needs to hear it. Every person who's sitting in a dark room wondering if they're going to make it. Every person who's staring at a hospital ceiling at four in the morning, asking God if this is the end. Every person who's looking at their own reflection and not recognizing who they've become. My story belongs to them. I'm just the one who gets to deliver it.

The speaking took off in a way I never expected. Word spread. Other churches asked. Then hospitals. Rehab centers. Corporate events. Nonprofit fundraisers. School assemblies. I went from standing in a church basement in front of twelve people to standing on a stage in front of five hundred. It happened fast. Too fast, sometimes. I'd look out at a crowd and feel the old panic rising — that voice in my head that said: *What are you doing here? You're not a speaker. You're not qualified. You're going to mess this up.*

But I'd take a breath. I'd feel the weight of my prosthetic. I'd think about all the mornings I'd said *I Can. I Will.* when I didn't believe it. And I'd start talking.

Every audience is different. Every speech has to be tailored to the people in front of me. I learned that early. You can't stand up and give the same talk to a group of corporate executives that you give to a room full of people in physical therapy. They need different things. They're in different places.

For a corporate event, I focus on resilience. On showing up when things are hard. On the discipline of getting better every day. I talk about how recovery is like building a business — you don't do it overnight, you do it one rep at a time, one day at a time, one decision at a time. I talk about the Lost Limb Riders, about community, about not trying to do it alone. The executives nod along because they're thinking about their quarterly targets and their team dynamics. But by the end, half of them are tearing up anyway. Because underneath all the business talk, everyone wants the same thing. They want to know that their struggle matters. That the hard work is worth it.

For a rehab center, everything changes. The energy is different. The room is different. There are wheelchairs and walkers and people in hospital gowns who didn't ask to be there. I don't talk about quarterly targets. I don't talk about business. I talk about the small victories. The first time I stood up. The first time I took a step. The first time I walked across the room without holding onto anything. I tell them:

> I walked again. Not because I was strong. Because I was stubborn. I fell down a thousand times and got up a thousand and one. You can do the same thing. It's going to hurt. It's going to be humiliating. Some days you're going to cry. But you're going to get up again. And eventually, that getting up becomes walking. And that walking becomes running.

For a recovery center — addiction, not injury — I take a different approach. I talk about the shame. The rock bottom that you have to hit before you're ready to climb. I tell them:

> I know what it's like to hate yourself. I know what it's like to wake up and not remember what you did the night before. I know what it's like to look in the mirror and not recognize the person looking back. That shame is heavy. It wants to keep you down. But shame doesn't have to be the end of the story. It can be the beginning. You can decide, right now, that you're done. That today is the day everything changes.

I've done dozens of these talks. Hundreds by now, maybe. I've lost count. I've spoken in churches with stained glass windows and in community centers with fluorescent lights that hum. I've spoken in hospital cafeterias and hotel ballrooms and once, memorably, in a VFW hall that smelled like old cigarettes and spilled beer. I've spoken to groups of five and groups of five thousand. And every single time, the same thing happens. Someone comes up to me afterward. A man. A woman. A kid. A parent. They shake my hand. Sometimes they hug me. Sometimes they just stand there, crying, not able to say anything.

And they tell me:

> I needed to hear that. Thank you.

Every speech I give ends the same way. I look at the audience. I pause. And I say:

> I Can. I Will. And so can you.

I mean it every time. Not because I think life is easy. Not because I think everybody can overcome everything. I know that's not true. Some things break you. Some things take more than stubbornness to fix. But I also know that most of us quit too early. We give up right before the breakthrough. We stop pushing one rep short of the finish line. And that's the tragedy. Not the failure. The quitting.

If I can stand in front of a room full of strangers, with my prosthetic leg and my scarred history and my shaky voice, and help one person decide not to quit — then every sleepless night, every painful therapy session, every moment I wanted to give up was worth it.

My story isn't mine. It belongs to everyone who needs to hear it. I'm just the messenger. And as long as people keep asking, I'll keep showing up. I'll keep telling the truth. And I'll keep ending the same way.

I Can. I Will. And so can you.
