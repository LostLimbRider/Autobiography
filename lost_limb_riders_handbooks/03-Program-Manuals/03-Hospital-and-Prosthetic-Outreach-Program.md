# Section 1: Program Foundation

## 1.1 Mission and Purpose

Lost Limb Riders exists to connect new amputees with the peer support, community resources, and adaptive recreation opportunities they need to thrive after limb loss. Our Hospital and Prosthetic Partner Outreach program extends that mission into the clinical setting. We go where amputees are when they need us most\u2014the hospital bed, the rehabilitation center, the prosthetic clinic. We meet them in the moment between diagnosis and adjustment, between discharge and the rest of their lives.

Clinical care stops at the hospital door. Peer support starts there. This program bridges the gap between medical treatment and real life. We give new amputees someone who has been where they are, someone who can say, \u201cI made it, and you will too.\u201d

Our purpose is to build and maintain relationships with healthcare providers so that every new amputee has access to peer support, community connection, and the knowledge that limb loss is not the end of their story.

## 1.2 Program Goals and Measurable Outcomes

**Goal 1: Increase early peer intervention.** Every new amputee should be offered peer connection within seventy-two hours of identified need or upon referral.

- Outcome: 80% of referred patients receive initial contact within seventy-two hours.
- Measurement: Outreach logs and referral tracking reports reviewed quarterly.

**Goal 2: Build lasting healthcare partnerships.** Establish formal partnership agreements with hospitals, prosthetic clinics, and rehabilitation facilities in each operational region.

- Outcome: At least two active partnership agreements per region within twelve months of program launch.
- Measurement: Executed partnership agreements on file and reviewed semi-annually.

**Goal 3: Track referral-to-connection conversion.** Monitor how many clinical referrals result in an active peer support relationship.

- Outcome: 60% of clinical referrals convert to at least one peer support contact.
- Measurement: Referral source tracking in participant database, reviewed quarterly.

**Goal 4: Maintain partner satisfaction.** Healthcare partners should view Lost Limb Riders as a reliable, professional, and valuable resource.

- Outcome: Annual partner satisfaction survey scores average 4 out of 5 or higher.
- Measurement: Anonymous survey distributed to all active partners each year.

**Goal 5: Standardize training for outreach personnel.** Every representative interacting with healthcare partners must complete standardized training before making independent contact.

- Outcome: 100% of outreach personnel complete training within thirty days of appointment.
- Measurement: Training records maintained by Director of Medical Partnerships.

## 1.3 Scope of Services

This program covers the following activities:

- Establishing and maintaining formal partnership agreements with healthcare organizations
- Coordinating in-hospital peer visits for new amputees upon referral and facility approval
- Providing educational materials and resources to clinical staff about the peer support model
- Training healthcare partners on referral procedures and program eligibility
- Connecting patients with prosthetic and orthotic resources through partner clinics
- Supporting discharge planning through community resource coordination
- Collecting outcome data to demonstrate program impact to healthcare partners
- Maintaining compliance with healthcare facility policies, HIPAA requirements, and infection control standards

This program does not provide medical care, prosthetic fitting, physical therapy, mental health treatment, or financial assistance.

## 1.4 Definitions and Terminology

**Amputee / Person with Limb Loss**: An individual who has undergone amputation or was born with limb difference.

**Clinical Partner**: A healthcare organization with a formal agreement to refer patients to Lost Limb Riders services.

**Healthcare Partner**: Any hospital, clinic, rehabilitation facility, or provider organization that collaborates with Lost Limb Riders for patient support.

**In-Hospital Visit**: A peer support visit that takes place in a healthcare facility with prior approval from the facility and the patient.

**New Amputee**: An individual who has undergone amputation within the past twelve months or is preparing for scheduled amputation.

**Outreach Representative**: A trained Lost Limb Riders volunteer or staff member authorized to interact with healthcare partners and patients in clinical settings.

**Peer Support**: Emotional, social, and practical support provided by someone with lived experience of limb loss.

**Referral**: A formal communication from a healthcare partner requesting Lost Limb Riders contact a specific patient.

**Regional Outreach Representative**: A volunteer or staff member responsible for partnership development and maintenance within a defined geographic region.

## 1.5 Ethical Principles

All outreach activities must follow these principles:

**Patient autonomy comes first.** The patient decides whether to accept peer support. No pressure, no coercion, no follow-up after a clear \u201cno.\u201d

**Clinical boundaries are absolute.** Outreach representatives are peers, not providers. They do not offer medical advice, interpret doctor instructions, recommend treatments, or interfere with care.

**Confidentiality is non-negotiable.** Patient information shared by healthcare partners is used only for program purposes. It is never shared outside the organization without written consent, except as required by law.

**Honesty in representation.** Outreach representatives accurately describe Lost Limb Riders, its services, and its limitations. No exaggerated claims, no promises of outcomes, no guarantees.

**Cultural humility.** Representatives respect each patient\u2019s background, beliefs, identity, and circumstances. Support is offered without judgment.

**No exploitation.** Outreach representatives never use their position for personal gain, romantic interest, religious proselytizing, political advocacy, or financial solicitation.

## 1.6 Program Limitations

Lost Limb Riders is a peer support and community connection organization. We are not a medical provider, insurance provider, or social service agency. The Hospital and Prosthetic Partner Outreach program cannot:

- Provide or recommend specific medical treatments, surgeons, or prosthetists
- Guarantee insurance coverage, reimbursement, or prosthetic device funding
- Offer mental health counseling, therapy, or crisis intervention beyond peer support
- Transport patients or serve as a medical transportation service
- Provide financial assistance for medical bills, prosthetics, or living expenses
- Assume clinical responsibility for patient outcomes
- Accept referral of patients without the patient\u2019s informed consent
- Operate in any facility without prior written authorization

All outreach representatives must clearly communicate these limitations to partners and patients.

# Section 2: Healthcare Partnership Framework

## 2.1 Hospitals

Hospitals are the most common entry point for new amputees. Whether through trauma, planned surgery, or complication, the hospital is where most people first wake up without a limb. Our partnerships with hospitals focus on emergency departments, surgical units, and discharge planning teams.

**Partnership activities:**
- Establish a point of contact in case management, social work, or patient advocacy
- Provide educational materials for hospital staff on the peer support model
- Train referral coordinators on the intake process
- Coordinate in-hospital peer visits for interested patients
- Participate in discharge planning meetings when invited

**Requirements:**
- Signed partnership agreement with facility administration
- Compliance with all facility credentialing, background check, and infection control policies
- Designated hospital liaison responsible for ongoing communication
- Quarterly check-ins with hospital point of contact

## 2.2 Trauma Centers

Trauma centers treat patients with sudden, catastrophic injuries\u2014many of which result in amputation. These patients often have no preparation time. The psychological impact is immediate and severe. Trauma center partnerships require sensitivity, speed, and coordination with critical care teams.

**Partnership activities:**
- Coordinate with trauma social workers and case managers
- Provide rapid-response peer support upon referral
- Offer materials appropriate for family members in waiting areas
- Respect critical care access restrictions and patient stability priorities

**Requirements:**
- Designated trauma center liaison (may be same as hospital liaison)
- Training for outreach representatives on trauma-informed communication
- Clear escalation protocol for patients experiencing acute psychological distress
- Strict adherence to visitor policies and ICU access rules

## 2.3 Rehabilitation Hospitals

Rehabilitation hospitals are where amputees learn to live again. The stay is longer, the work is harder, and the emotional arc is different from the acute phase. Patients here are processing their new reality. Peer support at this stage can have outsized impact.

**Partnership activities:**
- Regular in-person peer visits scheduled with facility approval
- Group support sessions for rehab patients when feasible
- Collaboration with physical and occupational therapy teams
- Resource sharing for adaptive equipment and community programs
- Participation in family education days or caregiver support events

**Requirements:**
- Signed partnership agreement with rehab facility administration
- Coordination with rehab therapy schedules to avoid disruption
- Regular attendance by assigned outreach representative
- Outcome reporting to demonstrate program value

## 2.4 Inpatient Rehabilitation Facilities

Inpatient rehabilitation facilities (IRFs) provide intensive, multidisciplinary therapy for patients recovering from major conditions, including amputation. IRFs may be standalone facilities or units within larger hospitals. Partnership approach mirrors rehabilitation hospitals with adjustment for institutional structure.

**Partnership activities:**
- Same as Section 2.3, adapted to facility policies
- Coordination with IRF medical director or program director
- Integration into existing patient education and support programming

**Requirements:**
- Signed partnership agreement
- Outreach representative assigned to each IRF location
- Semi-annual program review with IRF leadership

## 2.5 Prosthetic and Orthotic Clinics

Prosthetic and orthotic clinics are where amputees get their legs, arms, and devices. This is often the first place a new amputee goes after leaving the hospital. The prosthetist is a trusted figure in the amputee\u2019s new life. Partnering with these clinics puts peer support directly in the path of care.

**Partnership activities:**
- Provide clinic waiting rooms with Lost Limb Riders materials
- Offer peer support at the clinic with patient consent
- Train prosthetists and clinic staff on referral procedures
- Coordinate with clinics on prosthetic mentorship programs
- Share patient feedback (with consent) to improve clinic services

**Requirements:**
- Signed partnership agreement with clinic ownership or management
- Compliance with clinic scheduling and privacy requirements
- Quarterly check-ins with clinic lead prosthetist
- Annual satisfaction survey for clinic partners

## 2.6 Physical Therapy Providers

Physical therapists work with amputees from the first post-surgical movement through long-term gait training. They see patients at their most frustrated and their most determined. PT providers can identify patients who would benefit from peer support.

**Partnership activities:**
- Educational in-service for PT staff on the peer support model
- Referral materials in PT treatment areas
- Coordination with PT schedules for peer visit timing
- Resource sharing on adaptive recreation and fitness

**Requirements:**
- Referral protocol documented with each PT practice
- Outreach representative assigned to maintain relationship
- Annual review of referral patterns and outcomes

## 2.7 Occupational Therapy Providers

Occupational therapists help amputees relearn daily living\u2014dressing, cooking, bathing, working. Their work is deeply personal and practical. OT providers often witness the daily frustrations that peer support can help normalize.

**Partnership activities:**
- Same as Section 2.6, tailored to OT practice settings
- Emphasis on adaptive equipment and home modification resources

**Requirements:**
- Referral protocol documented
- Coordination between OT providers and Lost Limb Riders resource library
- Semi-annual relationship check-in

## 2.8 Home Health Agencies

Many amputees receive home health services after discharge. A home health partnership extends peer support into the home environment, where patients may feel isolated and overwhelmed.

**Partnership activities:**
- Provide home health agencies with referral materials
- Coordinate peer home visits with agency approval and patient consent
- Train home health staff on identifying patients who may benefit from peer support
- Share community resource information through home health channels

**Requirements:**
- Signed partnership agreement with agency administration
- Compliance with agency visit policies and safety protocols
- Background check clearance for any representative conducting home visits
- Documentation of each home visit in participant records

## 2.9 Skilled Nursing Facilities

Some amputees transition to skilled nursing facilities (SNFs) before returning home. SNF stays can be long, and patients in these settings are at high risk for depression and social withdrawal. Peer support can be a lifeline.

**Partnership activities:**
- On-site peer visits with facility approval and patient consent
- Group programming for SNF residents when feasible
- Staff training on peer support resources
- Coordination with SNF activity and social work departments

**Requirements:**
- Signed partnership agreement with SNF administration
- Outreach representative assigned per facility
- Compliance with all SNF policies, including infection control and visit scheduling
- Quarterly program review with SNF leadership

## 2.10 Veterans Affairs Facilities

Veterans represent a significant portion of the amputee population. VA facilities have their own structure, culture, and referral pathways. Partnerships with VA medical centers, VA prosthetic clinics, and Vet Centers require understanding of VA protocols and veteran-specific needs.

**Partnership activities:**
- Establish relationship with VA amputation care coordinator or prosthetics chief
- Provide peer support services to veterans through VA referral channels
- Coordinate with VA voluntary service office for outreach representative credentialing
- Participate in VA amputee support groups and events when invited
- Offer veteran-to-veteran peer matching when available

**Requirements:**
- Compliance with VA credentialing, background check, and volunteer onboarding
- Understanding of VA privacy rules and health information sharing limitations
- Signed partnership agreement with VA facility or regional office where required
- Designated outreach representative for VA partnership coordination

## 2.11 Community Health Organizations

Community health organizations include federally qualified health centers, community clinics, disability service organizations, and nonprofit health advocacy groups. These organizations serve patients who may lack access to major hospital systems. Partnerships here extend reach into underserved communities.

**Partnership activities:**
- Provide educational materials and referral forms
- Offer staff training on limb loss peer support resources
- Coordinate peer connection for patients referred through community health channels
- Collaborate on health fairs, community events, and awareness campaigns

**Requirements:**
- Signed partnership agreement or memorandum of understanding
- Designated contact person at each organization
- Annual review of partnership activities and outcomes
- Cultural competence training for outreach representatives serving diverse communities

# Section 4: Partner Onboarding and Documentation

## 4.1 Initial Contact Procedure

**Step 1: Identify the right contact.** Research the healthcare organization to determine the appropriate department and contact person. For hospitals, this is typically case management, social work, or patient experience. For prosthetic clinics, it is the clinic manager or lead prosthetist.

**Step 2: Make initial contact.** Send a professional introduction via email or phone. Include:
- Brief description of Lost Limb Riders
- Overview of the Hospital and Prosthetic Partner Outreach program
- Request for a meeting to discuss potential partnership
- Contact information for follow-up

**Step 3: Schedule an introductory meeting.** Meet in person when possible. Virtual meetings are acceptable for initial discussion. Attendees should include the potential partner decision-maker and the assigned outreach representative or Director of Medical Partnerships.

**Step 4: Present the partnership opportunity.** Provide:
- Program overview and mission alignment
- Referral process explanation
- Sample educational materials
- Partnership agreement template for review
- Outcomes data and testimonials if available

**Step 5: Address questions and concerns.** Be prepared to discuss:
- Privacy and HIPAA compliance
- Volunteer credentialing and background checks
- Program limitations
- Time commitment for partner staff
- Liability and insurance considerations

**Step 6: Follow up.** Send a thank-you note within forty-eight hours. Include any additional materials requested. Propose a timeline for next steps.

## 4.2 Partnership Agreement Process

All healthcare partnerships require a signed partnership agreement before outreach activities begin. The agreement must be reviewed by Lost Limb Riders legal counsel before execution.

**Agreement components:**
- Names and contact information for both parties
- Description of services to be provided
- Referral procedures and points of contact
- Privacy and confidentiality obligations
- Term of agreement and renewal provisions
- Termination clauses
- Liability and insurance requirements
- Signature blocks for authorized representatives

**Process:**
1. Director of Medical Partnerships drafts or reviews agreement
2. Agreement reviewed by Lost Limb Riders legal counsel
3. Agreement signed by authorized Lost Limb Riders representative (Executive Director or Board designee)
4. Agreement signed by authorized healthcare partner representative
5. Copy of executed agreement filed in partner records
6. Partner added to active partnership database
7. Outreach activities begin per agreement terms

**Renewal:**
- Partnerships are reviewed annually
- Renewal agreement or amendment is executed if terms change
- Partnerships with no activity for twelve consecutive months are subject to termination

## 4.3 Partner Documentation Requirements

For each active healthcare partner, the following documentation must be maintained:

- Signed partnership agreement
- Partner organization profile (type, location, services, contact information)
- Designated points of contact at partner organization
- Referral procedures documentation
- Training records for partner staff
- Outreach activity log
- Incident reports related to partnership activities
- Annual satisfaction survey results
- Correspondence and meeting notes

All documentation is maintained in the program database and is accessible to the Director of Medical Partnerships and authorized program staff.

## 4.4 Referral Procedures

Healthcare partners refer patients to Lost Limb Riders through the established referral process. Referrals may be made by phone, email, secure messaging, or written referral form.

**Referral information required:**
- Patient name and contact information
- Preferred method of contact
- Patient consent to be contacted (documented)
- Referral source (partner name and referring staff member)
- Brief context for referral (recent amputation, scheduled surgery, adjustment difficulty, etc.)
- Urgency level if applicable
- Any special considerations (language, accessibility, cultural needs)

**Referral processing:**
1. Hospital Outreach Coordinator receives referral
2. Referral information entered into program database
3. Patient consent verified
4. Appropriate peer support resource identified
5. Initial contact made within seventy-two hours of referral receipt
6. Outcome documented in referral record
7. Referring partner notified of connection status (without sharing patient details beyond general acknowledgment)

## 4.5 Credentialing and Compliance

Outreach representatives who enter healthcare facilities must comply with facility credentialing requirements, which may include:

- Background check (state and federal)
- Drug screening
- Proof of immunizations (including influenza, hepatitis B, TB screening)
- Health screening clearance
- Facility-specific orientation or training
- Confidentiality agreement or HIPAA training
- Badge or identification requirements

The Hospital Outreach Coordinator maintains credentialing records for all active outreach representatives and ensures compliance with each facility\u2019s requirements. Expired credentials result in suspension of facility access until renewed.

# Section 6: Patient Engagement

## 6.1 Initial Contact Standards

Once a referral is received and patient consent is verified, the initial contact must be made within seventy-two hours. This window is critical. The newly referred patient has already taken a step toward accepting help, and delay risks losing that momentum. Every hour between referral and outreach matters.

The initial contact method should match the patient\u2019s stated preference whenever possible. If the patient indicated a phone call, call. If they preferred text, text. If no preference was stated, a phone call is the default, with a follow-up text or email within twenty-four hours if the call goes unanswered. Voicemail messages must include the caller\u2019s full name, Lost Limb Riders affiliation, a brief non-clinical description of the call\u2019s purpose, and a return callback number. Voicemails may not disclose the patient\u2019s medical condition or the nature of the referral beyond \u201cI\u2019m following up on a referral made on your behalf.\u201d

A standardized script provides the framework for initial contact but must be delivered conversationally, not read verbatim. The script covers: introduction and affiliation, how the referral came about, a brief description of peer support services, an offer to meet or talk further, and confirmation of the patient\u2019s continued interest. No medical advice, no clinical language, no pressure. If the patient declines, the outreach representative thanks them, documents the decline respectfully, and closes the record. No follow-up is made unless the patient later requests it through the original referral source.

Every initial contact attempt must be documented in the program database regardless of outcome. Documentation includes: date and time of attempt, method of contact, whether contact was established, patient response (interested, not interested, request more time, request specific information), and any follow-up actions taken. If after three documented attempts over five business days no contact is established, the referral is returned to the referring partner with a request for updated contact information or guidance.

## 6.2 Bedside Visit Procedures

In-hospital bedside visits occur only with prior written approval from the healthcare facility and explicit consent from the patient. No exceptions. Bedside visits are a privilege granted by the facility, not a right of the program, and that privilege can be revoked at any time.

Before scheduling a bedside visit, the outreach representative must confirm the following through the Hospital Outreach Coordinator: facility approval for on-site peer visits is active and current, the specific patient has consented to an in-person visit, the patient\u2019s medical status is stable enough for a visitor, and the treating team has no objections. Visits are never conducted in intensive care units, post-surgical recovery units, or areas under isolation precautions without specific facility authorization.

Upon arrival, the outreach representative checks in at the facility\u2019s designated visitor entrance, presents identification, and follows all facility sign-in procedures. The representative proceeds directly to the patient\u2019s room. No wandering. No visiting other patients. No stopping in staff areas. The visit is with the referred patient only unless family members are present and welcomed by the patient.

Inside the room, the outreach representative positions themselves at a respectful distance\u2014never blocking monitors, medical equipment, or the call button. Visits are kept to fifteen to thirty minutes unless the patient clearly wants more time and the facility has no visiting restrictions. The representative reads the room: if the patient is tired, in pain, or distracted, they offer to return another time. No hard sell. No pressure.

The conversation follows the peer support model: shared experience, practical questions, listening. The representative shares their own limb loss journey only as it relates to the patient\u2019s concerns. They do not compare amputations, minimize the patient\u2019s experience, or offer unsolicited advice. They offer Lost Limb Riders materials, answer questions about the organization, and ask if the patient would like continued contact after discharge.

Before leaving, the representative thanks the patient, ensures the call button and personal items are within reach, and notifies nursing staff that the visit is complete. Visit notes are documented within twenty-four hours, including date, duration, patient engagement level, topics discussed, and any follow-up commitments.

## 6.3 Virtual Visits

Virtual visits provide peer support when in-person visits are not possible or when the patient prefers remote connection. Virtual visits may be offered as an alternative to in-hospital visits, as a follow-up after discharge, or as the primary mode of support for patients who live outside the geographic reach of outreach representatives.

Video calls must use a secure, HIPAA-compliant platform approved by Lost Limb Riders leadership. Approved platforms include those that offer end-to-end encryption, require no account creation for guests, and do not store recordings by default. Consumers of video calls\u2014FaceTime, Skype, or unencrypted Zoom\u2014are not permitted for clinical or referral-based patient contact. Telephone calls are acceptable when the patient lacks video capability or prefers audio only.

Before the virtual visit, the outreach representative tests their internet connection, camera, microphone, and lighting. Background must be neutral and professional\u2014no clutter, no personal photos visible, no distracting activity. The representative dresses according to the program dress code as if attending an in-person visit. Pets, children, and background noise are managed in advance.

The call begins with a confirmation of the patient\u2019s identity and verbal re-consent to proceed. The structure mirrors an in-person visit: introduction, peer connection, listening, resource sharing, and follow-up planning. The representative pays attention to the patient\u2019s environment and demeanor through the camera and adjusts accordingly. If the patient appears distracted, uncomfortable, or emotionally overwhelmed, the representative offers to reschedule or shorten the call.

Technical difficulties are handled gracefully. If the connection drops, the representative attempts to reconnect within five minutes. If reconnection fails, a follow-up call is placed within twenty-four hours to reschedule. No outreach activity is left unresolved without a documented attempt at follow-up.

Virtual visit documentation follows the same standards as in-person visits. Platform used, call duration, connection quality, and any technical issues are noted in addition to standard visit content documentation.

## 6.4 Family Meetings

Family meetings are offered when the patient requests them or when the referring partner or clinical team identifies that family involvement would benefit the patient\u2019s adjustment. Family members experience limb loss too\u2014they grieve, they worry, they don\u2019t know what to expect. Including them in the peer support conversation can make the difference between a supportive home environment and a fearful one.

Meetings may include the patient\u2019s spouse or partner, adult children, parents, siblings, or primary caregivers. The patient decides who participates. No family member is included without the patient\u2019s explicit consent. If a family member approaches the outreach representative separately, they are directed to discuss their interest with the patient first.

The meeting structure varies by setting. In-hospital family meetings are brief\u2014fifteen to twenty minutes\u2014and focused on immediate concerns: what to expect after discharge, how to support the patient without overwhelming them, and what resources are available. Virtual family meetings may be longer and more comprehensive. In both cases, the outreach representative facilitates the conversation, not lectures. They answer questions, share their own experience from the family perspective when relevant, and provide written resources for caregivers.

The representative does not mediate family conflicts, take sides, or disclose information the patient has shared in private. If a family member asks a question the representative cannot answer\u2014about medical prognosis, insurance coverage, or prosthetic options\u2014they direct the family member to the appropriate clinical provider and document the inquiry.

Follow-up contact with family members occurs only with the patient\u2019s ongoing consent and through the patient. The patient remains the central point of contact at all times.

## 6.5 Discharge Follow-Up

Discharge follow-up is one of the highest-impact touchpoints in the patient engagement cycle. The transition from hospital to home is where many new amputees feel most alone. The structure and routine of the hospital are gone. The family has returned to work. The patient is left with a body that does not work the way it used to and a list of instructions they may or may not understand.

Discharge follow-up contact occurs within seventy-two hours of the patient\u2019s discharge date. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator tracks discharge dates through partner referrals and patient self-reporting. If the discharge date is unknown, follow-up is scheduled for one week after the initial in-hospital or virtual visit.

The follow-up contact checks on: how the patient is settling in at home, whether they have questions about their recovery or next steps, whether they have connected with a prosthetist or have an appointment scheduled, their emotional state and overall morale, and whether they are interested in additional peer support or community resources. This is not a clinical assessment. It is a friendly check-in from someone who knows what this transition feels like.

If the patient reports difficulty with pain management, wound care, mobility, or emotional distress, the outreach representative listens empathetically and encourages them to contact their healthcare provider. The representative does not triage, diagnose, or advise. If the patient reports immediate danger to themselves or others, the representative follows the crisis escalation protocol: stay on the line, encourage the patient to call 911 or go to an emergency room, and notify the Hospital Outreach Coordinator immediately after the call ends.

Discharge follow-up is documented with the same thoroughness as initial contact. Notes include the patient\u2019s reported status, any concerns raised, resources provided, and follow-up plan. The discharge follow-up record becomes the baseline for the thirty-, sixty-, and ninety-day check-in sequence.

## 6.6 Thirty-, Sixty-, and Ninety-Day Follow-Up Procedures

The thirty-, sixty-, and ninety-day follow-ups form a structured check-in sequence designed to track the patient\u2019s progress, maintain connection, and identify emerging needs. These touchpoints are spaced to catch the common rough patches in the amputation adjustment timeline: the initial post-discharge overwhelm, the frustration of the first prosthetic fitting, and the long slog of rehabilitation.

**Thirty-day follow-up.** The focus is on practical adjustment. Has the patient connected with a prosthetist? Are they attending physical therapy? How is the household adaptation going? The conversation explores whether initial expectations match reality and whether the patient needs additional resources. If the patient has not yet seen a prosthetist, the representative offers the Lost Limb Riders prosthetic clinic directory. Documentation covers all topics discussed and any referrals made.

**Sixty-day follow-up.** This check-in targets prosthetic progress and emotional adjustment. Is the patient\u2019s first prosthesis in process or delivered? How is the fitting experience going? Are there frustrations with socket comfort, gait training, or device function? The representative validates these frustrations as normal and shares coping strategies from their own experience. This is also the appropriate time to introduce adaptive recreation opportunities. If the patient is mobile and interested, the representative offers information about Lost Limb Riders group rides, adaptive sports events, and community meet-ups.

**Ninety-day follow-up.** The ninety-day check-in assesses overall trajectory and transitions the patient toward ongoing peer community connection rather than structured outreach. The representative asks about quality of life, mobility goals, social engagement, and satisfaction with their prosthetic care. The patient is invited to attend a Lost Limb Riders event, join a local ride, or connect with the broader amputee community. If the patient has had a smooth transition, the ninety-day check-in may be the last structured contact, with an open invitation to reach out anytime. If the patient continues to struggle, the representative offers additional support and may schedule a sixty-day extension check-in.

All follow-up contacts are documented in the program database within forty-eight hours. Documentation includes: date of contact, method, patient-reported status, any concerns or barriers identified, resources or referrals provided, and the patient\u2019s expressed interest in ongoing engagement. If a patient cannot be reached after three attempts across two weeks, the record is marked \u201clost to follow-up\u201d and no further contact attempts are made unless the patient initiates contact.

# Section 7: Prosthetic Clinic Collaboration

## 7.1 Communication with Prosthetists

Prosthetists are among the most trusted professionals in an amputee\u2019s life. They spend more time with patients than most surgeons or physiatrists do. A strong relationship between Lost Limb Riders and prosthetic clinics multiplies the impact of both organizations\u2014the prosthetist provides the limb, and Lost Limb Riders provides the life that goes on it.

Communication with prosthetists follows a standardized but flexible framework. Each partner clinic is assigned a primary contact at Lost Limb Riders\u2014either the Hospital Outreach Coordinator or the Regional Outreach Representative. That contact is responsible for all information sharing with the clinic. Prosthetists should never receive conflicting information from multiple Lost Limb Riders representatives.

Referral forms from prosthetic clinics use a streamlined template that captures: patient name, preferred contact method, patient consent to share information with Lost Limb Riders, clinic name and referring prosthetist, date of initial consultation, anticipated fitting timeline, and any special considerations. The form is completed by clinic staff and submitted through secure channels.

Information sharing between Lost Limb Riders and prosthetic clinics is reciprocal. The clinic receives general updates on program availability, new resources, and community events. With patient consent, Lost Limb Riders may share non-clinical feedback about the patient\u2019s adjustment, motivation, and engagement level to help the prosthetist tailor their approach. The patient must sign a specific release authorizing this two-way communication before any information is exchanged.

The minimum frequency of communication between Lost Limb Riders and each partner clinic is monthly. Many clinics benefit from bi-weekly check-ins during the first sixty days of partnership. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator maintains a communication log for each clinic partner, documenting dates, topics discussed, materials provided, and action items.

## 7.2 Educational Materials Provided to Clinics

Each partner prosthetic clinic receives a starter kit of educational materials upon partnership activation and resupply as needed. Materials are designed for display in waiting rooms, treatment areas, and clinic offices. All materials carry the Lost Limb Riders branding and a clear call to action for patients and families.

The starter kit includes: trifold brochures describing Lost Limb Riders and the peer support model, a one-page referral form for clinic staff, a frequently asked questions sheet for new amputees, a prosthetic care and maintenance quick-reference card, a guide to community resources organized by region, and a poster for display in patient-facing areas. Digital versions of all materials are provided for clinic websites, patient portals, or electronic communication.

Materials are reviewed annually for accuracy and relevance. Outdated materials are replaced within thirty days of revision. Clinics are notified of material updates through the primary Lost Limb Riders contact.

Clinic staff receive a companion guide that explains how to talk to patients about peer support, how to make a referral, what to expect after a referral is submitted, and what Lost Limb Riders can and cannot do. This guide is reviewed with clinic staff during the partnership onboarding session and revisited during annual check-ins.

Lost Limb Riders does not produce clinical education materials. All materials focus on peer support, community connection, and adaptive recreation. Materials never imply medical advice or clinical endorsement.

## 7.3 Community Resource Information to Share with Patients

Prosthetic clinic staff frequently encounter patients who need help with things beyond the scope of a clinic visit: finding financial assistance for prosthetic devices, locating accessible housing, applying for disability benefits, or connecting with support groups. Lost Limb Riders serves as a resource hub for these needs.

A community resource guide is maintained by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator and updated quarterly. The guide is organized by category and includes: prosthetic financial assistance programs and foundations, insurance navigation resources, disability benefit application support, accessible transportation options, home modification and accessibility resources, adaptive fitness and recreation programs, mental health and counseling services specializing in amputation, peer support groups and online communities, and vocational rehabilitation services.

The guide is provided to each partner clinic in both print and digital formats. Clinics are encouraged to share the guide with every new amputee patient, not only those referred for peer support.

When a patient raises a need that Lost Limb Riders cannot directly meet, the outreach representative or clinic staff refers them to the appropriate resource from the guide. The representative follows up within two weeks to confirm the patient connected with the resource and to offer additional support. This follow-up is documented in the patient\u2019s outreach record.

Lost Limb Riders does not endorse specific providers or programs listed in the resource guide. The guide is presented as a starting point for patient research, not a prescriptive recommendation.

## 7.4 Patient Support after Prosthetic Fitting

The period immediately following the delivery of the first prosthesis is emotionally complex. Many patients expect to feel triumphant. Instead, they often feel disappointment, frustration, or even grief. The socket is uncomfortable. The gait feels wrong. The mirror shows someone they do not recognize. Peer support during this phase is critical.

When a partner clinic refers a patient for post-fitting support, the outreach representative contacts the patient within seventy-two hours. The conversation normalizes the experience: \u201cWhat you\u2019re feeling is normal. Almost everyone feels this way after their first fitting. It gets better.\u201d The representative shares their own fitting story honestly, including the hard parts.

The representative helps the patient identify specific concerns and encourages them to communicate those concerns to their prosthetist. Many patients hesitate to speak up about socket discomfort or alignment issues, afraid of seeming difficult or ungrateful. The representative empowers them to advocate for themselves and reassures them that a well-fitting prosthesis is the result of honest feedback, not silent suffering.

Follow-up after fitting includes a thirty-day check-in to assess progress and satisfaction. If the patient is struggling, the representative offers to facilitate communication with the clinic\u2014with the patient\u2019s written consent. The representative never contacts the clinic about a patient\u2019s fitting without explicit authorization.

If the fitting goes well and the patient is satisfied, the representative celebrates that milestone and transitions the conversation toward adaptive activity and community engagement. The prosthesis is not the finish line. It is the starting line.

## 7.5 Adaptive Activity Referrals

One of Lost Limb Riders\u2019 primary value propositions to prosthetic clinics is the ability to connect patients with adaptive recreation opportunities. A prosthetic device is medical equipment until someone uses it to ride a motorcycle, hike a trail, or throw a ball. Adaptive activities turn the prosthesis from a symbol of loss into a tool for living.

The adaptive activity referral process begins during the sixty- or ninety-day follow-up, or earlier if the patient expresses interest. The outreach representative assesses the patient\u2019s mobility level, interests, and comfort with group activities. No one is pushed into an event they are not ready for, but the invitation is always extended.

Connecting patients to Lost Limb Riders rides and events follows a structured handoff. The representative provides the patient with the Lost Limb Riders event calendar, explains what to expect at a typical ride or meet-up, and offers to introduce them to another member who attends regularly. If the patient is interested in riding a motorcycle, the representative connects them with the Lost Limb Riders ride coordinator for a one-on-one conversation about adaptive motorcycle modifications, training resources, and passenger options.

For non-riding adaptive activities, the representative maintains a directory of partner organizations offering adaptive sports, including handcycling, skiing, kayaking, rock climbing, and fitness programs. Referrals to these organizations are documented, and the representative follows up within thirty days to learn whether the patient participated.

Group events are the ultimate goal. Attending a Lost Limb Riders event connects the patient with dozens of amputees who are living full, active lives. The representative personally invites the patient to at least one event within ninety days of initial contact and offers to accompany them as a point of contact at the event.

# Section 8: Volunteer Requirements

## 8.1 Healthcare Professionalism Standards

Every outreach representative who enters a healthcare setting represents Lost Limb Riders to patients, families, and clinical staff. That representation must be professional at all times. Healthcare professionalism in this context means punctuality, preparation, emotional regulation, and respect for the clinical environment.

Outreach representatives arrive on time for every scheduled visit or meeting. Tardiness communicates disorganization and disrespect. If unavoidable delay occurs, the representative contacts the facility and the patient\u2014or the Hospital Outreach Coordinator, depending on the situation\u2014before the scheduled time. No-show is never acceptable.

Emotional regulation is a professional requirement. Outreach representatives work with patients who may be angry, grieving, frightened, or in pain. The representative remains calm, non-reactive, and supportive regardless of the patient\u2019s emotional state. If a representative feels personally triggered or overwhelmed during a visit, they excuse themselves politely and debrief with the Hospital Outreach Coordinator within twenty-four hours.

Representatives maintain appropriate boundaries at all times. They do not exchange personal contact information with patients outside the program\u2019s communication channels. They do not accept gifts, money, or personal items from patients. They do not develop personal or romantic relationships with patients. If a patient initiates contact outside approved channels, the representative redirects them to the program\u2019s official communication methods.

Professionalism extends to all written communication. Emails, notes, and reports use correct grammar, complete sentences, and professional tone. No slang, no casual abbreviations, no emotional language in documentation. Records are objective, factual, and focused on program activity.

## 8.2 Infection Prevention Protocols

Infection prevention is non-negotiable in healthcare environments. Amputees are particularly vulnerable to infection, especially in the post-surgical period. Outreach representatives must follow infection prevention protocols without exception.

Before every in-hospital visit, representatives conduct a self-screening. If they have any symptoms of illness\u2014fever, cough, sore throat, runny nose, gastrointestinal symptoms, skin infection, or unexplained rash\u2014they cancel the visit and reschedule. No exceptions. Show up healthy or do not show up at all.

Hand hygiene is performed immediately upon entering the facility, immediately before entering a patient\u2019s room, immediately after leaving a patient\u2019s room, and immediately before leaving the facility. Use alcohol-based hand sanitizer with at least sixty percent alcohol when hands are not visibly soiled. Use soap and water when hands are visibly soiled or after using the restroom. Follow the facility\u2019s specific hand hygiene protocols, which may be posted at entrances to patient areas.

Personal protective equipment is worn when required by facility policy or the patient\u2019s isolation status. Representatives comply with all posted isolation precautions without question. If a patient is under contact, droplet, or airborne precautions, the visit is conducted virtually or postponed until precautions are lifted. No in-person visit to an isolation room without specific facility authorization.

What NOT to do: do not touch patients without their permission, do not sit on patient beds, do not handle medical equipment, do not adjust wound dressings or prosthetic devices, do not eat or drink in patient care areas, do not use patient restrooms, do not touch your face during a visit, and do not wear gloves as a substitute for hand hygiene. Gloves are not a free pass to skip hand washing.

## 8.3 Hospital Etiquette

Hospitals are workplaces for the staff who keep them running. Outreach representatives are guests in that workplace and must behave accordingly. Hospital etiquette is about knowing your place, staying out of the way, and being so easy to work with that staff are glad to see you.

Representatives speak only to authorized personnel about program business. The primary contacts are: the facility\u2019s designated liaison (case manager, social worker, or patient experience representative), the nursing staff assigned to the patient\u2019s care unit, and the charge nurse or unit manager if the liaison is unavailable. Representatives do not approach physicians without prior arrangement. They do not interrupt clinical procedures, medication passes, shift changes, or report-outs.

Visitor policies vary by facility and must be reviewed before every visit. Some facilities require advance notification of all visitors, some limit visit duration, some restrict entry to certain units, and some prohibit visits during specific hours. The outreach representative confirms current visitor policies before scheduling and complies fully.

Noise level in patient care areas is kept to a minimum. Representatives speak quietly in hallways, silence their phones completely (not vibrate) in patient areas, and step away from patient rooms to take any necessary calls. Phone use in patient rooms is limited to showing the patient a resource or video directly related to the visit purpose. No personal calls, no texting, no social media scrolling in patient areas.

Visit duration respects the patient\u2019s energy and the facility\u2019s schedule. The standard in-hospital visit is fifteen to thirty minutes. Longer visits require approval from the patient and the nursing staff. If the patient is clearly tired, in pain, or has an impending procedure, the representative keeps the visit short and offers to return.

Personal space is maintained at all times. Representatives do not block doorways, hallways, nurse stations, or equipment alcoves. They stay with the patient in their room or in the designated visitor area. They do not wander.

## 8.4 Privacy Expectations

Privacy in healthcare is a legal requirement and an ethical obligation. Outreach representatives handle information that patients have shared in confidence and that healthcare partners have disclosed under trust agreements. Violating that trust ends the patient\u2019s willingness to engage and jeopardizes the organization\u2019s relationship with the partner facility.

Representatives never discuss patient information in public areas: elevators, hallways, waiting rooms, cafeterias, or parking lots. Conversations about patients take place in private settings with no possibility of being overheard. If a representative needs to discuss a patient while in the facility, they use a private office or phone away from public spaces.

Patient information is shared only on a need-to-know basis. Within Lost Limb Riders, only the Hospital Outreach Coordinator, Director of Medical Partnerships, and the assigned outreach representative have access to identifiable patient records. No information is shared with other volunteers, board members, or community members without a specific program need and authorization from the Director.

When speaking with family members, the representative confirms the patient has authorized the family member\u2019s involvement. The representative does not disclose information the patient has not authorized. If a family member pressures the representative for information, the representative politely redirects: \u201cI\u2019m glad to include you in conversations the patient has approved, but I need to respect what the patient has chosen to share.\u201d

Privacy expectations extend after the patient relationship ends. Records are retained according to the program\u2019s record retention schedule and destroyed securely when the retention period expires. The obligation to protect patient privacy does not expire when the patient stops participating.

## 8.5 Communication Standards

All communication by outreach representatives\u2014verbal, written, and electronic\u2014reflects on Lost Limb Riders. Consistency in communication quality builds trust with partners and patients. Inconsistency undermines it.

Verbal communication with patients uses plain language, not clinical jargon. Representatives speak at a pace and volume appropriate for the setting. They listen more than they talk. They ask open-ended questions and allow silence for the patient to process and respond. They never interrupt, talk over, or finish a patient\u2019s sentences.

Written communication includes visit notes, emails to partners, referral documentation, and internal reports. All written communication uses professional English, correct grammar, and accurate spelling. Visit notes are objective and factual, describing what was discussed and offered without subjective judgment. Emails to partners include a clear subject line, professional greeting, concise body, and complete contact information in the signature block.

Electronic communication with patients occurs through approved channels only. Text messaging is permitted for appointment reminders and logistical coordination only\u2014not for emotional support or ongoing conversation. Email is permitted for resource sharing and follow-up. Social media contact with patients is prohibited. Representatives do not accept friend requests, follow requests, or direct messages from patients on personal social media accounts.

Response time expectations: outreach representatives respond to patient messages within twenty-four hours, to partner inquiries within twenty-four hours, and to internal program communications within forty-eight hours. If a response requires more time to research or coordinate, the representative sends an acknowledgment within the response window and commits to a follow-up date.

## 8.6 Dress Code

The dress code for outreach representatives in healthcare settings is business casual with adjustments for the clinical environment. Representatives must look approachable, professional, and appropriate for a medical setting. The goal is to blend in well enough that staff assume you belong, but patients immediately recognize you as a visitor.

Specific requirements: collared shirts or blouses, clean and wrinkle-free. Slacks, khakis, or dark jeans without rips, tears, or excessive fading. Skirts or dresses of knee length or longer. Closed-toe shoes appropriate for walking hospital corridors\u2014no sandals, flip-flops, or open-backed shoes. Clean, neat personal grooming. Minimal fragrances\u2014many patients have respiratory sensitivities or allergies. Visible tattoos should be covered when possible. Facial piercings beyond standard ear piercings should be minimal and unobtrusive.

What is not permitted: scrubs (unless provided by the facility for volunteer credentialing), medical white coats, t-shirts with slogans or graphics, athletic wear, shorts, hats (except for religious or medical reasons), visible undergarments, and frayed or dirty clothing.

Lost Limb Riders branded apparel\u2014polo shirts, vests, or jackets\u2014is recommended when available and appropriate. Branded apparel helps staff and patients identify the representative\u2019s affiliation without confusion. When branded apparel is not available, a Lost Limb Riders identification badge is the minimum identifier required.

Representatives adjust their dress for the setting: a rehabilitation hospital visit allows slightly more casual attire than an acute care hospital. Representatives use their judgment and consult with the Hospital Outreach Coordinator if uncertain.

## 8.7 Identification Badges

Identification badges are required for every outreach representative entering a healthcare facility. Badges serve two purposes: they identify the representative to facility staff, and they signal to patients and families that the representative is authorized to be there.

Lost Limb Riders issues an organization identification badge to each active outreach representative. The badge displays the representative\u2019s full name, a current photograph, the Lost Limb Riders logo, and the title \u201cOutreach Representative.\u201d Badges are worn at all times while in healthcare facilities, clipped to the front of the representative\u2019s clothing at chest level. Badges are never worn on lanyards that dangle, swing, or present a safety hazard around patients or medical equipment. Breakaway lanyards are permitted when required by facility policy.

Some facilities require their own identification badge or visitor pass in addition to or instead of the Lost Limb Riders badge. Representatives comply with each facility\u2019s specific badging requirements. If a facility requires a background check for badge issuance, the representative completes the process before scheduling any visits at that facility.

Badges are the property of Lost Limb Riders and must be returned when the representative leaves the program. Lost badges are reported to the Hospital Outreach Coordinator immediately. A replacement badge is issued, and the lost badge is deactivated if the badge includes electronic access functionality.

Representatives never lend their badge to another person. Badges are non-transferable. Wearing another representative\u2019s badge or allowing another person to wear yours is grounds for immediate program suspension.

## 8.8 Annual Competency Training Requirements

All outreach representatives must complete annual competency training to maintain active status in the program. Training ensures that representatives remain current on program policies, privacy requirements, infection control standards, and effective communication practices.

The annual training curriculum includes: HIPAA and privacy compliance refresher (including recent regulatory updates), infection prevention and control update (including emerging infectious disease protocols), program policy review (covering any changes from the past year), communication skills refresher (including difficult conversations and boundary setting), crisis recognition and escalation review, facility-specific training for representatives who visit multiple healthcare partners, and a knowledge assessment covering all training modules.

Training is delivered in a combination of self-paced online modules and a live group session. The live session allows representatives to ask questions, share experiences, and practice skills. Both components must be completed within the annual training window.

Completion deadlines are set by the Director of Medical Partnerships and communicated at least sixty days in advance. Representatives who do not complete training by the deadline are placed on inactive status. Inactive representatives may not conduct any program activities until training is completed and competency is verified.

Training records are maintained by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator. Records include: date of completion, modules completed, assessment scores, and any remedial training required. Representatives who score below eighty percent on the knowledge assessment must complete remedial training within thirty days.

# Section 9: Confidentiality and Privacy

## 9.1 Confidentiality Agreements

Every outreach representative, staff member, and volunteer with access to patient information must sign a confidentiality agreement before engaging in any program activities. The agreement establishes the legal and ethical obligation to protect patient privacy and outlines the consequences of breach.

The confidentiality agreement covers: the definition of confidential information (including patient health information, referral details, personal contact information, and any information shared by patients or partners), the representative\u2019s obligation to protect confidential information, the prohibition on unauthorized access, use, or disclosure, the requirement to report suspected breaches immediately, the consequences of breach (including program suspension, termination, and potential legal liability), and the duration of the obligation (which survives the representative\u2019s departure from the program).

Agreements are signed upon appointment or acceptance into the outreach program. Digital signatures are acceptable. The signed agreement is filed in the representative\u2019s personnel or volunteer record by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator.

Confidentiality agreements are renewed annually in conjunction with annual competency training. The renewal process includes a review of the agreement terms and an acknowledgment that the representative continues to be bound by them. Expired agreements result in inactive status until renewal is completed.

Representatives who leave the program remain bound by their confidentiality agreement indefinitely. They are reminded of this obligation upon departure and may be asked to sign a reaffirmation of the agreement at exit.

## 9.2 HIPAA Awareness Training

Lost Limb Riders is not a covered entity under the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act. We do not bill insurance, transmit electronic health records, or provide clinical care. However, our outreach representatives operate in HIPAA-regulated environments and regularly encounter protected health information. HIPAA awareness training ensures that representatives understand their obligations and do not create liability for healthcare partners.

HIPAA awareness training covers: what protected health information includes under federal regulations, how HIPAA applies to the healthcare facilities where representatives work, what representatives may and may not do with patient information they encounter, the minimum necessary standard and how it applies to outreach activities, the difference between a covered entity and a business associate and why Lost Limb Riders is neither, and real-world scenarios illustrating proper and improper handling of patient information.

Training emphasizes that representatives must never remove patient information from the facility, must never access patient charts or electronic health records, must never photograph or record patients in clinical settings, and must never share patient information with anyone outside the program\u2019s authorized channels.

HIPAA awareness training is included in initial outreach training and repeated annually. Representatives receive a HIPAA awareness reference card for quick review. Questions about specific situations are directed to the Director of Medical Partnerships, who consults with legal counsel as needed.

The training explicitly states that Lost Limb Riders does not represent itself as a HIPAA-covered entity. All materials and communications accurately describe the organization\u2019s role as a peer support and community connection program.

## 9.3 Secure Record Handling

Patient records maintained by Lost Limb Riders are stored, transmitted, and destroyed according to established security procedures. The program maintains a lower volume of protected health information than a clinical provider, but the standard of care is the same.

Storage: All electronic patient records are stored in the program database, which is hosted on a HIPAA-compliant platform with encryption at rest and in transit. Access is controlled by role-based permissions. Only the Hospital Outreach Coordinator, Director of Medical Partnerships, and assigned outreach representative have access to identifiable records. Physical records, if any, are stored in locked filing cabinets in a locked office accessible only to authorized personnel. No patient records are stored on personal devices, home computers, or unencrypted portable media.

Transmission: Patient information transmitted electronically uses encrypted channels only. Email is not used for identifiable patient information unless encrypted. Approved transmission methods include the program database\u2019s secure messaging feature, encrypted email, or secure file transfer. Fax transmission is permitted only when the receiving fax machine is in a secured location and the sender confirms receipt. Voicemail may include only enough information for the patient to return the call\u2014never clinical details or the nature of the referral.

Destruction: Records are retained for the period specified in the program\u2019s record retention schedule, which is reviewed annually and complies with applicable state and federal requirements. At the end of the retention period, records are destroyed securely. Electronic records are deleted from the database and any backup systems. Physical records are shredded using a cross-cut shredder or professional shredding service. Destruction is documented, including the date, method, and authorizing staff member.

Breach notification: If patient information is accessed, disclosed, or acquired by an unauthorized person, the Director of Medical Partnerships is notified within twenty-four hours. Breach notification to affected patients and partners follows applicable law and program policy. The representative who discovered or caused the breach participates fully in the investigation and remediation.

## 9.4 Consent Requirements

Patient consent is the foundation of all outreach activities. No contact occurs without it. Consent is documented before any information is exchanged or any visit conducted. The form of consent\u2014verbal or written\u2014depends on the context and the partner facility\u2019s requirements.

Verbal consent is sufficient for initial peer support contact in most settings. The referring partner confirms that the patient has agreed to be contacted, or the representative obtains verbal consent at the beginning of the first interaction. Verbal consent is documented in the program database with the date, time, and a summary of what the patient agreed to. Verbal consent is appropriate for: initial peer support contact (phone, video, or in-person following a referral), ongoing check-ins during the standard follow-up sequence, resource sharing and community connection, and invitations to Lost Limb Riders events.

Written consent is required when the program shares information outside the direct patient-representative relationship. Written consent is documented on a Lost Limb Riders release form and includes: the specific information to be shared, the purpose of the sharing, the recipient of the information, the expiration date of the consent, and the patient\u2019s signature. Written consent is required for: two-way communication between Lost Limb Riders and a prosthetic clinic about a patient\u2019s progress, sharing patient information with a family member or caregiver, photography or video recording of a patient for program use, and sharing patient outcome data with healthcare partners.

Consent can be withdrawn at any time. If a patient withdraws consent, all outreach activities stop immediately. The withdrawal is documented, and any information-sharing agreements related to that patient are terminated. The patient is informed that withdrawal of consent does not affect their access to any other services.

## 9.5 Photography Restrictions

Photography in healthcare facilities is strictly regulated. Outreach representatives never take photographs or videos in clinical areas of any facility. This includes patient rooms, treatment areas, hallways in patient care units, nursing stations, and any area where patients may be present or identifiable.

Patient photographs are taken only with explicit written consent obtained on a Lost Limb Riders release form that specifies how the photograph will be used\u2014program materials, social media, website, or internal use\u2014and the duration of the authorization. A separate consent is required for each use. Consent for one purpose does not imply consent for another.

Even with consent, representatives exercise judgment about when and where photographs are taken. Photographs are never taken when the patient is in a vulnerable state: in a hospital gown, in pain, crying, or during a medical procedure. The patient has the right to decline at any time, even after signing a release.

Photographs taken for program use are stored securely, used only for the authorized purpose, and deleted when the authorized use is complete. They are never stored on personal devices. They are never shared on personal social media.

Healthcare facilities may have their own photography policies that are more restrictive than Lost Limb Riders policies. When facility policy and program policy conflict, the more restrictive policy governs.

## 9.6 Social Media Restrictions

Social media is a powerful tool for community building and program awareness. It is also a significant source of privacy risk. Outreach representatives must follow clear restrictions to protect patient privacy and maintain program integrity.

Representatives never post patient information on social media. This includes: patient names, photographs or videos of patients, descriptions of patient interactions or conversations, patient medical history or amputation details, patient location or facility information, and any information that could identify a patient, alone or in combination with other details.

Representatives never tag, mention, or check in at healthcare facilities in connection with program activities on personal social media accounts. No posts like \u201cAt St. Mary\u2019s visiting a new amputee\u2014what a great conversation!\u201d Even without naming the patient, such posts identify the location and imply the nature of the visit, which may indirectly identify the patient.

Lost Limb Riders official social media accounts are managed by designated staff or volunteers. Outreach representatives may share content from official accounts but may not create program-related content on personal accounts without authorization. If a representative wants to share a program event or success story on their personal account, they coordinate with the Hospital Outreach Coordinator first.

Patient photographs and testimonials for program promotion are obtained only through the written consent process described in Section 9.5. Even with written consent, the patient is given the opportunity to review the proposed post before publication. The patient\u2019s comfort is more important than the organization\u2019s promotion.

Violations of social media restrictions are treated as confidentiality breaches and addressed according to the incident reporting procedure in Section 5.4.

# Section 10: Hospital Visit Procedures

## 10.1 Scheduling

Hospital visits are scheduled through the Hospital Outreach Coordinator, who serves as the central point of contact for all visit coordination. Visits are never arranged directly between the outreach representative and the patient without coordinator involvement. This centralization ensures consistency, prevents scheduling conflicts, and maintains a clear record of all program activities.

The scheduling process begins when the Hospital Outreach Coordinator receives a referral and confirms the patient\u2019s interest. The coordinator checks the facility\u2019s current visitor policies and any visit restrictions that may apply to the patient\u2019s unit. The coordinator then contacts the facility liaison\u2014typically the case manager, social worker, or charge nurse\u2014to confirm that a visit can take place and to identify any timing considerations. Liaisons may advise avoiding certain hours due to shift changes, therapy schedules, or medical procedures.

Once facility approval is obtained, the coordinator contacts the patient or the patient\u2019s designated contact to propose visit times. The patient\u2019s preference is prioritized. When possible, visits are scheduled during times the patient is typically awake, alert, and free from scheduled procedures or therapy sessions. Weekend and evening visits may be arranged if the facility allows and the patient prefers.

The assigned outreach representative receives the visit details at least twenty-four hours in advance: patient name and room number, facility name and entrance instructions, facility contact person, approved visit window, and any special instructions from the facility or patient. The representative confirms their availability and reviews the visit details before proceeding.

Same-day or urgent visit requests are accommodated when possible but are subject to the same approval requirements. Facility approval cannot be bypassed due to urgency. The seventy-two-hour initial contact timeline is measured from referral receipt, not from visit completion. An urgent visit that cannot be scheduled within the seventy-two-hour window does not excuse missing the initial contact deadline\u2014telephone or video contact should be made in the interim.

## 10.2 Check-In Procedures

Upon arrival at the healthcare facility, the outreach representative proceeds to the designated entry point for visitors. This is typically the main visitor entrance or a volunteer services office, depending on the facility. Representatives familiarize themselves with each facility\u2019s check-in location before their first visit.

The representative presents a government-issued photo identification and their Lost Limb Riders identification badge. Some facilities require both; others accept the Lost Limb Riders badge alone if the representative is already credentialed in their system. The representative follows the facility\u2019s specific sign-in process, which may include: signing a visitor log, obtaining a visitor badge, completing a health screening questionnaire, passing a temperature check, and receiving a facility-specific orientation or instruction sheet.

The visitor badge is worn in addition to the Lost Limb Riders badge unless the facility specifies otherwise. The facility badge takes precedence for access purposes. The representative returns the visitor badge at the end of each visit unless the facility issues long-term badges for regular volunteers.

After check-in, the representative proceeds directly to the patient\u2019s unit. They do not stop in common areas, visit other patients, or explore the facility. If the representative is uncertain how to reach the patient\u2019s room, they ask at the information desk or nursing station.

Upon reaching the unit, the representative reports to the nursing station, introduces themselves, and confirms that the patient is available and ready for a visit. The representative does not enter the patient\u2019s room without notifying the nursing staff first. This notification allows staff to confirm that there are no scheduling conflicts, medical events, or visitor restrictions the representative needs to know about.

## 10.3 Staff Coordination

Effective coordination with facility staff is essential to the success of every hospital visit. Clinical staff are the patient\u2019s primary support during their hospital stay. The outreach representative supports the staff as a complement, not a disruption.

At every visit, the representative identifies and introduces themselves to at least two key staff members: the patient\u2019s assigned nurse and either the charge nurse or the unit\u2019s social worker or case manager. These introductions are brief and professional: \u201cI\u2019m [name] with Lost Limb Riders, here to visit [patient]. I just wanted to introduce myself in case you need anything from me or if there\u2019s anything I should know before I go in.\u201d

The representative asks the nursing staff if there is anything they should be aware of before entering the room. Staff may share information that affects the visit: the patient is awaiting a procedure, is in significant pain, has had a difficult morning, or prefers not to be visited after all. The representative takes this guidance seriously and adjusts accordingly.

During the visit, if the patient raises a question the representative cannot answer\u2014about medications, discharge timing, therapy orders, or test results\u2014the representative encourages the patient to speak with their nurse or doctor and offers to help notify the staff if the patient wishes. The representative does not attempt to answer clinical questions or interpret medical information.

After the visit, the representative checks out with the nursing staff: \u201cI\u2019m done with my visit. Thanks for letting me be here. If the patient has any questions for Lost Limb Riders, here\u2019s my card.\u201d The representative leaves a business card or program information card with the nursing station so staff can contact Lost Limb Riders if the patient requests follow-up.

Staff coordination also includes maintaining positive relationships over time. Representatives remember staff names, express appreciation for their work, and never complain about staff members to patients or families. If a concern arises about a staff interaction, the representative addresses it through the escalation process, not at the bedside.

## 10.4 Visitor Conduct

Outreach representatives are visitors in the healthcare facility, and visitor conduct standards apply at all times. Representatives are held to an even higher standard than the general visiting public because they represent Lost Limb Riders and are present by special arrangement with the facility.

Noise level must be minimal throughout the visit. The representative speaks in a normal or lowered voice in the patient\u2019s room. In hallways, elevators, and waiting areas, the representative speaks quietly or not at all. Loud laughter, raised voices, and animated conversation that carries outside the patient\u2019s room are inappropriate.

Phone use is prohibited in patient care areas unless the phone is being used to show the patient a specific resource related to the visit. The representative\u2019s phone is silenced\u2014not on vibrate\u2014while in the facility. Phone calls are taken outside the patient care unit, away from patient rooms. Texting and social media are not accessed during visits.

Personal space is observed scrupulously. The representative maintains a respectful distance from the patient\u2014close enough for comfortable conversation, far enough to avoid crowding. The representative does not sit on the patient\u2019s bed, touch the patient without permission, or handle the patient\u2019s personal belongings. If the patient wants to show the representative something\u2014a photo, a device, a wound\u2014the representative looks but does not touch unless specifically invited and the touch is clearly appropriate.

Visit duration is monitored. The standard in-hospital visit is fifteen to thirty minutes. The representative watches for signs the patient is fatigued: yawning, closing eyes, looking away, becoming less responsive, or expressing discomfort. When those signs appear, the representative wraps up the conversation, thanks the patient, and departs. The representative never overstays.

Children, family members, and friends of the representative are never brought on hospital visits. Visitors must be the patient\u2019s visitors, not the representative\u2019s companions. The representative visits alone.

## 10.5 Safety Procedures

Safety is the highest priority in every healthcare encounter. Outreach representatives follow all facility safety procedures and exercise personal awareness to protect themselves, patients, and staff.

Infection control procedures are followed as described in Section 8.2. In addition, representatives monitor the patient\u2019s room for posted isolation signs. If a sign is present that was not there when the visit was scheduled, the representative checks with nursing staff before entering. If the patient has been placed on isolation precautions since scheduling, the visit is conducted virtually or rescheduled.

Emergency exits are identified at every facility before the first visit. Representatives note the location of the nearest exit upon entering each unit. In the event of a fire alarm, code blue, or other emergency, the representative follows facility staff instructions immediately. The representative does not attempt to assist with patient evacuation unless specifically directed to do so by staff. The representative\u2019s only responsibility in an emergency is to follow instructions and get out of the way.

Fire safety: representatives know the facility\u2019s fire alarm signal and response protocol. They do not use elevators during a fire alarm. They do not block fire doors, exit corridors, or fire extinguisher access at any time.

If the representative observes a situation that seems unsafe\u2014a patient who appears to be at risk of fall or injury, equipment that seems improperly placed, or any condition that could cause harm\u2014the representative notifies nursing staff immediately. The representative does not attempt to correct the situation themselves.

Personal safety: representatives trust their instincts. If a representative feels unsafe in a patient\u2019s room, for any reason, they excuse themselves and leave. They notify the Hospital Outreach Coordinator and the facility liaison about the situation. No outreach activity requires a representative to remain in a situation where they feel threatened. Safety concerns are documented and reviewed through the incident reporting process.

## 10.6 Visit Documentation

Every hospital visit must be documented in the program database within twenty-four hours of the visit\u2019s completion. Documentation is the program\u2019s record of what happened, what was discussed, and what follow-up is needed. Incomplete or delayed documentation undermines program tracking and continuity of care.

The visit record includes: date and time of visit, facility name and unit, visit duration, patient engagement level (engaged, distracted, fatigued, declined conversation, etc.), topics discussed, resources provided, questions answered or referred to clinical staff, follow-up commitments made, and any concerns or observations relevant to program activity.

Documentation is written in objective, factual language. The representative describes what happened without subjective interpretation. For example: \u201cPatient reported frustration with socket discomfort and difficulty sleeping\u201d is appropriate. \u201cPatient seemed angry and would not listen to advice\u201d is not appropriate. Documentation reflects the patient\u2019s experience, not the representative\u2019s judgment.

If the patient declines further contact during the visit, that is documented with the patient\u2019s stated reason if offered. The record is closed, and no further outreach is initiated. If the patient requests follow-up, the specific follow-up actions are documented with deadlines.

Visit documentation is reviewed by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator on a weekly basis for completeness and quality. Incomplete records are returned to the representative for correction within forty-eight hours. Consistently incomplete or late documentation is addressed through performance feedback and, if unresolved, escalated to the Director of Medical Partnerships.

## 10.7 Escalation Process

Issues sometimes arise that cannot be resolved at the level of the individual visit or the routine partner relationship. The escalation process provides a clear path for addressing concerns in a timely, professional manner.

**Issues with facility staff.** If an outreach representative experiences a conflict with a staff member\u2014a disagreement about visitor policy, a concern about how they were treated, or a misunderstanding\u2014the representative does not argue at the scene. They acknowledge the staff member\u2019s position politely, comply with any instructions given, and complete the visit if possible. After the visit, the representative documents the interaction and notifies the Hospital Outreach Coordinator within twenty-four hours. The coordinator determines whether to address the issue with the facility liaison or escalate to the Director of Medical Partnerships.

**Issues with patients.** If a patient becomes aggressive, verbally abusive, or makes the representative feel unsafe, the representative ends the visit immediately and leaves the room. They notify nursing staff and the Hospital Outreach Coordinator as soon as possible. The patient\u2019s behavior is documented factually, and future visits are suspended pending review by the Director of Medical Partnerships. The safety of the representative takes precedence over program continuity.

**Issues with procedures or policies.** If a representative identifies a conflict between program procedures and facility requirements, or if a procedure is unclear in a specific situation, the representative stops and consults the Hospital Outreach Coordinator before proceeding. No representative is expected to interpret policy in the moment without support.

**Escalation contacts.** The primary escalation contact is the Hospital Outreach Coordinator, available by phone during business hours and by email at all times. If the coordinator is unreachable, the representative contacts the Director of Medical Partnerships. For emergent issues involving immediate safety risk, the representative contacts facility security or calls 911 before notifying program leadership.

All escalations are documented in the program database with the date, nature of the issue, actions taken, resolution, and any follow-up required. Escalation records are reviewed quarterly by the Director of Medical Partnerships to identify patterns and preventive measures.

**Media inquiries.** If a member of the media contacts an outreach representative about program activities, the representative does not comment. They take the media member\u2019s contact information and refer the inquiry to the Lost Limb Riders Executive Director. No exceptions. Media contact, like patient privacy, requires central coordination to ensure accurate and appropriate representation.

# Section 11: Crisis Management

## 11.1 Mental Health Concerns

Outreach representatives are not mental health professionals. They are peers with lived experience and training in recognizing signs of psychological distress. Every representative must be able to distinguish between normal adjustment reactions\u2014sadness, frustration, grief\u2014and symptoms that require clinical intervention. Normal reactions are addressed through active listening, validation, and peer connection. Clinical symptoms require escalation.

Signs that warrant concern include: persistent hopelessness beyond the expected grief period, expressions of worthlessness or being a burden, withdrawal from social interaction that exceeds typical recovery patterns, dramatic mood swings, disorientation or confusion, paranoia, hallucinations, or any mention of not wanting to be alive. Representatives should also note changes in a patient\u2019s behavior between visits\u2014a patient who was engaged and hopeful one week who becomes flat, disconnected, or agitated the next.

When an outreach representative observes a potential mental health concern, the first step is to remain calm and listen without judgment. The representative should not try to diagnose, treat, or minimize what the patient is experiencing. Reflective listening\u2014\u201cIt sounds like you\u2019re carrying a lot right now\u201d\u2014is appropriate. Asking directly about suicidal thoughts is also appropriate if concern exists. Research shows that asking does not increase risk. It opens the door for help.

After the interaction, the representative must document their observations in the outreach record and notify the Hospital Outreach Coordinator within twenty-four hours. If the concern rises to the level of imminent danger, the escalation procedures in Section 11.2 apply immediately. Routine mental health concerns that do not involve imminent risk are referred to the patient\u2019s healthcare team for follow-up.

The Hospital Outreach Coordinator maintains a current list of crisis hotlines, mental health referral resources, and hospital behavioral health contacts for each partner facility. This list is reviewed quarterly and distributed to all outreach representatives.

## 11.2 Suicide Risk Escalation

Suicide risk is the most serious situation an outreach representative may encounter. Speed and clarity are essential. There is no room for hesitation, no space for hoping the situation resolves on its own. If a patient expresses suicidal ideation, intent, or plan\u2014or if the representative has a reasonable belief that the patient may harm themselves\u2014the following steps are executed immediately.

**Step one: Do not leave the patient alone.** The representative stays with the patient until responsibility is transferred to a qualified professional. If the visit is in a healthcare facility, the representative immediately notifies the nearest clinical staff member and stays in the room or within sight of the patient until staff arrive. If the interaction is by phone, the representative keeps the patient on the line and signals a second person to call emergency services or the patient\u2019s listed emergency contact.

**Step two: Notify hospital staff or emergency services.** In a healthcare facility, the representative goes to the nearest nurse\u2019s station or calls the patient\u2019s nurse using the facility\u2019s call system. The representative states clearly: \u201cI am concerned that this patient may be at risk of suicide. I need clinical staff to evaluate them immediately.\u201d The representative does not downplay the concern, does not use vague language, and does not defer to someone else to make the notification. In a community setting, the representative calls 911 and provides the patient\u2019s location and the nature of the concern.

**Step three: Notify the Hospital Outreach Coordinator.** After the immediate safety situation has been transferred to appropriate authorities, the representative contacts the Hospital Outreach Coordinator to report the incident. This notification must occur within one hour of the situation being resolved, even if it means calling outside normal business hours.

**Step four: Complete an incident report.** The representative documents the situation in full, including what the patient said or did, what actions the representative took, who was notified, and what the outcome was. This report is submitted within twenty-four hours to the Director of Medical Partnerships.

**Step five: Participate in a debrief.** Within seventy-two hours of the incident, the representative participates in a debrief with the Director of Medical Partnerships and, if appropriate, a mental health professional. The purpose is to ensure the representative is emotionally supported, to review what went well and what could be improved, and to update procedures if gaps were identified.

No outreach representative is expected to handle a suicide crisis alone. The organization supports representatives fully through these situations, including offering mental health support for the representative themselves. No representative will be criticized for escalating a concern that turns out to be less serious than initially believed. It is always better to over-escalate than to under-respond.

## 11.3 Emotional Distress

Not every difficult emotion is a crisis. Patients who are newly amputated are processing loss, fear, and uncertainty. Crying, anger, frustration, and withdrawal are normal parts of that process. Outreach representatives must be able to sit with those emotions without rushing to fix them.

De-escalation techniques for managing emotional distress begin with the representative\u2019s own composure. A calm voice, relaxed body language, and steady eye contact communicate safety. The representative should speak slowly, use short sentences, and avoid overwhelming the patient with information. Acknowledging the emotion is the first step: \u201cI can see this is really hard. That makes sense.\u201d

Active listening is the primary tool. The representative reflects what they hear without interpreting or judging. They ask open-ended questions\u2014\u201cWhat is the hardest part right now?\u201d\u2014rather than leading questions\u2014\u201cAre you scared about going home?\u201d They give the patient space to talk without interrupting. Silence is acceptable. Sometimes the most valuable thing a peer can do is sit quietly and let someone feel what they need to feel.

If a patient becomes increasingly agitated, unable to be calmed, or expresses that they want the visit to end, the representative should pause or end the visit gracefully. The appropriate response is: \u201cI hear you. We can stop here. I want this to be helpful, not overwhelming. Would you like me to come back another time, or would you prefer I check in with your nurse?\u201d The representative then leaves the room, notifies the patient\u2019s nurse about the patient\u2019s distress level (without sharing details of what was discussed), and documents the interaction in the outreach record.

Representatives should not take a patient\u2019s emotional reaction personally. Distress is not about the representative or the organization. It is about the patient\u2019s circumstances. The representative\u2019s job is to be present, not to perform rescue.

## 11.4 Medical Emergencies

Medical emergencies that occur during an outreach visit are rare, but every representative must know exactly what to do. The rule is simple: call for clinical help immediately. Outreach representatives do not provide medical care.

If a patient exhibits signs of a medical emergency\u2014chest pain, difficulty breathing, sudden confusion, loss of consciousness, seizure, severe bleeding, or any other acute change in condition\u2014the representative takes the following actions:

**In a healthcare facility:** The representative presses the nurse call button or goes directly to the nurse\u2019s station. They state clearly: \u201cI need help in room [number]. The patient is [describe symptoms].\u201d They stay out of the way of clinical staff but remain nearby in case they can assist with nonmedical tasks such as contacting family or clearing the hallway. The representative does not attempt to reposition the patient, administer medication, operate medical equipment, or perform any clinical intervention.

**In a community setting:** The representative calls 911 immediately. They provide the patient\u2019s location, the nature of the emergency, and any relevant information about the patient\u2019s condition. They stay on the line with the dispatcher until emergency services arrive. If trained in basic first aid and CPR, the representative may provide those interventions only until professional help arrives. No outreach representative is required to perform CPR or first aid, and no representative will be penalized for choosing not to.

After emergency services have taken over, the representative notifies the Hospital Outreach Coordinator and completes an incident report. The representative also follows up with the patient or the patient\u2019s family within forty-eight hours to offer support, unless the situation involves the patient\u2019s death, in which case follow-up is coordinated through the Hospital Outreach Coordinator.

## 11.5 Security Incidents

Security incidents include aggressive or threatening behavior by a patient or family member, theft or property damage, unauthorized access to restricted areas, violations of facility visitor policies, or any situation that requires intervention by hospital security personnel. Outreach representatives should never attempt to manage a security situation alone.

If a representative encounters aggressive or threatening behavior, the priority is personal safety. The representative disengages from the interaction immediately, moves to a safe location, and notifies hospital security. The representative does not argue, escalate, or attempt to de-escalate a person who is actively threatening. Some situations cannot be talked down. Knowing when to walk away is a professional skill.

After the immediate situation is resolved, the representative notifies the Hospital Outreach Coordinator and completes an incident report. The report includes a description of the behavior, the location, any witnesses, and the outcome of security involvement. If the incident involved a patient with whom the representative had an established relationship, the Director of Medical Partnerships determines whether future visits with that patient should continue and under what conditions.

Hospital security protocols vary by facility. Outreach representatives receive facility-specific security orientation as part of the credentialing process for each partner hospital. Representatives must know how to contact security, where emergency exits are located, and what the facility\u2019s code system means (code blue, code silver, code gray, etc.). This information is reviewed at each new facility onboarding and annually thereafter.

## 11.6 Mandatory Reporting Requirements

Outreach representatives are mandatory reporters for certain categories of harm. The specific obligations vary by state, but the general categories include suspected abuse or neglect of children, vulnerable adults, and elders, as well as threats of harm to self or others. Representatives must understand the reporting requirements for the state in which they operate.

**Child abuse and neglect:** All outreach representatives are mandatory reporters of suspected child abuse or neglect. If, during the course of an outreach visit, a representative observes or suspects that a minor is being abused or neglected, they must report immediately to the state child protective services hotline or local law enforcement. Representatives do not investigate the suspicion. They do not confront the alleged abuser. They report and let the authorities determine the next steps.

**Vulnerable adult and elder abuse:** Many states require reporting of suspected abuse, neglect, or exploitation of vulnerable adults and elders. Amputees who are elderly or have cognitive impairments may fall into this category. Representatives must know the definition of vulnerable adult in their state and the reporting procedures.

**Threats of harm:** If a patient makes a credible threat to harm a specific person, the representative must report this to law enforcement and to the hospital social worker or security team. Patient confidentiality does not override the duty to protect someone from imminent harm.

**State-specific obligations:** The Director of Medical Partnerships maintains a state-by-state guide to mandatory reporting requirements for every jurisdiction in which the program operates. This guide is reviewed annually with legal counsel and is included in initial training and annual refresher training. Outreach representatives sign an acknowledgment that they have received and understand their reporting obligations.

Representatives who make a report in good faith are protected from civil and criminal liability under state law. Representatives who fail to report when required may face legal consequences and will be subject to program disciplinary action, including termination of their role.

# Section 12: Educational Resources

## 12.1 Patient Information Packets

Every new amputee referred to Lost Limb Riders should receive a patient information packet. The packet introduces the organization, explains peer support, and provides immediate resources for the journey ahead. It is designed to be accessible, encouraging, and useful for someone who may still be in shock or denial.

The packet contains the following items: a welcome letter from a Lost Limb Riders peer, an overview of peer support services, a list of common questions and answers for new amputees, contact information for the local outreach representative, a resource card with crisis hotlines and urgent support numbers, a brochure about adaptive recreation opportunities, and a feedback card that the patient can return anonymously.

Packets are printed on standard letter paper and assembled in a folder or pocket folder with the Lost Limb Riders logo. Materials use large, readable fonts\u2014minimum twelve-point\u2014and avoid clinical jargon. The tone is warm, direct, and hopeful without making promises about outcomes. Spanish-language versions are available in regions with significant Spanish-speaking populations, and translation services are available for other languages upon request.

Distribution occurs through healthcare partners. Packets are stocked in partner facilities\u2014at nurse\u2019s stations, in case management offices, in prosthetic clinic waiting rooms\u2014and are provided directly by outreach representatives during in-hospital visits. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator tracks packet inventory and reorders supplies monthly. Partner facilities may request additional packets at any time.

Packet contents are reviewed annually and updated as needed. Content updates are approved by the Director of Medical Partnerships before reprinting. Outdated packets are removed from distribution within thirty days of a revision being published.

## 12.2 Family Guides

Amputation does not happen to one person. It happens to a family. Spouses, children, parents, and siblings all experience the trauma and adjustment. Family guides are designed specifically for the people who love and care for a new amputee. They address the questions family members often hesitate to ask.

Family guides include: an explanation of what the amputee is experiencing emotionally and physically, guidance on how to offer support without smothering, signs of caregiver burnout and how to address it, resources for family counseling and support groups, tips for home modifications and adaptive equipment, advice on talking to children about amputation, and a list of books, websites, and videos recommended for families.

Guides are offered during every initial family contact. Outreach representatives offer the guide to family members who are present during an in-hospital visit. When family members are not present, the representative leaves a guide with the patient and explains that it is for their family. Guides are also available at partner facility waiting areas and family resource centers.

Family guides are printed in the same format as patient information packets\u2014letter size, folder-style, Lost Limb Riders branded. Digital versions are available on the Lost Limb Riders website and can be emailed to family members who prefer electronic access. Representatives should ask whether the family prefers print or digital.

## 12.3 Community Resource Directories

The community resource directory is a living document that lists organizations and services available to amputees in each region served by the program. It is one of the most practical tools the program provides. A well-maintained directory can save a patient weeks of frustrating searching.

Each regional directory includes: housing assistance programs, transportation services, financial aid programs, mental health providers with amputee experience, support groups (both amputee-specific and general), adaptive sports and recreation organizations, vocational rehabilitation offices, legal aid clinics, veteran services, and prosthetic funding assistance organizations.

Directories are maintained by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator with input from Regional Outreach Representatives. Each entry includes the organization name, a brief description of services, contact information, eligibility requirements, and any fees or costs. Entries note whether the representative has personally verified the organization and, if so, the date of last verification.

Directories are updated quarterly. Each quarter, every entry is reviewed for accuracy. Phone numbers are called, websites are checked, and eligibility requirements are confirmed. Entries that cannot be verified are flagged and removed if still unverifiable after sixty days. Representatives report broken or outdated entries immediately through the program database.

Directories are available in printed format at partner facilities and electronically through the Lost Limb Riders website. The electronic version is searchable by category and geographic region. Outreach representatives carry a current copy in their visit materials.

## 12.4 Program Brochures

Program brochures are the primary marketing tool for the Hospital and Prosthetic Partner Outreach program. They are designed for display in healthcare facility waiting rooms, exam rooms, and common areas. A brochure must communicate who Lost Limb Riders is, what the program offers, and how to access services\u2014all in the time it takes a patient to flip through while waiting for an appointment.

Brochures are tri-fold, full color, printed on heavyweight paper. The front panel displays the Lost Limb Riders logo and the tagline \u201cI Can. I Will.\u201d Inside panels describe the peer support model, list program services, and include testimonials from amputees who have been helped by the program. The back panel contains contact information and a QR code linking to the referral page on the Lost Limb Riders website.

Design standards require consistent branding across all materials. The logo must appear on every piece. The color palette is locked\u2014no deviations without approval from the Director of Medical Partnerships. Fonts are limited to the organization\u2019s approved typefaces. All photographs used in brochures must be authorized through photo release forms.

Availability is maintained at all active partner locations. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator manages inventory by region and reorders when stock falls below a two-month supply. Regional Outreach Representatives check brochure displays during each partner visit and restock as needed. Partners may request additional brochures through their designated point of contact.

Brochures are updated every two years or whenever program information changes significantly. Obsolete brochures are collected and recycled within sixty days of the new version being distributed.

## 12.5 Referral Cards

Referral cards are a simple, powerful tool. They fit in a pocket, hand them to a patient, and say \u201cCall this number.\u201d They remove friction from the referral process. Healthcare providers prefer them because they are easy to stock and easy to give.

Each referral card measures three-and-a-half by two inches\u2014standard business card size. The front displays the Lost Limb Riders logo, the program name, and the phone number. The back has a brief message: \u201cYou are not alone. Call us to connect with someone who has been there.\u201d Cards are printed on heavy cardstock with a matte finish for easy writing.

Cards are stocked at every partner location. Placement locations include: nurse\u2019s stations, discharge planning offices, case management desks, prosthetic clinic check-in counters, physical therapy treatment areas, and patient education boards. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator tracks distribution by partner and restocks monthly.

Regional Outreach Representatives carry referral cards at all times\u2014in their vehicle, in their visit bag, in their pocket. Giving a card to a patient or family member does not require a formal referral. It is an invitation. If a patient calls the number on the card, the Hospital Outreach Coordinator processes the contact as a self-referral and proceeds with the standard intake.

## 12.6 Discharge Packet Inserts

Discharge planning is a critical window for connecting new amputees to community support. Patients leaving the hospital are overwhelmed with paperwork, prescriptions, and instructions. A discharge packet insert cuts through the noise. It is designed to be added directly to the standard discharge packet that hospitals provide to every patient.

The insert is a single page, printed front and back, on bright white cardstock. It stands out from the rest of the discharge papers. The front side introduces Lost Limb Riders in three sentences and lists the top three reasons to call. The back side has a tear-off card with the program phone number and website. No additional information is needed on the insert\u2014its only job is to get the patient to make the first call.

Coordination with hospital discharge planners is essential for successful insert distribution. The outreach representative meets with each partner facility\u2019s discharge planning team to explain the insert and agree on where it will be placed in the discharge packet. Some hospitals include it in every surgical discharge packet. Others include it only for amputee patients. Others prefer that discharge planners hand it out selectively.

The Hospital Outreach Coordinator maintains a list of which partner facilities use the inserts and how they are distributed. Inserts are delivered to each facility in bulk, typically in quantities of five hundred. Facilities reorder through their program point of contact. Insert content is reviewed annually and updated if needed to reflect current contact information or program offerings.

# Section 13: Community Resource Network

## 13.1 Housing Resources

Housing instability is a reality for some amputees, particularly those whose amputation resulted in job loss, medical debt, or disability. Accessible housing is another challenge\u2014many homes and apartments are not designed for wheelchair use or mobility aids. The community resource network includes housing resources that address both affordability and accessibility.

Housing resources maintained in the directory include: emergency shelter programs, short-term housing assistance for patients displaced by medical treatment, accessible housing rental listings, home modification programs (ramps, widened doorways, roll-in showers), Section 8 and other housing voucher information, and nonprofit organizations that build or retrofit homes for people with disabilities.

The referral process begins when an outreach representative identifies a housing need during a peer visit or follow-up contact. The representative offers the patient the relevant section of the community resource directory and, if needed, helps the patient make the first phone call. The representative does not transport the patient to housing appointments, co-sign leases, or provide housing advocacy beyond resource connection.

Vetting standards require that every housing resource listed in the directory has been contacted within the past six months to confirm that the organization is still operating, still accepting clients, and still providing the listed services. Organizations with complaints filed against them with the Better Business Bureau or state consumer protection agency are not listed. Organizations that charge application fees to low-income applicants are flagged in the directory.

## 13.2 Transportation Assistance

Transportation is a barrier for many amputees, especially in the early months after amputation when driving may not be possible and public transit may not be accessible. The transportation section of the community resource network connects patients with options for getting to medical appointments, prosthetic fittings, support groups, and other essential destinations.

Resources include: non-emergency medical transportation services (NEMT) available through Medicaid and Medicare Advantage plans, volunteer driver programs operated by local nonprofit organizations, reduced-fare paratransit services for people with disabilities, rideshare voucher programs where available, and veterans transportation services through VA beneficiary travel.

Eligibility criteria vary by program. Medicaid NEMT requires active Medicaid coverage and advance scheduling. Paratransit requires ADA eligibility certification through the local transit authority. VA transportation requires enrollment in VA healthcare. Representatives help patients understand the eligibility requirements for each option but do not complete applications on the patient\u2019s behalf.

The outreach representative\u2019s role is to provide information and, when appropriate, help the patient complete initial contact with the transportation provider. Representatives do not transport patients themselves. This prohibition exists for liability, insurance, and boundary reasons. If a patient has no transportation options and is at risk of missing critical medical care, the representative escalates the situation to the Hospital Outreach Coordinator, who may coordinate with the referring healthcare partner\u2019s social work or case management department.

## 13.3 Financial Assistance

The financial impact of amputation is severe. Medical bills, lost income, prosthetic costs, home modifications, and ongoing care expenses can overwhelm even patients with good insurance. The financial assistance section of the community resource network helps patients find and apply for available programs.

Resources include: Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) and Supplemental Security Income (SSI) application guidance, state disability insurance programs, prosthetic funding assistance organizations (such as the Challenged Athletes Foundation, the Amputee Coalition, and local nonprofit foundations), pharmaceutical assistance programs for medication costs, utility assistance programs, food assistance programs (SNAP, WIC, local food banks), and charitable organizations that provide one-time grants for medical or living expenses.

Application support is provided within clearly defined boundaries. Representatives may help patients locate application forms, explain program eligibility requirements, and connect patients with professional application assistance services. Representatives do not complete applications for patients, handle patients\u2019 personal financial information, provide tax or legal advice, or recommend specific insurance plans. Patients who need professional assistance with SSDI or SSI applications are referred to a disability advocate or attorney.

Limitations are communicated clearly and early. Lost Limb Riders does not provide direct financial assistance. The organization does not maintain emergency funds, pay patient bills, or provide loans. If a patient expresses urgent financial need that cannot be met through any listed resource, the representative escalates to the Hospital Outreach Coordinator, who works with the referring partner\u2019s social work team to identify options.

## 13.4 Mental Health Providers

Peer support is not therapy. It complements mental health treatment but does not replace it. The community resource network includes a curated list of mental health providers who have experience working with amputees, chronic illness patients, or individuals adjusting to disability.

Providers listed in the directory include: licensed therapists and counselors who specialize in medical trauma, grief, or disability adjustment, support groups facilitated by mental health professionals, psychiatrists who accept patients with amputation-related depression or anxiety, hospital-based behavioral health programs, and teletherapy platforms that offer accessible options for patients with mobility limitations.

Referral criteria require that providers hold an active, unrestricted license in their state, accept the insurance plans commonly held by amputees in the region (Medicare, Medicaid, major private insurers), and have availability for new patients within a reasonable time frame. Providers are not endorsed or recommended over other providers. They are listed as available resources, and patients make their own choice about whom to contact.

Vetting includes an initial phone or email contact with each provider to confirm licensure, availability, and willingness to receive referrals from community organizations. Providers are re-contacted every twelve months to verify continued availability. Patients should always verify with their insurance plan that a provider is in-network before scheduling an appointment.

Privacy considerations are paramount. Representatives do not share a patient\u2019s name or contact information with a mental health provider without the patient\u2019s written consent. The patient contacts the provider directly. The representative\u2019s role is limited to providing the list and, if the patient requests it, helping the patient understand what to ask when calling to schedule.

## 13.5 Support Groups

Support groups are a cornerstone of amputee peer support. While one-on-one peer connection is powerful, group settings offer something different: the realization that this struggle is shared, that others are further along in the journey, and that belonging to a community of amputees changes how you see yourself.

Support group resources maintained in the directory include: Lost Limb Riders chapter meetings (both in-person and virtual), general amputee support groups operated by hospitals and rehabilitation centers, condition-specific groups (for example, groups for diabetic amputees or trauma survivors), caregiver support groups for family members, and online communities and forums with active amputee participation.

In-person groups are listed with meeting location, frequency, time, and any access information (parking, wheelchair entry, elevator access). Virtual groups are listed with platform information (Zoom, Microsoft Teams, etc.), meeting link or access instructions, and any registration requirements. Groups that require advance registration are clearly marked.

Representatives offer support group information to every patient during the first follow-up contact after an in-hospital visit. Some patients are ready for group support immediately. Others need months of individual adjustment before they feel comfortable in a group setting. Both responses are normal. The representative respects the patient\u2019s readiness and offers the information again at later follow-up points.

## 13.6 Adaptive Recreation

Adaptive recreation is where Lost Limb Riders lives. It is the organization\u2019s origin and its identity. The adaptive recreation section of the community resource network connects amputees with the activities that prove\u2014more powerfully than any words\u2014that life after amputation includes joy, challenge, and adventure.

Resources include: adaptive sports programs (skiing, cycling, swimming, basketball, rock climbing, surfing), motorcycle riding programs and groups (including Lost Limb Riders rides and events), outdoor recreation organizations that accommodate mobility disabilities, adaptive fitness and gym programs, hunting and fishing programs for people with disabilities, and recreational prosthetic resources for sports-specific devices.

Referrals to adaptive recreation programs follow the same process as other resource referrals. The representative provides the resource information and helps the patient make initial contact if needed. For Lost Limb Riders specific activities\u2014group rides, chapter events, social gatherings\u2014the representative offers a direct invitation and, if the patient is interested, connects them with the local chapter leadership.

Representatives share their own recreation experiences when appropriate but do not pressure patients to participate in activities the patient is not ready for. Some patients want to see photos of other amputees skiing before they are ready to try it themselves. Others want to talk about riding a motorcycle before they have learned to walk on their prosthesis. The representative meets the patient where they are.

## 13.7 Employment Services

Returning to work after amputation is a major milestone. For some patients, it is the goal that drives their entire recovery. For others, it is a source of anxiety about discrimination, accommodation, and capability. The employment services section connects patients with resources that support successful return to work.

Resources include: state vocational rehabilitation agencies (which provide job training, education funding, and job placement services for people with disabilities), Social Security\u2019s Ticket to Work program, employer outreach organizations that connect people with disabilities to inclusive employers, disability employment counselors and job coaches, job accommodation resources (including Job Accommodation Network), self-employment and entrepreneurship programs for people with disabilities, and resume and interview preparation services.

Vocational rehabilitation is the primary resource for most amputees seeking employment services. Each state has a vocational rehabilitation agency that is funded by the federal Rehabilitation Services Administration. Services can include career counseling, skills assessment, job training, education assistance, job placement, and follow-up support. Eligibility requires a physical or mental impairment that creates a barrier to employment, which amputees meet.

The representative\u2019s role is to explain what vocational rehabilitation offers and help the patient locate their state agency. Representatives do not attend vocational rehabilitation appointments, advocate for specific services, or provide employment references. If the patient encounters difficulty accessing services, the representative can offer guidance on filing an appeal or requesting a patient advocate.

## 13.8 Legal Aid

Amputees face legal questions that range from disability discrimination to Social Security appeals to insurance disputes. The legal aid section of the community resource network connects patients with legal resources appropriate to their situation.

Resources include: legal aid organizations that provide free or low-cost civil legal services to low-income individuals, disability rights organizations that handle discrimination and accessibility cases, lawyer referral services operated by state bar associations, Social Security disability attorneys who work on contingency, fair housing organizations that handle accessible housing complaints, and pro bono legal clinics offered through law schools or bar associations.

Common legal issues amputees face include: disability discrimination by employers, denial of Social Security disability benefits, insurance denial of prosthetic coverage or medical necessity determinations, accessible housing and reasonable accommodation requests, veterans benefits appeals, and medical debt and collection issues.

Representatives do not provide legal advice, recommend specific attorneys, or interpret legal documents. The directory is a starting point. Patients are encouraged to consult with an attorney for any legal matter, and representatives help patients understand what questions to ask when contacting a legal resource.

## 13.9 Veteran Services

Veterans make up a significant portion of the amputee population. They have access to benefits and services through the Department of Veterans Affairs that can substantially improve their quality of life after amputation. The veteran services section of the community resource network is dedicated to connecting veteran amputees with these resources.

Resources include: VA healthcare enrollment assistance, VA prosthetic and sensory aids service (which provides prosthetic devices and repairs at no cost to service-connected veterans), VA compensation and pension benefits for service-connected disability, VA vocational rehabilitation and employment (VR&E) program, VA adaptive sports programs (including the National Veterans Wheelchair Games and Winter Sports Clinic), VA home modification grants (SAH and SHA grants for service-connected veterans), and veteran service organizations (VSOs) such as the American Legion, VFW, and DAV that provide benefits counseling and claims assistance.

Benefits counseling is a critical service. Many veterans are not aware of the full range of benefits available to them. A VSO-accredited benefits counselor can help veterans understand their eligibility, file claims, and appeal denied claims. The directory lists VSO contacts by region, including the specific service officers who handle disability compensation claims.

Representatives ask every patient who is a veteran whether they are connected to VA healthcare and VA benefits. If the veteran is not connected, the representative offers the veteran services directory and explains the benefits of establishing VA eligibility. Representatives do not complete VA forms, submit claims, or provide benefits advice. Veterans who need claims assistance are referred to an accredited VSO representative or VA benefits counselor.

# Section 14: Quality Assurance

## 14.1 Referral Response Times

Speed matters in peer support. A patient who is contacted within twenty-four hours of referral feels valued. A patient who waits a week may feel forgotten or may have already lost the motivation to engage. Response time standards ensure that every referred patient receives timely contact.

The target response time is within twenty-four hours of referral receipt. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator processes incoming referrals at least once per business day. Referrals received on business days are assigned before the end of the next business day. Referrals received on weekends or holidays are processed on the next business day, and the assigned representative makes initial contact within twenty-four hours of assignment.

The maximum acceptable response time is forty-eight hours. Any referral that exceeds forty-eight hours without initial contact triggers an automatic notification to the Director of Medical Partnerships. If a referral cannot be contacted within forty-eight hours due to incorrect contact information, patient unavailability, or other barriers, the referral is documented with the reason and the referring partner is notified of the delay.

Response time is tracked in the program database. Each referral is timestamped at receipt, at assignment, and at initial contact. Monthly reports show average response time by region, by partner, and by outreach representative. Response time data is reviewed in monthly quality assurance meetings, and patterns of delayed response are addressed through additional training, resource allocation, or process improvement.

## 14.2 Partner Satisfaction Surveys

Healthcare partners are the gateway to patients. Their satisfaction with Lost Limb Riders directly affects referral volume, access, and program reputation. Measuring partner satisfaction is essential to maintaining strong relationships and identifying areas for improvement.

Partner satisfaction surveys are distributed annually to every active healthcare partner. The survey is sent by email from the Director of Medical Partnerships and is completed electronically. Paper versions are available upon request. Partners are given three weeks to complete the survey, with a reminder sent at the one-week and one-day marks.

The survey covers: ease of the referral process, responsiveness of Lost Limb Riders communication, quality and availability of educational materials, professionalism of outreach representatives, value of peer support to patients, likelihood of continuing the partnership, likelihood of recommending Lost Limb Riders to other healthcare organizations, and open-ended questions for additional feedback.

Survey responses are anonymous by default. Partners who choose to identify themselves may do so, but anonymity is offered to encourage honest feedback. Results are tabulated by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator and reviewed by the Director of Medical Partnerships.

Action on results follows a structured process. Any item with an average score below three out of five is flagged for review. The Director of Medical Partnerships develops a corrective action plan for each flagged item within thirty days of survey results being compiled. Partners who reported concerns are contacted within sixty days to discuss the corrective actions taken. Survey results and action plans are reported to the Executive Director and the Board of Directors in the annual program evaluation.

## 14.3 Patient Satisfaction Surveys

Patient satisfaction is the ultimate measure of program effectiveness. If patients find value in peer support, the program is working. If they do not, the program must change. Patient satisfaction surveys capture the patient perspective at multiple points in the peer support journey.

Surveys are administered at three points: after the first peer support contact, at the thirty-day follow-up, and at the ninety-day follow-up. The first-contact survey captures immediate impressions\u2014whether the patient felt welcomed, heard, and respected. The thirty-day survey captures whether the connection is developing into a meaningful relationship. The ninety-day survey captures overall impact and whether the patient would recommend the program to others.

Surveys are short\u2014five to eight questions\u2014and take less than five minutes to complete. They are administered by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator via phone, text, or email, depending on the patient\u2019s stated preference. Surveys are never administered by the patient\u2019s assigned peer support representative to reduce bias in responses.

Anonymity is offered to all patients. Responses are identified only by a unique survey code that is not linked to the patient\u2019s name in the survey database. Patients who waive anonymity are thanked but not encouraged to do so. The goal is honest feedback, not attribution.

Follow-up on complaints follows a defined process. Any survey response that indicates dissatisfaction, a negative experience, or a specific complaint is reviewed by the Director of Medical Partnerships within seven business days. The patient is contacted to discuss the concern if contact information is available and the patient has not opted out of follow-up. Corrective action is documented and, if appropriate, the patient is informed of what changed as a result of their feedback.

## 14.4 Outcome Tracking

Outcome tracking transforms anecdotal success stories into measurable program impact. It answers the question that every healthcare partner asks: does this program actually make a difference? Without outcome data, the program relies on faith. With outcome data, the program makes its case.

Outcomes tracked include: percentage of referred patients who complete at least one peer support contact, average number of peer contacts per patient within the first thirty, sixty, and ninety days, patient-reported improvement in emotional well-being, patient-reported confidence in managing life after amputation, patient-reported connection to at least one community resource, and conversion rate from referral to ongoing peer relationship.

Measurement methods combine program database tracking with survey data. Contact frequency and duration are captured through outreach logs. Emotional well-being and confidence are measured using a simple one-to-five scale in patient surveys. Community resource connection is confirmed through follow-up contact where the patient reports whether they used a referred resource and found it helpful.

Reporting cadence is quarterly. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator compiles outcome data at the end of each quarter and produces a summary report. The Director of Medical Partnerships reviews the report and identifies trends, gaps, and opportunities. The quarterly report is shared with outreach representatives, the Executive Director, and the Board of Directors. An annual outcome report is produced for healthcare partners.

## 14.5 Program Audits

Program audits are a systematic review of program operations, documentation, and compliance. They ensure that the program is running as designed, that policies are being followed, and that risks are identified before they become problems.

Audits are conducted annually. The audit scope covers: referral processing accuracy and timeliness, documentation completeness for outreach interactions, credentialing and training compliance for outreach representatives, partner agreement currency and completeness, incident report review and follow-up completion, survey administration and action on results, and data security and privacy compliance.

The audit is conducted by the Director of Medical Partnerships or by an external reviewer appointed by the Executive Director. Using an external reviewer every three years provides an independent perspective. The auditor reviews a sample of records\u2014no fewer than twenty percent of active referrals and twenty percent of active partner files\u2014and compares them against program standards.

The audit produces a written report with findings, recommendations, and a corrective action timeline if deficiencies are identified. The report is submitted to the Executive Director and the Board of Directors within sixty days of audit commencement. Findings are shared with outreach representatives in a summary format that focuses on improvement rather than individual blame.

## 14.6 Corrective Action Procedures

When a quality assurance review, audit, incident, or feedback identifies a deficiency, corrective action is initiated. The purpose of corrective action is not punishment. It is improvement. The process ensures that issues are addressed systematically and do not recur.

Identifying issues is the first step. Issues may be identified through: audit findings, partner or patient complaints, incident reports, survey results, monitoring of response times or documentation compliance, or observation by Director of Medical Partnerships. Any staff member or volunteer may raise a concern through the program\u2019s open reporting process.

Once an issue is identified, the Director of Medical Partnerships determines its severity. Minor issues\u2014a single documentation error, a one-time delay in response time\u2014are addressed through direct feedback and coaching. Major issues\u2014pattern of non-compliance, partner relationship damage, privacy breach, recurring patient dissatisfaction\u2014require a formal corrective action plan.

The corrective action plan includes: a clear description of the issue, the root cause analysis (why did this happen, not just what happened), specific actions to address the root cause, a responsible party for each action, a timeline for completion (not to exceed ninety days unless justified), and measurable criteria for determining that the issue is resolved.

Verification occurs after the timeline expires. The Director of Medical Partnerships reviews evidence that each action was completed and that the issue has been resolved. If verification confirms resolution, the corrective action is closed and documented. If verification finds that the issue persists, a new corrective action plan is developed with increased oversight and, if warranted, personnel action.

# Section 15: Performance Metrics

## 15.1 Number of Hospital Partners

This metric counts the total number of healthcare facilities with an active, signed partnership agreement. Hospitals, trauma centers, rehabilitation hospitals, inpatient rehabilitation facilities, and skilled nursing facilities each count as one partner. Multiple agreements with the same health system are counted individually if each involves a separate physical facility.

This metric is reported monthly to the Executive Director and quarterly to the Board of Directors. The report includes the total number, the number of new partners added in the reporting period, the number of partners that have terminated or been inactive for more than twelve months, and the net change from the previous period. Regional breakdowns are included when the program operates in multiple geographic areas.

The target for partnership growth is established annually as part of the program planning process. Growth targets consider organizational capacity, outreach representative availability, and geographic coverage goals. The metric is reported in the annual program evaluation alongside qualitative assessment of partnership health and depth.

## 15.2 Number of Prosthetic Partners

This metric counts prosthetic and orthotic clinics with active partnership agreements. It is tracked separately from hospital partners because prosthetic clinics represent a different referral pathway and require different relationship management approaches.

Reporting follows the same cadence and format as hospital partner metrics: monthly to the Executive Director, quarterly to the Board, with new additions, losses, and net change included. Prosthetic partner metrics are reported in a separate section of the performance report to allow focused analysis of this partner type.

Partnership depth is tracked alongside raw numbers. A prosthetic clinic that refers no patients in twelve months is flagged for relationship review regardless of signed agreement status. The goal is active, referring partners, not paper agreements.

## 15.3 Referrals Received

This metric counts the total number of referrals accepted by the program in a reporting period. A referral is counted when the Hospital Outreach Coordinator receives a referral request from a healthcare partner and sufficient information is provided to proceed with contact. Duplicate referrals for the same patient are consolidated into a single referral record.

Referrals received are tracked by source (partner type, specific facility, referring department), by urgency level, and by geographic region. Monthly reports show referral volume trends, seasonal patterns, and changes by source. A sudden increase or decrease from a specific partner triggers a quality assurance check.

The Hospital Outreach Coordinator monitors incoming referral volume against program capacity. If volume consistently exceeds the program\u2019s ability to respond within target response times, capacity planning is initiated. This may involve adding outreach representatives, adjusting geographic assignments, or temporarily limiting new partner onboarding.

## 15.4 Referrals Completed

A referral is counted as completed when the referred patient has been contacted and has either accepted peer support (at least one completed interaction) or declined further contact. Referrals that are still in process\u2014contact attempted but not yet made, or patient in process of being connected\u2014are tracked separately as open referrals.

The referral completion rate is calculated as the percentage of referrals received in a reporting period that reach a completed status within sixty days. This metric is reported monthly and reviewed for trends. A completion rate below seventy percent triggers a review of referral processing procedures, patient contact barriers, and resource availability.

Referrals that are not completed are analyzed by reason: unable to reach patient, patient declined, patient ineligible, referral information insufficient, or other. This breakdown identifies systemic barriers. For example, a high rate of \u201cunable to reach patient\u201d may indicate that referring partners need to verify patient contact information before submitting referrals.

## 15.5 Average Response Time

Average response time measures the hours between referral receipt and the first successful contact with the patient. It is calculated as the average across all completed referrals in a reporting period. Response time is tracked in hours, not business days, to accurately reflect the program\u2019s commitment to rapid connection.

This metric is reported weekly to the Hospital Outreach Coordinator and monthly to the Director of Medical Partnerships. Weekly reporting enables immediate corrective action if response times are slipping. Monthly reporting identifies trends and the impact of process changes over time.

Response time distribution is as important as the average. A program can have a twenty-four-hour average but still have individual referrals that took three days. Performance reports include the percentage of referrals contacted within twenty-four hours and within forty-eight hours, the average response time, and the maximum response time for the period. The goal is to maintain a twenty-four-hour average with no individual referral exceeding forty-eight hours.

## 15.6 Patients Served

This metric counts the number of unique patients who have received at least one peer support contact through the program in the reporting period. A patient is counted once per reporting period regardless of how many contacts they had. Patients who received services in a previous period and continue to receive services are counted again in each subsequent period if contact occurred.

Patients served is reported by new versus continuing. New patients are those who had their first program contact during the reporting period. Continuing patients had their first contact in a prior period. The ratio of new to continuing patients indicates whether the program is bringing in new referrals while maintaining ongoing relationships.

Demographic breakdowns are reported when available and when patient consent allows: age range, amputation cause (trauma, vascular, cancer, infection, congenital), amputation level, geographic region, and referral source. These breakdowns help the program understand who it is reaching and who it may be missing.

## 15.7 Family Members Served

Amputation affects families. This metric tracks the number of family members who have received direct program services, including: family members who attended a peer support visit with the patient, family members who received a family guide or other educational resource directly, family members who participated in a family support group or caregiver program through Lost Limb Riders, and family members who contacted the program independently for support.

Family members are counted separately from patients in program reporting. Each family member is recorded with their relationship to the patient (spouse, adult child, parent, sibling, other) and the type of service provided. Identifying information for family members is protected with the same privacy standards applied to patients.

This metric is reported quarterly. The Director of Medical Partnerships reviews family engagement data to assess whether the program is adequately reaching caregivers. Low family engagement may indicate that outreach representatives are not consistently offering family resources or that family-specific programming needs to be expanded.

## 15.8 Volunteer Hours Logged

Volunteer hours are the lifeblood of Lost Limb Riders. This metric tracks the total hours contributed by program volunteers in a reporting period, disaggregated by activity type: in-hospital peer visits, community peer visits, administrative support, event participation, training attendance, and travel time.

Each volunteer logs their hours through the program database. Hours are recorded in fifteen-minute increments and are verified by the Regional Outreach Representative or Hospital Outreach Coordinator before being accepted into the record. Volunteers submit their hours weekly, and any hours not submitted within thirty days are not counted.

This metric is reported monthly. Volunteer hour trends indicate program engagement and sustainability. A decline in volunteer hours may signal volunteer burnout, scheduling conflicts, or dissatisfaction. The Director of Medical Partnerships reviews volunteer hour trends quarterly and follows up with volunteers who show a significant decline in participation.

## 15.9 Follow-Up Completion Rates

Follow-up is what transforms a single peer contact into a lasting support relationship. This metric tracks the percentage of patients who complete follow-up contacts at thirty, sixty, and ninety days after initial connection.

A follow-up is considered completed when the outreach representative or assigned peer mentor successfully contacts the patient and documents the interaction. The follow-up must occur within a window of five days before to five days after the target date to be counted as on time. Follow-ups completed outside that window are counted but flagged as late.

Completion rates are tracked separately for each follow-up interval. A patient who completes all three follow-ups is counted as a fully engaged participant. The program target is seventy-five percent completion at thirty days, sixty percent at sixty days, and fifty percent at ninety days. Patients who decline further follow-up are removed from the denominator rather than counted as incomplete.

Monthly reports show completion rates by interval, by region, and by outreach representative. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator reviews individual representative completion rates and provides coaching or support when rates fall below program targets.

## 15.10 Reporting Format and Frequency

Performance metrics are reported through a structured reporting system that ensures the right information reaches the right people at the right time. Reports are formatted consistently so that trends are visible and anomalies are obvious.

**Weekly Dashboard:** The Hospital Outreach Coordinator produces a weekly dashboard that includes referrals received, referrals completed, average response time, and open referral count. The dashboard is reviewed in a weekly fifteen-minute stand-up meeting with the Director of Medical Partnerships. Format: single-page PDF or spreadsheet with current week and prior four weeks for trend comparison.

**Monthly Report:** The Hospital Outreach Coordinator produces a monthly performance report that includes all fifteen metrics defined in this section. The report includes current month figures, prior month figures, year-to-date totals, and comparison to annual targets. Format: spreadsheet with accompanying narrative summary of notable trends, issues, and recommendations. The monthly report is submitted to the Director of Medical Partnerships by the fifth business day of the following month.

**Quarterly Report:** The Director of Medical Partnerships produces a quarterly performance report that includes all metrics with deeper analysis, trend identification over multiple quarters, and strategic recommendations. The quarterly report is presented to the Executive Director and the Board of Directors within thirty days of quarter end. Format: slide presentation or written report with data visualizations, narrative analysis, and action items.

**Annual Report:** The Director of Medical Partnerships produces an annual program evaluation that synthesizes performance data from all four quarters, compares outcomes to program goals, and sets targets for the coming year. The annual report is presented to the Board of Directors and distributed to healthcare partners. Format: written report with executive summary, data analysis, and strategic plan for the next year.

All reports are archived in the program database with version control. Historical data is retained for a minimum of seven years to enable longitudinal analysis and to demonstrate program impact over time.

# Section 16: Risk Management

## 16.1 Liability Considerations

Every outreach activity carries inherent risk. Lost Limb Riders manages that risk through a multilayered approach: participant and volunteer waivers, organizational insurance coverage, clear scope definitions, and consistent adherence to safety protocols. Risk management is not an administrative burden. It is what allows the program to operate safely, sustainably, and professionally in clinical environments.

All patients receiving peer support through the program must sign a Release of Liability and Assumption of Risk form before any visit or interaction occurs. This form clearly states that Lost Limb Riders provides peer support, not medical care, and that participation is voluntary. The form must be signed, dated, and filed in the participant\u2019s record before the first contact. For hospital visits, the facility\u2019s own waiver or volunteer acknowledgment may also be required. Outreach representatives must confirm with the facility which forms are needed before any patient interaction.

Lost Limb Riders maintains general liability insurance with minimum coverage of $1,000,000 per occurrence and $2,000,000 aggregate. Additional coverage may be required by specific healthcare partners. The Director of Medical Partnerships maintains current certificates of insurance and provides them to partners upon request. Each partnership agreement specifies insurance requirements, and no outreach activity may begin until the partner has confirmed acceptable coverage on file.

The scope of volunteer activities is clearly defined in the partnership agreement and in the outreach representative training. Volunteers do not provide medical advice, interpret clinical information, recommend treatments or providers, or assume any clinical responsibility. Volunteers are not employees of the healthcare facility and are not covered by the facility\u2019s professional liability insurance. All volunteers must acknowledge in writing that they understand these limitations before conducting any outreach.

## 16.2 Professional Boundaries

Professional boundaries protect everyone\u2014the patient, the volunteer, the healthcare partner, and the organization. Outreach representatives operate in clinical environments where patients are vulnerable, staff are busy, and the stakes are high. Boundary violations damage trust, endanger partnerships, and can cause real harm to patients.

The most critical boundary is the prohibition on medical advice. Outreach representatives may share their own lived experience with limb loss, but they may not recommend specific doctors, surgeons, prosthetists, medications, treatments, or rehabilitation protocols. They may not interpret physician instructions, explain surgical outcomes, or offer opinions about a patient\u2019s medical care. When a patient asks a medical question, the appropriate response is: \u201cI can\u2019t answer that, but I can help you write down your questions for your doctor.\u201d

Outreach representatives may not offer clinical opinions about a patient\u2019s condition, prognosis, recovery timeline, or prosthetic options. They may not speculate about outcomes or compare one patient\u2019s experience to another\u2019s in a way that could create false expectations. Every patient\u2019s medical journey is unique. The peer\u2019s role is to support, not to diagnose or predict.

Physical contact with patients must be minimal and appropriate. A handshake is acceptable. A brief hug initiated by the patient may be acceptable if it feels natural and consensual. Sustained physical contact, touch without permission, or any contact that could be perceived as intimate is prohibited. Outreach representatives must ask permission before touching a patient for any reason, including a reassuring hand on the shoulder. When in doubt, keep your hands to yourself.

Outreach representatives maintain appropriate emotional boundaries as well. They are peers, not therapists. They listen, share, and support, but they do not take on the emotional burden of a patient\u2019s trauma, do not promise outcomes they cannot deliver, and do not continue relationships beyond the program\u2019s scope. If a representative feels personally overwhelmed by a patient interaction, they must debrief with their supervisor and, if necessary, request reassignment.

## 16.3 Volunteer Safety

Outreach representatives enter healthcare facilities where they may encounter infectious disease exposure, agitated patients or family members, emotionally intense situations, and physically demanding environments. Their safety is the organization\u2019s responsibility. Every representative has the right to a safe work environment and the obligation to follow safety protocols.

The visit buddy system is mandatory for certain high-risk interactions. Any in-hospital visit to a patient with a known history of violence, aggression, or behavioral health crisis must be conducted by two outreach representatives. Home visits, if conducted, always require two representatives. Any visit scheduled outside normal business hours or in a remote or unfamiliar location requires buddy coverage. Representatives may request a buddy for any visit where they feel unsafe, without question and without needing to justify the request.

Check-in and check-out procedures apply to every visit. Before entering a patient room or facility, the outreach representative must notify the Hospital Outreach Coordinator or designated contact with their location, expected duration, and contact information. Upon completing the visit, the representative checks out with the same contact. If a representative does not check out within thirty minutes of their expected completion time, the coordinator initiates a check-in call. If the representative cannot be reached, the coordinator contacts the facility security office. This protocol applies to all visits, including hospital bedside visits, clinic visits, home visits, and any other in-person interaction.

Incident reporting for volunteer safety concerns follows the same process as any other incident. Any injury, exposure, threat, or safety concern must be reported within twenty-four hours. Needlestick injuries, blood or bodily fluid exposures, and physical altercations require immediate notification and appropriate medical follow-up. Representatives who experience a safety incident receive support from the program, including debriefing, medical referral, and time off if needed.

## 16.4 Hospital Compliance

Each healthcare facility has its own credentialing, vaccination, training, and access requirements. Outreach representatives must comply with every requirement of every facility they enter. Noncompliance is grounds for immediate suspension of facility access and may jeopardize the entire partnership.

Credentialing requirements typically include a criminal background check at the state and federal levels, drug screening, and proof of professional liability insurance if the representative is a licensed professional. Background checks must be renewed every two years or as required by each facility. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator maintains a credentialing file for each representative, including copies of all clearances, and tracks expiration dates. Credentialing must be completed before the representative makes any independent visit to the facility.

Vaccination requirements vary by facility but commonly include influenza (annual), hepatitis B series, MMR (measles, mumps, rubella), varicella (chickenpox), Tdap (tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis), and tuberculosis screening or chest X-ray. Some facilities require COVID-19 vaccination. Representatives must provide documentation of all required immunizations. Representatives who cannot receive vaccinations for medical or religious reasons must work with the Director of Medical Partnerships to determine whether accommodations are possible or whether assignment to that facility is feasible.

Facility-specific training may include HIPAA training, infection control orientation, fire safety, workplace violence prevention, and electronic medical record access training. Some facilities require online training modules that must be completed before badge issuance. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator tracks completion of all facility-specific training and ensures renewals are completed on time. Representatives whose credentials or training expire are immediately suspended from facility access until renewals are documented.

Badge or identification requirements must be followed at all times. Representatives wear their facility-issued badge visibly while in the facility. If a facility does not issue badges, the representative wears a Lost Limb Riders identification badge provided by the organization. Loss of a facility badge must be reported to both the facility security office and the Hospital Outreach Coordinator within twenty-four hours.

## 16.5 Documentation Standards

Documentation is the backbone of program accountability. Every outreach interaction must be documented completely, timely, and factually. Documentation serves multiple purposes: it creates a record of services provided, supports outcome measurement, protects the organization in the event of a dispute, and demonstrates professionalism to healthcare partners.

Complete documentation includes the date, time, and location of the interaction; the names and roles of all participants; the purpose of the interaction; a factual summary of what occurred; any resources or referrals provided; any follow-up actions needed; and the name of the person completing the documentation. No required field may be left blank. If information is not available, the documenter notes the reason.

Documentation must be completed within twenty-four hours of the interaction. Memory fades quickly, and incomplete or delayed documentation undermines credibility. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator reviews documentation for completeness and accuracy on a weekly basis. Representatives with consistently incomplete or late documentation receive corrective coaching. Chronic noncompliance may result in suspension from outreach activities.

Documentation must be factual and objective. Representatives document what they observed, heard, and did. They do not include opinions, assumptions, diagnoses, judgments, or speculation. For example, a representative documents: \u201cThe patient stated they were feeling frustrated with the recovery process,\u201d not \u201cThe patient was angry and difficult.\u201d Factual documentation protects the patient\u2019s dignity, the representative\u2019s professionalism, and the organization\u2019s legal position.

All documentation is stored in the program\u2019s secure database with access controls. Paper documentation, if used, must be stored in a locked file and transferred to digital storage within seventy-two hours. Documentation is retained for a minimum of seven years. Participants may request access to their own records, and such requests must be fulfilled within thirty days.

## 16.6 Incident Reporting

An incident is any event that deviates from normal program operations and results in or has the potential to result in harm, complaint, policy violation, or liability. Outreach representatives are trained to recognize reportable incidents and to understand that reporting is mandatory, not discretionary. Failure to report an incident is a serious policy violation that may result in removal from the program.

Reportable incidents include patient complaints about an outreach representative; partner complaints about program conduct; privacy or confidentiality breaches; injury or exposure to an outreach representative during a visit; patient safety concerns identified during outreach; facility policy violations; boundary violations by any program participant; behavioral incidents involving patients, family members, or staff; and any situation requiring facility security, law enforcement, or emergency medical services involvement. Near misses\u2014situations that could have resulted in harm but did not\u2014must also be reported so preventive measures can be implemented.

The incident report form captures the following information: date, time, and location of the incident; names and roles of all individuals involved; names and contact information of witnesses; a detailed factual description of what occurred, written as a chronological narrative; any injuries or damages; any emergency services response; actions taken during and immediately after the incident; notifications made and to whom; and follow-up actions required. The form must be signed and dated by the person completing it.

The reporting timeline is strict. The outreach representative must notify the Hospital Outreach Coordinator within twenty-four hours of the incident. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator completes the incident report within forty-eight hours and submits it to the Director of Medical Partnerships. The Director reviews the report within seventy-two hours and determines whether further investigation, corrective action, partner notification, or legal review is needed. Incidents involving imminent harm, criminal activity, or mandatory reporting obligations are escalated immediately, bypassing normal timelines.

The review process includes a root cause analysis for any incident involving injury, privacy breach, or significant partner impact. The Director of Medical Partnerships convenes a review within seven days. Corrective actions are assigned with responsible parties and deadlines. The outcome of the review is documented and filed. An annual incident review is conducted to identify trends, recurring issues, and preventive measures. Incident data is reported to the Executive Director and Board of Directors as part of the annual program evaluation.

# Section 17: Marketing and Professional Relations

## 17.1 Presentations to Hospitals

Presenting Lost Limb Riders to hospital audiences requires preparation, professionalism, and a clear understanding of what each audience needs to hear. A presentation to a hospital executive is different from a presentation to a floor nurse. The message must be tailored to the listener\u2019s role, concerns, and decision-making authority.

The target audience for hospital presentations includes case managers, social workers, discharge planners, nurse managers, medical directors, patient experience officers, trauma program managers, rehabilitation department heads, and C-suite executives when seeking partnership approval. Each presentation must identify the primary audience before scheduling and tailor content accordingly. A presentation to case managers focuses on referral procedures and patient eligibility. A presentation to executives focuses on outcomes data, liability protection, community benefit, and partnership structure.

Presentation content includes an overview of Lost Limb Riders and its mission; the Peer Connection Program model; the Hospital and Prosthetic Partner Outreach program structure; the referral process with step-by-step instructions; patient eligibility criteria; program limitations (what we do not provide); data on outcomes and patient satisfaction if available; liability and insurance coverage information; credentialing and compliance requirements; and a clear call to action for partnership. Presentations should last twenty to thirty minutes with ten to fifteen minutes for questions.

Presentations are scheduled through the hospital\u2019s designated point of contact. The outreach representative or Director of Medical Partnerships requests a slot on an existing meeting agenda rather than requesting a standalone meeting, which is more likely to be accepted. Appropriate meetings include case management staff meetings, social work team huddles, patient experience committee meetings, trauma performance improvement meetings, and nursing grand rounds. The representative should confirm the meeting format (in-person, virtual, or hybrid) and any audiovisual needs at least one week in advance.

Follow-up after a presentation is critical. Within forty-eight hours, the presenter sends a thank-you note to the meeting organizer and any attendees who expressed interest. The note includes a digital copy of the presentation, a referral form, and the presenter\u2019s contact information. Interested attendees are added to a follow-up list and contacted within two weeks to answer additional questions, schedule a one-on-one meeting, or begin the partnership agreement process.

## 17.2 Medical Conferences

Medical conferences offer a concentrated opportunity to reach dozens or hundreds of healthcare professionals in a single event. Lost Limb Riders exhibits at conferences focused on amputation care, trauma, rehabilitation, physical medicine, prosthetics, case management, and patient experience. Conference participation raises the organization\u2019s profile, generates referral relationships, and demonstrates the peer support model to a professional audience.

Booth setup must be professional, inviting, and informative. The standard booth configuration includes a banner or backdrop with the Lost Limb Riders logo and tagline; a table with printed materials (program overview, referral forms, partnership information, and business cards); a digital display showing a brief video or photo slideshow if power and space permit; and a giveaway item such as a pen, sticker, or card with contact information. The booth should be staffed at all times during exhibit hours. Materials must be neat, organized, and restocked throughout the day.

Staffing the booth requires two representatives at a minimum. At least one representative should have lived experience with limb loss and be comfortable sharing their personal story in a professional context. Representatives must be trained on the key messages, know the referral process, and be able to describe partnership opportunities clearly. Representatives should be approachable, engage passersby rather than waiting to be approached, and have a system for capturing contact information from interested attendees.

Materials distributed at conferences include the program overview brochure, a one-page referral process guide, partnership information sheets, business cards, and a conference-specific signup sheet for follow-up. Materials must be reviewed for accuracy before the conference and updated if any program information has changed. All materials should carry the Lost Limb Riders branding and contact information. The cost of conference participation, including registration, travel, materials, and shipping, is budgeted annually by the Director of Medical Partnerships.

Follow-up after a medical conference begins within one week. All contacts collected at the booth receive a personalized email referencing the conversation at the conference. Contacts who expressed interest in partnership are added to the partnership development pipeline and contacted within two weeks for a follow-up meeting. Contacts who expressed interest in referring patients receive the referral form and program overview. The Director of Medical Partnerships tracks conference-generated leads in the program database and reports conversion rates as part of the annual program evaluation.

## 17.3 Prosthetic Conferences

Prosthetic conferences are distinct from medical conferences. Attendees are prosthetists, orthotists, clinic owners, prosthetic technicians, and industry vendors. These professionals work with amputees every day and are often the first to recognize when a patient needs peer support. Building relationships with prosthetists is essential because they have continuous, long-term contact with patients throughout the lifespan of their prosthetic use.

Exhibitor opportunities at prosthetic conferences include tabletop exhibits, vendor showcase slots, and sponsored speaking sessions. Lost Limb Riders targets conferences hosted by the American Orthotic and Prosthetic Association, state prosthetic associations, and regional prosthetic study groups. The goal is not to sell a product but to establish the organization as a trusted community resource that prosthetists can confidently recommend to their patients.

Booth materials for prosthetic conferences emphasize the practical value of peer support to the prosthetic practice. Materials should address questions prosthetists commonly ask: How does peer support affect patient outcomes? Will it interfere with my clinical recommendations? How do I refer a patient? What happens after I refer? The booth should also feature materials specifically designed for clinic waiting rooms, including brochures and posters that patients can see while waiting for appointments.

Networking at prosthetic conferences goes beyond the booth. Outreach representatives attend educational sessions, participate in meals and social events, and seek one-on-one conversations with clinic owners and lead prosthetists. The objective is relationship building, not volume. A single strong relationship with a prosthetist at a conference can lead to dozens of patient referrals over the life of the partnership.

Demonstrations and presentations may be offered at prosthetic conferences when the opportunity arises. Topics include the impact of peer support on prosthetic outcomes, integrating peer support into prosthetic clinical workflows, and the role of peer mentors in the rehabilitation team. Presenters must submit proposals well in advance of the conference deadline and be prepared to deliver a professional, evidence-informed presentation.

## 17.4 Community Education

Community education extends the program\u2019s reach beyond clinical partners into the general public, support groups, and community organizations. These activities raise awareness about limb loss, reduce stigma, and connect individuals with Lost Limb Riders services who may not have been reached through hospital referrals.

Health fairs are a primary community education venue. Lost Limb Riders maintains a health fair kit containing a tabletop banner, printed materials, a sign-up sheet, and a small interactive element such as a prosthetic component display or a \u201cWhat Would You Do?\u201d discussion card. Health fair staffing requires one or two representatives who can engage attendees, share the organization\u2019s mission, and collect contact information from interested individuals. Health fair participation is coordinated by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator and scheduled based on community need and volunteer availability.

Support groups include amputee support groups, chronic illness support groups, caregiver support groups, and grief support groups. Representatives attend as invited guests, present on Lost Limb Riders services, and offer peer connection to group members. Support group presentations are shorter and more conversational than hospital presentations. They focus on personal stories, available resources, and how to access peer support. Representatives should be prepared to handle emotional responses and to provide resource information to group facilitators.

Public speaking engagements include Rotary clubs, Kiwanis, Lions clubs, chambers of commerce, faith communities, veteran service organizations, disability advocacy groups, and civic organizations. These audiences may not include amputees themselves but are often connected to people who need services. A fifteen-minute talk with slides and handouts is the standard format. The speaker must clearly explain what Lost Limb Riders does, what it does not do, and how audience members can help connect people with services.

All community education activities must be documented in the program database, including the date, location, audience type, estimated attendance, number of contacts collected, and any referrals generated. This data supports outcome reporting and helps the organization identify which community education activities yield the highest return on investment.

## 17.5 Professional Networking

Professional networking is an ongoing activity, not a campaign. Every interaction with a healthcare professional is an opportunity to strengthen the program\u2019s relationships, build credibility, and generate referrals. Outreach representatives are trained to identify and pursue networking opportunities in their daily activities.

Referring physician outreach targets the specific physicians who most commonly refer to the program: trauma surgeons, orthopedic surgeons, vascular surgeons, physiatrists, and primary care physicians who treat amputees. Outreach to physicians is conducted through their practice managers or clinical coordinators, not through cold calls to the physician directly. The representative requests a brief five-minute meeting to introduce the program, leave materials, and establish a connection. Follow-up with the practice manager includes a referral pad and contact information.

Relationship building with physician practices requires consistency and patience. A single contact is rarely sufficient. Representatives make quarterly check-in calls or visits to active referring practices, bring updated materials, ask about the practice\u2019s experience with referrals, and address any concerns. Practices that have not referred in six months receive a re-engagement call to determine whether there are barriers or changes in the practice that need to be addressed.

Professional networking also includes relationships with case managers, social workers, discharge planners, patient navigators, and rehabilitation therapists. These professionals are often the ones who identify the need for peer support and initiate the referral. Representatives should know these professionals by name, maintain regular contact, and express appreciation for their referrals. A handwritten thank-you note for a referral is a small gesture that builds significant goodwill.

All professional networking contacts and interactions are documented in the program database with the date, contact person, outcome, and follow-up needed. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator reviews networking activity monthly to ensure consistent outreach across all partner types and to identify gaps in relationship coverage.

## 17.6 Annual Partner Appreciation Events

Each year, Lost Limb Riders hosts a partner appreciation event to recognize the healthcare organizations and professionals who make the Hospital and Prosthetic Partner Outreach program possible. The event is an investment in relationship maintenance and demonstrates that the organization values its partners beyond the referral numbers they generate.

The format of the appreciation event may vary by chapter and region. Options include an evening reception, a luncheon, a breakfast meeting, a barbecue or picnic, or a casual gathering at a location affiliated with Lost Limb Riders such as a Ride Forward event. The event should include recognition of partner contributions, a brief program update from the Director of Medical Partnerships, testimonials from patients or families who have benefited from the partnership, and time for informal networking. The tone should be celebratory and appreciative, not transactional.

Recognition is a key component of the event. Partners may be recognized for total referrals, longest partnership duration, most engaged referrals, or exceptional collaboration in a specific case. Awards should be meaningful and presented with sincerity. Recognition categories might include Partner of the Year, Most Referrals, Outstanding Collaboration, Community Impact, and New Partner Achievement. Each award includes a certificate or plaque and public acknowledgment.

The event also serves as an opportunity to report outcomes to partners. The Director of Medical Partnerships presents key program metrics for the year: number of referrals received, number of patients connected, referral-to-connection conversion rate, patient satisfaction data, and program outcomes. This reporting shows partners the tangible impact of their collaboration and reinforces the value of the partnership.

Planning for the appreciation event begins at least three months in advance. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator manages the logistics: date selection (avoiding conflicts with major medical conferences and holidays), venue booking, invitation list, catering, audiovisual needs, awards preparation, and guest speaking coordination. Invitations are sent six weeks in advance, with a follow-up reminder two weeks before the event. Attendance is tracked, and materials from the event are shared with partners who could not attend.

# Section 18: Forms and Documentation

## 18.1 Partnership Application

The partnership application is the formal document through which a healthcare organization expresses interest in partnering with Lost Limb Riders. The application collects the information needed to evaluate the prospective partner and begin the partnership agreement process.

The application includes the following sections: organization information (name, type, address, website, tax identification number); primary contact information (name, title, phone, email, preferred contact method); organization type (hospital, trauma center, rehabilitation hospital, prosthetic clinic, PT/OT provider, home health agency, skilled nursing facility, VA facility, community health organization, or other); services provided (list of relevant clinical services, patient populations served, estimated annual amputee patient volume); partnership goals (what the organization hopes to achieve through the partnership, specific patient populations they want to connect); referral capacity (estimated number of patients they expect to refer annually, referral process preferences); facility compliance information (credentialing requirements, vaccination requirements, background check process, facility orientation requirements); and authorized signatory information (name and title of person authorized to sign the partnership agreement).

The application is submitted electronically to the Director of Medical Partnerships. The Director reviews the application within seven business days and determines whether the prospective partner aligns with program goals and capacity. If approved, the Director initiates the partnership agreement process. If declined, the Director provides a written explanation and, where appropriate, suggests alternative ways to collaborate. The application is retained in the partner records file regardless of outcome.

## 18.2 MOU Template

The Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) is the formal agreement that governs the relationship between Lost Limb Riders and each healthcare partner. Every MOU must include the following required clauses, reviewed by legal counsel before execution:

Parties and purpose: Names and addresses of both organizations, effective date, and a statement of purpose describing the mutual goals of the partnership. Scope of services: A description of the services Lost Limb Riders will provide and a description of what the partner organization will provide (referrals, facility access, credentialing support, etc.). Referral procedures: The process by which the partner refers patients, including required information, consent requirements, and expected response times. Privacy and confidentiality: A HIPAA-aligned confidentiality clause specifying that patient information will be used only for program purposes and will not be shared without consent except as required by law. Credentialing and compliance: The partner\u2019s credentialing, vaccination, and training requirements and the organization\u2019s obligation to ensure compliance. Insurance: Minimum insurance coverage requirements for Lost Limb Riders, including requirement to provide certificates of insurance upon request. Term and renewal: Effective date, initial term (typically one year), and renewal provisions. Termination: Grounds for termination, notice period (typically thirty days), and procedures for transitioning patients upon termination. Liability: A mutual hold harmless clause (subject to state law and insurance requirements) and a statement that neither party assumes the professional liability of the other. Indemnification: Mutual indemnification provisions as negotiated and approved by legal counsel. Independent relationship: A statement that Lost Limb Riders volunteers and staff are not employees or agents of the healthcare partner. Signatures: Signature blocks for authorized representatives of both organizations, including name, title, date, and notarization if required.

Signature authority for Lost Limb Riders rests with the Executive Director or a Board-designated representative. No other person may sign an MOU. The signed original is retained in the partner records file, and a copy is provided to the partner. MOUs are reviewed annually and renewed or amended as needed.

## 18.3 Referral Form

The referral form is the document used by healthcare partners to refer a patient for peer support. The form may be physical (paper) or electronic, depending on the partner\u2019s preference and the program\u2019s capabilities. A standardized referral form ensures that all necessary information is collected consistently every time a referral is made.

The referral form includes the following fields: referring organization and department; referring staff name, title, phone, and email; patient name and preferred name; patient phone number and email address; preferred contact method and best times to contact; patient date of birth (for verification purposes only; not stored unless required); patient consent confirmed (checkbox with date and staff initials); amputation type (above knee, below knee, upper extremity, multiple, congenital, preparing for surgery); amputation date or scheduled surgery date; referral reason (recent amputation, adjustment difficulty, request for peer support, family support, resource needs, other); urgency level (routine, urgent\u2014contact within 24 hours, immediate\u2014contact today); special considerations (language needs, accessibility needs, cultural considerations, mental health concerns); and additional notes or context.

The referral form includes a clear privacy notice stating how patient information will be used, that it will not be shared outside Lost Limb Riders without consent, and that the patient has the right to decline services at any time. The referring staff member must confirm that the patient has consented to the referral before submitting the form.

Referral forms are submitted through the partner\u2019s agreed-upon channel: secure email, encrypted portal, fax, or in-person handoff. Upon receipt, the Hospital Outreach Coordinator sends an acknowledgment of receipt to the referring party within twenty-four hours. The acknowledgment confirms that the referral has been received and is being processed but does not include any patient health information.

## 18.4 Intake Form

The intake form is completed with the referred patient during the initial contact. It collects comprehensive information needed to match the patient with appropriate peer support and to establish a participant record in the program database.

The intake form captures the following categories: personal information (name, address, phone, email, date of birth, emergency contact name and phone); demographic information (optional but encouraged for grant reporting: race, ethnicity, gender, veteran status); amputation information (date, cause, level, type, surgical site, complications, upcoming surgeries); rehabilitation status (current treatment providers, therapy schedule, prosthetic status); psychosocial assessment (self-reported mood, coping, support system, isolation risk, previous mental health history, current mental health treatment); resource needs (housing, transportation, employment, financial, prosthetic funding, insurance navigation, family support, adaptive recreation); communication preferences (phone, text, email, in-person, video call; best days and times; language preference, interpreter needs); goals for participation (what the patient hopes to gain from peer support, specific concerns or questions); and consent documentation (signed consent form, HIPAA acknowledgment, release of liability, photo release if applicable).

The intake form also includes a risk screening section with questions to identify patients who may need immediate mental health support: thoughts of self-harm, current crisis, recent loss or trauma, substance use concerns, and domestic safety. Responses to these questions are flagged in the database and trigger an escalation protocol if needed.

The intake is conducted by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator or a trained intake specialist. It may be completed by phone, video call, or in person. The patient must be informed of the purpose of the intake, how their information will be used, their rights regarding their information, and the voluntary nature of participation before any information is collected. The completed intake form is stored securely in the participant database.

## 18.5 Consent Form

The consent form is the document that authorizes Lost Limb Riders to provide peer support services and to use the participant\u2019s information for program purposes. The form must be HIPAA-compliant, written in plain language, and signed by the participant before any services are provided. If the participant is a minor or lacks capacity to consent, the form must be signed by a parent, guardian, or authorized representative.

The consent form includes the following elements: description of services (what Lost Limb Riders provides and what it does not provide); acknowledgment of program limitations (peer support is not medical care, not therapy, not crisis intervention); authorization to use and disclose information (specifically what information may be used, for what purposes, and to whom it may be disclosed); expiration of consent (the participant may revoke consent at any time by written request; otherwise, consent remains valid for the duration of program participation); right to revoke (clear instructions on how to revoke consent and what the effect of revocation will be); re-disclosure notice (a statement that information shared with Lost Limb Riders may be subject to re-disclosure by Lost Limb Riders and may no longer be protected by HIPAA if disclosed to non-covered entities); complaint procedures (how to file a complaint about privacy practices with Lost Limb Riders and with the Department of Health and Human Services); and signatures (participant name, signature, and date; witness name, signature, and date; if applicable, parent/guardian name, signature, and relationship).

The consent form must be reviewed with the participant verbally before signing. The reviewer must confirm that the participant understands the form and has had their questions answered. A copy of the signed form is provided to the participant. The original is filed in the participant record. Consent is reviewed annually and renewed if the participant remains enrolled.

## 18.6 Visit Log

The visit log is the daily record of every outreach interaction conducted by a representative. It serves as the program\u2019s primary documentation tool for tracking activity volume, service delivery, and partner engagement. Each representative maintains a current visit log and submits it weekly to the Hospital Outreach Coordinator.

The visit log captures the following for each interaction: date, start time, and end time; type of interaction (hospital bedside visit, clinic visit, home visit, phone call, video call, email, partner meeting, presentation, event, other); location or facility name; staff contact name (for partner interactions) or patient identifier (for patient interactions); purpose of the interaction; summary of what occurred (factual, objective, no opinions); resources provided or referrals made; follow-up actions needed and by whom; and any concerns or observations requiring escalation.

The visit log must be completed within twenty-four hours of each interaction. Representatives may not batch log entries at the end of the week. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator reviews visit logs weekly for completeness, accuracy, and compliance with documentation standards. Incomplete or late logs are returned to the representative for correction. Chronic noncompliance is addressed through the corrective action process.

Visit log data is aggregated monthly to produce activity reports showing the number and types of interactions, facility distribution, time spent per interaction, and referral patterns. These reports support program evaluation and outcome reporting.

## 18.7 Follow-Up Checklist

The follow-up checklist ensures that every referred patient receives consistent, timely follow-up at thirty, sixty, and ninety days after initial contact. The checklist standardizes the follow-up process so that no patient is overlooked and that all required actions are completed.

The thirty-day follow-up checklist includes: confirm that initial peer connection was made; assess patient satisfaction with initial connection; verify that patient has received resource information; check whether patient has attended any referred appointments; screen for emerging crisis indicators (mood, isolation, self-harm thoughts); update contact information if changed; document patient\u2019s self-reported progress or challenges; confirm consent still valid; schedule next contact or confirm ongoing mentorship plan; and update participant record with all findings.

The sixty-day follow-up checklist includes: assess ongoing peer relationship quality; verify minimum monthly contact compliance; check resource referral follow-through; screen for crisis indicators again; document any changes in patient status (return to work, new prosthetic, complications, relocation); update resource needs assessment; and update participant record.

The ninety-day follow-up checklist includes: complete follow-up satisfaction survey; assess progress toward goals identified at intake; determine whether continued enrollment is appropriate or whether case closure should be considered; update all participant record fields; document any recommendations for continued support or transition; and schedule next review if enrollment continues.

Each checklist item must be completed and documented before the follow-up is considered complete. If a patient cannot be reached after three documented attempts across different days and times, the outreach representative documents the attempts and marks the follow-up as incomplete with a note to re-attempt at the next interval. The Hospital Outreach Coordinator reviews all follow-up checklists for completeness.

## 18.8 Incident Report

The incident report form is used to document any reportable incident as defined in Section 16.6. The form is structured to capture all information needed for investigation, corrective action, and legal review.

The incident report includes the following sections: incident identification (unique incident number assigned by the database, date report completed, person completing the report); incident details (date, time, and location of incident; type of incident from the defined categories); people involved (names, roles, contact information, relationship to program); witnesses (names, roles, contact information); factual narrative (chronological description of what occurred, written objectively and factually with no opinions or assumptions; what was observed, heard, and done); injuries or damages (description of any physical injury, property damage, or other harm); notifications (who was notified, when, and how; include facility security, partner contact, emergency services, family members, and organizational leadership as applicable); actions taken (immediate response actions, steps taken to secure safety, evidence preservation, communication with involved parties); follow-up actions needed (investigative steps, corrective actions, partner communication, policy review); and confidentiality notice (the report is confidential, shared only with authorized program personnel, legal counsel, and insurance representatives as needed).

The completed incident report is submitted to the Director of Medical Partnerships within forty-eight hours. The Director reviews the report, initiates any needed investigation, and determines next steps. The report is retained in a secure incident file separate from participant and partner records. Incident reports are reviewed annually to identify trends and preventive measures.

## 18.9 Partner Evaluation

The partner evaluation is a structured assessment of each healthcare partner\u2019s engagement, satisfaction, and program alignment. Evaluations are conducted annually to identify strong partnerships to celebrate and struggling partnerships to support or restructure.

Evaluation criteria include: referral volume (total referrals submitted during the evaluation period, trend compared to prior period); referral quality (completeness of referral forms, accuracy of patient consent documentation, appropriateness of referrals); responsiveness (timely responses to program communications, participation in scheduled meetings, cooperation with credentialing and compliance); staff engagement (participation in training, use of program materials, advocacy for the program within the organization); patient satisfaction (feedback from patients referred by this partner, if available and with consent); partnership compliance (adherence to MOU terms, compliance with program policies, timely renewal); and overall partnership health (subjective assessment by assigned outreach representative based on relationship quality, communication ease, and collaborative spirit).

Each criterion is scored on a scale of one to five. The scores are averaged to produce an overall partner health score. Partners scoring four or above are recognized at the annual appreciation event. Partners scoring below three are flagged for intervention, including a meeting with the Director of Medical Partnerships to identify barriers and develop an improvement plan. Partners consistently scoring below three for two consecutive years may be considered for partnership termination.

The partner evaluation is completed by the assigned outreach representative and reviewed by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator and Director of Medical Partnerships. The results are shared with the partner in a constructive conversation focused on partnership improvement, not criticism.

## 18.10 Patient Satisfaction Survey

The patient satisfaction survey measures the quality and impact of peer support services from the patient\u2019s perspective. The survey is administered at ninety days after initial contact and at case closure. Results are used to improve program quality and to demonstrate outcomes to healthcare partners and funders.

The survey includes the following questions: How satisfied are you with the peer support you received? (five-point scale from very satisfied to very dissatisfied); Did your peer mentor understand what you were going through? (yes/no/not sure); Did you feel respected and listened to during your interactions? (yes/no/not sure); Did you receive information about resources or services that were helpful? (yes/no/not sure); Has peer support helped you feel less alone in your experience? (yes/no/not sure); How likely are you to recommend Lost Limb Riders to another amputee? (five-point scale from very likely to very unlikely); What was the most helpful part of your experience? (open response); What could we do better? (open response); Is there anything else you would like to share? (open response).

The survey is anonymous. No identifying information is collected with the responses. Surveys may be administered by phone, online form, or paper, depending on the patient\u2019s preference and accessibility needs. If administered by phone, the caller must be someone other than the patient\u2019s assigned peer mentor to avoid response bias.

Survey results are compiled quarterly and reported to the Director of Medical Partnerships. Aggregate results are shared with healthcare partners as part of outcome reporting. Individual responses are used for program improvement and mentor coaching.

## 18.11 Annual Review Forms

Annual review forms serve two distinct purposes: partner self-assessment and program assessment. Both are completed annually as part of the program evaluation cycle.

The partner self-assessment form is completed by each healthcare partner to provide their perspective on the partnership. The form includes: overall satisfaction with the partnership (five-point scale); quality of communication from Lost Limb Riders; ease of referral process; usefulness of program materials; impact of peer support on their patients; suggestions for improvement; and interest in expanding the partnership. The form is distributed by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator six weeks before the annual review meeting and collected within three weeks.

The program assessment form is completed by the Director of Medical Partnerships to evaluate the program as a whole. The form includes: referral data analysis (volume, sources, conversion rates, trends); partner health scores (average scores, number of partners in each tier); patient satisfaction results; outreach activity summary (visits, presentations, events, conference participation); budget performance (actual versus budgeted expenditures); staffing and volunteer capacity; training compliance rates; incident summary; goal achievement against prior year goals; and strategic priorities for the coming year.

Both forms are reviewed during the annual program evaluation meeting attended by the Director of Medical Partnerships, Hospital Outreach Coordinator, and Executive Director. The findings inform the next year\u2019s program plan, budget, and strategic priorities.

# Section 19: Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)

## 19.1 SOP: Receiving a Referral through Case Closure

**Purpose:** To ensure every patient referral is received, processed, connected, and followed through to case closure in a consistent, timely, and documented manner.

**Scope:** This SOP applies to all referrals received from healthcare partners, community organizations, self-referrals, and any other referral source. It covers the entire lifecycle from receipt to case closure.

**Prerequisites:** Referral form received with patient consent confirmed; active partnership agreement with referring organization (if applicable); Hospital Outreach Coordinator or designee available to process referral; patient database accessible.

**Steps:**

1. Receive referral through established channel (email, phone, portal, fax, in-person). Log receipt immediately in the referral tracking database with timestamp.
2. Assign a unique referral identification number and enter into the participant database.
3. Verify patient consent is documented. If consent is missing or unclear, contact the referring party within four hours to obtain confirmation.
4. Review referral information for completeness. If critical information is missing (contact information, amputation type, urgency level), contact the referring party to obtain it.
5. Assess urgency level. For urgent referrals (contact within twenty-four hours), proceed immediately to Step 6. For routine referrals, proceed within the next business day.
6. Assign an outreach representative or peer mentor based on geographic location, availability, language match, and patient needs.
7. Initiate patient contact within seventy-two hours of referral receipt using the patient\u2019s preferred contact method. Make a minimum of three attempts across different days and times before marking as unreachable.
8. If contact is made: complete intake process per SOP 19.8. Obtain signed consent form. Match patient with peer mentor per matching criteria.
9. If contact is not made after three attempts: document attempts in the database. Notify the referring party that the patient could not be reached. Close the referral as \u201cunable to contact\u201d and file.
10. Once the patient is connected with a peer mentor, mark the referral as \u201cconverted\u201d and notify the referring party with a general acknowledgment (no patient health information).
11. Monitor the case through thirty-, sixty-, and ninety-day follow-ups per SOP 19.3. If the patient requests case closure, becomes unreachable for ninety consecutive days, or completes their goals, initiate case closure.
12. Complete case closure documentation: final progress note, closure summary, reason for closure, patient satisfaction survey (if appropriate). Mark case as closed in the database.
13. File all documentation in the participant record. Retention period begins from the case closure date.

**Documentation Required:** Referral form, intake form, signed consent form, contact log, progress notes, follow-up checklists, case closure summary, patient satisfaction survey (if completed).

**Completion Criteria:** Referral is closed in the database with a documented outcome (connected and active, connected and closed, unable to contact, declined services, referred elsewhere).

## 19.2 SOP: Conducting a Hospital Bedside Visit

**Purpose:** To provide in-hospital peer support to new amputees in a manner that is safe, respectful, compliant with facility policies, and supportive of the patient\u2019s emotional and practical needs.

**Scope:** This SOP applies to all in-hospital peer visits conducted by outreach representatives at partner healthcare facilities. It covers visit preparation, execution, and documentation.

**Prerequisites:** Active partnership agreement with the facility; outreach representative credentialed and compliant with facility requirements; referral received and patient consent confirmed; visit scheduled with facility point of contact; patient confirmed as willing to receive visitor.

**Steps:**

1. Confirm visit details twenty-four hours in advance: patient name, room number, facility unit, scheduled time, facility point of contact, and any access instructions or visitor restrictions.
2. Gather materials: Lost Limb Riders identification badge, facility-issued badge (if applicable), program brochure, resource list, consent form (if not already signed), business card, and a small comfort item (stress ball, pen, or card).
3. Check in at facility reception or nursing station upon arrival. Present identification. Confirm patient\u2019s willingness to receive a visitor. Receive any facility-specific instructions.
4. Approach the patient room. Knock or announce yourself. Wait for permission to enter. Introduce yourself by first name and as a representative of Lost Limb Riders.
5. Begin the conversation by asking the patient how they are doing. Let the patient set the pace. Share your connection to Lost Limb Riders and, if applicable, your personal experience with limb loss. Keep the focus on the patient\u2019s experience.
6. Listen more than you talk. Acknowledge the patient\u2019s emotions without minimizing them. Offer encouragement without false reassurance. Do not make promises about outcomes or recovery timelines.
7. Provide information about Lost Limb Riders services and how the patient can access peer support after discharge. Leave a brochure and your contact information.
8. Ask if the patient has any questions or concerns. Answer within your scope. If a question is outside your scope, say so and offer to help the patient find the answer through appropriate channels.
9. Before leaving, confirm next steps: whether the patient wants follow-up contact, preferred contact method, and any immediate resource needs. If the patient consents, initiate the referral process.
10. Thank the patient for their time. Thank the nursing staff on your way out.
11. Complete the visit log within twenty-four hours. Include the date, duration, location, patient interaction summary, any resources provided, any follow-up needed, and any concerns requiring escalation.
12. If the patient declined services or follow-up, document this clearly. Do not pursue further contact unless the patient initiates it.

**Documentation Required:** Visit log entry, consent form (if signed during visit), referral form (if initiated), resource tracking if materials were provided.

**Completion Criteria:** Visit log completed and submitted; any new referral or consent documented in the database; follow-up actions assigned if applicable.

## 19.3 SOP: Completing a Thirty-Day Follow-Up

**Purpose:** To ensure that every patient who has been connected with peer support receives a structured follow-up at thirty days to assess progress, identify emerging needs, and maintain engagement.

**Scope:** This SOP applies to all patients who have been connected with a peer mentor and are in active enrollment. The thirty-day follow-up is counted from the date of the patient\u2019s first peer contact.

**Prerequisites:** Patient is in active enrollment with a documented first contact date; assigned peer mentor or outreach representative has been identified; patient\u2019s preferred contact method is on file; follow-up checklist is accessible.

**Steps:**

1. Locate the patient record and confirm the first contact date. Calculate the thirty-day follow-up due date. Schedule the call at least three days before the due date.
2. Review the patient\u2019s intake form, initial contact log, and any progress notes to refresh context before the call.
3. Contact the patient using their preferred method at their preferred time. If the patient does not answer, leave a voicemail or send a text message and note the attempt. Make three attempts over five business days before marking as unreachable.
4. When connected, begin by asking how the patient is doing. Use open-ended questions to encourage conversation. Refer to the follow-up checklist and address each item.
5. Assess the quality of the peer relationship. Ask whether the patient feels the match is working. If the patient expresses dissatisfaction, offer to discuss reassignment with the coordinator.
6. Assess resource needs. Ask whether the patient has followed up on any referrals provided. Identify any new needs that have emerged. Provide additional resources as appropriate.
7. Screen for crisis indicators. Ask about mood, sleep, social connection, and coping. If the patient endorses thoughts of self-harm, suicidal ideation, or other crisis indicators, follow the crisis escalation SOP immediately.
8. Update the participant record with all information gathered during the follow-up. Complete each item on the thirty-day follow-up checklist. Note any follow-up actions required.
9. If the patient confirms ongoing interest: schedule the next contact (sixty-day follow-up), remind them of their peer mentor\u2019s availability, and encourage continued engagement.
10. If the patient expresses desire to end services: respect their decision. Complete case closure documentation. Notify the peer mentor. File the record.

**Documentation Required:** Thirty-day follow-up checklist, progress note, updated participant record, updated contact log.

**Completion Criteria:** All checklist items are addressed and documented; participant record is updated; next follow-up interval (sixty-day) is scheduled or case closure is initiated.

## 19.4 SOP: Onboarding a New Hospital Partner

**Purpose:** To establish a new hospital partnership in a structured, consistent manner that ensures compliance, clear communication, and a strong foundation for the relationship.

**Scope:** This SOP applies to the onboarding of any new hospital, trauma center, or rehabilitation facility partner. It begins after the signed MOU is executed and covers all steps to operationalize the partnership.

**Prerequisites:** Signed MOU on file; insurance certificate provided to partner; Director of Medical Partnerships has approved the partnership; partner\u2019s credentialing requirements documented.

**Steps:**

1. Assign a point of contact at Lost Limb Riders and confirm the partner\u2019s point of contact. Enter both into the partnership database.
2. Determine the partner\u2019s credentialing requirements for outreach representatives (background check, vaccinations, drug screening, training modules, badge process). Document each requirement in the partner\u2019s file.
3. Submit credentialing documentation for each outreach representative who will visit the facility. Track submission dates and follow up weekly until all clearances are received.
4. Complete any facility-specific training or orientation required. Retain completion certificates in the credentialing file.
5. Schedule a joint training session for the partner\u2019s referral staff. Cover: the peer support model, who is eligible for referral, how to complete the referral form, how to document patient consent, what information is required, what happens after a referral is submitted, and program limitations.
6. Deliver referral materials to the partner: referral forms (paper and electronic template), program brochures for patient distribution, a referral process one-pager for staff reference, and business cards for the outreach representative.
7. Establish communication protocols: preferred contact method for referrals (email, phone, portal), expected response times, escalation contacts for urgent issues, and scheduling process for in-hospital visits.
8. Conduct a kickoff meeting with the partner\u2019s key stakeholders (department heads that will use the referral process) to review the partnership, answer questions, and build buy-in.
9. Place a follow-up call to the partner two weeks after the kickoff to address any issues, answer questions, and assess early experience with the referral process.
10. Schedule the first quarterly check-in meeting. Set the date and agenda at least four weeks in advance. Enter the check-in schedule into the program calendar.

**Documentation Required:** Signed MOU, credentialing records for each representative, training completion records, partner communication log, kickoff meeting notes.

**Completion Criteria:** At least one outreach representative is fully credentialed and cleared for facility access; referral training has been delivered; referral materials have been delivered; kickoff meeting has been conducted; first quarterly check-in is scheduled.

## 19.5 SOP: Reporting and Resolving an Incident

**Purpose:** To ensure every incident is reported promptly, documented completely, investigated thoroughly, and resolved with appropriate corrective action.

**Scope:** This SOP applies to all reportable incidents as defined in Section 16.6, including patient complaints, partner complaints, privacy breaches, injuries, safety concerns, facility policy violations, and near misses.

**Prerequisites:** Incident has occurred or been identified; person reporting the incident has basic information available; Hospital Outreach Coordinator or Director of Medical Partnerships is reachable.

**Steps:**

1. Ensure immediate safety. If there is an ongoing threat to anyone\u2019s safety, call 911 or facility security first. Provide first aid if trained and safe to do so. Do not leave the scene until the situation is stabilized.
2. Notify the Hospital Outreach Coordinator within twenty-four hours of the incident. For incidents involving imminent harm, criminal activity, or mandatory reporting obligations, notify immediately by phone regardless of time of day.
3. Secure any evidence related to the incident: documents, emails, voicemails, photographs, video footage, or physical items. Do not alter or destroy any potential evidence.
4. Complete the incident report form. Write a factual chronological narrative. Include only what you observed, heard, and did. Do not include opinions, assumptions, or judgments. Identify all involved parties and witnesses.
5. Submit the completed incident report to the Director of Medical Partnerships within forty-eight hours.
6. The Director reviews the report within seventy-two hours and determines the level of response: no further action needed; corrective action with the individual involved; partner notification and apology; formal investigation; or legal counsel notification.
7. If a formal investigation is initiated: the Director appoints an investigator (may be internal or external depending on severity), interviews all involved parties and witnesses within seven days, reviews all evidence, and produces an investigation report with findings and recommendations.
8. Implement corrective actions. Assign responsibility and a deadline for each action. Document completion in the incident file.
9. If the incident involved a healthcare partner, notify the partner\u2019s point of contact with an appropriate level of detail. Provide assurance that the matter has been addressed. If the incident involved a patient complaint, inform the patient of the outcome where appropriate.
10. File the complete incident record, including the initial report, investigation findings, corrective action documentation, and communication records, in the secure incident file.

**Documentation Required:** Completed incident report form, investigation report (if applicable), corrective action documentation, communication records, evidence inventory.

**Completion Criteria:** Incident is documented, investigated, resolved, and filed; corrective actions are completed or scheduled with responsible parties; partner is notified (if applicable); lessons learned are documented for preventive measures.

## 19.6 SOP: Escalating a Mental Health Crisis

**Purpose:** To ensure that patients experiencing a mental health crisis receive immediate, appropriate intervention from qualified professionals while maintaining the safety of everyone involved.

**Scope:** This SOP applies when an outreach representative or peer mentor identifies that a patient is experiencing a mental health crisis, including suicidal ideation, homicidal ideation, self-harm, acute psychosis, severe emotional dysregulation, or imminent danger to self or others.

**Prerequisites:** Crisis indicators have been identified during a program interaction; the representative has received crisis recognition training; emergency contact information for the patient and local crisis resources are available.

**Steps:**

1. Stay calm and stay with the person if it is safe to do so. Do not leave the person alone if they are at immediate risk of self-harm.
2. Ask directly about suicidal or homicidal thoughts. Do not be afraid to ask. Research shows that asking does not increase risk. Use clear language: \u201cAre you thinking about killing yourself?\u201d
3. If the patient answers yes or indicates imminent risk: call 911 immediately or instruct the patient to go to the nearest emergency room if safe and appropriate. Do not leave the patient alone until emergency services arrive.
4. If the patient answers no or indicates passive ideation without plan or intent: connect them with the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988). Offer to stay on the call with them while they connect.
5. If the patient is unable or unwilling to engage with crisis resources and you believe they are at risk: call 911. Provide the dispatcher with the patient\u2019s location and the nature of the concern. Err on the side of caution.
6. Notify the Hospital Outreach Coordinator or Director of Medical Partnerships immediately by phone after ensuring the patient is safe. Do not wait until the situation is fully resolved to make this call.
7. Complete a written record of the interaction, including the crisis indicators observed, questions asked, responses given, actions taken, and resources connected. Do not document clinical opinions. Document facts and actions.
8. The Director of Medical Partnerships follows up within forty-eight hours to confirm the patient\u2019s safety, offer support to the outreach representative, and determine whether any additional program actions are needed (such as temporarily suspending outreach to allow the patient to stabilize).
9. The outreach representative participates in a debriefing session with the Director or a qualified supervisor within one week of the incident. The purpose is to process the experience and identify any additional support or training needed.

**Documentation Required:** Crisis escalation record, written summary of interaction, notification log, debriefing record.

**Completion Criteria:** Patient is connected with appropriate crisis resources; representative has debriefed; documentation is complete; any needed follow-up with the patient is scheduled or case is placed on hold pending stabilization.

## 19.7 SOP: Conducting a Partner Annual Review

**Purpose:** To assess the health and effectiveness of each healthcare partnership annually and to identify opportunities for improvement, expansion, or restructuring.

**Scope:** This SOP applies to every active healthcare partner with a signed MOU. Reviews are conducted annually within sixty days of the partnership anniversary date.

**Prerequisites:** Partnership has been active for at least twelve months; partner evaluation form is complete; partner self-assessment has been received (if submitted); referral data for the review period is compiled.

**Steps:**

1. Sixty days before the partnership anniversary date: gather all data for the review period, including referral volume, referral quality scores, outreach activity logs, partner communication records, incident reports involving the partner (if any), and patient satisfaction data for patients referred by this partner (if available).
2. Thirty days before the anniversary date: distribute the partner self-assessment form to the partner\u2019s designated point of contact. Set a submission deadline of fourteen days before the review meeting.
3. Complete the partner evaluation form using the criteria in Section 18.9. Score each criterion and calculate the overall partner health score.
4. Schedule the annual review meeting with the partner\u2019s point of contact. Offer in-person, video, or phone format. Confirm the meeting date at least two weeks in advance. Provide a brief agenda in advance.
5. Conduct the review meeting. Discuss: referral activity and trends, program impact on the partner\u2019s patients, any challenges or barriers identified, the partner\u2019s feedback from the self-assessment, opportunities for growth or expansion, and any changes to the partnership agreement needed.
6. Document the meeting outcomes in a partnership review summary. Include: key discussion points, agreed-upon action items and deadlines, any changes to the partnership agreement or referral process, and the partnership health score.
7. If the partner health score is below three: develop a partnership improvement plan with specific goals, timelines, and support from Lost Limb Riders. Schedule a sixty-day check-in meeting to assess progress.
8. If the partner health score is four or above: document recognition for the annual appreciation event. Identify the partner as a candidate for program testimonials, case studies, or expanded collaboration.
9. File the complete review documentation in the partner records file. Update any partnership agreement terms if changes were agreed upon.
10. Submit a summary of all completed partner annual reviews to the Director of Medical Partnerships for inclusion in the annual program evaluation.

**Documentation Required:** Partner evaluation form, partner self-assessment, referral data report, partnership review summary, partnership improvement plan (if applicable).

**Completion Criteria:** Review meeting is completed; partnership review summary is documented and filed; partner health score is recorded; action items are assigned; improvement plan is implemented if needed.

## 19.8 SOP: Processing a Patient Intake and Assignment

**Purpose:** To ensure every referred patient completes a thorough intake process and is assigned to an appropriate peer mentor or outreach resource in a timely, consistent manner.

**Scope:** This SOP applies to all patients referred to the program who have consented to services and are ready to begin the intake process. It covers the intake call and the mentor assignment process.

**Prerequisites:** Referral has been received and entered into the database; patient consent is confirmed; patient has agreed to be contacted; intake form is ready; available peer mentors are identified.

**Steps:**

1. Review the referral information. Note any urgency indicators, special considerations (language, accessibility, cultural needs), and patient preferences for contact method and timing.
2. Contact the patient using their preferred method within seventy-two hours of referral receipt. Introduce yourself, confirm you are calling from Lost Limb Riders, and confirm they are still interested in learning about services.
3. If the patient confirms interest: explain the intake process, how long it will take, what information will be collected, and how the information will be used. Answer any questions. Obtain verbal consent to proceed.
4. Complete the intake form. Ask questions from each section. Allow the patient to skip any question they are not comfortable answering. Document responses clearly and factually.
5. Pay particular attention to the risk screening questions. If any crisis indicators are present, pause the intake and follow the crisis escalation SOP before proceeding.
6. After the intake is complete: review the information to identify the best match for the patient. Matching criteria include: geographic proximity, amputation type and cause (when relevant), age range, gender preference (if expressed), veteran status, interests and activities, and availability compatability.
7. Identify one to three potential mentor matches from the available mentor pool. Consider current caseload of each mentor (maximum of five active participants per peer mentor unless approved by the Coordinator).
8. Contact the selected mentor to confirm availability and willingness to accept the assignment. Provide the mentor with relevant information about the patient (excluding any information the patient asked to keep confidential).
9. If the preferred mentor accepts: notify the patient that a mentor has been identified. Provide the mentor\u2019s first name and a brief relevant background. Confirm that the patient is comfortable with the match. If the patient has concerns, offer an alternative.
10. If no mentor is available: inform the patient that the program is working to identify a match. Provide interim support resources. Set a follow-up date to reassess mentor availability. Do not leave the patient without any support pathway.
11. Confirm the match in the database. Update the mentor\u2019s caseload. Notify the mentor with the patient\u2019s contact information and any relevant context. Instruct the mentor to make first contact within seventy-two hours.
12. Document all actions in the participant record: intake completion date, match date, mentor assigned, first contact deadline. Notify the referring party that the patient has been connected (general acknowledgment only).

**Documentation Required:** Completed intake form, signed consent form, mentor assignment record, notification records.

**Completion Criteria:** Intake is complete; patient is matched with a mentor or interim support is provided; match is documented in the database; first contact deadline is set.

# Section 20: Five-Year Development Plan

## 20.1 Year 1: Regional Hospital Networks

Year one focuses on establishing the foundational infrastructure of the Hospital and Prosthetic Partner Outreach program. The primary objective is to build regional hospital networks in three to five operational regions, each anchored by at least one major hospital partner.

Specific targets for year one include executing partnership agreements with a minimum of three hospitals per region for a total of nine to fifteen new hospital partners; credentialing at least two outreach representatives per region; developing and delivering referral training to each partner\u2019s staff; processing a minimum of fifty referrals across all partners; achieving a referral-to-connection conversion rate of at least sixty percent; and establishing a quarterly check-in cadence with every active partner.

Year one also includes building the operational backbone: finalizing all forms and documentation templates; implementing the participant database; developing the initial training curriculum; hiring or appointing the Director of Medical Partnerships; recruiting and training the initial cohort of outreach representatives; and establishing relationships with at least two prosthetic clinics per region to complement the hospital partnerships.

Success in year one is defined by signed partnership agreements, trained outreach representatives, active referral flow, and demonstrated program demand. The program should end year one with a clear understanding of what works, what needs adjustment, and what resources are needed to scale.

## 20.2 Year 2: Statewide Referral System

Year two takes the regional partnerships and connects them into a statewide referral system. The goal is to ensure that a patient referred at any partner hospital in the state can access peer support regardless of geography, and that the referral process is standardized across all chapters and partners.

Specific targets for year two include expanding to a minimum of twenty total hospital partnerships across the state; adding rehabilitation hospitals, inpatient rehabilitation facilities, and skilled nursing facilities to the partner network; standardizing the referral process across all partners using a uniform referral form and protocol; implementing a centralized referral intake system managed by the Hospital Outreach Coordinator; cross-training outreach representatives to cover multiple facilities and regions; and developing a referral routing protocol for patients who live outside the service area of the referring facility.

Year two also includes expanding the prosthetic and orthotic clinic network to a minimum of eight clinics; initiating outreach to home health agencies; developing a patient resource directory organized by region and service type; and launching a quarterly partner newsletter to maintain communication and share program updates.

The statewide referral system requires consistent data collection and reporting. Year two includes implementing a standardized data dashboard for tracking referral volume, conversion rates, geographic distribution, and partner engagement. This data informs year three planning.

## 20.3 Year 3: Electronic Referral Portal

Year three transitions the program from paper-based and email-based referrals to a secure, HIPAA-aware electronic referral portal. The portal streamlines the referral process, reduces data entry errors, improves response times, and provides real-time tracking for partners.

The electronic referral portal must meet the following specifications: HIPAA-compliant data transmission and storage; role-based access controls for partner staff and program personnel; secure user authentication with individual login credentials; standardized referral form with required fields and validation; automated acknowledgment of receipt to the referring party; referral status tracking visible to the partner (received, in process, connected, closed); automated escalation for referrals not acted upon within the service standard; reporting capability for partner organizations to view their own referral history; and mobile-responsive design for access on tablets and phones.

Development of the portal may be pursued through custom software development, configuration of an existing HIPAA-compliant platform, or partnership with a health IT vendor. The Director of Medical Partnerships leads the requirements gathering and vendor selection process with support from the Board of Directors and any available technical advisors.

Implementation milestones for year three include completing requirements gathering by end of first quarter; selecting a development approach or vendor by end of second quarter; completing development and testing by end of third quarter; piloting the portal with three partner organizations in fourth quarter; and conducting full rollout to all partners by the end of the year. Training materials and user guides are developed alongside the portal to support partner adoption.

## 20.4 Year 4: Medical Advisory Committee

Year four establishes a Medical Advisory Committee (MAC) composed of clinical professionals who provide guidance, credibility, and strategic direction to the Hospital and Prosthetic Partner Outreach program. The MAC ensures that the program operates with clinical relevance and that its practices align with medical best practices and standards of care.

The MAC includes the following positions, recruited through professional networks, partner organizations, and medical societies: a physiatrist or rehabilitation medicine physician with amputee care experience; a trauma surgeon; a prosthetist or orthotist; a registered nurse with case management or amputation care experience; a physical therapist with amputee rehabilitation expertise; a mental health professional with experience in medical trauma or adjustment to disability; and a patient advocate or community health representative with lived experience of limb loss.

The MAC\u2019s responsibilities include reviewing program policies and procedures annually for clinical appropriateness; advising on patient safety and quality improvement initiatives; providing input on training curriculum for outreach representatives; serving as a resource for complex cases or clinical questions; reviewing outcome data and suggesting program improvements; representing Lost Limb Riders in professional medical forums; and assisting with grant applications and research initiatives.

Bylaws for the MAC are drafted in year four and include terms of service (staggered three-year terms), meeting frequency (quarterly), officer positions (chair, vice chair), conflict of interest policies, and decision-making procedures. The Director of Medical Partnerships serves as the staff liaison to the MAC.

Recruitment of MAC members begins in the first quarter of year four. An initial meeting is held by the end of the second quarter to review and approve bylaws. The MAC is fully operational by the end of year four, having completed at least two regular meetings and provided input on at least one program policy or initiative.

## 20.5 Year 5: National Healthcare Partnerships

Year five represents the program\u2019s expansion beyond the home state into multi-state healthcare partnerships. The program pursues partnership agreements with healthcare systems that operate across state lines, national rehabilitation organizations, and multi-state prosthetic clinic chains.

Specific targets for year five include executing partnership agreements with at least one multi-state healthcare system (three or more hospitals across at least two states); establishing partnerships in a minimum of three states beyond the home state; developing a credentialing reciprocity framework that allows outreach representatives to work across state lines efficiently; negotiating master partnership agreements that cover multiple facilities within a single health system; expanding the outreach representative network to support multi-state coverage; and developing a chapter partnership model that supports local chapters in their own outreach efforts under the national framework.

Year five also includes establishing a standardized credentialing package that meets the requirements of most healthcare facilities, reducing the time and duplication involved in credentialing representatives for each new partner. The credentialing package includes a universal background check, standardized vaccination documentation, HIPAA training certificate, and a uniform facility orientation module that can be supplemented with facility-specific content.

The national expansion requires additional staffing. Year five includes hiring or appointing a second Hospital Outreach Coordinator to manage the expanded partner network, and adding Regional Outreach Representatives in each new state. The Director of Medical Partnerships shifts from direct partnership development to strategic oversight, systems management, and national partner relationship management.

## 20.6 Accreditation Readiness

Throughout the five-year development plan, the program prepares for accreditation by a recognized healthcare quality or peer support accreditation body. Accreditation demonstrates that the program meets national standards of quality, safety, and effectiveness.

The accreditation readiness process begins in year one with an evaluation of the applicable accreditation standards. Potential accreditation bodies include CARF International (Commission on Accreditation of Rehabilitation Facilities), which accredits rehabilitation and community services, and other peer support accreditation programs recognized by national peer support organizations.

In year one and year two, the program conducts a gap analysis comparing current operations against accreditation standards. Gaps are documented and prioritized for remediation. Common gaps in peer support programs include formalized peer mentor training curricula, supervision and evaluation standards, outcome measurement systems, participant rights documentation, and quality improvement processes.

In year three and year four, the program implements the remediation plan. Policy gaps are addressed through policy development. Documentation gaps are addressed through form and system improvements. Training gaps are addressed through curriculum enhancement. Outcome measurement gaps are addressed through data system improvements.

In year five, the program conducts a pre-accreditation self-study and mock survey. Identified deficiencies are corrected. The program submits the accreditation application and prepares for the on-site survey. Accreditation is targeted for year five completion or early year six, depending on the accrediting body\u2019s schedule.

## 20.7 Research and Outcome Reporting

Research and outcome reporting are integral to the program\u2019s long-term credibility and sustainability. The program must demonstrate not just that it provides services, but that those services improve patient outcomes. A formal research and outcome reporting initiative begins in year one and matures through year five.

In year one, the program establishes baseline data collection: referral volume, referral sources, connection rates, patient demographics, and patient satisfaction scores. This baseline provides the foundation for all future outcome comparisons. Data collection must be consistent from day one.

In year two, the program identifies specific outcomes to measure: reduction in self-reported isolation, improvement in mood and coping scores, time from amputation to peer connection, resource referral follow-through rates, and partner satisfaction scores. Standardized assessment tools are selected or developed, with input from the Medical Advisory Committee when formed.

In year three, the program begins analyzing outcome data and preparing internal reports. Findings are shared with partners, the Board of Directors, and funders. The program also submits abstracts to relevant conferences: the American Academy of Orthotists and Prosthetists Annual Meeting, the Amputee Coalition National Conference, and the American Congress of Rehabilitation Medicine Annual Conference.

In year four, with the Medical Advisory Committee in place, the program develops a formal research protocol for submission to an Institutional Review Board (IRB). The program seeks IRB approval through a partner hospital\u2019s IRB or an independent IRB. Research questions focus on the impact of peer support on patient-reported outcomes after amputation.

In year five, the program publishes its first research findings in a peer-reviewed journal or presents at a national conference. The program also applies for grant funding to support continued research, targeting foundations and government agencies that fund peer support, amputation care, and rehabilitation research. Publishable outcomes, conference presentations, and grant-funded studies establish the program as a credible contributor to the evidence base for peer support in amputation care.
