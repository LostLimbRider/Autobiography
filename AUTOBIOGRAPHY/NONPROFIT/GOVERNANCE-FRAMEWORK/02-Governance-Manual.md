# Lost Limb Riders Governance Manual

## 1. Governance Philosophy

Lost Limb Riders is governed by a volunteer Board of Directors that protects mission, charitable assets, participant dignity, public trust, and long‑term sustainability. The organization may be founder‑inspired, rider‑powered, volunteer‑supported, and community‑driven, but it is not owned by any person. The Board governs on behalf of the mission and the public benefit.

## 2. Governance Model

The governance model separates oversight from management.

Board of Directors  
Executive Officers  
Executive Director or Founder/CEO when appointed by the Board  
Department Directors  
Program Coordinators  
Volunteers, riders, members, and participants

The Board approves mission, bylaws, strategy, budgets, policies, executive appointments, financial controls, risk limits, chapter standards, and major commitments. Executive leadership manages daily work within those boundaries.

## 3. Role Definitions

### Board of Directors

The Board sets direction, hires and evaluates executive leadership when applicable, approves annual budgets, monitors financial performance, adopts policies, protects legal compliance, manages risk, leads fundraising accountability, and evaluates organizational results.

### Executive Officers

Officers carry out board leadership functions described in the bylaws. Officers do not possess independent authority unless granted by bylaws, policy, job description, or board resolution.

### Executive Director

The Executive Director manages operations, staff, volunteers, programs, reporting, partnerships, and implementation of board directives.

### Founder/CEO

The Founder/CEO may serve as public ambassador, chief mission storyteller, program strategist, executive leader, or board member depending on the formal appointment approved by the Board. The role must be documented in writing and reviewed for conflict, compensation, and authority.

### Advisory Board

The Advisory Board provides expertise and community insight without governing authority.

### Standing Committees

Standing committees perform recurring board work and report to the full Board.

### Temporary Committees

Temporary committees handle time‑limited assignments and expire when complete.

### Departments

Departments may include Administration, Finance, Programs, Family Support, Volunteer Services, Safety and Risk, Fundraising, Communications, Membership, Chapter Development, and Community Partnerships.

## 5. Decision Authority Matrix

Board approval is required for bylaws, annual budget, strategic plan, executive hiring or removal, compensation of senior executives, loans, leases, real estate, litigation strategy, dissolution, merger, chapter charter approval or revocation, major policy adoption, and commitments above policy thresholds.

Executive leadership may approve routine operations, volunteer assignments, program scheduling, vendor purchases within budget, communications consistent with policy, and emergency action necessary to protect safety.

Committee approval is limited to recommendations unless delegated by the Board.

## 6. Board Calendar

January — annual officer election if not completed, conflict disclosures, policy review calendar.  
February — budget confirmation and fundraising plan.  
March — insurance and risk review.  
April — program evaluation and accessibility review.  
May — board recruitment assessment.  
June — midyear financial review.  
July — chapter and membership review.  
August — safety training and event readiness review.  
September — strategic planning.  
October — budget development.  
November — executive evaluation and committee evaluations.  
December — annual report, records archive, and next‑year priorities.

## 7. Required Governance Practices

Directors must attend meetings, prepare for votes, disclose conflicts, protect confidentiality, comply with policies, participate in committees, support fundraising, and act in the best interests of Lost Limb Riders.

## 8. Recommended Governance Practices

The Board should maintain a skills matrix, conduct annual self‑assessment, recruit for lived experience and professional expertise, use consent agendas for routine matters, hold executive sessions when appropriate, and review legal compliance annually.
