# **Chapter 13: Roxy**

The front door had barely cracked open when I heard her. The click-clack of nails on hardwood, accelerating into a skid, and then the full-body slam of forty pounds of dog hitting the door from the inside. She didn't care that I'd been gone for six hours. She didn't care that I was exhausted, that my prosthetic was rubbing raw, that I'd spent the morning in a surgeon's waiting room trying not to think about what the scans might show. She cared about one thing: I was home.

I pushed the door open and she burst through, spinning in tight circles, her tail a blur, her whole hind end wagging so hard she nearly tipped over. She looked up at me with those big brown eyes and I swear she was smiling. Dogs don't smile. But Roxy does.

I crouched down, balanced on my good leg, and let her attack my face with licks.

> Alright, alright, I said. I missed you too.

She didn't stop. She never stops. That's the thing about Roxy. She doesn't hold back. She doesn't play it cool. When she's happy, the whole world knows it. And when she's sad — well, she's never sad. That's the other thing about her. I've never seen that dog have a bad day. Not one. And I've had enough bad days for both of us.

I got her eight years ago, in a roundabout way that I should have seen coming. My daughter — the one who always seems to know what I need before I know it myself — called me up one afternoon.

> Dad, she said. You need to come with me somewhere.

I was in the middle of trying to adjust my prosthetic. It had been about four months since the amputation, and I was still figuring out how to make the thing work without destroying the rest of my leg. I was frustrated. I was sore. I was in no mood for errands.

> Where? I asked.

> Just get in the truck, she said. Don't argue.

So I got in the truck. Because when your daughter talks to you like that, you listen. She drove me out to a farm on the edge of the county. A friend of a friend had a dog who'd had a litter, and the puppies were ready for homes. I didn't know that's where we were going until we pulled into the gravel driveway and I saw a woman standing by a barn with a pen full of wiggling fur.

> I don't need a dog, I said.

My daughter looked at me. That look. The one that says: *You don't know what you need, but I do.*

> Just look at them, she said. That's all I'm asking.

So I got out. Walked over to the pen. There were six puppies in there, maybe seven. They were all over each other, biting ears, tumbling over, yapping. All except one. One was off to the side, sitting by herself, watching me. She was the smallest. The runt. Her coat was a little duller, her eyes a little quieter. She didn't bounce around like the others. She just sat there, head tilted, one ear up and one ear down, looking at me like she was trying to figure out who I was and whether I measured up.

I crouched down. She stood up, walked over slowly, sniffed my hand. Then she climbed into my lap. Climbed. Like I was a chair she'd been waiting to sit in. She turned around twice, curled up, and let out a sigh that seemed to come from somewhere deep.

I looked at my daughter. She was already smiling.

> She picked you, she said.

I wanted to argue. I wanted to say I wasn't ready. That I didn't have the energy. That a dog was a commitment and I wasn't sure I could commit to anything when my whole life felt like it was held together with tape and stubbornness. But the puppy was already asleep in my lap. Her little ribcage rising and falling. Her paws twitching like she was running somewhere in her dreams.

I took her home. Named her Roxanne, after the song, because I'd always loved that name and because it felt like a name that could grow into something. But she was Roxy from the first day. The runt who climbed into my lap and decided I was hers.

She's not a beautiful dog. I'll be honest about that. She's got a lopsided face, ears that don't match, a coat that looks like she rolled through a pile of leaves and decided to keep the look. She runs funny, with her back end cocked to one side. When she sleeps, she snores. Loud. The kind of snoring that rattles the windows and makes you wonder if you should check her pulse. But none of that matters. She's the best dog I've ever had. The best dog I'll ever have.

Here's what I mean. There are days when I wake up and everything hurts. The phantom pain. The socket of my prosthetic pressing into flesh that's never quite healed right. The memories that show up whether I invite them or not. Days when I don't want to get out of bed. Days when the weight of everything I've been through sits on my chest like a concrete slab. On those days, I don't have to tell Roxy. She knows. She always knows. She'll come over to the side of the bed, rest her chin on the mattress, and just look at me. No barking. No jumping. Just those big brown eyes, patient and steady, saying: *I'm here. Take your time. But eventually you're going to have to get up and let me out, so maybe don't take too long.*

And then there's the other side of her. The side that saves me in a completely different way. She'll come up to me for no reason, turn around, and look back at her butt like she's trying to communicate a very important message. The message is: *Scratch right here, Dad. Right by the tail. That's the spot.*

And I do. I reach down and scratch that spot above her tail, and her back feet start hopping. Her hips start wiggling. She looks back at me with this look of pure bliss, like I've just solved every problem she's ever had.

I have a whole routine with her. I'll scratch her back and say it slow, in a sing‑song voice:

> Roxy... Roxanne... scan, scan, scan.

I don't know where the "scan, scan, scan" came from. It just came out one day and it stuck. She doesn't know what the words mean. She doesn't care. She knows it's our thing. She knows that when I say those words, I'm paying attention to her. I'm in the moment with her. Her tail goes crazy. Her whole body wags. She presses into my hand like she's trying to push the scratches deeper into her fur.

Those moments are the ones I don't talk about when people ask me about my story. They want to hear about the survival stuff. The hospital drama. The comeback. And that's all real. But the moments that actually keep me going are smaller than that. They're scratching my dog's butt while she does her happy dance. They're laughing at her snoring. They're watching her chase a squirrel across the yard, knowing full well she's never going to catch it, but watching her run anyway, full speed, no hesitation, like this is the most important thing in the world.

Roxy doesn't care that I'm an amputee. Doesn't care that I've got a prosthetic that squeaks when I walk. Doesn't care that I've been through hell and come out the other side with scars inside and out. She cares that I'm home. She cares that I scratch her back. She cares that I'm here. Not the amputee version of me. Not the cancer survivor version of me. Just me. John. The guy who feeds her and scratches her butt and talks to her in a silly voice.

And some days, that's exactly what I need. A dog who loves me without conditions. Who doesn't want anything from me except my presence. Who doesn't care if I'm having a bad day or a good day or a day I'd rather forget entirely. She just wants to be near me.

There's a lesson in that. I'm not saying dogs are better than people. I'm saying there's something pure about a relationship that has no agenda. Roxy doesn't want my story. She doesn't want my explanation. She doesn't need me to be inspirational or strong or brave. She just wants me to show up. To be present. To scratch the spot by her tail.

Roxy saved me. Not the way a doctor saves you, with medicine and scalpels. Not the way a therapist saves you, with insight and questions. She saved me the way a warm fire saves you on a cold night. She gave me something to come home to. Something that needed me. Something that reminded me, every single day, that joy is still available. That laughter is still an option. That even on the worst days, there's a dog waiting for you, ready to wiggle her butt and hop her back feet and remind you that life is still good.

You just have to be willing to scratch for it.
