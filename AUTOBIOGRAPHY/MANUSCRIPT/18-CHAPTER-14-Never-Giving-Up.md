# **Chapter 14: Never Giving Up**

The first time I said it, I was alone.

It was three in the morning, maybe four. The hospital room was dark except for the glow of the monitors and the thin strip of light coming through the blinds from the parking lot. I was staring at the ceiling, trying to remember what it felt like to sleep without machines beeping around me. My leg — what was left of it — was throbbing. The phantom pains had started a few days earlier, and they were worse than anything I'd felt before. My brain kept sending signals to a foot that wasn't there anymore. Itching. Burning. Cramping. I'd reach down to scratch it and my hand would meet empty space and bandages, and for a split second I'd forget. Then I'd remember. And then I'd have to go through the whole thing again — the realization, the grief, the small death of accepting that my leg was gone.

I couldn't sleep. Couldn't eat. Couldn't think about anything except the fact that my life had been ripped apart and I had no idea how to put it back together.

That's when I said it. Not out loud at first. Just in my head. A whisper to myself in the dark:

*I can do this. I will do this.*

I didn't believe it. Not even a little. But I said it anyway. Because I needed something to hold onto. And those four words were the only thing I had.

I Can. I Will.

People ask me all the time where those words came from. They think there's a story behind them. A moment of inspiration. A teacher who said something. A book I read. A sermon I heard. There's none of that. They came from the bottom. They came from a hospital bed at four in the morning when I had nothing else. No answers. No certainty. No guarantee that things would ever get better. Just those four words, repeated over and over, like a prayer I wasn't sure I believed in.

I said them during chemo, when the poison was dripping into my veins and I could feel it burning through me. I said them sitting on the edge of my bed, staring at my prosthetic, trying to convince myself to put it on and try to stand. I said them in physical therapy, sweat pouring down my face, my arms shaking, my good leg screaming, while the therapist told me I had five more reps and I knew I couldn't do five more and I did them anyway. I said them in the middle of the night, alone, afraid, wondering if all of this was worth it.

I Can. I Will.

It wasn't confidence. I've never been a confident person. Not in the way people think. I didn't walk around believing everything would work out. I walked around terrified, most of the time, pretending I wasn't. And those four words weren't a declaration of certainty. They were a decision. A choice I made every single morning, sometimes every single hour. I chose to keep going. Not because I felt brave. Not because I was strong. Because I had decided that quitting wasn't an option.

Some days I believed the words. Some days I didn't. But I said them anyway. I learned something important in that process: action comes before motivation. Everybody waits to feel ready. Everybody waits for the perfect moment, the surge of inspiration, the lightning bolt of motivation that's going to carry them through. That lightning bolt doesn't come. Or if it does, it doesn't last. What lasts is the habit of showing up. The discipline of doing the thing even when you don't want to. The choice to act your way into feeling, instead of waiting to feel your way into acting.

I had a therapist early on — a guy named Dave, tough old bird, probably sixty-five years old, been in the business for thirty years. He'd seen every kind of trauma, every kind of loss, every kind of grief. I was sitting in his office one afternoon, maybe three months after the amputation, complaining about how hard everything was. How I didn't have the energy. How I didn't see the point. How I felt like I was pushing a boulder up a hill and it kept rolling back down.

He listened. Nodded. Let me finish. Then he leaned forward and said:

> John, you're waiting to feel better so you can start living. But that's not how it works. You start living, and the feeling better follows. You don't get ready by waiting. You get ready by doing. So stop waiting.

I wanted to punch him. I also wanted to hug him. He was right, and I hated him for it.

That advice changed everything. I stopped waiting to feel ready. Stopped waiting to feel motivated. Stopped waiting for some magical moment when everything would click into place. I just started. I put on the prosthetic even when I didn't want to. I did the exercises even when I was sore. I showed up to therapy even when I wanted to cancel. I said I Can. I Will. even when every part of me was screaming that I couldn't and I wouldn't.

People ask me how I survived everything. How I made it through the addiction, the heart attack, the cancer, the amputation, the long road of recovery. They want a secret. A formula. Some piece of wisdom they can write down and apply to their own lives.

The answer is boring. It's not inspirational. It's not profound. It's not going to make a good TED Talk.

I just refused to quit.

That's it. That's the whole thing. I was too stubborn to die. Too stubborn to stay down. Too stubborn to let life win.

And that stubbornness — that plain, hard‑headed refusal to give up — carried me through every single thing that tried to take me out. Not my intelligence. Not my talent. Not my faith, even, though my faith helped. It was stubbornness. Pure, simple, ugly stubbornness. The kind that looks at a mountain and says: *I'm going to climb you, even if it takes me the rest of my life.*

I think about the people who didn't make it. The ones I met in recovery, in the hospital, in the support groups. Good people. Strong people. People who had every reason to make it. And some of them didn't. Not because they were weaker. Not because they didn't want it enough. Sometimes the mountain is just too high. Sometimes the weight is just too much. I'm not naive about that. I know I got lucky in ways I can't explain. I know there were moments when I was one bad decision away from falling back into the pit.

But I also know this: every single time I wanted to quit, I didn't. Every single time I thought about giving up, I found a reason to keep going. Sometimes that reason was my daughter. Sometimes it was Roxy. Sometimes it was a verse from the Bible I hadn't thought about in years. Sometimes it was just the memory of a man named Dave telling me that you get ready by doing.

I Can. I Will.

Those two sentences. Those four words. They're not a motto. They're not a brand. They're not something I put on a T-shirt and sell at a speaking event. They're the truth of my life. I can do this. I will do this. Not because I'm special. Because I made a decision. And I kept making it, over and over, every day, for years.

Some days I still have to say them. Even now. Even with all the surgeries behind me and the recovery mostly behind me and the speaking career in front of me. There are still mornings when I wake up and everything feels heavy. When the phantom pains are bad. When the doubt creeps in. When I wonder if I've got anything left to give.

And I say it again. Under my breath. A whisper to myself in the dark:

I Can. I Will.

And I do.
