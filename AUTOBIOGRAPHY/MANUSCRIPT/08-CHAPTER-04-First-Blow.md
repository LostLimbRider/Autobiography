# **Chapter 4: First Blow**

It started with a cough. Just a cough. Nothing that would make any man stop and think twice. I’d been working twelve‑hour shifts at the plant, breathing in air that wasn’t exactly clean, sleeping four or five hours a night if I was lucky. A cough was part of the deal.

But the cough didn’t go away. It hung around for weeks, then months. I’d hack into my sleeve on the assembly line and tell myself it was allergies, or the change of seasons, or that Chinese food I ate too late. I wasn’t worried. I was never the worrying type.

Then the fatigue hit. Not the usual tired after a long shift. Something deeper. Something that felt like my bones were made of lead. I’d wake up after eight hours of sleep feeling like I hadn’t closed my eyes. I’d drag myself through the day, counting the hours until I could go home and collapse. My foreman noticed. He pulled me aside one afternoon and said:

> “Thompson, you look like hell. You need to see a doctor.”

I told him I was fine. He said:

> “I didn’t ask if you were fine. I said you need to see a doctor. Take tomorrow off. That’s an order.”

I didn’t argue. Partly because he was my boss, and partly because some part of me knew he was right.

The doctor’s office smelled like antiseptic and paper. I sat in the waiting room flipping through a Sports Illustrated from two years ago, trying to ignore the feeling that something was wrong. When they called my name, I followed the nurse down a long hallway and into a small room with a paper‑covered table and a poster about blood pressure on the wall.

The doctor was a thin man with wire‑rimmed glasses and a calm voice that made me nervous. Calm voices in doctor’s offices never meant good news. He asked me questions, took some blood, listened to my chest, and told me he’d call with the results.

I didn’t think much of it. I went home, ate dinner, watched TV, fell asleep on the couch.

Three days later, the phone rang. It was the doctor’s office. They asked me to come in.

I knew before I walked through the door. You can tell from the way the receptionist looks at you when you sign in. You can tell from the way the nurse avoids eye contact. You can tell from the fact that they didn’t want to tell me over the phone.

The doctor sat me down in his office, not the exam room. That was the first clue. He had a folder in front of him, and he opened it slowly, like he was giving me time to prepare. He said:

> “John, your test results came back. There are some things we need to talk about.”

He used words I didn’t want to hear. Words like *malignancy* and *aggressive* and *immediate treatment*. I sat there and nodded, but the words weren’t really landing. They were bouncing off some wall I’d put up in my head. I heard them, but I didn’t absorb them. Not yet.

He asked me if I had any questions. I asked:

> “Am I going to die?”

He paused. Then he said:

> “I can’t promise you you won’t. But I can promise you we’re going to do everything we can.”

That wasn’t the answer I wanted. But it was the honest one.

I walked out of that office in a daze. I remember standing in the parking lot, staring at my truck, not knowing what to do next. The sun was shining. People were going about their day. The world was still turning. But my world had just stopped.

I don’t remember the drive home. I don’t remember parking the truck. I remember walking through the front door and seeing Genie in the kitchen, stirring something on the stove. She looked up at me and I must have had something on my face, because she put the spoon down and said:

> “John? What happened?”

I couldn’t say it. I opened my mouth and nothing came out. She walked over and took my hands and said:

> “Tell me.”

So I did. I told her everything the doctor had said. I told her the words I didn’t want to say out loud. And when I was done, she didn’t cry. She didn’t fall apart. She just held me and said:

> “Okay. Then we fight it.”

That was it. No panic. No drama. No asking why us or why now. She just said *we fight it*, and she meant it. And in that moment, I loved her more than I had ever loved anything in my life.

The treatments started two weeks later. They were brutal in ways I couldn’t have imagined. The chemicals they pumped into my body made me sick in ways I didn’t know a person could be sick. I vomited until there was nothing left. I lost my hair in clumps. I lost weight I couldn’t afford to lose. I’d lie in bed and stare at the ceiling and wonder if this was what dying felt like.

Genie was there every day. She’d hold a cool cloth to my forehead. She’d sit beside me and read magazines out loud, even when I was too weak to respond. She’d make me eat when I didn’t want to. She’d force me to get up and walk when every part of my body screamed to stay still.

My parents came when they could. My mother would sit and hold my hand and not say much. My father would stand in the corner with his arms crossed, looking like he wanted to trade places with me. He said:

> “I’d take this from you if I could, son.”

I said:

> “I know you would, Dad.”

Friends called and visited and brought food and offered help. I was surrounded by people who wanted to see me make it through. And I was grateful for every single one of them. But there was a part of the fight that was mine alone. Nobody could sit in that hospital bed for me. Nobody could get sick for me. Nobody could decide, every single morning, whether to keep going or to give up.

There were mornings I wanted to give up. I won’t pretend otherwise. I’d wake up and the nausea would hit before I even opened my eyes. I’d think about what I looked like now — thin, bald, weak. I’d think about Genie and the girls and wonder if they’d be better off without me dragging them through this. I’d think about how easy it would be to just close my eyes and stop fighting.

But then I’d hear the girls in the other room, laughing about something, fighting over a toy, living their lives the way children do. And I’d think: I can’t leave them. I can’t let them grow up without a father. I can’t let them remember me as the man who gave up.

So I’d get up. I’d force myself to eat. I’d take the next round of poison they called medicine. I’d do the work.

One night, after a particularly rough treatment, I was lying in bed and Genie was sitting beside me. I asked her:

> “Are you scared?”

She didn’t pretend. She said:

> “Every day.”

I said:

> “I’m sorry.”

She said:

> “Don’t be sorry. Just keep fighting.”

And I did.

The recovery was slow. Measured in inches, not miles. I gained back a little weight. My hair grew back, thin and gray in places it hadn’t been before. I started walking farther, then running, then lifting things I couldn’t have lifted a month earlier. Every small victory felt like a miracle. Every good day was a gift.

About six months after the diagnosis, I went back to work. The guys at the plant clapped me on the back and told me I looked good. Carl brought a cake. My foreman shook my hand and said:

> “Glad to have you back, Thompson.”

I was glad to be back. But something had changed in me. I looked at the plant floor and saw it differently now — not just a place to earn a paycheck, but a place I’d been given the chance to see again.

I thought that was the end of it. I thought I’d been through the worst and life would cut me a break. I went to bed every night thinking the hard part was over. I looked at Genie and the girls and felt like we’d made it through the fire.

I didn’t know that the fire was just the beginning. I didn’t know that surviving the illness would lead me into a different kind of darkness. I didn’t know that the hardest test was still waiting for me.

But that’s the thing about life. You never know what’s coming next. You just have to be ready to face it when it arrives.
