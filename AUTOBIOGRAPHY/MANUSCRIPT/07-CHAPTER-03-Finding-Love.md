# **Chapter 3: Finding Love**

The summer I turned twenty‑seven, I wasn’t looking for anything serious. I was working double shifts at the plant, drinking with the guys on weekends, and telling myself I had everything I needed. I had my independence. I had my truck — a rusted‑out Ford F‑150 with more miles on it than I wanted to count. I had a roof over my head that I paid for myself. What else did a man need?

Then my buddy Carl started in on me at the shop one Friday. He said:

> “You gotta come to this barbecue at my cousin’s place tomorrow. There’s gonna be food, beer, and I heard his wife’s got a friend who just moved back to town.”

I wiped the grease off my hands and told him I wasn’t interested in being set up. Carl laughed and said:

> “Nobody’s setting you up, John. I’m just saying there’ll be food. You like food.”

I did like food. So I went.

The barbecue was at a house on the outskirts of town, a little brick ranch with a chain‑link fence and a grill the size of a small car. There were maybe thirty people there. Guys from Carl’s family, their wives, kids running around the yard, music playing from a boombox on the porch. I grabbed a beer and a paper plate and found a spot in the shade.

That’s where I saw her for the first time.

She was standing by the grill, talking to Carl’s cousin’s wife, holding a plastic cup of iced tea. She wasn’t trying to be the center of attention. She wasn’t dressed to impress — just jeans and a plain white T‑shirt. She was laughing at something, her head tilted back, completely comfortable in her own skin. I’d seen plenty of women try too hard. Genie wasn’t trying at all. That’s what got me.

I walked over. Introduced myself. She shook my hand — firm grip, looked me right in the eye — and said:

> “You’re John. Carl’s told me about you.”

I asked her what Carl had said, expecting the usual friendly nonsense. She smiled and said:

> “He said you’re stubborn, hard‑headed, and you work too much.”

I laughed. That sounded about right. I said:

> “Sounds about right. What else did he say?”

She looked me right in the eye and said:

> “He said you’re a good man. I’m still deciding if I believe him.”

That stopped me. Most women I’d met would have smiled and nodded and told me what they thought I wanted to hear. Genie looked at me like she was taking my measure, like she wasn’t going to be impressed until I gave her a reason to be. I liked that. I didn’t know it at the time, but I needed that.

We talked for the rest of the afternoon. About nothing and everything. She told me she’d just come back from spending two years taking care of her mother, who had been sick. She didn’t say it like she was looking for sympathy. She said it like it was a fact — she’d done what needed to be done and now she was moving on. That impressed me more than anything. I’d known plenty of people who talked about what they were going to do. Genie talked about what she’d already done.

I asked her if she wanted to get dinner sometime. She said:

> “I might. Ask me again tomorrow.”

So I did. I called her the next day from a payphone outside the plant, still smelling like grease and sweat. She answered on the third ring and I said:

> “This is John. From the barbecue. I’m asking you to dinner.”

She laughed and said:

> “You don’t waste time, do you?”

I said:

> “Never saw the point.”

Our first date was at a little Italian place on the south side of town. Nothing fancy. Red‑and‑white checkered tablecloths, candles in Chianti bottles, a jukebox that played Sinatra and Dean Martin. I was nervous, which surprised me. I wasn’t the nervous type. But there was something about her that made me want to get it right.

We ordered spaghetti and split a bottle of wine. She told me about growing up in a house with three brothers and a father who worked on the railroad. She told me about the first time she fell in love and how it fell apart. She told me she wanted kids someday, but she wasn’t in a hurry. She said she wanted to build something first — something solid — so that when the kids came, they’d have a foundation to stand on.

I listened more than I talked. That wasn’t like me either. But Genie had a way of drawing things out of you without demanding them. By the end of the night, I’d told her things I hadn’t told anyone. About my own father, and the way he worked himself into an early grave. About the fear I carried that I’d end up the same way. About the nights I lay awake wondering if there was more to life than shifts and weekends and waiting for something to happen.

She reached across the table and put her hand on mine. She said:

> “You don’t have to have it all figured out tonight, John. You’ve got time.”

Nobody had ever said that to me before. Everybody always wanted me to be further along — to have more, to be more. Genie looked at me and saw someone who was still becoming, and she was okay with that.

We saw each other every day after that. I’d pick her up after work and we’d go for drives. We’d sit on the hood of my truck and watch the sunset. We’d talk about our families, our fears, the things we hoped for but were afraid to say out loud. I fell in love with her slowly and then all at once. One day I looked at her and realized I couldn’t imagine my life without her in it. That was the day I knew.

I didn’t propose in any dramatic way. There was no fancy restaurant, no ring in a champagne glass. I just asked her one night on my porch, sitting in an old rocking chair that creaked every time I moved. I said:

> “Genie, I’m not good with words. But I know what I feel. And what I feel is that I want to spend the rest of my life with you. I don’t have much to offer. I’ve got a job, a truck, and this house I’m still paying off. But what I have, I’d give to you. All of it.”

She looked at me for a long time. Then she said:

> “That’s the most honest thing anyone’s ever said to me. Yes.”

We got married at the courthouse three weeks later. Her brothers came. Carl was my best man. My mother cried. My father shook my hand and said:

> “She’s a good one, son. Don’t mess it up.”

I promised him I wouldn’t.

The first few years weren’t easy. We had money problems. We had arguments about where to live, how to spend, when to start a family. I was still stubborn and hard‑headed. Genie was patient, but she wasn’t a pushover. She’d stand her ground when she had to. We had one fight where I walked out and drove around for two hours, tires squealing around corners, radio blasting, feeling sorry for myself. When I came back, she was sitting at the kitchen table with a cup of coffee, waiting. She said:

> “You done?”

I said I was. She said:

> “Good. Now sit down and talk to me.”

That was Genie. She never let me run. She never let me hide. She made me face the things I didn’t want to face, and she did it without shame or blame. She did it because she loved me.

When our first daughter was born, I wasn’t ready. I don’t think any man is, no matter how much they prepare. I held her in my arms — this tiny, wrinkled, screaming bundle of life — and I felt something shift in my chest. Suddenly I wasn’t the most important person in my own world anymore. This little girl was. I looked at Genie, exhausted and glowing, and I said:

> “She’s perfect.”

Genie smiled and said:

> “Of course she is. She’s got my genes.”

I laughed. But I also cried. I didn’t want her to see it, so I turned away and pretended to be looking out the window. But Genie saw. Genie always saw.

Our second daughter came two years later. Same hospital, same room, same feeling of terrified wonder. I held her and thought: I have two of them now. Two little people who depend on me. Two reasons to get up in the morning. Two reasons to be better than I was.

The years that followed were a blur of diapers and doctor’s appointments and school plays and sleepless nights. Genie handled most of it. She was the one who remembered the pediatrician appointments. She was the one who packed the lunches and signed the permission slips and stayed up late sewing Halloween costumes. I was the one working the overtime shifts, coming home exhausted, falling asleep on the couch. I told myself I was providing for them. And I was. But Genie was providing something I couldn’t: presence.

She’d sit with our girls for hours, reading them stories, answering their questions, listening to their tales about school and friends and the small dramas that filled their world. She was patient in ways I wasn’t. She was soft when I was hard. When I’d raise my voice, she’d lower hers. When I wanted to punish, she wanted to teach. We balanced each other out.

One particular night has stayed with me. I’d had a rough day at work — a machine broke down, a foreman chewed me out, I came home with my jaw tight and my shoulders aching. The girls were laughing too loud, running through the living room, and I snapped. I yelled at them to settle down. They went quiet. I saw their faces fall, and I felt like the biggest piece of garbage in the world.

Later that night, after I’d apologized and put them to bed, Genie came and sat next to me on the couch. She was tired — I could see it in her eyes. But she was still there. Still beside me. I said:

> “I don’t know why you put up with me.”

She leaned her head on my shoulder and said:

> “Because I know who you are, John. Even when you forget.”

In that moment, sitting in our cramped little living room with the TV playing softly in the background and the sound of our daughters breathing through the thin walls, I felt something I’d never felt before. Contentment. Not happiness that comes and goes. Something deeper. Something that stays.

I didn’t know, sitting there, what was coming for us. I didn’t know that my body would betray me. I didn’t know that I’d lose my way so completely that I wouldn’t recognize myself. I didn’t know that the storms ahead would test everything we’d built.

But I knew one thing: Genie would be there. And my daughters would be there. And as long as I had them, I had something worth fighting for.
