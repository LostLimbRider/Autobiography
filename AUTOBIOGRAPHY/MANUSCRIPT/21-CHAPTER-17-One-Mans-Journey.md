# **Chapter 17: One Man's Journey**

So here I am.

I'm sitting at my desk, looking out the window at a tree I've been watching for three years. It's an oak, maybe a hundred years old, with branches that reach out like arms stretching after a long sleep. I've watched it lose its leaves in the fall and go bare through the winter. I've watched the buds come back in the spring and the full canopy spread in the summer. I've watched it through every season, through every change, through cancer and chemo and amputation and recovery. It's still there. Still standing. Still doing what a tree does.

I like to think I'm a little bit like that tree.

I'm not saying I'm wise or ancient or majestic. I'm saying I've been through a lot of seasons, and I'm still here. Still standing. Or at least, still sitting upright in this chair, with my prosthetic propped against the desk and my dog snoring at my feet.

Roxy is curled up under the desk. She's been there for the last hour, her head on my foot, her body rising and falling with each breath. Every now and then she kicks in her sleep, chasing some dream-squirrel through some dream-yard. I look down at her and I think about how many times she's been right here, in this exact spot, while I was writing, thinking, crying, laughing, or just staring at the wall trying to figure out how to keep going.

She doesn't care what I'm doing. She just wants to be near me. That's her whole philosophy. Be near. Be loyal. Be present. I could learn a lot from that dog.

I've lived more lives than most people. I don't say that to brag. I say it because it's true, and it's strange to sit with. When I look back at everything I've been through, it feels like I'm looking at a whole shelf of different people, all of whom share my name and my face, but each of whom lived through something different.

There was the young John, the drinking John, the one who showed up to work hungover and spent his nights at the bottom of a bottle. That John was lost. He was angry. He was looking for something to fill a hole he didn't know how to name. He thought alcohol was the answer. It took him years to realize it was the problem.

There was the John who had the heart attack. The one who felt his chest cave in and thought: *This is it. This is how I die.* That John was forty-six years old, lying on a hospital gurney, watching the ceiling lights blur past as they wheeled him into surgery. That John made a promise to God that he wasn't sure he'd keep.

There was the John who got clean. The one who sat in recovery meetings and felt like an imposter. The one who white-knuckled his way through the first year, not because he was strong, but because he was too ashamed to go back to the life he'd left. That John learned that shame can either crush you or push you forward. He chose forward.

There was the John who heard the word *cancer* come out of a doctor's mouth. The John who sat in a small examination room with bad lighting and a poster about prostate health on the wall, trying to process what the doctor was saying while the words bounced around his head like pinballs. The John who went through chemo, lost his hair, lost his energy, lost his sense of taste, lost everything except the will to keep fighting.

And there was the John who lost his leg. That John woke up from surgery and looked down at a body he didn't recognize. That John spent months learning how to walk again, how to balance, how to navigate a world that wasn't built for people like him. That John cried more times than he'd ever admit. That John also learned something he couldn't have learned any other way: that losing a limb doesn't mean losing yourself.

All those Johns live inside me now. They're not gone. They're not ghosts. They're chapters in a book I'm still writing. Some of those chapters are hard to read. Some of them I'd rather tear out and burn. But I don't. Because they're mine. And because every single one of those Johns — the drunk, the heart attack patient, the cancer survivor, the amputee — every one of them is part of the reason I'm still sitting here today.

I didn't survive any of that alone. I had people who refused to let me go. My family. My daughters, who looked at me with love even when I didn't deserve it. My wife, who stuck with me through all of it — through sickness and health, through poverty and plenty, through the worst days and the long nights. She never once made me feel like a burden. She never once looked at me like I was broken. She looked at me like I was still the man she married, even when I didn't recognize myself.

I had my faith. I don't talk about it a lot, because faith is a private thing for me. But I had moments — lying in a hospital bed, staring at the ceiling, the machines beeping around me — when I felt something bigger than me in the room. I don't know how to explain it. I don't try. But I know that in the moments when I was too weak to carry myself, something carried me. I've never needed to name it to believe it.

And I had the people I met along the way. The therapists. The doctors. The nurses. The guy in the support group who laughed like a cement mixer and wore a shirt with a skeleton on a motorcycle. The recovery meeting friends who called me when I was struggling and told me to show up, just show up, even if I didn't feel like it. The strangers who came up to me after a speech and told me their own stories, trusting me with their pain because I'd trusted them with mine.

We don't make it on our own. That's something I believe more now than I ever did. We're not meant to. The strong, silent, lone-wolf type — that's a myth. A good story. But the truth is, we need each other. We need someone to scratch our back when we're hurting. Someone to tell us to keep going when we want to stop. Someone to sit in the dark with us until the light comes back.

If you're reading this and you're in the middle of your own fight, I want you to know something. You don't have to be brave. You don't have to be strong. You don't have to have it all figured out. You just have to not quit. That's it. That's the whole thing. Quitting is permanent. Everything else is temporary. The pain — it passes. The fear — it fades. The uncertainty — it clears, eventually. But if you quit, you never get to see what's on the other side.

I've seen the other side. It's worth it.

I'm not saying everything is perfect. It's not. I still have bad days. I still have phantom pains that wake me up at night. I still have moments when I look at my prosthetic and feel a wave of grief for the leg I used to have. I still struggle. I still doubt. I still wonder sometimes if I've got what it takes to keep going.

But I also have a woman who loves me. A woman who's been with me through all of it and still chooses to stay. I have daughters who make me proud. Daughters who grew into strong, kind, stubborn women who know how to fight for what they want. I have a dog who wiggles her whole back end when I scratch the spot above her tail, who curls up under my desk while I write, who greets me at the door like I've been gone for years even when I've only been gone for ten minutes.

I have a community of amputees who ride motorcycles and laugh too loud and remind me every time I see them that life didn't end when I lost my leg. It changed. But it didn't end.

I have a faith that carried me when I couldn't carry myself. I have a story that isn't mine anymore — it belongs to everyone who needs to hear it.

And I have four words that I say to myself every morning, whether I believe them or not.

I Can. I Will.

I don't know what's next. Life has surprised me enough times that I've stopped trying to predict it. I used to make plans. I used to map out my future and tell myself exactly how things were going to go. Then life came along and showed me that I don't run the show. I don't know if tomorrow will bring a new opportunity or a new challenge. I don't know if my health will hold up. I don't know what the next chapter of this story looks like.

But I know this: whatever comes, I'll face it.

I'll put on my prosthetic. I'll scratch Roxy's back. I'll call my daughters. I'll ride with the Lost Limb Riders. I'll stand in front of a room full of strangers and tell them the truth. And I'll keep saying the same four words, over and over, until I don't need to say them anymore.

I Can. I Will.

And you can too.
