# **Chapter 12: Family**

If you asked me what saved my life, I’d tell you: my family.

Not the doctors. Not the medicine. Not the surgeries or the treatments or the prosthetics. Those things helped. Those things were necessary. But they weren’t the reason I kept going when every instinct told me to stop. The reason was sitting in the waiting room. The reason was holding my hand. The reason was driving me to appointments and making sure I took my pills and telling me I was still the same man she married, even when I felt like half of one.

The reason was Genie.

I remember one morning, about two weeks after the amputation. I was in a dark place. The kind of dark where you don’t want to talk to anyone, don’t want to eat, don’t want to get out of bed. I was lying there, staring at the wall, feeling sorry for myself. I heard Genie come into the room. I didn’t turn around. She sat down on the edge of the bed, put her hand on my shoulder, and said:

> “John. You need to get up.”

I said:

> “I don’t want to.”

She said:

> “I know. But you need to.”

I didn’t move. She sat there for a full minute, not saying anything, just letting her hand rest on my shoulder. Then she said something I’ll never forget:

> “I didn’t marry you for your leg. I married you for you. And you’re still here. So get up.”

I got up.

That was Genie. She never let me wallow. She never let me quit. She was gentle when I needed gentle and tough when I needed tough, and she always knew the difference. She was with me for every surgery, every treatment, every breakdown. She was the one who made sure the bills got paid when I couldn’t work. She was the one who talked to the insurance companies, who argued with the doctors, who filled the prescriptions, who cooked the meals I couldn’t eat and cleaned up the mess when I couldn’t hold them down. She never wavered. Not once.

People say “for better or for worse” at weddings, but they don’t really know what “worse” looks like. Genie knew. She lived it. And she never walked away.

My daughters — they were the other reason. Watching them grow into strong, capable women gave me something to fight for when I wanted to quit. I wanted to see their weddings. I wanted to meet my grandchildren. I wanted to be there for them the way they had been there for me. My oldest daughter, she came to visit me in the hospital after the cancer diagnosis, and she sat in the chair next to my bed with this look on her face — the look of a daughter trying not to cry in front of her father. She said:

> “You’re going to be okay, Dad.”

I said:

> “I know.”

I didn’t know. But I knew she needed to hear me say it. And sometimes, saying something out loud is the first step toward making it true.

My youngest daughter was the one who made me laugh when I didn’t think I could. She came to visit after the amputation, and she was trying so hard to be normal, to act like everything was fine. She was telling me about her job, about some drama with a coworker, and she was using her hands the way she always does when she talks. She knocked over a cup of water on the nightstand. Water went everywhere. She jumped up, grabbed a towel, and started apologizing. I started laughing. She looked at me like I was crazy. I said:

> “You’re still clumsy. That’s good. Some things never change.”

She laughed. And for a few minutes, we were just a father and a daughter, not a patient and a visitor.

My parents came to see me too. My father wasn’t the kind of man who said much, but he showed up. He’d sit in the corner of the room, quiet, just being there. Sometimes he’d stay for an hour, sometimes longer. He didn’t need to say anything. His presence was enough. My mother would bring food — casseroles, mostly, because that’s what mothers do. They bring food. As if a tuna casserole could cure cancer. But it wasn’t about the food. It was about the effort. It was about the message: *I’m thinking about you. I care. I’m here.*

My siblings called. My cousins checked in. Friends I hadn’t spoken to in years reached out. Some of them I hadn’t talked to since before the drinking got bad, but they heard I was sick, and they called. That meant something. It meant that the connections I’d built over a lifetime — even the ones I’d neglected — were still there, waiting to be picked back up.

There was one friend, a guy named Dave, who I’d known since we were kids. We’d drifted apart over the years, the way people do. Life gets busy. You move. You lose touch. But Dave heard about the amputation, and he drove three hours to come see me. He walked into my hospital room with a six‑pack of root beer and a deck of cards. He said:

> “You look like hell.”

I said:

> “Thanks. You too.”

We played cards for two hours. Didn’t talk about the leg. Didn’t talk about the cancer. Talked about old times. Laughed about stupid things we did when we were teenagers. When he left, he shook my hand and said:

> “Call me if you need anything.”

I knew he meant it. And knowing that — knowing there was someone out there who would drop everything to come play cards with me in a hospital room — that made more difference than any medication.

I remember a specific moment during the chemo treatments. I was lying on the couch, too weak to move, too nauseous to sleep. Genie was in the kitchen, and I could hear her on the phone with someone. Her voice was low, and I could tell she didn’t think I could hear her. She was saying:

> “I don’t know if he’s going to make it. But I’m not going to let him give up.”

I lay there and listened to her talk, and I realized that she was carrying more than I knew. She was being strong for me, but she was scared too. She was fighting her own battle — the battle of watching someone she loved suffer and not being able to stop it. And she never complained. She never said, “This is too hard.” She just kept going. Day after day. Appointment after appointment. Crisis after crisis.

That’s love. Not the kind you see in movies. The kind that keeps showing up even when there’s nothing in it for you.

My daughter’s wedding happened during this whole mess. I was between treatments, and I wasn’t sure I’d have the strength to walk her down the aisle. I told her I might need a cane. She said:

> “I don’t care if you need a walker, Dad. I want you there.”

So I went. I wore my best suit. I borrowed a cane from a friend. I walked her down the aisle one slow step at a time, and I didn’t cry until I handed her off to her new husband. But I cried then. So did she. So did Genie. So did half the people in the church. It wasn’t just a wedding. It was a victory. It was proof that I had made it to a day I wasn’t sure I’d ever see.

I learned something through all of this: family isn’t about blood. It’s about who shows up when everything falls apart. There are people I’m related to who I don’t talk to anymore. People who share my last name and my DNA but weren’t there when I needed them. And there are people with no blood connection who I’d trust with my life. Dave. Some of the nurses. Carlos, my prosthetist. A few of the guys from my old job who called every week just to check in.

That’s the real family. The people who stand beside you when it’s hard. The people who don’t run when things get ugly. The people who love you not because you’re perfect, but because you’re theirs.

I’ve been blessed with a lot of those people. Genie. My daughters. My parents. My true friends. Every one of them played a part in keeping me alive, whether they knew it or not. They were the reason I got out of bed. They were the reason I kept fighting. They were the reason I’m still here, sitting at this keyboard, writing these words.

And I try every day to be that person for someone else. I call my daughters just to say hello. I check on my friends when they’re going through hard times. I show up. I don’t ask if they need me — I just come. Because that’s what family does. Family shows up.

Not because it’s easy. Because it matters.
