# **Chapter 8: Cancer**

I was sitting in the exam room on a cold Tuesday morning in November. The kind of morning where the sky is gray and the air has that bite that tells you winter is coming. I’d been having some issues for a few weeks. Nothing I wanted to admit was serious. Just tired. Losing weight without trying. A cough that wouldn’t quit. I figured it was stress. I figured it was age. I figured it was everything else I’d already been through, just piling up like junk mail.

The doctor came in. Dr. Morrison. Good man. Straight shooter. The kind of doctor who looks you in the eye when he talks to you, which is the first sign that what he’s about to say is not going to be easy to hear. He sat down on the little rolling stool, held a file folder in his hands, and looked at me for a second without saying anything. Then he said:

> “John, the biopsy came back. It’s cancer.”

And the room got quiet.

Not the kind of quiet where you could hear a pin drop. The kind of quiet where sound itself gets up and leaves the building. I could see his mouth moving, telling me more. Something about the type. Something about the stage. Something about treatment options. But the words were coming through like a radio station with bad reception. Every few syllables broke through — “lung” — “aggressive” — “we caught it” — and then static again.

I remember thinking: *Again? Really? How much more am I supposed to take?*

I didn’t say it out loud. I just nodded. Shook his hand. Thanked him, because that’s what you do. Then I walked out to the waiting room where Genie was sitting, and she took one look at my face and she knew. She didn’t ask. She just stood up, walked over, and put her arms around me. We stood there for a long time. People walking past us. Nurses at the desk. Someone calling a name over the intercom. And we just stood there holding each other.

The treatments started two weeks later.

Chemotherapy — everybody thinks they know what it looks like. You’ve seen the movies. You’ve seen the commercials. The patient sits in a comfortable chair, blanket over their lap, IV dripping, a nurse with a kind smile checking in every few minutes. And there’s some truth to that. The chairs were comfortable. The nurses were kind. But what the movies don’t show you is the smell. There’s a smell in a chemo ward that I still remember. It’s not medicine. It’s not antiseptic. It’s something else. Something chemical and clinical that gets into your clothes and your hair and your skin, and no matter how many showers you take, you can still smell it on yourself when you’re trying to fall asleep at night.

The first session, I thought I was ready. I’d done my research. I’d talked to people who’d been through it. I knew what the side effects were supposed to be. Knowledge is helpful, but knowledge doesn’t prepare you for the feeling of poison going into your veins and knowing that the poison is the only thing standing between you and dying.

By the third session, I couldn’t keep food down. By the fifth, my hair was coming out in the shower in clumps. I remember standing in front of the bathroom mirror, looking at myself, not recognizing the person looking back. He was thinner. Paler. His eyes had dark circles under them that wouldn’t go away. He looked old. He looked tired. He looked like someone who was fighting a war in a country where he didn’t speak the language.

Genie came to every appointment. Every single one. She sat in the chair next to mine, reading her book, holding my hand when the nausea hit hard. She brought snacks I couldn’t eat and water I couldn’t keep down, and she never once complained about the hours she spent in that room. One afternoon, after a particularly rough session, I looked at her and said:

> “I don’t know if I can keep doing this.”

She looked me right in the eye and said:

> “You can. And you will. Because we’re not done yet.”

She didn’t say “you’re not done yet.” She said “we’re not done yet.” And that made all the difference in the world.

My daughters came to visit on weekends. They’d sit with me, watch TV shows I wasn’t really watching, tell me stories about their jobs and their lives. They tried to act normal, like everything was fine, like their dad wasn’t sitting in a recliner with a bucket next to him in case he needed to throw up. I tried to act normal too. I cracked jokes. I asked questions. I made sure they knew I was still in there, still fighting, still their old man. We were all pretending, and we all knew it. But pretending is better than giving up. Pretending is a form of hope. It’s saying: *I’m going to act like this is temporary, because if I act like it’s temporary, maybe it will be.*

There was one night — I’ll never forget it — when I was alone in the house. Genie had gone to the pharmacy to pick up a prescription. The house was quiet. I was lying in bed, staring at the ceiling, and I felt the fear wash over me like a wave. Not the surface-level fear you feel when you’re watching a scary movie. The deep fear. The kind that sits in your bones and whispers: *What if this doesn’t work? What if this is it?*

I started to cry.

I don’t say that lightly. I wasn’t raised in a house where men cried. My father was a good man, but he was from a generation that believed tears were a sign of weakness. I’d carried that with me my whole life, this idea that you push through, you suck it up, you don’t let anyone see you break. But that night, alone in the dark, I broke. I cried for myself. I cried for Genie. I cried for my daughters. I cried for all the years I’d wasted being angry and scared and stubborn when I could have been grateful.

And then I stopped crying. I wiped my face. I sat up. I said out loud, to no one in particular:

> “I’m not done.”

I don’t know who I was saying it to. God. The universe. Myself. But saying it made it real. Saying it made it a promise.

The months that followed were hard. Harder than anything I’d done before, and I’d already done some hard things. There were days when I didn’t have the strength to get out of bed. There were days when eating a single cracker felt like a victory. There were days when hope felt like a luxury I couldn’t afford, like buying a sports car when you can’t pay the rent.

But there were good days too. Days when the nausea lifted and I could taste food again. Days when a friend would call and tell a joke that made me laugh so hard I forgot, for a minute, what I was going through. Days when Genie would crawl into bed next to me and just hold my hand, not saying anything, just being there, and that was enough.

Six months after the diagnosis, I went in for the scan that would tell us if it had worked. I remember sitting in the waiting room, trying to read a magazine, not reading a single word. Genie was next to me, squeezing my hand so hard I think she left bruises. When Dr. Morrison came out, he had the same look on his face that he’d had that first day. The look that says: *I have news.*

He sat down and said:

> “John, the scan is clear. No evidence of active cancer.”

I didn’t react the way I thought I would. I didn’t cry. I didn’t cheer. I just sat there, letting the words sink in. *Clear. No evidence. Active cancer.* They were just words. But they were the best words I’d ever heard.

Genie threw her arms around me. I hugged her back. And then I walked out of that hospital, got in the car, and sat there for ten minutes before I could bring myself to start the engine.

I beat cancer. I say that like it’s simple, but it wasn’t. It was months of fear, pain, and uncertainty. It was waking up every day not knowing if the treatments were working. It was trying to find hope when hope felt like a luxury I couldn’t afford. It was leaning on the people who loved me, even when I didn’t feel like I deserved to be loved.

But I beat it. And I thought: *That’s it. I’ve paid my dues. Life is done with me.*

I was wrong again.
