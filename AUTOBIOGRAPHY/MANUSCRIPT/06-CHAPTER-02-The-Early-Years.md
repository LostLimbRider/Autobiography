# **Chapter 2: The Early Years**

My first job was at a body shop.

I was probably sixteen, maybe seventeen. I didn’t know anything about cars, but I knew I wanted to work. The owner was a guy named Frank — old‑school, bald, always had a cigarette behind his ear that he never seemed to light. He looked me up and down, took measure of me in about two seconds, and said:

> “You ever turn a wrench?”

I said:

> “No, sir.”

He said:

> “Good. Then I don’t have to break any bad habits.”

He put me on cleanup. Sweeping, organizing, hauling out scrap metal, degreasing parts in a tank of solvent that burned your hands if you left them in too long. The shop smelled like bondo, paint thinner, and years of hard use. There was always a radio playing somewhere — classic rock, low enough to ignore, loud enough to keep you company.

I came home every night with black gunk caked under my fingernails and grease in my hair. My mother made me leave my boots on the porch before I came in the house.

I didn’t mind the work. I liked using my hands. I liked that when you finished something, you could see what you’d done. A clean floor. A stacked shelf. A part that was covered in grime when you started and gleaming when you were done. There was dignity in it. Nobody had to tell you if you’d done a good job — you could see it for yourself.

There were other guys working there. Older men who’d been turning wrenches since before I was born. They didn’t talk much. They’d communicate in grunts and nods. But they noticed when you worked hard. Every now and then, one of them would toss me a ten‑dollar bill at the end of the day and say:

> “Don’t spend it all in one place.”

That meant more to me than any paycheck.

Frank didn’t say much either, but when he did, it counted. One day I was struggling to get a bolt loose — must have been seized from years of rust. I was cursing under my breath, about to give up. He walked over, stood next to me for a second, and said:

> “You’re fighting it. Stop fighting it. Get some penetrating oil, let it sit, come back. The bolt ain’t going anywhere.”

I thought he was talking about the bolt. Later I realized he was talking about everything.

That job taught me something I’ve carried my whole life: you don’t get anywhere by being the smartest guy in the room. You get there by being the one who doesn’t quit when it gets hard.

I worked a lot of jobs after that. Construction, framing houses in the summer heat. I remember one site where the temperature hit a hundred and the foreman kept bringing us water whether we asked for it or not, because he knew we wouldn’t stop on our own. We were setting trusses that day — swinging them into place, balancing on wall plates, nailing them down before the wind caught them. By noon my gloves were soaked through and my shirt looked like I’d been standing in the rain. But nobody quit, so I didn’t either.

Warehouses after that, loading trucks until my back screamed and my knuckles were raw from strapping down freight. I remember one night we had a trailer that needed to be loaded before morning — sixty skids, tight fit, and the forklift broke down two hours in. The supervisor said we could wait for repairs, come back tomorrow. I said:

> “Give me a hand truck. I’ll get it done.”

He looked at me like I was crazy. But I was already dragging the first skid toward the trailer.

I worked until three in the morning, one skid at a time, sweating through my shirt in the middle of December. When I finished, the place was quiet except for the hum of the freezer units. I sat on the loading dock for a minute, just breathing. My hands were blistered. My shoulders felt like they’d been pulled apart and put back wrong.

But the truck was loaded and ready to go. And when the supervisor showed up the next morning, he walked past it, looked inside, and didn’t say a word. Just gave me a nod. That was enough.

Delivery driving after that, living out of a van, eating gas‑station sandwiches between stops, sleeping in the truck when the drive was too long to turn around. I worked whatever I could find. I didn’t care what it was. If someone needed a guy, I was the guy.

I never said no. That was my rule, even when I should have. If a boss asked who could stay late, my hand went up before I thought about it. If a crew needed someone to show up on Saturday, I was there. I measured myself by what I could produce, what I could provide, what I could get done. My word was my bond, and my work was my signature.

Some people called it a work ethic. Maybe it was. But looking back, I think part of it was fear. Plain and simple. I was afraid that if I stopped, even for a minute, I’d fall behind. And once you fall behind, it’s hard to catch up. I’d seen guys who took their foot off the gas — they never got it back.

There were good years in there too. Late nights with friends I don’t see anymore. Weekends that felt like they’d never end. Cold beer and louder laughter than you’d hear anywhere else. A few of us would pile into somebody’s truck and just drive, windows down, nowhere to be, nothing to prove. I remember sitting on a tailgate one night, looking up at the stars, thinking this was what life was supposed to feel like. No plan. No pressure. Just being alive and young and dumb enough to think it would last forever.

The kind of freedom you only have before life hands you the bills, the responsibilities, the hard choices that narrow your world down to what really matters.

I felt invincible. I thought I had all the time in the world.

I didn’t know I was building habits that would take years to undo. I didn’t know that pushing through every wall would eventually push back. I didn’t know that the same drive that made me a good worker would one day make it hard to ask for help, hard to slow down, hard to admit I was in over my head.

I look back on those years with a lot of fondness, but I don’t romanticize them. I made plenty of bad choices along with the good ones. I burned bridges I shouldn’t have. I let pride get in the way more than once. But even the mistakes taught me something.

They were preparation. Every job, every person, every mistake, every night I pushed through when I should have slept — it was all leading somewhere.

I just didn’t know where yet. But I’d find out soon enough.
