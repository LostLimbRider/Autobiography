# **Chapter 10: The Stump**

Nobody warns you what a stump looks like.

After a below‑the‑knee amputation, the shape of what’s left is strange. Muscle, soft tissue, swelling, surgical scars. It doesn’t look like a leg anymore. It looks like something else entirely. Something unfinished. Something that’s still healing and might not ever look right again.

The first time the bandages came off for good, I was sitting on the edge of the hospital bed. A nurse named Rachel was unwrapping the gauze, layer by layer, talking to me about discharge instructions, trying to keep me distracted. I wasn’t distracted. I was watching her hands, watching the gauze come off, watching more and more of my new body come into view. When the last layer peeled away, I looked down at what was left of my right leg.

And I laughed.

I couldn’t help it. After all the serious shit — the illness, the addiction, the heart attack, the cancer — here I was, missing a leg, and the damn thing looked like a giant penis. I mean, that’s the first thing that popped into my head. I looked at this swollen, rounded, scarred piece of flesh and thought: *That’s the ugliest thing I’ve ever seen, and I’ve seen some ugly things.*

Rachel stopped mid‑sentence and looked at me.

> “Are you okay, John?”

I was laughing too hard to answer. I waved my hand, trying to catch my breath, and finally managed to say:

> “I’m fine. I just — it’s funny.”

She looked at the stump. Looked at me. Looked at the stump again. And then she started laughing too. Not a polite nurse laugh. A real laugh. She shook her head and said:

> “I’ve been doing this job for twelve years, and I’ve never heard anyone describe it that way.”

I said:

> “Well, somebody had to say it.”

When I told Genie later that day, she laughed even harder than I did. Then she shook her head, wiped her eyes, and said:

> “Only you, John. Only you could look at something like that and make a joke.”

I told a few close friends. They laughed. I told my daughters the next time they came to visit. They groaned, covered their faces, and told me to stop. My youngest daughter said:

> “Dad, that’s disgusting.”

I said:

> “It’s not disgusting. It’s honest. And if I can’t laugh at my own body, what can I laugh at?”

She didn’t have an answer for that.

Humor became my coping mechanism. It still is. People ask me how I stay so positive, and I tell them the truth: I’m not always positive. Some days I wake up and the first thing I see when I sit up in bed is that stump. Some days I look at it and feel a wave of anger so hot it surprises me. Some days I’m tired — bone‑tired, soul‑tired — of dealing with it. But anger doesn’t help. Being pissed off doesn’t put the leg back. So I laugh instead. Because laughing feels better than crying. And because if I can make someone else laugh about it too, then maybe they’ll see that this isn’t the end of the world.

Learning to take care of the stump was a whole education in itself. You have to wash it every day with special soap. You have to dry it thoroughly, because moisture leads to infection, and infection with a residual limb can land you back in the hospital. You have to check the skin for pressure sores, for redness, for any sign that the prosthetic socket is rubbing in the wrong place. You have to put a special liner on it — a gel sleeve that rolls up over the stump like a sock — before you can even think about putting the prosthetic on. The first time I tried to put the liner on myself, I dropped it three times. It kept rolling up on itself like a stubborn tube sock. I sat there on the edge of the bed, liner in one hand, staring at it, and said:

> “This is ridiculous.”

Genie walked in, picked up the liner, and rolled it onto my stump without saying a word. Then she handed me the prosthetic and said:

> “You got the rest?”

I nodded. She walked out. And I sat there thinking: *She’s a saint.*

The prosthetic socket rubbing raw — that was a recurring problem in the beginning. The skin on a residual limb is not designed to bear weight. It’s soft. Sensitive. It’s used to being protected by the rest of the leg. Now suddenly it’s pressed against a hard carbon‑fiber socket, taking pounds of pressure with every step. The first few weeks, I would walk for maybe ten minutes before the pain became too much. I’d take the prosthetic off and find red marks, blisters, raw spots where the skin had broken down. Carlos, my prosthetist, would adjust the fit. The next day, new spots. It was a constant battle. A constant negotiation between my body and the machine that was supposed to replace what my body had lost.

And then there was the phantom pain.

Nobody warned me about phantom pain. Or maybe they did, and I didn’t understand what they meant. You hear the word “phantom” and you think it’s going to be subtle. A ghost sensation. A faint memory of a limb that used to be there. That’s not what it is. Phantom pain is real pain. Sharp pain. Electric pain. The feeling of a cramp in a muscle that doesn’t exist. The feeling of someone driving a nail through a foot that was amputated six months ago. It comes out of nowhere, usually at night, and it leaves you gasping. The first time it hit me, I was lying in bed, half asleep, and suddenly I felt this searing pain in my right big toe. I grabbed my leg — my good leg — and then remembered that my right foot wasn’t there anymore. I sat up in bed, sweating, confused, in agony, and I said out loud:

> “That’s not possible.”

Genie woke up. She asked what was wrong. I told her. She didn’t say anything. She just rubbed my back until the pain passed, which it did — slowly, reluctantly, like a guest who didn’t want to leave.

The doctors gave me medication for it. Gabapentin. It helped, but it also made me foggy. I had to decide which was worse: the pain or the fog. Most days, I chose the fog. Some days, I chose the pain.

I started meeting other amputees at physical therapy. There was a guy named Mike, about my age, who’d lost his leg to a motorcycle accident. He was six months ahead of me in the process. He could walk with a cane. He could drive. He could climb stairs. I watched him move and thought: *That’s going to be me.* We talked one afternoon in the waiting room. I told him I was having trouble with the phantom pain. He nodded like he’d heard it a hundred times before, and said:

> “It gets better. Not all at once. But it gets better. You just have to give it time.”

I asked him:

> “How much time?”

He shrugged and said:

> “Different for everybody. For me, it took about a year before I stopped wanting to punch a wall every time it happened.”

A year. I sat with that. A year of phantom pain. A year of socket adjustments. A year of learning how to move through the world in a body that didn’t move the way it used to. It sounded like a long time. But I’d already been through so much that a year didn’t scare me.

There was one moment, about three months after the amputation, when I was sitting on the back porch, looking out at the yard, and I realized I wasn’t thinking about my leg. For the first time since the surgery, I had gone a full hour without thinking about the stump, the prosthetic, the pain, the inconvenience. I was just sitting. Just existing. Like a normal person. I sat there and let that feeling soak in. It was small. It was ordinary. But it felt like a victory.

That’s the thing about losing a body part. It doesn’t just change how you walk. It changes how you think. It changes how you see yourself. You spend every hour of every day aware of this new reality, like a stone in your shoe that you can’t take out. And then one day, you realize you’ve stopped noticing the stone. Not because it’s gone, but because you’ve learned to walk with it.

Children are the most honest. I had a neighbor’s little boy, maybe five years old, come up to me in the driveway one afternoon. He looked at my leg — at the metal pylon and the prosthetic foot sticking out below my shorts — and he pointed and asked:

> “What happened to your leg?”

His mother started to apologize. I waved her off. I knelt down — as best I could — and said:

> “It got sick, so the doctors had to take it off. But I got a new one. See?”

I tapped the carbon fiber. He stared at it for a second, then looked up at me with the serious expression only a five‑year‑old can have, and said:

> “Cool.”

Then he ran off to play.

*Cool.* I thought about that word for the rest of the day. That’s the word a kid uses when he sees something he likes. Not scary. Not sad. Cool. Maybe children understand something that adults forget: that life changes, bodies change, and that’s not always a tragedy. Sometimes it’s just different. And different can be cool.

I’ve had people tell me they don’t know how I stay so positive. The truth is, I’m not always positive. Some days I’m angry. Some days I’m tired. Some days I look at my stump and I’m pissed off that this is my life now. But anger doesn’t help. Being pissed off doesn’t put the leg back.

So I laugh instead. Because laughing feels better than crying. And because if I can make someone else laugh about it too, then maybe they’ll see that this isn’t the end of the world.

It’s just a different world.

And you can still find plenty of reasons to smile in it.
